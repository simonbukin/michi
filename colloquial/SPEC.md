# Colloquial Japanese Patterns: A Reference for Comprehension
## Full Specification and Table of Contents

---

## Overview

This document is the complete editorial specification for *Colloquial Japanese Patterns: A Reference for Comprehension* — a pattern-dictionary reference book for learners of Japanese at approximately JLPT N4 level who want to understand how native speakers actually speak and write casually. It is the direct input for generating the book's content.

**What this book is:**
A lookup reference organized by colloquial surface form, anchored to the formal equivalent the learner already knows. Every entry bridges textbook Japanese to real-world usage. The book is comprehension-oriented: the reader does not need to produce these patterns, only recognize and decode them.

**What this book is not:**
- A grammar textbook (it assumes N4 grammar knowledge throughout)
- A phrase book (patterns are explained structurally, not memorized as fixed phrases)
- A production guide (examples show register and social context so the reader understands what they're hearing, not to enable them to produce the same)

**Target reader:** Has completed Genki I–II or equivalent. Understands plain form grammar in principle. Struggles to follow native-speed drama, anime, variety TV, manga dialogue, or casual digital text.

---

## Format Philosophy

The book follows the Chino / Kodansha sentence-pattern dictionary format as its closest ancestor, with two key structural departures:

1. **Colloquial form as headword, formal form as secondary.** Every entry leads with what the learner heard or saw, not with the textbook form. The formal equivalent is labeled clearly beneath. This enables lookup from the point of confusion.

2. **The Gap Note as a first-class element.** Every entry explicitly states what textbooks say (or fail to say) about this pattern, and where that creates a comprehension failure. This is the central editorial voice of the book.

**Script policy:** No romaji anywhere in the book. Japanese text uses kanji and kana throughout. Furigana is provided on the first occurrence of any vocabulary item above JLPT N4 level within each entry. All example sentences and dialogues are in kanji/kana with an English translation on the following line. There is no intermediate romaji line.

## Spoken / Written Distinction

This is a fundamental structural decision for the book.

### Sections 1–8: Spoken Japanese (主要部)

All patterns in the main body of the book are **spoken-first**. They describe phenomena that manifest in real-time speech — patterns a listener needs to recognize when watching drama, anime, or variety TV, or listening to a conversation. Some of these patterns also appear in written casual Japanese (e.g., trailing けど in a LINE message), but their primary context is spoken. Where a pattern has a notable written-casual variant or divergence, a **Written note** block appears at the end of that entry pointing to the appendix.

Spoken patterns are identified by their occurrence in:
- Drama and film dialogue
- Anime (especially slice-of-life, romance, contemporary settings)
- Variety TV and talk shows (spontaneous speech)
- Radio and podcast conversation
- Direct informal conversation

### Appendix: Written Casual Japanese (付録：書き言葉のくだけた表現)

The appendix covers patterns that are **written-primary or written-only** — conventions that exist specifically because writing lacks prosody, and speakers have developed orthographic and lexical tools to compensate. These are not "casual speech written down." They are a distinct register.

Written casual patterns are identified by their occurrence in:
- LINE and messaging apps
- Twitter/X and SNS posts
- Internet forums (5ch, etc.)
- Manga speech bubbles
- Casual email and digital notes

**Cross-reference rule:** Any spoken entry whose pattern also has a significant written-casual realization carries a `→ See Appendix: [entry code]` note. Appendix entries that derive from spoken patterns carry a `← Spoken base: [section.entry]` note.

### Formula Notation

Formulas use the following abbreviations. Keep formulas to one line.

| Symbol | Meaning |
|--------|---------|
| `[V]` | Any verb in dictionary form |
| `[V-te]` | Verb in て-form (unvoiced stem) |
| `[V-de]` | Verb in て-form (voiced stem, i.e., で-form) |
| `[V-ta]` | Verb in た-form (plain past) |
| `[V-nai]` | Verb in ない-form (plain negative) |
| `[V-dict]` | Verb in dictionary form specifically |
| `[i-adj]` | い-adjective (any form) |
| `[na-adj]` | な-adjective stem |
| `[N]` | Noun |
| `[S]` | Full sentence or clause |
| `∅` | Zero — element is dropped/absent |
| `→` | Transforms to |
| `( )` | Optional element — may be present or absent |
| `/ ` | Alternates — either form is possible |

**Example formulas:**

`[V-te] + しまう → [V-te] + ちゃう` (unvoiced)
`[V-de] + しまう → [V-de] + じゃう` (voiced)

`[S (plain)] + んだ` (explanatory frame — no formal→colloquial arrow; んだ is itself the pattern)

`[V-nai root] + ければ(ならない) → [V-nai root] + なきゃ(いけない)`

### Register Tags

Every entry carries a **Register** field with two components: a frequency tag and a gender/age tendency tag where relevant.

**Frequency tags** (always present):

| Tag | Meaning |
|-----|---------|
| `★★★ core` | Extremely high frequency; will be encountered in almost any casual speech |
| `★★ common` | High frequency; regular in casual conversation and media |
| `★ marked` | Lower frequency or socially marked; important to recognize but not universal |

**Gender/age tendency tags** (include only when relevant; omit for neutral patterns):

| Tag | Meaning |
|-----|---------|
| `masculine tendency` | More common in male speech; use by women is marked |
| `feminine tendency` | More common in female speech; use by men is marked |
| `youth` | Strongly associated with younger speakers; older speakers may find it unusual |
| `older/regional` | Associated with older speakers or specific regional speech |
| `anime/fiction` | Common in fictional speech; may be exaggerated relative to real speech |

**Important:** Gender and age tags describe tendency, not exclusivity. The book never says a pattern *cannot* be used by a particular group — only that it is statistically associated with one.

---

### Medium Tags

Every entry carries a **Medium** field listing where the pattern occurs. Use all that apply.

| Tag | Meaning |
|-----|---------|
| `spoken — all` | Occurs across all informal spoken contexts |
| `spoken — drama/film` | Common in scripted dramatic dialogue |
| `spoken — anime` | Common in anime dialogue (may be slightly stylized) |
| `spoken — variety TV` | Occurs in spontaneous, unscripted speech on variety shows |
| `spoken — conversation` | Attested in real informal conversation |
| `written — LINE/text` | Occurs in messaging app text |
| `written — SNS` | Occurs on Twitter/X, Instagram captions, etc. |
| `written — manga` | Occurs in manga speech bubbles |
| `written — forums` | Occurs on 5ch, Reddit Japan, etc. |

Appendix entries typically have `written —` tags only. Main body entries may have both spoken and written tags where the pattern crosses over; the written tag should be accompanied by a Written note pointing to the appendix if the written form has its own conventions.

---

### Dialogue Specification

Every main-body entry includes one dialogue. Dialogues follow these rules:

- **3–6 lines total** (lines = individual speaker turns)
- **Speaker labels:** Single letters (A, B) with a register description before the dialogue: `[casual / two coworkers / A senior to B]`
- **No English gloss inside the dialogue** — English translations appear as a second line under each Japanese line, indented, in brackets: `[Translation here]`
- **Adjacent patterns:** Each dialogue must naturally include at least one other pattern from the book alongside the headword pattern. This is not forced — dialogues are written to sound natural first. The adjacent patterns are not called out in the dialogue itself; the reader encounters them in context.
- **Realistic speech:** Dialogues reflect how people actually speak. Sentences are incomplete where natural. Backchannels appear where natural. No one speaks in textbook complete sentences.

---

### Appendix Entry Specification

Appendix entries follow the same template with two modifications:

1. **No Spoken equivalent required** — some appendix patterns have no spoken parallel (e.g., terminal っ for emphasis, laughter markers like w/草). For these, the `← 教科書の形` field is replaced with `← 話し言葉の対応:` and the spoken equivalent is named, or the field is marked `[written-only form]`.

2. **Medium field is written-only** — all appendix entries have `written —` medium tags.

#### Section 1: The Plain Form in Use
*Plain-form Japanese is not informal grammar — it is the default grammar of casual speech. This section establishes what that means in practice.*

**Spoken. ★★★ core throughout.**

| Code | Headword | ← Formal base | Notes |
|------|----------|---------------|-------|
| 1.1 | だ（述語） | 〜です | Copula in plain form; the foundational register shift |
| 1.2 | ∅（い形容詞文末） | 〜いです | い-adj as complete predicate; no copula needed |
| 1.3 | ∅（述語省略） | 〜です / 〜だ | Zero copula with na-adj and noun predicates |
| 1.4 | 〜ない | 〜ません | Plain negative; blunter than polite form |
| 1.5 | 〜た | 〜ました | Plain past |
| 1.6 | 〜よう / 〜ようか | 〜ましょう / 〜ましょうか | Volitional; ようか adds softer invitation nuance |
| 1.7 | 〜て / 〜てよ / 〜てくれ | 〜てください | Request spectrum: て (neutral) → てよ (mild) → てくれ (blunt) |
| 1.8 | 〜だろう / 〜だろ | 〜でしょう | でしょ (soft) → だろ (rough); intonation-sensitive |
| 1.9 | 〜？（上昇調） | 〜ですか / 〜ますか | Rising intonation replaces か; か on plain form = aggressive |
| 1.10 | 〜の？ / 〜の | 〜ですか | の as soft question marker; feminine tendency in questions |

#### Section 2: Topic, Subject, and Object Drop
*Japanese does not drop elements carelessly — it drops them systematically. Knowing what gets dropped and why is a prerequisite for parsing almost any natural utterance.*

**Spoken. ★★★ core throughout.**

| Code | Headword | Notes |
|------|----------|-------|
| 2.1 | ∅ + 述語（ゼロ主題） | Topic inferred from context; default structure, not shortcut |
| 2.2 | ∅ + 状態動詞 | Subject omitted with stative verbs: 分かる, ある, いる |
| 2.3 | は省略（主題連鎖） | Once topic established, は disappears for the exchange |
| 2.4 | を省略（目的語） | Object particle absent when referent is clear |
| 2.5 | 後置き（主題後置） | 食べた？昨日買ったアイス — topic appended after predicate |
| 2.6 | 動詞先行倒置 | 見た？あの人？ — verb fires before topic surfaces |

#### Section 3: The んだ / のだ Family
*The explanatory-register marker that saturates natural conversation. Learners who don't have んだ fully internalized will misread the intention behind roughly half of all casual utterances.*

**Spoken (and written casual — see cross-references). ★★★ core throughout.**

| Code | Headword | Function | Notes |
|------|----------|----------|-------|
| 3.1 | 〜んだ / 〜んです | Explanatory assertion | "The thing is..." — frames utterance as context/background |
| 3.2 | 〜んだけど（文末） | Soft trailing setup | Leaves utterance open; invites response; softens implicit request |
| 3.3 | 〜んだよ | Insistent explanation | よ pushes explanation onto listener; can read as frustrated |
| 3.4 | 〜んだね / 〜んだよね | Shared understanding | Collaborative confirmation of mutual understanding |
| 3.5 | 〜んだって | Reported hearsay | "Apparently..." / "I heard that..." — gossip register |
| 3.6 | 〜んじゃないか / 〜んじゃん | Casual rhetorical / confirmation | じゃん = strong Tokyo register marker |
| 3.7 | 〜の（文末, 説明） | Soft explanatory / question | Softened んだ; feminine tendency but not exclusive |
| 3.8 | 〜のに（逆接） | Frustrated contrast | "Even though...!" — emotional charge; unmet expectation |

**Written-primary or written-only throughout. Medium tags are all written.**

## Generation Instructions for Claude Code

The following instructions govern how the book content is to be generated entry by entry.

### Scope

Generate all entries in Sections 1–8 and the Appendix. Each section is a separate output file. Front matter (preface, how-to-use, sample entry) is a separate file. Back matter indexes are generated last, after all entries exist.

### Per-entry generation rules

1. **Follow the entry template exactly.** Field order is fixed. Do not reorder or merge fields.

2. **No romaji anywhere.** Japanese text is kanji/kana only. Furigana notation for above-N4 vocabulary uses the format `漢字《ふりがな》` on first occurrence within each entry.

3. **Example sentences must sound natural.** They are not invented textbook sentences. Write them as a native speaker would actually say them in the labeled context. Avoid textbook vocabulary clusters (do not make every example about school or shopping trips unless warranted).

4. **Context labels are mandatory on every example.** Format: `[context: casual / speaker A is explaining to a close friend]`. Be specific about register, relationship, and medium.

5. **The Gap Note is the editorial heart of the book.** It must be specific — name the textbook (Genki, Minna no Nihongo, JLPT N4 grammar lists) or describe the treatment, and state the specific failure mode. "Textbooks mention this" is not acceptable. "Genki II introduces 〜てしまう at N4 but presents it in polite-form examples only, meaning learners who encounter 〜ちゃった in drama have no anchor to connect the two forms" is acceptable.

6. **Dialogues must include at least one adjacent pattern** from another entry in the book. Do not force it — write the dialogue naturally and then confirm it naturally uses a second pattern. If it doesn't, rewrite until it does.

7. **Cross-references must be accurate.** Only reference entries that exist in this table of contents. Use the entry code format (e.g., `5.3`, `A.2`).

8. **The spoken/written boundary is firm.** Main body entries (Sections 1–8) are spoken-first. If a spoken pattern has a written-casual counterpart with distinct conventions, add a Written note block — do not import the written convention into the main entry. Appendix entries do not bleed back into the main text.

9. **Register and gender tags must be consistent with the table of contents.** If the ToC marks a pattern ★★★ core and neutral, the entry body must not describe it as rare or gendered. Any discrepancy found during generation should flag a note for editorial review.

10. **Variation sub-entries are optional but preferred** when a pattern has 2–4 closely derived forms that don't warrant their own full entries. Sub-entries get: formula + one example + See also. No Gap Note, no Dialogue.

11. **Entry length target:** Main entries: approximately 400–600 words of prose (Gap Note + How the transformation works + contextual notes). Examples and dialogue are additional. Variation sub-entries: approximately 80–120 words each. Appendix entries: approximately 200–350 words (shorter Gap Notes, fewer examples).

12. **Section introductions:** Each section begins with a 100–200 word introduction explaining what unifies the patterns in that section and why they appear where they do in the book's sequence. This is the only place where the book speaks directly about its own organization.
