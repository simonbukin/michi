# Chapter 26 — Comprehensive Review and Extended Reading

You have reached the end of Stage 1. Over the previous twenty-five chapters, you built a complete foundation in Japanese: the writing system, the verb and adjective systems, particles, sentence structure, politeness registers, time expressions, giving and receiving, and the grammar of reasons, desires, obligations, and temporal clauses. That is not a small amount. It is, in fact, everything you need to begin reading real Japanese at an elementary level.

This chapter has two purposes. First, it serves as a reference: a consolidated grammar table covering every pattern introduced since Chapter 5. When you encounter a structure in the wild and need to remind yourself how it works, come back here. Second, it serves as a final assessment: three extended reading passages, each written using the full range of Stage 1 grammar, with no translations — only comprehension questions. If you can read these passages and answer the questions, you are ready for Stage 2.

### Verb Forms

**ます-form: four variants (Chapter 8)**

| Variant | Formation | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Non-past affirmative | stem + ます | たべます |
| Non-past negative | stem + ません | たべません |
| Past affirmative | stem + ました | たべました |
| Past negative | stem + ませんでした | たべませんでした |

Formation of the stem depends on verb class. 一段: drop る. 五段: change the final う-row sound to the corresponding い-row sound. する → し. くる → き.

**て-form (Chapter 10)**

| Verb class | Rule | Example |
|---|---|---|
| 一段 | drop る, add て | たべる → たべて |
| 五段 (く) | く → いて | かく → かいて |
| 五段 (ぐ) | ぐ → いで | およぐ → およいで |
| 五段 (す) | す → して | はなす → はなして |
| 五段 (つ/る/う) | つ/る/う → って | まつ → まって |
| 五段 (ぬ/ぶ/む) | ぬ/ぶ/む → んで | のむ → のんで |
| する | して | べんきょうする → べんきょうして |
| くる | きて | くる → きて |
| いく (exception) | いって | いく → いって |

**て-form applications (Chapter 11)**

| Pattern | Formation | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| ている | て-form + いる | progressive/resultative | いま たべている。 |
| てください | て-form + ください | polite request | ここに すわってください。 |
| てもいい | て-form + もいい | permission | しゃしんを とってもいいですか。 |
| てはいけない | て-form + はいけない | prohibition | ここで たばこを すってはいけません。 |
| てから | て-form + から | "after doing" | ごはんを たべてから、でかけます。 |

**Plain forms: full paradigm (Chapter 12)**

| | Non-past | Past |
|---|---|---|
| Affirmative | たべる (dictionary form) | たべた (た-form) |
| Negative | たべない (ない-form) | たべなかった |

五段 verbs: ない-form changes final う-row to あ-row + ない (exception: う → わない). た-form follows the same sound-change rules as the て-form, replacing て/で with た/だ.

| Verb | ない-form | た-form |
|---|---|---|
| かく | かかない | かいた |
| のむ | のまない | のんだ |
| はなす | はなさない | はなした |
| まつ | またない | まった |
| する | しない | した |
| くる | こない | きた |
| ある | ない | あった |

### Keigo Recognition (Chapter 24)

| Pattern | Type | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| いらっしゃる | 尊敬語 (sonkeigo) | いる/いく/くる (honorific) | せんせいは きょうしつに いらっしゃいます。 |
| おっしゃる | 尊敬語 | いう (honorific) | せんせいが おっしゃいました。 |
| ごらんになる | 尊敬語 | みる (honorific) | こちらを ごらんになってください。 |
| めしあがる | 尊敬語 | たべる/のむ (honorific) | どうぞ めしあがってください。 |
| おいでになる | 尊敬語 | いく/くる/いる (honorific) | あしたも おいでになりますか。 |
| お/ご～になる | 尊敬語 pattern | honorific action | せんせいが おはなしになります。 |
| お/ご～する | 謙譲語 (kenjougo) | humble action | わたしが おもちします。 |
| まいる | 謙譲語 | いく/くる (humble) | わたしが まいります。 |
| もうす | 謙譲語 | いう (humble) | やまだと もうします。 |
| いたす | 謙譲語 | する (humble) | わたしが いたします。 |
| おる | 謙譲語 | いる (humble) | ただいま せきを はずしております。 |

At this level, you do not need to produce keigo. You need to recognize it: when a speaker uses いらっしゃる, they are saying いる, いく, or くる about someone they are elevating. When they use まいる, they are saying いく or くる about themselves or their in-group, lowering themselves. Understanding these substitutions lets you parse polite Japanese — announcements, customer service interactions, business conversations — without confusion.

### Temporal Clauses (Chapter 25)

| Pattern | Formation | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| まえに | dictionary form + まえに / noun の + まえに | before | ねるまえに、はを みがきます。 |
| あとで | た-form + あとで / noun の + あとで | after | ごはんを たべたあとで、さんぽします。 |
| とき | plain form + とき | when / at the time of | にほんに いくとき、パスポートが いります。 |
| ながら | ます-stem + ながら | while doing | おんがくを ききながら、べんきょうします。 |

Note on とき: the tense of the clause before とき matters. Dictionary form + とき means "when (about to do / on the way to do)." た-form + とき means "when (having done / after doing)." This distinction is subtle but important:

> にほんに いくとき、くうこうで おみやげを かいました。
> "When I was going to Japan (= on the way there), I bought souvenirs at the airport."

> にほんに いったとき、おてらを たくさん みました。
> "When I went to Japan (= while I was there), I saw many temples."

## 26.2 Extended Reading Passage 1 — とうきょうの いちにち

Read the following passage. No translation is provided. Use the comprehension questions below to check your understanding.

## 26.4 Extended Reading Passage 3 — ともだちへの メール

---

ゆいへ

ひさしぶり。げんき？

### Comprehension Questions

1. この メールを かいたのは だれですか。だれに かいていますか。
2. まきさんは さいきん なにが たいへんでしたか。
3. まきさんは らいしゅうの きんようび なにを したいですか。
4. あたらしい カフェは どこに ありますか。
5. ゆいさんは なにが すきですか。
6. まきさんは テストの べんきょうについて どう おもっていますか。

### Register Notes

This passage is written in casual register. Notice the following features:

- **だ instead of です:** おわったんだ, たいへんだった. The plain copula だ replaces です in casual speech and writing.
- **Dropped particles:** ゆい、パンケーキ すきだったよね — the は after ゆい and the が after パンケーキ are omitted. This is standard in casual Japanese.
- **Trailing けど:** なかったんだけど... The sentence trails off. The unspoken conclusion is something like「だから たいへんだった」or similar.
- **んだ for explanation:** おわったんだ, できたんだけど, やばいんだけど. Casual speech uses んだ (the plain form of んです) constantly for background explanation.
- **Contracted forms:** はたらいてて is a contraction of はたらいていて. The い of ている is dropped. This is universal in casual speech.
- **Question without か:** ひま？ いかない？ In casual speech, questions are formed by rising intonation alone, without the particle か.
- **よね for seeking agreement:** いいよね, すきだったよね. The combination よ + ね asserts something while inviting the listener to confirm.
- **って for hearsay:** おいしいらしいよ。あるって。 The particle って is the casual form of と (quotation). あるって is short for あるといっていた or あるそうだ.

Every one of these features appears constantly in real Japanese. If you can read this email and understand the register shifts, you are already reading authentic casual Japanese.

## 26.5 Extended Listening Notes

Reading and listening are different skills, and the gap between them is larger in Japanese than in most languages. Written Japanese, even casual written Japanese like the email above, is organized and relatively complete. Spoken Japanese at natural speed is compressed, contracted, and full of features that never appear in textbooks. Here is what to expect.

### Vowel devoicing

In natural speech, the vowels い and う are frequently devoiced (whispered or dropped entirely) when they fall between two voiceless consonants or at the end of an utterance. For example:

- すき sounds closer to "ski" — the う is barely audible
- です at the end of a sentence often sounds like "des" — the final う disappears
- ～ました often sounds like "mashta" — the い is devoiced

This is not sloppy speech. It is standard Tokyo pronunciation. Once you are aware of it, you will start hearing it everywhere.

### Particle dropping

In casual speech, particles are frequently omitted when context makes them unnecessary.

- ごはん たべた？ (を omitted)
- あした ひま？ (は omitted)
- どこ いくの？ (へ/に omitted)

The grammatical relationships are understood from word order and context. が, を, は, and に are the most commonly dropped particles. で, から, まで, and と are dropped less frequently because their meanings are harder to infer.

### Contracted forms

Natural speech compresses certain patterns into shorter forms. The most common at this level:

| Full form | Contracted form | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| ている | てる | progressive/resultative |
| ていく | てく | "go on doing" |
| てしまう | ちゃう / じゃう | completion/regret |
| ておく | とく | "do in advance" |
| ては | ちゃ | in てはいけない → ちゃいけない |
| なければ | なきゃ | in なければならない → なきゃ |
| というのは | ってのは | "the thing called" |

You do not need to produce these forms. You need to recognize them. When you hear なきゃ in conversation, know that it means なければ. When you hear ちゃった, know that it is てしまった.

### あいづち — Back-channel responses

Japanese listeners do not stay silent while someone speaks. They constantly produce small responses — あいづち — to show they are listening and following along.

- うん (casual yes / I see)
- ええ (polite yes)
- そうですか (is that so)
- そうですね (that's right)
- なるほど (I see / that makes sense)
- へえ (really? / huh)
- ほんとうに？ (really?)

These are not interruptions. They are expected. A Japanese speaker who receives no あいづち may feel that the listener is not paying attention or does not care. When you listen to Japanese conversation, you will hear あいづち after nearly every sentence. Do not mistake them for the listener's turn to speak — they are signals of engagement, not responses.

### What to listen to at this level

You have enough grammar and vocabulary to begin listening practice with material designed for learners. Recommended approaches:

**Graded listening material.** NHK World's やさしい日本語ニュース (Easy Japanese News) uses simplified grammar and vocabulary at a controlled speed. It is an excellent bridge between textbook Japanese and natural speech.

**Podcasts for learners.** Several podcasts target N5-N4 listeners, speaking slowly and using grammar within the Stage 1 range. Search for materials labeled "beginner" or "N5" and listen repeatedly to short segments.

**Anime and drama with subtitles.** Japanese media uses casual speech extensively. Watch with Japanese subtitles if possible. The gap between what you read and what you hear will teach you about contractions, devoicing, and natural rhythm. Do not expect to understand everything. Focus on recognizing patterns you know.

**Repeated listening.** Choose a short clip — thirty seconds to two minutes — and listen to it five or ten times. On the first pass, catch the words you know. On the second, try to identify the grammar structures. On the third, pay attention to the sounds between the words you recognize. This is more effective than listening to hours of material once.

The goal at this stage is not comprehension of every word. It is pattern recognition: hearing a て-form, catching a particle, recognizing the shape of a ない-form, noticing when someone trails off with けど. Every structure you learned in the previous twenty-five chapters has a sound. Train your ear to hear those sounds in real time.

## 26.7 What Comes Next

Stage 1 is complete. You have a working foundation in Japanese grammar, vocabulary, and the writing system. You can read simple texts, follow basic conversations, and parse the structures that make up elementary Japanese.

Stage 2 (N4) builds on everything here. It introduces more complex sentence structures — relative clauses, passive and causative forms, volitional forms, more conditionals (ば, なら), hearsay (そうだ, らしい, ようだ), and extended keigo. It also deepens your understanding of particles, particularly the は/が distinction, and introduces the grammar of formal writing.

But before moving to Stage 2, spend time with real Japanese at the N5 level. Read graded readers. Listen to simplified news. Watch shows you enjoy with Japanese subtitles. The grammar you learned in these twenty-six chapters is not theoretical — it appears in every sentence of every text you will ever read in Japanese. The more exposure you get now, the more automatic your recognition will become, and the smoother your transition to Stage 2 will be.

You have built the foundation. Now use it.
