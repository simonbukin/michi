## Part One: Completing the Verb System

**Chapter 1 — The Potential Form: "Can Do"**
1.1 Formation: 五段 (change -u to -e, add る: 書く→書ける), 一段 (remove る, add られる: 食べる→食べられる), irregular (する→できる, くる→来られる)
1.2 Potential verbs conjugate as 一段 regardless of original class
1.3 Particle shift: を→が is common with potential (日本語が話せる)
1.4 ら抜き言葉 — 食べれる as spoken reality; recognize both forms
1.5 ことができる — alternative potential construction (dictionary form + ことができる)
1.6 見える/聞こえる vs 見られる/聞ける — spontaneous perception vs ability
1.7 Reading passage — discussing abilities, travel capabilities
1.8 Vocabulary list

**Chapter 2 — The Volitional Form: "Let's / I'll"**
2.1 Formation: 五段 (change -u to -おう: 書く→書こう), 一段 (remove る, add よう: 食べる→食べよう), irregular (する→しよう, くる→来よう)
2.2 As casual "let's" — 行こう (= 行きましょう in casual)
2.3 ～ようと思う — "I'm thinking of doing" (planning)
2.4 ～ようとする — "try to do" / "be about to do"
2.5 Vocabulary list

**Chapter 3 — The Passive Form**
3.1 Formation: 五段 (change -u to -a, add れる: 書く→書かれる), 一段 (remove る, add られる: 食べる→食べられる), irregular (する→される, くる→来られる)
3.2 Direct passive — 本が読まれている (the book is being read); agent marked with に
3.3 Indirect / adversative passive (迷惑の受身) — 雨に降られた (I was rained on, to my detriment); uniquely Japanese, no English equivalent
3.4 Creation passive — に**よって** for agent (この小説は漱石によって書かれた)
3.5 Passive in everyday speech — Japanese uses passive far more than English (会議で決められた "it was decided at the meeting")
3.6 一段 passive = 一段 potential (食べられる): context disambiguates
3.7 Reading passage — news article about an event (passive-heavy)
3.8 Vocabulary list

**Chapter 4 — The Causative Form**
4.1 Formation: 五段 (change -u to -a, add せる: 書く→書かせる), 一段 (remove る, add させる: 食べる→食べさせる), irregular (する→させる, くる→来させる)
4.2 "Make someone do" vs "let someone do" — disambiguated by context and particles (を for coercion, に for permission with intransitive verbs)
4.3 Causative in hierarchical contexts — 先生は学生に本を読ませた (the teacher made the students read)
4.4 させてください — "please let me do" (polite request for permission)
4.5 Vocabulary list

**Chapter 5 — The Causative-Passive Form**
5.1 Formation: 書かせる→書かせられる; the contracted 五段 form 書かされる
5.2 Meaning: "was made to do" — combines causation and suffering
5.3 Full form vs contracted form — both are standard; contracted is more common in speech for 五段 verbs
5.4 野菜を食べさせられた — "I was made to eat vegetables"
5.5 Reading passage — childhood memories, workplace complaints (causative-passive in natural context)
5.6 Vocabulary list

**Chapter 6 — The Imperative and Prohibitive**
6.1 Imperative formation: 五段 (change -u to -e: 書け), 一段 (remove る, add ろ: 食べろ), irregular (しろ, 来い)
6.2 Prohibitive: dictionary form + な (書くな, 食べるな)
6.3 Where you encounter these: signs (立入禁止), sports (走れ！), manga, emergencies (逃げろ！), rough speech
6.4 なさい — softened command (お母さん語: 食べなさい, 早く起きなさい)
6.5 Recognition only — these are not for polite conversation
6.6 Vocabulary list

## Part Two: Complex Sentences

**Chapter 7 — Relative Clauses (連体修飾節)**
7.1 Japanese relativizes by gapping — no relative pronoun, no "who/which/that"
7.2 The modifying clause precedes the noun: [clause] + noun
7.3 Subject-gap: 本を読んでいる人 (the person who is reading a book)
7.4 Object-gap: 友達が作ったケーキ (the cake my friend made)
7.5 Location/time-gap: 去年行った店 (the restaurant I went to last year)
7.6 Stacking relative clauses — parsing left-branching modification
7.7 Reading passage — descriptive text with multiple embedded relative clauses
7.8 Vocabulary list

**Chapter 8 — Quotation and Reported Speech**
8.1 と言う — direct and indirect quotation
8.2 と思う — review from Stage 1, now with complex embedded clauses
8.3 と聞いた — "I heard that..."
8.4 と書いてある — "it says (in writing) that..."
8.5 って — casual quotation particle (= と), extremely common in speech
8.6 そうだ (hearsay) — plain form + そうだ (雨が降るそうだ "I hear it will rain")
8.7 Distinguishing hearsay そうだ from appearance そうだ (stem + そうだ, taught next chapter)
8.8 Reading passage — relaying information, gossip, news summaries
8.9 Vocabulary list

**Chapter 9 — Appearance, Hearsay, and Conjecture**
9.1 そうだ (appearance) — stem + そうだ (雨が降りそうだ "it looks like it'll rain"); conjugation (そうな + noun, そうに + verb)
9.2 ようだ / みたいだ — "it seems like" (based on evidence); みたいだ is more casual
9.3 らしい — "apparently" (based on indirect information); also suffix meaning "-like" (男らしい)
9.4 Contrastive summary: そうだ(hearsay) vs そうだ(appearance) vs ようだ vs らしい — what evidence type each encodes
9.5 はずだ — "should be" / "expected to be" (based on logic or knowledge)
9.6 Reading passage — detective-style narrative using all conjecture forms
9.7 Vocabulary list

**Chapter 10 — Conditional Forms: と, ば, たら, なら**
10.1 と — natural/automatic consequence; habitual result; CANNOT be used with requests/commands in the main clause
10.2 ば — hypothetical; formation: 五段 (change -u to -e + ば: 書けば), 一段 (remove る, add れば: 食べれば), い-adj (remove い, add ければ: 高ければ), な-adj/noun (であれば)
10.3 たら — "when/if (completed)"; most versatile; works with requests; formation: た-form + ら
10.4 なら — "if (what you say is true)"; responds to information from the listener; main clause can precede condition chronologically
10.5 Contrastive analysis with parallel examples showing where each is required, preferred, or impossible
10.6 もし — optional "if" intensifier, usable with ば/たら/なら
10.7 ても/でも — "even if" (て-form + も)
10.8 Reading passage — advice column, hypothetical situations
10.9 Vocabulary list

## Part Three: Giving, Receiving, and Social Grammar

**Chapter 11 — て-Form Giving and Receiving (授受表現)**
11.1 Review of あげる/もらう/くれる (object exchange) from Stage 1
11.2 てあげる — do something for someone (away from speaker): 荷物を持ってあげた
11.3 てもらう — have someone do something for you (toward speaker, receiving): 友達に手伝ってもらった
11.4 てくれる — someone does something for you (toward speaker, giver's action): 友達が手伝ってくれた
11.5 How perspective choice reveals gratitude, social debt, and in-group identification
11.6 The pragmatic weight: てくれる implies gratitude; てもらう implies the speaker initiated or benefited; てあげる can sound condescending if misused
11.7 Polite variants: てさしあげる, ていただく, てくださる (preview — full keigo treatment in Ch. 17)
11.8 Reading passage — narrative about friends helping each other, favors exchanged
11.9 Vocabulary list

**Chapter 12 — Experience, Decision, Habituality**
12.1 ことがある (past) — experience: 日本に行ったことがある "I have been to Japan"
12.2 ことがある (present) — occasional occurrence: 朝ごはんを食べないことがある "sometimes I skip breakfast"
12.3 ことにする — "decide to": 来年留学することにした "I decided to study abroad"
12.4 ことになる — "it has been decided that" (agentless, institutional): 来月転勤することになった "it's been decided I'll transfer"
12.5 ことになっている — "it is the rule/arrangement that": この会社では６時に終わることになっている
12.6 Why Japanese prefers agentless ことになる over ことにする in many contexts — diffusing responsibility, avoiding directness
12.7 Reading passage — life changes, company announcements
12.8 Vocabulary list

**Chapter 13 — Purpose: ために and ように**
13.1 ために — purpose with volitional verb: 日本語を勉強するために日本に来た "I came to Japan to study Japanese"
13.2 ために — reason/cause: 台風のために電車が止まった "trains stopped because of the typhoon"
13.3 ように — purpose with non-volitional verb or potential: 聞こえるように大きい声で話した "spoke loudly so it could be heard"
13.4 ようにする — "make an effort to / make sure to": 毎日運動するようにしている
13.5 ようになる — "come to be able to / reach a state where": 日本語が話せるようになった "I became able to speak Japanese"
13.6 Distinguishing ために vs ように — volitionality as the deciding factor
13.7 Reading passage — goals, lifestyle changes
13.8 Vocabulary list

**Chapter 14 — Despite and Concession**
14.1 のに — "despite, even though" (plain form + のに): 勉強したのに、テストに落ちた
14.2 のに vs けど/が — のに carries stronger emotional weight (frustration, surprise)
14.3 ても/でも — "even if" (review and expansion from Ch. 10)
14.4 Review of けど/けれども — contrastive and as sentence softener
14.5 Reading passage — complaints, unexpected outcomes
14.6 Vocabulary list

## Part Four: The Keigo System

**Chapter 15 — Understanding Register: Why Keigo Exists**
15.1 The social logic — keigo is not "being polite," it is encoding relative social position
15.2 The three situations that trigger keigo: talking to/about superiors, talking to strangers/customers, formal settings
15.3 The five-part system overview (for comprehension): 尊敬語, 謙譲語I, 謙譲語II/丁重語, 丁寧語, 美化語
15.4 You already know some: です/ます (丁寧語), ください (from くださる, 尊敬語), おねがいします, お/ご prefixes (美化語)

**Chapter 16 — 尊敬語: Respectful Language (Elevating Others)**
16.1 Irregular respectful verbs: いらっしゃる (=いる/行く/来る), おっしゃる (=言う), ご覧になる (=見る), 召し上がる (=食べる/飲む), なさる (=する), くださる (=くれる)
16.2 The productive pattern お〜になる: お読みになる, お帰りになる, お使いになる
16.3 Passive as respectful — 社長は何と言われましたか (passive form used as lighter keigo)
16.4 Respectful nouns and prefixes: お名前, ご住所, ご家族
16.5 Recognition practice — parsing keigo in business greetings, customer service, formal announcements
16.6 Vocabulary list: all irregular 尊敬語 verbs with their plain equivalents

**Chapter 17 — 謙譲語: Humble Language (Lowering Yourself)**
17.1 謙譲語I (object-oriented humble): 伺う (=聞く/行く/訪ねる), 申し上げる (=言う), 差し上げる (=あげる), お目にかかる (=会う), 拝見する (=見る), いただく (=もらう/食べる/飲む)
17.2 The productive pattern お〜する / お〜いたす: お持ちする, お送りいたす
17.3 謙譲語II / 丁重語 (addressee-oriented humble): 参る (=行く/来る), 申す (=言う), いたす (=する), おる (=いる) — used to maintain dignity before the listener regardless of action target
17.4 The difference between 謙譲語I and II — I humbles toward a specific person, II humbles your speech in general
17.5 Vocabulary list: all irregular 謙譲語 verbs with their plain equivalents

**Chapter 18 — Keigo in Action: Reading and Listening**
18.1 Keigo in business email: お世話になっております, ～していただければ幸いです, 何卒よろしくお願いいたします
18.2 Keigo in customer service: いらっしゃいませ, 少々お待ちください, こちらでございます
18.3 Keigo in announcements: まもなく電車が参ります
18.4 Common errors and hypercorrection for recognition: させていただく overuse, 二重敬語 (お読みになられる)
18.5 Extended reading passage — business email exchange
18.6 Extended reading passage — customer service interaction
18.7 Vocabulary list

## Part Seven: Aspect, Colloquial Forms, and Review

**Chapter 27 — Aspect: ていく, てくる, てしまう, ておく**
27.1 ていく — directional (持っていく "take") and temporal change going forward (暑くなっていく "it's going to get hotter")
27.2 てくる — directional (持ってくる "bring") and temporal change up to now (増えてきた "has been increasing")
27.3 てしまう — completion (読んでしまった "finished reading") and regret/unintended (忘れてしまった "ended up forgetting"); casual contractions ちゃう/じゃう
27.4 ておく — preparatory action (買っておく "buy in advance"); casual contraction とく
27.5 てある — resultative from intentional action (窓が開けてある "the window has been [intentionally] opened")
27.6 These as a coherent aspect system — how they interact and combine
