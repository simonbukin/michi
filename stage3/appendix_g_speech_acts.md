# Appendix G — Speech Act Quick Reference

This appendix organizes the key speech acts taught in Stage 3 — refusing, apologizing, thanking, requesting, and complimenting — by formality level. Each entry shows the Japanese expression, its literal or functional meaning, and notes on when to use it.

---

## How to Read This Reference

**Formality Levels:**

- **Casual** — Friends, close family, peers of similar age
- **Polite** — Standard です/ます level; acquaintances, colleagues, strangers
- **Formal** — Business superiors, customers, official contexts
- **Very Formal** — Written correspondence, speeches, customer service

Expressions are listed from most casual to most formal within each speech act.

---

## Refusing

Japanese refusals are typically indirect. A direct "no" (いいえ、できません) is grammatically possible but socially rare in most contexts. The more polite the situation, the more indirect the refusal becomes.

### Expressions

| Expression | Level | Meaning / Function | Notes |
|-----------|-------|-------------------|-------|
| 無理。 | Casual | No way. / Can't. | Blunt. Friends only. |
| ちょっと... | Casual–Polite | Well, it's a bit... | The trailing off IS the refusal. Most common indirect refusal in Japanese. |
| ちょっと難しいです。 | Polite | It's a bit difficult. | = "No." Does not mean "it's actually difficult but possible." |
| ちょっと都合が悪くて... | Polite | My schedule doesn't work out... | Polite refusal citing scheduling. |
| その日はちょっと予定がありまして... | Polite–Formal | I have plans that day... | The trailing off softens further. |
| 考えさせてください。 | Polite–Formal | Let me think about it. | Usually = "No." In genuine consideration contexts, follow-up specifies when. |
| せっかくですが、今回は遠慮させていただきます。 | Formal | Though it's kind of you, I'll refrain this time. | せっかく acknowledges the offer's value. 遠慮する is the formal refusal verb. |
| 申し訳ございませんが、お受けしかねます。 | Very Formal | I'm terribly sorry, but I am unable to accept. | かねる (Ch. 12) for "find it difficult to." Maximum politeness refusal. |
| ご期待に沿えず申し訳ございません。 | Very Formal | I'm sorry we cannot meet your expectations. | Business/customer service. Written or spoken. |

### Key Principles

1. **Indirectness increases with formality.** The more polite you need to be, the less directly you say "no."
2. **Trailing off (...)** is a complete communicative act. The listener understands.
3. **Reason-giving** is optional and often vague. 都合が悪い ("my circumstances don't work") is sufficient. Detailed excuses can sound suspicious.
4. **せっかく** before a refusal shows you value the invitation/offer and regret declining.

## Apologizing

Japanese has a rich apology system. The choice depends on the severity of the offense, the relationship, and the social context.

### Expressions

| Expression | Level | Meaning / Function | Notes |
|-----------|-------|-------------------|-------|
| ごめん。 | Casual | Sorry. | Friends, family. Light offenses. |
| ごめんね。 | Casual | Sorry (softened). | ね adds friendliness. |
| ごめんなさい。 | Casual–Polite | I'm sorry. | Children to adults; adults in semi-casual contexts. |
| すみません。 | Polite | I'm sorry. / Excuse me. | Multi-purpose: apology, getting attention, expressing gratitude for trouble caused. |
| すみませんでした。 | Polite | I'm sorry (for what happened). | Past-tense version. Acknowledges a specific past incident. |
| 失礼しました。 | Polite–Formal | Excuse me (for what I did). | Mild formal apology. Often for social missteps (interrupting, being late). |
| 申し訳ありません。 | Formal | I have no excuse. / I'm deeply sorry. | Standard business apology. |
| 申し訳ございません。 | Very Formal | I'm terribly sorry. | Maximum politeness. Customer service, addressing superiors. |
| 申し訳ございませんでした。 | Very Formal | I was terribly sorry. | Past-tense version for formal contexts. |
| お詫び申し上げます。 | Very Formal | I offer my sincerest apologies. | Written apologies, public statements, press conferences. |
| 深くお詫び申し上げます。 | Very Formal | I offer my deepest apologies. | The strongest formal apology. Corporate scandal level. |

### Key Principles

1. **すみません** does triple duty: apology, attention-getter, and expression of gratitude for trouble. Context determines which.
2. **申し訳ない** literally means "there is no excuse." It acknowledges wrongdoing without offering justification.
3. **Bowing** accompanies apologies physically. Deeper bow = more serious apology.
4. **Repeated apologies** are normal. Apologizing once and moving on can seem insufficient in formal Japanese contexts.

## Thanking

Japanese thanking often overlaps with apologizing — the speaker thanks by acknowledging the trouble they caused the other person.

### Expressions

| Expression | Level | Meaning / Function | Notes |
|-----------|-------|-------------------|-------|
| ありがとう。 | Casual | Thanks. | Friends, family. |
| ありがとうね。 | Casual | Thanks (warm). | ね adds warmth. |
| ありがとうございます。 | Polite | Thank you. | Standard polite thanks. |
| ありがとうございました。 | Polite | Thank you (for what you did). | Past-tense: thanks for a completed action. |
| どうもありがとうございます。 | Polite | Thank you very much. | どうも intensifies. |
| すみません。 | Polite | Thank you (for the trouble). | = "Sorry to have troubled you" → functions as thanks. Very common. |
| 助かりました。 | Polite | That was a great help. | Acknowledges the practical benefit of someone's action. |
| 恐れ入ります。 | Formal | I'm grateful / I'm sorry to trouble you. | Humble acknowledgment. Can be thanks or apology depending on context. |
| お世話になりました。 | Formal | Thank you for taking care of me. | Used when leaving a job, ending a project, or after receiving sustained help. |
| お世話になっております。 | Formal | Thank you for your ongoing support. | Business greeting. Standard opening in emails to business contacts. |
| ご配慮いただきありがとうございます。 | Very Formal | Thank you for your consideration. | Written/formal spoken. Acknowledges thoughtful treatment. |
| 心より感謝申し上げます。 | Very Formal | I express my heartfelt gratitude. | Speeches, formal letters. |

### Key Principles

1. **すみません as thanks** is not an error — it is standard Japanese pragmatics. When someone goes out of their way for you, acknowledging the trouble (sumimasen) is a form of gratitude.
2. **助かります / 助かりました** is increasingly popular as a warm, practical thank-you. It focuses on the result rather than the obligation.
3. **お世話になっております** is the default business email greeting. It does not require a specific favor to have occurred.

## Requesting

Japanese requests operate on an indirectness scale. The more indirect, the more polite.

### Expressions

| Expression | Level | Meaning / Function | Notes |
|-----------|-------|-------------------|-------|
| Vて。 | Casual | Do [X]. | Friends only. 窓開けて。 |
| Vてくれる？ | Casual | Can you do [X]? | Request to a peer. Implies "for me." |
| Vてくれない？ | Casual | Won't you do [X]? | Negative question = slightly more indirect. |
| Vてもらえる？ | Casual | Could I get you to do [X]? | もらう perspective = slightly more polite than くれる. |
| Vてもらえますか。 | Polite | Could you do [X] for me? | Standard polite request. |
| Vていただけますか。 | Formal | Could you kindly do [X]? | いただく = humble receiving. Business standard. |
| Vていただけないでしょうか。 | Formal | Would it be possible for you to...? | Negative + でしょうか = maximum spoken indirectness. |
| Vていただければ幸いです。 | Very Formal | I would be grateful if you could... | Written requests. Emails to superiors/clients. |
| Vていただけますようお願い申し上げます。 | Very Formal | We respectfully request that you... | Formal notices, official correspondence. |
| お願いいたします。 | Formal | I humbly request. | General-purpose formal request closer. |
| お手数ですが... | Formal | I'm sorry for the trouble, but... | Precedes a request to acknowledge the burden on the listener. |
| 恐れ入りますが... | Formal | I'm sorry to trouble you, but... | Even more humble than お手数ですが. |

### Key Principles

1. **The indirectness scale** (てくれる → ていただければ幸いです) is not just about words — it reflects the speaker's assessment of the social distance and power difference.
2. **Negative questions** are more polite than positive ones: てもらえませんか is softer than てもらえますか.
3. **Prefacing with an apology** (お手数ですが, 恐れ入りますが) is standard in formal requests. It is not optional etiquette — it is expected.
4. **お願いいたします / お願い申し上げます** can close almost any formal request. It is the universal request finisher.

## Complimenting and Deflecting

Japanese compliments are common, but the expected response is deflection, not acceptance. Accepting a compliment directly can seem arrogant.

### Giving Compliments

| Expression | Level | Meaning / Function | Notes |
|-----------|-------|-------------------|-------|
| すごい！ | Casual | Amazing! | General-purpose casual praise. |
| さすが（だね）。 | Casual–Polite | As expected of you. | Acknowledges someone's known ability. Always positive. |
| 上手ですね。 | Polite | You're good at it. | Standard compliment for skills. |
| お上手ですね。 | Polite–Formal | You're very skilled. | お adds politeness. Can also mean flattery (context-dependent). |
| 素晴らしいですね。 | Polite | That's wonderful. | General praise, slightly formal. |
| 見事ですね。 | Formal | That's splendid / magnificent. | Strong praise, formal. |
| 感心しました。 | Formal | I'm impressed. | Sincere admiration. Use with care — can sound condescending to a superior. |

### Deflecting Compliments

| Expression | Level | Meaning / Function | Notes |
|-----------|-------|-------------------|-------|
| そんなことないよ。 | Casual | That's not true. / Not at all. | Standard casual deflection. |
| いやいや、全然。 | Casual | No no, not at all. | Emphatic casual denial. |
| まだまだです。 | Polite | I still have a long way to go. | The most classic deflection. Implies continued effort. |
| いいえ、まだまだです。 | Polite | No, I still have much to learn. | Adding いいえ makes it slightly more emphatic. |
| とんでもないです。 | Polite–Formal | Not at all. / You're too kind. | Strong but polite deflection. |
| とんでもございません。 | Formal | Not at all. (very humble) | Maximum formality deflection. Note: grammatically non-standard but widely accepted. |
| お褒めにあずかり光栄です。 | Very Formal | I'm honored by your praise. | Formal acceptance — used only in very formal contexts (speeches, ceremonies). This is the rare case where accepting is appropriate. |
| 恐れ入ります。 | Formal | I'm humbled. | Accepts implicitly while expressing humility. |

### Key Principles

1. **Default response to compliments is denial**, not thanks. Saying ありがとうございます to a compliment is not wrong but feels un-Japanese to many native speakers.
2. **まだまだです** is the safest all-purpose deflection. It works in nearly any context.
3. **Complimenting a superior's skill** requires care. 上手ですね can sound evaluative (as if you are qualified to judge). Use 素晴らしい or express what you learned instead.
4. **さすが** is always safe because it attributes the achievement to the person's known character or ability.

## Cross-Reference: Overlapping Expressions

Several expressions serve multiple speech-act functions:

| Expression | As Apology | As Thanks | As Request Softener |
|-----------|------------|-----------|-------------------|
| すみません | "I'm sorry" | "Thank you for the trouble" | (rare) |
| 恐れ入ります | "I'm sorry to trouble you" | "I'm grateful" | "I'm sorry to ask, but..." |
| 恐縮です | "I'm apologetic" | "I'm deeply grateful" | "I hate to impose, but..." |
| 申し訳ありません | "I'm deeply sorry" | (uncommon) | (uncommon) |
| 失礼します | "Excuse me" (leaving) | (uncommon) | "Pardon me" (interrupting) |

The boundary between apology and gratitude in Japanese is intentionally blurred. Acknowledging the burden your existence or needs place on others is simultaneously an apology and an expression of thanks.
