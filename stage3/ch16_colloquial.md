# Chapter 16 — Colloquial Grammar: Systematic Recognition

In Stage 2, Chapter 28 introduced the major contraction patterns and sentence-final particles of casual Japanese. You learned to recognize ている→てる, てしまう→ちゃう, なければ→なきゃ, and the basic sentence-final particles よ, ね, かな, and っけ. This chapter builds on that foundation with a systematic, expanded treatment of colloquial grammar — the patterns that define how Japanese actually sounds in daily life, in dramas, in manga, and in the conversations you will overhear on trains, in restaurants, and between friends.

The goal remains recognition, not production. You are not being asked to adopt casual speech patterns — that will come naturally with exposure. You are being asked to understand them reliably when you encounter them, so that the gap between "textbook Japanese" and "real Japanese" stops being a barrier to comprehension.

## 16.1 Contraction Review and Expansion

Stage 2 covered the core contractions. Here we review them briefly and then expand into patterns that were not previously addressed, including past-tense and negative contracted forms, stacked contractions, and contractions that appear in polite-casual hybrid speech.

### Review: The Core Contractions

| Full Form | Contracted | Example (Full) | Example (Contracted) |
|---|---|---|---|
| ている | てる | 食べている | 食べてる |
| ておく | とく | 買っておく | 買っとく |
| てしまう | ちゃう | 忘れてしまう | 忘れちゃう |
| でしまう | じゃう | 飲んでしまう | 飲んじゃう |
| なければ | なきゃ | 行かなければ | 行かなきゃ |
| なくては | なくちゃ | 飲まなくては | 飲まなくちゃ |
| のだ | んだ | テストなのだ | テストなんだ |
| という | っていう / って | 田中という人 | 田中って人 |
| ではない | じゃない | 本当ではない | 本当じゃない |

These should already be familiar. If any feel shaky, review Stage 2, Chapter 28 before proceeding.

### Expansion: Past-Tense and Compound Contracted Forms

The contractions above do not exist only in present tense. They conjugate further, producing forms that can look opaque if you have not seen them before.

**てしまう → ちゃう in all tenses:**

| Tense | Full Form | Contracted |
|---|---|---|
| 非過去 | 忘れてしまう | 忘れちゃう |
| 過去 | 忘れてしまった | 忘れちゃった |
| 否定 | 忘れてしまわない | 忘れちゃわない |
| て形 | 忘れてしまって | 忘れちゃって |

The past tense ちゃった is extremely common — arguably more common than the present ちゃう. You will hear this constantly.

> 財布を忘れちゃった。
> "I forgot my wallet." (with a nuance of regret or "oops")

> やばい、遅刻しちゃった。
> "Oh no, I'm late." (it happened and there is nothing to be done)

> せっかく作ったのに、全部食べちゃった。
> "Even though I made it specially, they ate all of it."

The voiced version じゃった follows the same pattern:

> 雨に降られて、服が濡れちゃった。いや、ズボンも汚れじゃった。
> "I got rained on and my clothes got wet. No wait, my pants got dirty too."

Note: 汚れる takes で-form (汚れで...) so its contraction uses じゃ.

**ている → てる in all tenses:**

| Tense | Full Form | Contracted |
|---|---|---|
| 非過去 | 食べている | 食べてる |
| 過去 | 食べていた | 食べてた |
| 否定 | 食べていない | 食べてない |
| て形 | 食べていて | 食べてて |

> 昨日、何してた？
> "What were you doing yesterday?"

> まだ読んでない。
> "I haven't read it yet."

> ここで待ってて。
> "Wait here (for me)." (the second て is the request て-form)

The last example deserves attention. 待っていて contracts to 待ってて — two て in a row. This sounds odd on paper but is completely natural in speech.

**ておく → とく in all tenses:**

| Tense | Full Form | Contracted |
|---|---|---|
| 非過去 | 調べておく | 調べとく |
| 過去 | 調べておいた | 調べといた |
| て形 | 調べておいて | 調べといて |
| 否定 | 調べておかない | 調べとかない |

> 明日の分、もう買っといたよ。
> "I already bought tomorrow's share in advance."

> これ、コピーしといて。
> "Copy this (in preparation), will you?"

> 先に予約しとかないと。
> "We need to make a reservation ahead of time." (with implied ダメだ/いけない dropped)

### Stacked Contractions

Contractions can stack on top of each other, producing forms that are far removed from the full version. Learning to unpack these is critical.

> 食べちゃってた (= 食べてしまっていた)
> "Had ended up eating"

Here, てしまう→ちゃう gives 食べちゃう, then ている→てる gives 食べちゃってる, then past tense gives 食べちゃってた.

> やっとかなきゃ (= やっておかなければ)
> "Have to do it in advance"

Here, ておく→とく gives やっとく, then the negative obligation form なければ→なきゃ is applied to the negative stem: やっとかなきゃ.

> 言っとけばよかった (= 言っておけばよかった)
> "I should have said it (in advance)"

> 忘れちゃってたんだ (= 忘れてしまっていたのだ)
> "The thing is, I had ended up forgetting"

This last example stacks three contractions: てしまう→ちゃう, ている→てる (past: てた), and のだ→んだ. Once you see the layers, the form is transparent.

### と言う → って — Expanded Uses

The contraction って replaces と言う, but in casual speech it has expanded far beyond simple quotation. Recognizing its range is essential.

**As quotation (= と言った):**

> 田中さん、明日来ないって。
> "Tanaka said he's not coming tomorrow."

**As hearsay (= そうだ):**

> 来週、テストがあるんだって。
> "I heard there's a test next week."

**As a topic marker (= は, emphasizing):**

> 勉強って、つまらないよね。
> "Studying — it's boring, isn't it."

> あの映画って、面白いの？
> "That movie — is it good?"

**As "what is called" (= という):**

> なんていうか、ちょっと違うんだよね。
> "How should I put it, it's a bit different."

> 「もったいない」って、英語でなんて言うの？
> "How do you say もったいない in English?"

This versatility makes って one of the most frequently heard sounds in casual Japanese. If you hear って and are not sure which function it is serving, look at what comes after it: if a verb of saying or thinking follows, it is quotation; if the sentence continues with a comment, it is functioning as a topic marker; if nothing follows (sentence-final), it is hearsay.

## 16.2 Casual Copula: じゃん and Regional Variants

### じゃん — "Isn't it? / Right? / Obviously!"

Stage 2, Chapter 28 introduced じゃん as a contraction of じゃないか. Here we examine it more systematically.

じゃん serves three overlapping functions:

**1. Confirmation-seeking (= じゃないか, "isn't it?"):**

> 明日、休みじゃん。何か予定ある？
> "Tomorrow's a day off, right? Do you have any plans?"

> この道、前に来たことあるじゃん。覚えてない？
> "We've been on this road before, haven't we? Don't you remember?"

**2. Discovery or realization (= "oh, it turns out..."):**

> あ、結構おいしいじゃん。
> "Oh hey, this is pretty good!" (speaker is pleasantly surprised)

> できるじゃん！
> "You can do it!" (speaker discovers this, possibly after doubting)

**3. Obvious-fact assertion (= "I mean, obviously..."):**

> だって、無理じゃん。
> "I mean, it's impossible, right?" (treating this as self-evident)

> しょうがないじゃん。
> "It can't be helped, can it?" (resigned)

**Attachment rules:**

| 品詞 | 例 |
|---|---|
| 動詞（辞書形） | 行けるじゃん |
| 動詞（た形） | 言ったじゃん |
| い形容詞 | 高いじゃん |
| な形容詞 | 簡単じゃん |
| 名詞 | 学生じゃん |

Note that for な-adjectives and nouns, じゃん replaces だ. You do not say 簡単だじゃん — it is 簡単じゃん.

### やん — Kansai Awareness

In the Kansai region (大阪, 京都, 神戸, and surrounding areas), the equivalent of じゃん is やん, contracted from やないか.

> おいしいやん。
> "This is good, isn't it!"

> できるやん。
> "You can do it!"

> もう終わったやん。
> "It's already done, right?"

やん is functionally identical to じゃん but immediately signals Kansai dialect to any Japanese listener. Through comedy shows, Kansai-based television, and the cultural prominence of 大阪, やん has high recognition even among non-Kansai speakers. You do not need to produce it, but you should recognize it instantly when you hear it.

A further variant, やんか, adds emphasis:

> 昨日言ったやんか。
> "I told you yesterday, didn't I!" (slightly stronger than やん alone)

### Register Note

Both じゃん and やん are strictly casual. They would not appear in business meetings, formal writing, or conversations with superiors. They are, however, used across all age groups and genders in appropriate casual contexts.

## 16.3 Dropped Particles in Natural Speech

Textbook Japanese includes every particle. Natural speech does not. Understanding which particles get dropped — and which ones resist dropping — is essential for parsing real conversation.

### を — The Most Commonly Dropped Particle

The direct object marker を is dropped so frequently in casual speech that its presence can actually sound slightly formal or deliberate.

> ビール飲む？ (= ビールを飲む？)
> "Want some beer?"

> テレビ見てた。 (= テレビを見ていた)
> "I was watching TV."

> 宿題やった？ (= 宿題をやった？)
> "Did you do your homework?"

> ご飯食べよう。 (= ご飯を食べよう)
> "Let's eat."

In all of these, the meaning is completely clear without を because the noun-verb relationship is obvious from context and word order.

### は — Dropped When the Topic Is Obvious

The topic marker は is dropped when the topic is understood from context — which is very often in conversation, where both speakers know what they are talking about.

> 明日、来る？ (= 明日は来る？)
> "Coming tomorrow?"

> これ、おいしいね。 (= これはおいしいね)
> "This is good, isn't it."

> 俺、もう帰る。 (= 俺はもう帰る)
> "I'm heading home now."

> あの店、高いよ。 (= あの店は高いよ)
> "That place is expensive."

### に — Dropped in Some Contexts

The particle に is dropped less aggressively than を or は, but it does disappear in certain common patterns, especially with time expressions and destinations that are contextually obvious.

> 三時、来て。 (= 三時に来て)
> "Come at three."

> 週末、何するの？ (= 週末に何をするの？)
> "What are you doing this weekend?"

> 東京、行ったことある？ (= 東京に行ったことがある？)
> "Have you ever been to Tokyo?"

### が — Rarely Dropped

The subject marker が resists dropping more than other particles. This makes linguistic sense: が marks new information — it tells you who or what the sentence is about. Dropping it would create genuine ambiguity.

However, が does get dropped in a few fixed patterns:

> 誰来たの？ (= 誰が来たの？)
> "Who came?"

> 何あったの？ (= 何があったの？)
> "What happened?"

These work because the question word (誰, 何) already signals that the following verb needs a subject. Outside of question words, が is usually preserved.

### Multiple Particle Drops in One Sentence

In very casual speech, multiple particles can be dropped simultaneously:

> 昨日、映画、見た。 (= 昨日、映画を見た。)
> "Saw a movie yesterday."

> 俺、明日、仕事、休む。 (= 俺は明日仕事を休む。)
> "I'm taking off work tomorrow."

The sentence becomes a string of content words with pauses (marked by commas in writing) where particles would be. Word order and context carry the grammatical load.

### When Particles Cannot Be Dropped

Some particles almost never drop, even in the most casual speech:

- **で** (locative/instrumental): 電車で行く cannot become 電車行く without ambiguity
- **から / まで** (from/until): 三時から cannot be reliably communicated without から
- **と** (with, and): 友達と行く needs と to distinguish "going with a friend" from other relationships

The pattern: particles drop when their absence creates no ambiguity. Particles remain when their absence would make the sentence genuinely unclear.

## 16.4 わ, さ, な as Fillers and Softeners

Stage 2, Chapter 28 covered these as sentence-final particles. Here we focus on their use **mid-sentence** as fillers, rhythm markers, and softeners — a function that is distinct from their sentence-final role and equally important for comprehension.

### さ — The All-Purpose Filler

さ inserted after a phrase creates a brief pause and a casual, conversational rhythm. It does not change meaning. It signals "I'm thinking as I talk" or "let me tell you something in a casual way."

> それはさ、ちょっと違うと思うんだよね。
> "That, you know, I think that's a bit different."

> 昨日さ、すごいことがあったんだよ。
> "So yesterday, something amazing happened."

> 俺さ、来月から大阪に転勤なんだ。
> "So, I'm transferring to Osaka from next month."

> でもさ、それって本当なの？
> "But like, is that really true?"

さ is common in Tokyo and standard casual speech. It is used by all genders. When you encounter さ mid-sentence, mentally translate it as a brief conversational pause and move on — it carries no grammatical content.

### な — Thinking-Aloud Softener

Mid-sentence な signals that the speaker is processing their thoughts. It is similar to さ but has a slightly more reflective quality.

> うーん、それはな、ちょっと説明が難しいんだけど...
> "Hmm, that, well, it's a bit hard to explain..."

> あの人はな、信用できるよ。
> "That person, you know, you can trust them."

At the end of a clause (but not the end of the whole sentence), な softens a statement or adds a moment of reflection:

> 難しいな、これ。
> "This is tough, isn't it." (thinking aloud)

> 高いな、でも買おうかな。
> "It's expensive... but maybe I'll buy it."

### わ — Regional and Generational Variation

As noted in Stage 2, sentence-final わ in Tokyo Japanese has traditionally been marked as feminine speech. However, its mid-sentence use as a softener or filler does not carry the same gender association in all regions.

In Kansai, わ is used freely by all genders, typically with a different intonation pattern — flatter, not rising:

> 知らんわ。 (Kansai, all genders)
> "I don't know." (blunt but not harsh)

> もう行くわ。 (Kansai, all genders)
> "I'm going now."

In standard Japanese, sentence-final わ with falling intonation has been shifting. Younger male speakers in casual settings occasionally use わ in a way that would have been considered feminine a generation ago:

> あー、疲れたわ。
> "Ah, I'm tired." (casual, younger male speakers also use this)

For recognition purposes: when you hear わ, do not automatically assign a gender to the speaker. Context — regional origin, age, tone of voice — matters more than any simple rule.

## 16.5 ～っす as Casual-Polite Hybrid

### What It Is

～っす is a shortened version of ～です that has emerged primarily among younger male speakers (teens through thirties) as a way to be polite without being fully formal. It occupies a middle ground: more polite than plain form, but more casual than proper です/ます.

> お疲れっす。 (= お疲れさまです)
> "Thanks for your work." (casual-polite)

> そうっすね。 (= そうですね)
> "Yeah, that's right."

> マジっすか。 (= 本当ですか)
> "Seriously?" (casual-polite)

> 大丈夫っす。 (= 大丈夫です)
> "I'm fine." / "No problem."

> ありがとうございまっす。 (= ありがとうございます)
> "Thanks!" (very casual — ます itself gets shortened)

### Where It Appears

～っす is most commonly heard:

- From younger male employees speaking to slightly senior colleagues (not top management, but 先輩)
- In sports teams (後輩 to 先輩)
- In part-time jobs, especially casual workplaces
- Among male friends when there is a slight age or status gap that makes pure plain form feel too rough, but full です/ます feels too stiff

### Social Perception

～っす is widely understood but not universally accepted. Some older speakers find it sloppy or disrespectful. In job interviews, meetings with clients, or academic settings, it would be inappropriate. Think of it as the Japanese equivalent of saying "yeah, for sure" to a slightly senior coworker instead of "yes, certainly" — comprehensible, not rude in casual contexts, but not appropriate in formal ones.

### Formation

っす replaces です after any predicate that would take です:

| Standard | っす Version |
|---|---|
| いいです | いいっす |
| 寒いです | 寒いっす |
| 元気です | 元気っす |
| 学生です | 学生っす |
| 行きます | 行きまっす (rare — っす primarily replaces です, not ます) |

The ます→まっす pattern exists but is less systematic. ～っす primarily attaches where です would go.

### For Learners

Recognize ～っす as a casual politeness marker. You will hear it frequently in slice-of-life anime, sports anime, workplace dramas featuring younger characters, and in real life from younger Japanese speakers. You do not need to produce it — using standard です is always safe and appropriate — but you should parse it without hesitation.

## 16.6 Listening Passage — Natural Conversation with Contractions

The following conversation takes place between two university students, ゆうき and あかり, who run into each other on campus. Read it first, noting all contracted forms, then check the full-form version and translation below.

### Passage

> ゆうき：あ、あかり。久しぶりじゃん。最近どうしてたの？
>
> あかり：うん、ちょっと忙しくてさ、バイト増やしちゃったんだよね。週五で入ってて、もう毎日ヘトヘト。
>
> ゆうき：マジで？それ、きつくない？授業もあるじゃん。
>
> あかり：まあね。でもさ、来月旅行に行きたくて、お金貯めとかなきゃって思って。
>
> ゆうき：どこ行くの？
>
> あかり：沖縄。友達に誘われちゃってさ、断れなかったんだよね。
>
> ゆうき：いいじゃん、沖縄！俺も行きたいな。でも金ないわ。
>
> あかり：バイトすればいいじゃん。
>
> ゆうき：いや、俺はさ、レポートやらなきゃいけないのが三つもあってさ、バイトどころじゃないんだよ。
>
> あかり：あー、大変だね。まあ頑張って。じゃ、私もう行かなくちゃ。バイトの時間なんだ。
>
> ゆうき：うん、また連絡して。気をつけてね。

### Contraction Analysis

| Contracted Form | Full Form | Location |
|---|---|---|
| じゃん | じゃないか | 久しぶりじゃん |
| どうしてたの | どうしていたの | 最近どうしてたの |
| 増やしちゃった | 増やしてしまった | バイト増やしちゃった |
| 入ってて | 入っていて | 週五で入ってて |
| きつくない？ | きつくないか？ | それ、きつくない？ |
| 貯めとかなきゃ | 貯めておかなければ | 貯めとかなきゃ |
| って思って | と思って | って思って |
| どこ行くの | どこに行くの | どこ行くの |
| 誘われちゃって | 誘われてしまって | 誘われちゃってさ |
| 断れなかった | (no contraction) | — |
| ないわ | (softened assertion) | 金ないわ |
| やらなきゃいけない | やらなければいけない | やらなきゃいけない |
| 行かなくちゃ | 行かなくては | 行かなくちゃ |
| なんだ | なのだ | バイトの時間なんだ |

Also note the dropped particles: ビール飲む pattern appears as バイト増やしちゃった (を dropped), どこ行くの (に dropped), 金ないわ (が dropped).

### Full-Form Version

> ゆうき：ああ、あかり。久しぶりじゃないか。最近どうしていたの？
>
> あかり：うん、ちょっと忙しくて、アルバイトを増やしてしまったんだよね。週に五回入っていて、もう毎日ヘトヘトだ。
>
> ゆうき：本当に？それは、きつくないか？授業もあるじゃないか。
>
> あかり：まあね。でも、来月旅行に行きたくて、お金を貯めておかなければと思って。
>
> ゆうき：どこに行くの？
>
> あかり：沖縄。友達に誘われてしまって、断れなかったんだよね。
>
> ゆうき：いいじゃないか、沖縄！俺も行きたいな。でもお金がないよ。
>
> あかり：アルバイトをすればいいじゃないか。
>
> ゆうき：いや、俺は、レポートをやらなければいけないのが三つもあって、アルバイトどころではないんだよ。
>
> あかり：ああ、大変だね。まあ頑張って。では、私はもう行かなくてはいけない。アルバイトの時間なのだ。
>
> ゆうき：うん、また連絡して。気をつけてね。

### English Translation

> Yuuki: Oh, Akari. Long time no see! What have you been up to lately?
>
> Akari: Yeah, I've been kind of busy — I ended up picking up more shifts at my part-time job. I'm working five days a week now, and I'm just exhausted every day.
>
> Yuuki: Seriously? Isn't that tough? You have classes too, right?
>
> Akari: Yeah, I guess. But the thing is, I want to go on a trip next month, so I figured I'd better save up some money.
>
> Yuuki: Where are you going?
>
> Akari: Okinawa. A friend invited me and I couldn't turn them down.
>
> Yuuki: Nice, Okinawa! I want to go too. But I don't have any money.
>
> Akari: So just get a part-time job, right?
>
> Yuuki: Nah, I've got like three reports I have to write — a part-time job is out of the question.
>
> Akari: Ah, that's rough. Well, hang in there. Anyway, I gotta go. It's time for my shift.
>
> Yuuki: Sure, get in touch sometime. Take care.
