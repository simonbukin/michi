# Chapter 17 — Speech Acts: How Japanese Does Things with Words

Every language has standard ways of performing social actions — refusing, apologizing, thanking, requesting, complimenting, inviting. What makes Japanese distinctive is not that these actions exist (every language has them) but how they are performed: with what degree of indirectness, what assumptions about shared knowledge, and what social calculations are running beneath the surface.

This chapter is about pragmatics — the study of how language is used in context to accomplish social goals. The grammar you need is mostly grammar you already know. What is new here is understanding how that grammar is deployed to do specific social work, and how to interpret what Japanese speakers mean when what they say is, on the surface, something quite different.

A warning: this chapter will repeatedly show you cases where the literal meaning of a Japanese utterance is not its intended meaning. This is not because Japanese is vague or mysterious. It is because Japanese pragmatic conventions prioritize preserving the listener's face and maintaining social harmony — and the mechanisms for doing so are systematic and learnable.

## 17.1 Refusing — The Art of Not Saying No

Direct refusal in Japanese is rare. The word いいえ exists, and ダメ (no good) exists, and いやだ (I don't want to) exists — but using them to refuse an offer, invitation, or request is blunt to the point of rudeness in most social contexts. Instead, Japanese refusals work through indirection, and the listener is expected to read the refusal from contextual cues.

### すみません、ちょっと... — The Trailing-Off Refusal

The single most important refusal pattern in Japanese is the incomplete sentence. The speaker begins to explain why they cannot comply, then trails off. The trailing off IS the refusal.

> A：今度の土曜日、映画に行かない？
> B：あー、土曜日はちょっと...
>
> A: "Want to go to a movie this Saturday?"
> B: "Ah, Saturday is a little..." (= no)

B does not say what Saturday is a little of. B does not need to. The ちょっと followed by silence or a trailing-off tone is universally understood as a refusal. To press further ("A little what?") would be socially inappropriate — it would force B to either invent a specific excuse or deliver the refusal explicitly, both of which create discomfort.

More examples:

> A：この仕事、手伝ってもらえますか。
> B：すみません、今日はちょっと...
>
> A: "Could you help me with this work?"
> B: "I'm sorry, today is a little..." (= I can't today)

> A：来週の飲み会、来られる？
> B：来週かあ... ちょっと予定が...
>
> A: "Can you come to the drinking party next week?"
> B: "Next week, huh... my schedule is a little..." (= probably not)

### ～のはちょっと... — "That's a Bit..."

A more structured version of the trailing-off refusal. The nominalized action + はちょっと says "doing that is a bit..." and leaves the negative conclusion unstated.

> 今日残業するのはちょっと...
> "Working overtime today is a bit..." (= I'd rather not)

> そこまで行くのはちょっと難しいかもしれません。
> "Going all the way there might be a bit difficult." (= I don't want to)

> 一万円出すのはちょっと...
> "Paying ten thousand yen is a bit..." (= too expensive, I decline)

Note that 難しい (difficult) and 厳しい (tough) in refusal contexts almost never mean literally difficult or tough. They are softened ways of saying "no."

> A：金曜日までにできますか。
> B：金曜日はちょっと厳しいですね...
>
> A: "Can you have it done by Friday?"
> B: "Friday is a bit tough..." (= I cannot do it by Friday)

### 考えさせてください — "Let Me Think About It"

This phrase literally means "please let me think about it." In practice, it very often means "no." The speaker is declining to commit, and in many contexts, that non-commitment is the refusal. No follow-up "I've thought about it and yes" ever comes.

> A：うちの会社に来ませんか。いい条件ですよ。
> B：ありがとうございます。少し考えさせてください。
>
> A: "Won't you come work at our company? The conditions are good."
> B: "Thank you. Please let me think about it." (= probably no)

Whether 考えさせてください is a genuine request for time or a soft refusal depends entirely on context: tone, relationship, the nature of the request. As a learner, be aware that it can function as either.

### せっかくですが... — "Even Though You Went to the Trouble..."

せっかく acknowledges the other person's effort or kindness before declining. It softens the refusal by validating the offer.

> せっかくですが、その日は先約がありまして...
> "I appreciate the offer, but I already have plans that day..." (= no)

> せっかくお誘いいただいたのに、申し訳ありません。
> "I'm sorry even though you kindly invited me." (= I must decline)

### Cultural Note: Why Indirectness?

Japanese refusal indirectness is not about dishonesty. It serves a specific social function: it allows both parties to maintain 面子 (face). The refuser does not have to deliver a blunt rejection, and the person being refused does not have to hear one. Both can pretend the door is technically still open, even when both know it is closed. This saves the relationship for future interactions.

As a learner, the practical implication is: when you hear ちょっと... with trailing off, 難しい, 厳しい, or 考えさせてください in response to a request or invitation, assume the answer is probably no unless strong evidence suggests otherwise.

## 17.2 Apologizing — すみません and the Apology Spectrum

Japanese has a rich system of apology expressions that vary by formality, severity, and social context. Apologizing is one of the most frequently performed speech acts in Japanese — far more frequent than in English — because the threshold for what warrants an apology is lower.

### ごめん / ごめんなさい — Casual to Semi-Polite

ごめん is the most casual apology. ごめんなさい is its polite extension. Both are used for minor transgressions among friends, family, and people of equal or lower status.

> ごめん、遅れた！
> "Sorry, I'm late!"

> ごめんなさい、忘れてました。
> "I'm sorry, I had forgotten."

> ごめんね、待たせちゃって。
> "Sorry for making you wait."

ごめん/ごめんなさい would not be used to a superior in a professional context. It is too casual for that purpose.

### すみません — The Workhorse

すみません is the most versatile apology (and, as we will see in 17.3, also serves as a thank-you). It works across a wide range of situations:

> すみません、少し遅れます。
> "I'm sorry, I'll be a bit late."

> すみません、もう一度お願いします。
> "Excuse me, could you say that one more time?"

> すみません、ここは禁煙です。
> "Excuse me, this is a no-smoking area." (polite correction)

すみません literally derives from 済む (to be settled) in the negative — "it is not settled," meaning "I cannot rest easy about this." This origin explains its use for both apology and gratitude: in both cases, the speaker feels an unresolved social debt.

### 申し訳ありません / 申し訳ございません — Formal Apology

申し訳ありません (and its more formal variant 申し訳ございません) is used for serious apologies, professional mistakes, and situations involving superiors or customers.

> 大変申し訳ございません。こちらの手違いでした。
> "We are deeply sorry. It was an error on our part."

> 申し訳ありません、お待たせいたしました。
> "I apologize for keeping you waiting."

> ご迷惑をおかけして、申し訳ございません。
> "I apologize for causing you trouble."

申し訳 means "excuse" or "justification." 申し訳ない literally means "there is no excuse" — the speaker acknowledges that no justification is adequate for their error.

### 失礼しました — "I Was Rude"

失礼しました is used when the speaker has committed a breach of manners or etiquette, rather than causing material harm.

> 失礼しました。お名前を間違えてしまいました。
> "I apologize. I got your name wrong."

> 先ほどは失礼しました。
> "I'm sorry about earlier." (apologizing for a social misstep)

### The Apology Scale

| Expression | Formality | Context |
|---|---|---|
| ごめん | Very casual | Friends, family |
| ごめんなさい | Casual-polite | Friends, acquaintances |
| すみません | Standard polite | General use, strangers |
| 申し訳ありません | Formal | Workplace, customers |
| 申し訳ございません | Very formal | Business, serious errors |
| 失礼しました | Formal (social) | Etiquette breaches |

### Cultural Note: Apologizing Preemptively

Japanese speakers often apologize before the offense has even occurred, or for things that would not warrant an apology in English:

> お忙しいところすみませんが... (before asking a favor)
> "I'm sorry to bother you when you're busy, but..."

> 突然のご連絡で申し訳ありません。 (beginning of an email)
> "I apologize for contacting you suddenly."

These preemptive apologies are not signs of excessive guilt. They are social lubricant — they acknowledge the imposition before it happens and express awareness that the listener's time and space are being encroached upon.

## 17.3 Thanking — ありがとう and Beyond

### ありがとうございます — Standard Thanks

ありがとうございます is the standard polite thank-you. ありがとう is its casual counterpart. Both are straightforward.

> ありがとうございます。とても助かりました。
> "Thank you. That was a great help."

> ありがとう！嬉しい！
> "Thanks! I'm happy!"

Past tense ありがとうございました is used when thanking for something that has been completed:

> 今日は本当にありがとうございました。
> "Thank you so much for today."

### すみません as Gratitude

This is one of the most important pragmatic facts about Japanese: すみません functions as a thank-you in many contexts. When someone goes out of their way for you, causes themselves inconvenience on your behalf, or gives you something, すみません acknowledges the social debt you have incurred.

> (Someone picks up something you dropped)
> すみません。
> "Thank you." (literally: "I'm sorry" — but functioning as gratitude)

> (A colleague stays late to help you)
> すみません、お手数おかけしました。
> "Thank you, I'm sorry for the trouble." (both apology and gratitude simultaneously)

> (Someone gives you a gift)
> あ、すみません、わざわざ...
> "Oh, you shouldn't have..." (literally: "I'm sorry, going to such trouble...")

The logic: receiving a favor or gift creates an obligation. すみません acknowledges that obligation. It says "I recognize that you have inconvenienced yourself for my sake, and I feel the weight of that."

For learners, the practical takeaway: when you hear すみません and no one has done anything wrong, it is functioning as a thank-you, not an apology.

### お世話になりました / お世話になっております

お世話になりました ("I have been in your care") is used when thanking someone for ongoing support, help, or a relationship. It is extremely common in business and institutional contexts.

> 長い間、お世話になりました。
> "Thank you for everything over this long period." (said when leaving a job, school, etc.)

The present progressive form お世話になっております is a standard business greeting, used at the beginning of phone calls and emails:

> いつもお世話になっております。
> "Thank you for your continued support." (standard business email opener)

### 助かりました / 助かります

助かりました ("I was saved / helped") is a warm, slightly casual expression of gratitude that emphasizes how much the help meant to the speaker.

> 本当に助かりました。ありがとうございます。
> "You really saved me. Thank you."

> 手伝ってくれて助かった。
> "Thanks for helping — that was a real help."

The present tense 助かります is used when someone offers help:

> A：荷物、持ちましょうか。
> B：あ、助かります。
>
> A: "Shall I carry that for you?"
> B: "Oh, that would be a great help."

### Gratitude Scale

| Expression | Formality | Context |
|---|---|---|
| ありがとう | Casual | Friends, family |
| ありがとうございます | Standard polite | General use |
| ありがとうございました | Standard polite (completed) | After an event or favor |
| すみません | Polite (debt-acknowledging) | When someone inconveniences themselves |
| お世話になりました | Formal | Leaving an institution, end of relationship |
| お世話になっております | Business formal | Ongoing professional relationship |
| 助かりました | Warm, semi-casual | When help was genuinely needed |

## 17.4 Requesting — The Indirectness Scale

Japanese has a rich gradient of request forms, from direct to extremely indirect. The more indirect the form, the more polite it is — because indirectness gives the listener more room to refuse without losing face.

### Direct: てくれる？

The most direct casual request. Used with friends, family, and people of equal or lower status.

> ちょっと手伝ってくれる？
> "Can you help me a bit?"

> 塩取ってくれる？
> "Can you pass the salt?"

> 駅まで送ってくれる？
> "Can you give me a ride to the station?"

### One Step Up: てもらえる？

もらえる (potential form of もらう — "to receive") frames the request as "is it possible for me to receive the favor of you doing X?" This is slightly more polite because it focuses on the speaker's receiving rather than the listener's doing.

> ちょっと手伝ってもらえる？
> "Could you help me a bit?"

> この書類、見てもらえる？
> "Could you look at this document?"

### Polite: ていただけますか

いただく is the humble form of もらう. ていただけますか is its potential form in polite speech. This is the standard polite request form used with strangers, superiors, and in professional contexts.

> すみません、ここに名前を書いていただけますか。
> "Excuse me, could you write your name here?"

> もう少しゆっくり話していただけますか。
> "Could you speak a little more slowly?"

> 資料を送っていただけますか。
> "Could you send me the materials?"

### Very Polite: ていただけませんか

Adding the negative ませんか makes the request even more indirect — "would it not be possible for me to receive...?"

> お時間をいただけませんか。
> "Could I possibly have some of your time?"

> ご確認いただけませんでしょうか。
> "Would you be so kind as to check this?"

### Extremely Polite: ていただければ幸いです

This means "if I could receive the favor of you doing X, it would be my good fortune." It is used in formal business correspondence and very polite spoken requests.

> ご検討いただければ幸いです。
> "I would be grateful if you could consider this."

> お返事いただければ幸いです。
> "I would be grateful if you could reply."

> 来週中にご連絡いただければ幸いでございます。
> "I would be most grateful if you could contact me within next week."

### The Full Scale

| Form | Directness | Typical Context |
|---|---|---|
| てくれ | Very direct (male, rough) | Close friends, subordinates |
| てくれる？ | Direct casual | Friends, peers |
| てもらえる？ | Slightly indirect casual | Friends, slight distance |
| てくれませんか | Polite | General polite use |
| ていただけますか | Formal polite | Superiors, strangers |
| ていただけませんか | Very formal | Business, formal situations |
| ていただければ幸いです | Extremely formal | Business correspondence |

### Softening with Qualifiers

Requests at any level can be further softened with qualifiers:

> もしよろしければ... (if it's all right with you...)
> お忙しいところ恐れ入りますが... (I'm sorry to trouble you when you're busy, but...)
> お手数ですが... (I know it's trouble, but...)
> もし可能であれば... (if it's possible...)

These phrases appear before the actual request and serve to acknowledge the imposition:

> もしよろしければ、お電話番号を教えていただけますか。
> "If it's all right with you, could you tell me your phone number?"

> お忙しいところ恐れ入りますが、ご確認いただけますでしょうか。
> "I'm sorry to bother you when you're busy, but could you check this?"

## 17.5 Complimenting and Deflecting

### Giving Compliments

Japanese compliments are structurally straightforward — they use the adjectives and expressions you already know:

> 日本語がお上手ですね。
> "Your Japanese is very good."

> この料理、すごくおいしいですね。
> "This food is really delicious."

> その服、似合ってるね。
> "Those clothes look good on you."

> さすがですね。
> "As expected of you." (acknowledging someone's skill or reputation)

### Deflecting Compliments — The Expected Response

What is culturally distinctive is the response. In English, the expected response to a compliment is "thank you." In Japanese, the expected response is deflection — denying or minimizing the compliment. Accepting a compliment directly can sound arrogant or socially tone-deaf.

**いいえ、まだまだです — "No, I still have a long way to go":**

> A：日本語がお上手ですね。
> B：いいえ、まだまだです。
>
> A: "Your Japanese is very good."
> B: "No, I still have a long way to go."

This is the single most common compliment deflection. まだまだ ("not yet, not yet") conveys humility by insisting that one's level is far from satisfactory.

**とんでもないです — "Not at all" / "That's too much":**

> A：プレゼンテーション、素晴らしかったですよ。
> B：とんでもないです。皆さんのおかげです。
>
> A: "Your presentation was wonderful."
> B: "Not at all. It's thanks to everyone."

とんでもない literally means "absurd" or "outrageous" — the speaker is saying the compliment is an exaggeration. Adding です makes it polite. The phrase 皆さんのおかげです ("it's thanks to everyone") redirects credit away from the individual.

**そんなことないです — "That's not the case":**

> A：本当に料理が上手ですね。
> B：そんなことないですよ。まだ失敗ばかりです。
>
> A: "You're really good at cooking."
> B: "That's not true. I still fail all the time."

**いえいえ — "No, no":**

> A：いつも助けてくれてありがとう。
> B：いえいえ、大したことないよ。
>
> A: "Thanks for always helping me."
> B: "No, no, it's nothing."

### Cultural Note: Sincerity in Deflection

Compliment deflection in Japanese is not false modesty. It is a social convention that both parties understand. The complimenter knows the compliment is genuine. The receiver knows the complimenter knows it is genuine. The deflection is a ritual that maintains social harmony by demonstrating that the receiver does not consider themselves above others.

As a learner, you should: (1) deflect compliments in Japanese contexts rather than saying ありがとうございます directly (though adding a thank-you after the deflection is fine), and (2) not take Japanese speakers' self-deprecation at face value — when someone says まだまだです, they are being polite, not genuinely claiming incompetence.

## 17.6 Inviting — Accepting, Hedging, and Declining

### Extending Invitations

Invitations in Japanese typically use the negative question form, which is softer than a direct proposal:

> 今度、一緒にご飯でも食べませんか。
> "Would you like to have a meal together sometime?"

> 週末、映画を見に行かない？
> "Want to go see a movie this weekend?"

> よかったら、お茶でもいかがですか。
> "If you'd like, would you care for some tea?"

The でも in these invitations ("a meal or something," "tea or something") is a deliberate vagueness that makes the invitation feel lighter and easier to decline.

### Accepting — ぜひ

The most enthusiastic acceptance uses ぜひ ("by all means"):

> A：来週の食事会、来られますか。
> B：ぜひ！楽しみにしています。
>
> A: "Can you come to the dinner party next week?"
> B: "Absolutely! I'm looking forward to it."

> A：今度うちに遊びに来ませんか。
> B：ぜひ、お邪魔させてください。
>
> A: "Won't you come visit my home sometime?"
> B: "By all means, I'd love to come."

Other acceptance patterns:

> いいですね。行きましょう。
> "That sounds good. Let's go."

> もちろん。
> "Of course."

> 喜んで。
> "I'd be delighted." (more formal)

### Hedging — Buying Time Without Committing

When the answer is uncertain (or when the speaker wants to decline but is not ready to do so), hedging phrases create space:

> ちょっと予定を確認してみます。
> "Let me check my schedule." (may be genuine, may be a soft decline)

> ちょっと考えさせてください。
> "Let me think about it."

> 行けたら行きます。
> "If I can go, I'll go." (very often means "I probably won't go")

The last pattern — 行けたら行く — deserves special attention. To English speakers, "if I can go, I'll go" sounds like a reasonable conditional. In Japanese pragmatics, it is widely understood as a polite way of saying "probably not." There are internet discussions and memes in Japanese about this exact phrase and its true meaning. When someone says 行けたら行く, do not count on their attendance.

### Declining Indirectly

Declining an invitation uses the same indirect strategies covered in 17.1:

> A：日曜日、バーベキューするんだけど、来ない？
> B：日曜日かあ... ちょっと用事があって...
>
> A: "We're having a barbecue on Sunday — want to come?"
> B: "Sunday, huh... I have some things to do..." (= no)

> A：今度の飲み会、来られる？
> B：うーん、その日はちょっと厳しいかも。
>
> A: "Can you come to the next drinking party?"
> B: "Hmm, that day might be a bit tough." (= probably no)

> A：一緒にカラオケ行かない？
> B：ごめん、今日はちょっと疲れてて...
>
> A: "Want to go to karaoke together?"
> B: "Sorry, I'm kind of tired today..." (= no)

### Recognizing the Full Cycle

A typical invitation sequence might look like this:

> A：来週の土曜日、暇？
> B：土曜日？たぶん大丈夫だけど。
> A：実は友達の誕生日パーティーがあるんだけど、一緒に来ない？
> B：え、いいの？ぜひ行きたい！
> A：じゃあ、また詳しいこと連絡するね。
> B：うん、楽しみにしてる！

> A: "Are you free next Saturday?"
> B: "Saturday? Probably, yeah."
> A: "Actually, there's a friend's birthday party — want to come?"
> B: "Really, is that okay? I'd love to go!"
> A: "Okay, I'll send you the details later."
> B: "Sure, I'm looking forward to it!"

Note how A checks availability first (暇？) before extending the actual invitation. This two-step process is common — it gives B an early exit point (if B says 土曜日はちょっと... at this stage, the invitation never needs to be explicitly refused).

## 17.7 Reading Passage — Indirect Refusals and Social Navigation

### Passage

The following is a workplace dialogue between 山本 (a section chief) and 鈴木 (a younger colleague). 山本 is trying to recruit 鈴木 for an extra project.

> 山本：鈴木さん、ちょっといいですか。
>
> 鈴木：はい、何でしょうか。
>
> 山本：来月から新しいプロジェクトが始まるんですが、鈴木さんにもチームに入ってもらえないかと思いまして。
>
> 鈴木：あ、ありがとうございます。新しいプロジェクトですか。
>
> 山本：ええ。鈴木さんの企画力はチームに必要だと思うんです。
>
> 鈴木：いえ、そんな... まだまだ力不足ですが... ただ、実は今、田中課長の案件もいくつか抱えておりまして、少しスケジュール的に厳しいかもしれないのですが...
>
> 山本：そうですか。もちろん、今の仕事が優先ですからね。無理はしなくていいですよ。
>
> 鈴木：申し訳ありません。もしスケジュールが落ち着きましたら、ぜひお声がけいただければと思います。
>
> 山本：分かりました。また状況を見て相談しましょう。
>
> 鈴木：はい、ありがとうございます。よろしくお願いいたします。

### Analysis

This short exchange contains several speech act patterns from this chapter:

1. **山本's request** uses もらえないかと思いまして — a very indirect request form. He does not say "join the team." He says "I was thinking whether it might be possible for you to join."

2. **鈴木's compliment deflection**: When praised for 企画力, she immediately says まだまだ力不足ですが ("I'm still lacking in ability, but...").

3. **鈴木's refusal**: She never says いいえ or できません. She says スケジュール的に厳しいかもしれない ("schedule-wise it might be tough") — a classic ちょっと厳しい refusal. She cushions it further with 実は and いくつか抱えておりまして (humble form of 抱えている).

4. **山本 reads the refusal**: He immediately says 無理はしなくていいですよ ("you don't have to push yourself"), accepting the refusal gracefully without making 鈴木 say "no" explicitly.

5. **鈴木's face-saving closer**: She says ぜひお声がけいただければ ("please do ask me again if..."), leaving the door symbolically open even though both parties understand the answer is no for now.

6. **Mutual courtesy**: The exchange ends with ありがとうございます and よろしくお願いいたします — standard business closers that restore social equilibrium.

### English Translation

> Yamamoto: Suzuki-san, do you have a moment?
>
> Suzuki: Yes, what is it?
>
> Yamamoto: A new project is starting next month, and I was wondering if you might be able to join the team.
>
> Suzuki: Oh, thank you. A new project?
>
> Yamamoto: Yes. I think the team needs your planning skills.
>
> Suzuki: Oh no, I'm still quite lacking... The thing is, I'm currently handling several matters for Section Chief Tanaka, so schedule-wise it might be a bit difficult...
>
> Yamamoto: I see. Of course, your current work takes priority. You don't need to push yourself.
>
> Suzuki: I'm sorry. If my schedule settles down, I would very much appreciate it if you'd consider asking me again.
>
> Yamamoto: Understood. Let's discuss it again depending on how things look.
>
> Suzuki: Yes, thank you. I look forward to working with you.
