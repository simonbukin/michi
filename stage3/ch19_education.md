# Chapter 19 — Society and Education

At N3 level, learners are ready to engage with the substance of Japanese society -- not just ordering food or asking directions, but understanding how institutions shape daily life. No institution looms larger in Japanese society than the education system. From the moment a child enters elementary school, a clearly defined path stretches ahead: six years of 小学校, three years of 中学校, three years of 高校, and for many, four years of 大学. Each stage carries its own vocabulary, its own culture, and its own pressures.

This chapter introduces the vocabulary and cultural context of Japanese education. You will encounter grammar from earlier chapters in Stage 3 -- compound particles, sentence-ending modality, formal written style, and discourse connectors -- woven naturally into explanations and examples about education. The goal is twofold: expand your thematic vocabulary and reinforce the grammar you have already studied.

## 19.1 The Japanese Education System

### Structure

The Japanese education system follows a **6-3-3-4** structure:

| Level | Japanese | Reading | Duration | Ages | Compulsory? |
|-------|----------|---------|----------|------|-------------|
| Elementary school | 小学校 | しょうがっこう | 6 years | 6-12 | Yes |
| Middle school | 中学校 | ちゅうがっこう | 3 years | 12-15 | Yes |
| High school | 高校 / 高等学校 | こうこう / こうとうがっこう | 3 years | 15-18 | No |
| University | 大学 | だいがく | 4 years | 18-22 | No |
| Graduate school | 大学院 | だいがくいん | 2-5 years | 22+ | No |

Compulsory education (義務教育, ぎむきょういく) covers only elementary and middle school -- nine years total. However, over 98% of students advance to high school, making it effectively universal. University enrollment has risen steadily as well; roughly half of all high school graduates now enter a four-year university.

> 日本では義務教育は９年間であるが、高校への進学率はほぼ100%に近い。

> "In Japan, compulsory education is nine years, but the rate of advancement to high school is close to 100%."

Note the use of であるが (formal written style from Chapter 14) to state the fact and then pivot to a contrasting point.

### The School Year

The Japanese school year begins in **April** and ends in **March**, divided into three terms:

| Term | Japanese | Reading | Months |
|------|----------|---------|--------|
| First term | 一学期 | いちがっき | April-July |
| Second term | 二学期 | にがっき | September-December |
| Third term | 三学期 | さんがっき | January-March |

Between terms, students have vacations:

| Vacation | Japanese | Reading | Approximate period |
|----------|----------|---------|--------------------|
| Summer vacation | 夏休み | なつやすみ | Late July - August |
| Winter vacation | 冬休み | ふゆやすみ | Late December - Early January |
| Spring vacation | 春休み | はるやすみ | Late March - Early April |

The **entrance ceremony** (入学式, にゅうがくしき) in April marks the beginning of a student's time at a school, while the **graduation ceremony** (卒業式, そつぎょうしき) in March marks the end. Cherry blossoms (桜, さくら) bloom during entrance-ceremony season, giving this transition a poetic quality deeply embedded in Japanese culture.

> 桜が咲く季節に入学式が行われるため、日本人にとって桜は「始まり」の象徴である。

> "Because entrance ceremonies are held in the season when cherry blossoms bloom, for Japanese people cherry blossoms are a symbol of 'beginnings.'"

Here にとって (Chapter 4) marks the perspective holder, and である closes with the formal copula.

### 受験 -- The Examination Culture

The word 受験（じゅけん）refers to the process of taking entrance examinations, and it carries enormous cultural weight. Unlike systems where geographic residence determines school assignment, advancement to competitive high schools and universities in Japan depends on passing entrance exams. This creates a culture of intense preparation.

> 受験に合格するために、多くの学生が塾に通っている。

> "In order to pass entrance exams, many students attend cram schools."

Key vocabulary:

| Japanese | Reading | Meaning |
|----------|---------|---------|
| 受験 | じゅけん | taking an entrance exam |
| 受験生 | じゅけんせい | exam candidate (student preparing for exams) |
| 入試 | にゅうし | entrance examination (abbreviation of 入学試験) |
| 合格（する） | ごうかく | passing (an exam) |
| 不合格 | ふごうかく | failing (an exam) |
| 志望校 | しぼうこう | school one hopes to enter |
| 偏差値 | へんさち | deviation value (statistical ranking of schools/students) |

The 偏差値 system deserves a note. Schools are ranked by a numerical deviation score, and students are similarly scored based on practice exams. A student with a 偏差値 of 65 would aim for a school with a similar or lower 偏差値. This numerical matching between student ability and school prestige is central to how the system operates.

### 塾 -- Cram Schools

The 塾（じゅく）is a private after-school tutoring institution. Most are commercial businesses, though some are small neighborhood operations run by a single teacher. Students attend 塾 to supplement their regular schoolwork, especially in preparation for entrance exams.

> 小学生の頃から塾に通い始める子どもも少なくない。

> "There are not a few children who begin attending cram school from elementary school age."

> 塾に通わなければ、いい高校に入れないと考える親もいる。

> "There are also parents who think that if a child doesn't attend cram school, they can't get into a good high school."

The pattern ～も少なくない (are not few = there are quite a few) is a common understatement pattern in written Japanese. The conditional なければ reviews the conditional forms from Stage 2.

### 部活動 -- Club Activities

Club activities (部活動, ぶかつどう, often shortened to 部活, ぶかつ) are a defining feature of Japanese middle and high school life. Students choose a club -- sports or cultural -- and commit to it throughout their school career. Commitment is intense: daily practice after school, weekend tournaments, and summer training camps (合宿, がっしゅく) are standard.

> 日本の中学校や高校では、ほとんどの生徒が何らかの部活動に参加している。

> "At Japanese middle schools and high schools, almost all students participate in some kind of club activity."

Common club types:

| Japanese | Reading | Type | Example |
|----------|---------|------|---------|
| 野球部 | やきゅうぶ | Sports | Baseball club |
| サッカー部 | サッカーぶ | Sports | Soccer club |
| テニス部 | テニスぶ | Sports | Tennis club |
| 吹奏楽部 | すいそうがくぶ | Cultural | Brass band |
| 美術部 | びじゅつぶ | Cultural | Art club |
| 文芸部 | ぶんげいぶ | Cultural | Literary club |

The 先輩・後輩（せんぱい・こうはい）hierarchy is particularly strong within 部活動. First-year members (一年生) are expected to defer to second- and third-year members, handling equipment, cleaning up, and using respectful language. This early training in hierarchical relationships shapes workplace behavior later in life.

> 部活動を通じて、先輩と後輩の関係が自然と身につく。

> "Through club activities, the senpai-kouhai relationship is naturally acquired."

Here を通じて (Chapter 5) means "through (the medium of)."

### 学歴社会 -- The Credential Society

Japan is often described as a 学歴社会（がくれきしゃかい）-- a society where academic credentials heavily influence one's career prospects. The university one graduates from can determine hiring at major companies, and this reputation persists throughout a career.

> 学歴社会と言われる日本では、どの大学を卒業したかが就職に大きな影響を与えることがある。

> "In Japan, which is called a credential society, which university one graduated from can have a large influence on employment."

> しかし、近年では学歴よりも実力を重視する企業も増えてきている。

> "However, in recent years, companies that place more importance on actual ability than academic credentials have been increasing."

The phrase ～と言われる ("is said to be") is a passive construction used for general assertions. The connector しかし (Chapter 13) introduces a contrasting point.

## 19.2 School Life Vocabulary and Culture

### Daily School Life

A typical school day illustrates several cultural features unique to Japan:

**朝礼（ちょうれい）** -- Morning assembly. Students gather in the schoolyard or gymnasium for announcements and sometimes a brief address from the principal (校長先生, こうちょうせんせい).

**掃除（そうじ）** -- Cleaning. Japanese schools generally do not have janitors for classrooms. Students clean their own classrooms, hallways, and toilets daily. This practice is rooted in the belief that cleaning one's own space builds character and responsibility.

> アメリカの学校とは異なり、日本の学校では生徒自身が掃除を行う。

> "Unlike American schools, at Japanese schools the students themselves do the cleaning."

The expression ～とは異なり means "unlike, differing from" and is a formal written-style phrase. The は in とは adds contrastive emphasis (Chapter 1).

**給食（きゅうしょく）** -- School lunch. In elementary and many middle schools, lunch is served in the classroom. Students take turns as 給食当番（きゅうしょくとうばん, lunch duty), serving food to classmates while wearing white coats and masks. Everyone eats the same meal.

> 給食は単なる食事ではなく、食育の一環として位置づけられている。

> "School lunch is not merely a meal; it is positioned as one part of food education."

Here ～ではなく (formal negative of だ) denies the simple interpretation, and 一環として means "as part of."

**ホームルーム** -- Homeroom. A period at the start or end of the day when the homeroom teacher (担任, たんにん) addresses the class about scheduling, behavior, or events.

### School Events

| Japanese | Reading | English | Typical timing |
|----------|---------|---------|----------------|
| 入学式 | にゅうがくしき | Entrance ceremony | April |
| 始業式 | しぎょうしき | Opening ceremony (start of term) | April, Sept, Jan |
| 終業式 | しゅうぎょうしき | Closing ceremony (end of term) | July, Dec, March |
| 卒業式 | そつぎょうしき | Graduation ceremony | March |
| 運動会 | うんどうかい | Sports day | May or October |
| 文化祭 | ぶんかさい | Culture festival | October-November |
| 修学旅行 | しゅうがくりょこう | School trip | Varies by grade |
| 遠足 | えんそく | Day trip / excursion | Spring or autumn |

The 文化祭 is a highlight of the school year. Each class prepares a project -- a cafe, a haunted house, a play, a display -- and the school opens to families and sometimes the public. Preparations happen over weeks and build class solidarity.

> 文化祭の準備を通して、クラスの仲間との絆が深まるものだ。

> "Through preparations for the culture festival, bonds with one's classmates deepen -- that's how it goes."

The sentence-ending ものだ (Chapter 8) here expresses a general truth.

### 制服 -- School Uniforms

Most middle and high schools require uniforms (制服, せいふく). The traditional styles are:

- **詰め襟（つめえり）** -- Stand-collar uniform for boys (also called 学ラン, がくラン)
- **セーラー服（セーラーふく）** -- Sailor-style uniform for girls
- **ブレザー** -- Blazer-style uniform (increasingly common for both genders)

Rules about uniforms can be strict: skirt length, sock color, hairstyle, and even the angle at which a ribbon is tied may be regulated. These school rules (校則, こうそく) are sometimes controversial.

> 校則が厳しすぎるのではないかという議論が近年増えている。

> "Debates about whether school rules are too strict have been increasing in recent years."

The pattern ～のではないか (Chapter 16 colloquial grammar, also used in formal style) expresses a tentative concern or suggestion.

### 不登校 -- School Refusal

An important social issue related to education is 不登校（ふとうこう）-- prolonged absence from school, often translated as "school refusal." The causes range from bullying (いじめ) to academic pressure to mental health difficulties.

> いじめや学業のプレッシャーが原因で不登校になる生徒が増加傾向にある。

> "Students who become school-refusing due to bullying and academic pressure are on an increasing trend."

The expression ～傾向にある (Chapter 14) marks an observed trend.

## 19.3 Reading Passage -- The Japanese Education System

### 日本の教育制度について

日本の教育制度は、小学校六年、中学校三年、高校三年、大学四年という「六・三・三・四制」に基づいている。このうち小学校と中学校の九年間が義務教育である。高校は義務教育ではないが、進学率は98%を超えており、事実上すべての子どもが高校に進む。

日本の学校の特徴として、まず掃除が挙げられる。生徒が自分たちで教室や廊下を掃除するのは、日本では当たり前のことだが、外国人にとっては驚きである。また、部活動も重要な要素である。放課後に行われる部活動を通じて、生徒は協調性や上下関係を学ぶ。

一方で、受験に対する批判もある。志望校に合格するために、塾に通う生徒が多く、小学生のうちから夜遅くまで勉強する子どもも少なくない。学歴社会の問題を指摘する声も増えてきている。しかし、教育の質そのものは高く評価されており、日本の生徒の学力は国際的に見ても上位に位置している。

### English Translation

**About the Japanese Education System**

The Japanese education system is based on a "6-3-3-4 system" of six years of elementary school, three years of middle school, three years of high school, and four years of university. Of these, the nine years of elementary and middle school constitute compulsory education. High school is not compulsory, but the advancement rate exceeds 98%, and virtually all children proceed to high school.

As a distinctive feature of Japanese schools, cleaning can be mentioned first. It is taken for granted in Japan that students clean their own classrooms and hallways, but for foreigners it is a surprise. Additionally, club activities are also an important element. Through club activities conducted after school, students learn cooperativeness and hierarchical relationships.

On the other hand, there is also criticism of the examination system. In order to pass their desired schools' exams, many students attend cram schools, and there are not a few children who study until late at night from elementary school age. Voices pointing out problems with the credential society have also been increasing. However, the quality of education itself is highly evaluated, and the academic ability of Japanese students ranks in the upper tier even from an international perspective.
