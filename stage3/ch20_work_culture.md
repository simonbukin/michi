# Chapter 20 — Work Culture

Japanese work culture is a topic that learners cannot avoid. Whether you plan to work in Japan, do business with Japanese companies, or simply want to understand the society depicted in films and novels, the vocabulary and social dynamics of the workplace are essential knowledge. This chapter covers the lifecycle of a Japanese career -- from the highly ritualized job-hunting process through the daily dynamics of the office -- and introduces the cultural frameworks that govern professional interaction.

Throughout this chapter, you will see Stage 3 grammar at work: compound particles like に対して and に基づいて, modality patterns like べきだ and わけだ, formal written constructions like である, and discourse connectors like 一方で and したがって. The examples are not exercises in grammar drilling; they are how Japanese people actually talk and write about work.

## 20.1 Job Hunting: 就職活動

### The System

Japanese university students typically begin formal job hunting (就職活動, しゅうしょくかつどう, commonly abbreviated to 就活, しゅうかつ) in the spring of their third year. The process is synchronized: most major companies follow a shared schedule dictated by guidelines from the Japan Business Federation (経団連, けいだんれん). This means thousands of students are doing the same thing at the same time -- attending company information sessions, submitting applications, and sitting for interviews.

> 大学三年生の春から就職活動が始まるため、学業との両立が難しいと言われている。

> "Because job hunting begins in the spring of the third year of university, it is said to be difficult to balance with academics."

Key terms of the 就活 process:

| Japanese | Reading | Meaning |
|----------|---------|---------|
| 就職活動 | しゅうしょくかつどう | job hunting (formal) |
| 就活 | しゅうかつ | job hunting (abbreviation) |
| 新卒 | しんそつ | new graduate |
| 新卒採用 | しんそつさいよう | new-graduate hiring |
| 中途採用 | ちゅうとさいよう | mid-career hiring |
| 会社説明会 | かいしゃせつめいかい | company information session |
| エントリーシート | エントリーシート | entry sheet (ES; application form) |
| 履歴書 | りれきしょ | resume |
| 面接 | めんせつ | interview |
| 内定 | ないてい | informal job offer |
| 内定式 | ないていしき | informal-offer ceremony |

### The Timeline

The typical 就活 timeline looks like this:

1. **三年生・夏** -- Internships (インターンシップ or インターン). Many students participate in short-term internships to explore industries.

2. **三年生・秋～冬** -- Self-analysis (自己分析, じこぶんせき) and industry research (業界研究, ぎょうかいけんきゅう). Students identify their strengths and target industries.

3. **三年生・三月** -- Company information sessions begin. Companies hold large-scale events (合同説明会, ごうどうせつめいかい) where students visit booths.

4. **四年生・六月** -- Formal interviews begin. Multiple rounds are common: group discussion (グループディスカッション), individual interview (個人面接, こじんめんせつ), and final interview (最終面接, さいしゅうめんせつ) with senior executives.

5. **四年生・夏～秋** -- 内定 (informal offers) are extended. Students may receive multiple offers and must choose.

6. **四年生・十月** -- 内定式. Companies hold ceremonies for students who have accepted offers, formally welcoming them before they graduate.

> 就活においては、自己分析に基づいて志望動機を明確にすることが重要だとされている。

> "In job hunting, it is considered important to clarify one's motivation for applying based on self-analysis."

Here において (Chapter 5) serves as the formal locative ("in the context of"), and に基づいて (Chapter 5) means "based on."

### エントリーシート

The エントリーシート (often abbreviated ES) is an application form unique to Japanese 就活. Unlike a Western resume that lists qualifications, the ES typically asks open-ended questions:

- 自己PRをしてください。 ("Please introduce your strengths.")
- 学生時代に力を入れたことは何ですか。 ("What did you put effort into during your student years?" -- often abbreviated as ガクチカ)
- 志望動機を教えてください。 ("Please tell us your motivation for applying.")

> ESでは、単に経験を書くだけではなく、そこから何を学んだかを示すべきである。

> "In the ES, one should not merely write about experiences, but demonstrate what one learned from them."

The pattern べきである (Chapter 10 + Chapter 14) combines the obligation modality with the formal copula.

### 内定 and Its Weight

Receiving 内定 is not a legally binding contract, but in practice it functions almost like one. Companies rarely rescind offers, and students who accept 内定 from one company are expected to decline other offers promptly (内定辞退, ないていじたい). Holding multiple offers without deciding is considered socially problematic.

> 内定をもらったからといって、安心するわけにはいかない。入社までにしっかり準備すべきだ。

> "Just because you received an informal offer, you can't simply relax. You should prepare properly before joining the company."

Here わけにはいかない (Chapter 7) expresses "cannot afford to / it is not acceptable to."

## 20.2 The Workplace: Hierarchy and Communication

### Workplace Roles and Titles

Japanese workplaces are organized by a clear hierarchy, and titles are used constantly in daily communication. Addressing a colleague by title rather than name is standard practice.

| Japanese | Reading | Meaning |
|----------|---------|---------|
| 社長 | しゃちょう | company president / CEO |
| 副社長 | ふくしゃちょう | vice president |
| 専務 | せんむ | senior managing director |
| 常務 | じょうむ | managing director |
| 部長 | ぶちょう | department head |
| 課長 | かちょう | section chief |
| 係長 | かかりちょう | team leader |
| 主任 | しゅにん | senior staff |
| 上司 | じょうし | superior / boss (general term) |
| 部下 | ぶか | subordinate |
| 同僚 | どうりょう | colleague (same rank) |
| 先輩 | せんぱい | senior (joined earlier) |
| 後輩 | こうはい | junior (joined later) |

> 上司に対しては、常に敬語を使うのが当然のことである。

> "It is a matter of course to always use keigo toward one's superiors."

The compound particle に対して (Chapter 4) here marks the target of one's behavior. The sentence-ending ことである provides a formal, declarative tone.

### 報連相 -- The Communication Triangle

One of the most frequently cited principles of the Japanese workplace is 報連相（ほうれんそう）, a portmanteau of three words:

| Component | Full word | Reading | Meaning |
|-----------|-----------|---------|---------|
| 報 | 報告 | ほうこく | reporting (results, outcomes) |
| 連 | 連絡 | れんらく | communicating (schedule changes, updates) |
| 相 | 相談 | そうだん | consulting (before making decisions) |

The term is a pun on ほうれん草（ほうれんそう）, meaning "spinach." New employees are taught that 報連相 is the foundation of workplace communication. Failing to 報告 when something goes wrong, neglecting to 連絡 when plans change, or making a decision without 相談 can cause serious friction.

> 報連相を怠ると、小さな問題が大きなトラブルになりかねない。

> "If you neglect reporting, communicating, and consulting, a small problem might well become a large one."

The ending ～かねない (Chapter 12) expresses the risk of an undesirable outcome.

> 分からないことがあったら、自分で判断せずに上司に相談すべきだ。

> "If there is something you don't understand, you should consult your superior rather than deciding on your own."

### Working Hours and 残業

Standard working hours are typically 9:00 to 17:00 or 18:00, but overtime (残業, ざんぎょう) is deeply embedded in Japanese work culture. Although labor reforms (働き方改革, はたらきかたかいかく) have been introduced to reduce excessive overtime, the culture of staying late -- sometimes because one's superiors have not yet left -- persists in many companies.

> 上司がまだ帰っていないのに、先に帰るわけにはいかないと感じる社員も多い。

> "Many employees feel they can't very well leave before their boss has gone home."

Related vocabulary:

| Japanese | Reading | Meaning |
|----------|---------|---------|
| 残業 | ざんぎょう | overtime |
| 残業代 | ざんぎょうだい | overtime pay |
| サービス残業 | サービスざんぎょう | unpaid overtime |
| 有給休暇 | ゆうきゅうきゅうか | paid vacation |
| 有給 | ゆうきゅう | paid leave (abbreviation) |
| 休暇届 | きゅうかとどけ | leave request form |
| 定時 | ていじ | regular working hours end |

> 有給休暇は労働者の権利であるにもかかわらず、取得率は依然として低い。

> "Despite the fact that paid vacation is a worker's right, the utilization rate remains low."

### 飲み会 -- After-Work Drinking

After-work social drinking (飲み会, のみかい) has traditionally been an important bonding ritual. Attending the 飲み会 is considered part of building workplace relationships (人間関係, にんげんかんけい). The first party is called 一次会（いちじかい）, and it is common to continue to a second venue (二次会, にじかい).

> 飲み会への参加を断りにくいと感じる若手社員も少なくないが、最近では無理に参加する必要はないという考え方も広がっている。

> "There are not a few young employees who find it hard to decline participation in drinking parties, but recently the view that there is no need to participate forcibly has been spreading."

## 20.3 名刺交換 -- Business Card Culture

The exchange of business cards (名刺交換, めいしこうかん) is one of the most formalized rituals in Japanese business. It is not a casual exchange -- it follows specific rules that reflect the importance of social positioning and mutual respect.

### The Procedure

1. **Stand up.** 名刺交換 is always performed standing.

2. **Hold the card correctly.** Hold your own card with both hands, text facing the recipient so they can read it. Your fingers should not cover your name or company name.

3. **Present and receive simultaneously.** When both parties are exchanging, cards are presented with the right hand and received with the left, then both hands are brought to hold the received card.

4. **Bow slightly** while presenting. Say your company name and your own name:

   > 「○○会社の田中と申します。よろしくお願いいたします。」

   > "(Company name)'s Tanaka. I look forward to working with you."

5. **Read the received card carefully.** Do not immediately put it away. Glance at the name and title. If you are unsure of the reading, it is acceptable to ask:

   > 「お名前はどのようにお読みすればよろしいでしょうか。」

   > "How should I read your name?"

6. **During a meeting**, place received cards on the table in front of you, arranged in seating order. The card of the highest-ranking person goes on top of your card case (名刺入れ, めいしいれ).

7. **After the meeting**, put cards away carefully into your 名刺入れ. Never write on a card in front of the person who gave it to you.

### Common Mistakes to Avoid

> 名刺を片手で渡したり、受け取った名刺にすぐにメモを書いたりするのは失礼に当たる。

> "Handing over a business card with one hand, or immediately writing notes on a received card, constitutes rudeness."

The pattern ～たり～たりする lists representative actions. 失礼に当たる means "constitutes rudeness / is considered rude."

> 名刺交換の作法は、日本のビジネスにおいて最も基本的なマナーの一つである。

> "The etiquette of business card exchange is one of the most fundamental manners in Japanese business."

## 20.4 Reading Passage -- Japanese Work Culture

### 日本の働き方について

日本の企業文化には、独特な慣習がいくつもある。まず、新卒一括採用という制度がある。これは、毎年決まった時期に大学の卒業予定者をまとめて採用する仕組みである。この制度に基づいて、大学三年生から就職活動を始めるのが一般的となっている。

職場に入ると、まず「報連相」を徹底するように指導される。これは報告・連絡・相談の頭文字を取った言葉で、上司や同僚との円滑なコミュニケーションに欠かせないものとされている。特に新入社員にとっては、何でも一人で判断するのではなく、まず相談するという姿勢が求められる。

一方で、日本の働き方にも変化が見られる。かつては終身雇用や年功序列が当たり前であったが、近年では転職する人が増え、成果主義を導入する企業も出てきた。また、働き方改革により、残業の削減や有給休暇の取得促進が進められている。

日本で働くことを考えている人は、こうした文化的背景を理解した上で、自分に合った働き方を見つけることが大切であろう。

### English Translation

**About Japanese Work Styles**

Japanese corporate culture has a number of distinctive customs. First, there is a system called mass new-graduate hiring. This is a mechanism in which companies hire university students who are expected to graduate at a fixed time each year all at once. Based on this system, it has become standard to begin job hunting from the third year of university.

Upon entering the workplace, one is first instructed to thoroughly practice "hourensou." This is a word formed from the initial characters of reporting, communicating, and consulting, and it is considered indispensable for smooth communication with superiors and colleagues. Especially for new employees, the attitude of consulting first rather than making every judgment alone is expected.

On the other hand, changes can be observed in Japanese work styles as well. Lifetime employment and seniority-based advancement were once taken for granted, but in recent years people who change jobs have increased, and companies introducing performance-based evaluation have appeared. Additionally, through work-style reform, reductions in overtime and promotion of taking paid vacation are being advanced.

For people considering working in Japan, it is probably important to find a work style that suits them after understanding this kind of cultural background.
