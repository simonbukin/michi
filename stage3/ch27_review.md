This is the final chapter of Stage 3. It serves three purposes: to consolidate every grammar pattern from this stage into a comprehensive reference table, to test your reading comprehension with five extended passages spanning different registers and text types, and to provide a self-assessment checklist for evaluating your readiness for N3-level material.

The reading passages are the longest and most complex in the textbook so far. Each one uses grammar from across the entire stage in natural combination. Read them slowly, refer to the grammar table as needed, and use the translations to check your understanding.

## 27.2 Extended Reading Passage 1 — "The Japanese Education System"

### 本文

日本の教育制度は、小学校六年、中学校三年、高等学校三年、大学四年という「六・三・三・四制」を基本としている。このうち、小学校と中学校の九年間は義務教育であり、ほぼ全ての子どもが公立または私立の学校に通っている。高等学校への進学率は約九十八パーセントに達しており、事実上、高校教育もほぼ普遍的なものとなっている。

日本の教育の特徴として、まず学校生活の中心に「集団」があることが挙げられる。給食の配膳、教室の掃除、学校行事の運営など、多くの活動が生徒による自主的な共同作業として行われる。これは単なる教育方法ではなく、協調性や責任感を育てるための意図的な仕組みである。

次に、部活動の存在が大きい。放課後に行われる部活動は、運動部と文化部に分かれ、中学生・高校生の大多数が何らかの部に所属している。部活動を通じて、先輩・後輩関係や努力の大切さを学ぶ機会が得られるとされている。一方で、部活動の過度な負担が生徒の学習時間や休息時間を圧迫しているという批判もある。

日本の教育制度が直面している課題は少なくない。少子化による学校の統廃合、教員の長時間労働、いじめ問題、不登校の増加などが挙げられる。また、画一的な教育に対する疑問の声も高まっており、個性を重視した教育への転換が求められている。

受験制度も大きな論点である。高校入試、大学入試に向けた準備は、多くの生徒にとって大きなストレスとなっている。塾に通う生徒の割合は高く、「受験戦争」という表現は今でも使われることがある。近年、大学入試改革が進められ、知識の暗記だけでなく思考力や表現力を測る方向へと変化しつつあるが、その効果についてはまだ評価が分かれている。

教育は社会の基盤である。日本の教育制度の長所を生かしながら、変化する社会のニーズに応じた改革を続けていくことが、今後の課題であると言えるだろう。

### Translation

The Japanese education system is fundamentally based on the "6-3-3-4 system": six years of elementary school, three years of middle school, three years of high school, and four years of university. Of these, the nine years comprising elementary and middle school are compulsory education, and nearly all children attend either public or private schools. The rate of advancement to high school has reached approximately 98 percent, making high school education, in practical terms, nearly universal.

As a characteristic of Japanese education, first, the centrality of the "group" in school life can be noted. Many activities — serving school lunch, cleaning classrooms, managing school events — are carried out as voluntary collaborative work by the students themselves. This is not merely an educational method but an intentional system for cultivating cooperativeness and a sense of responsibility.

Next, the presence of club activities is significant. Club activities, held after school, are divided into sports clubs and cultural clubs, and the vast majority of middle and high school students belong to some club. It is said that through club activities, students gain opportunities to learn about senpai-kouhai relationships and the importance of effort. On the other hand, there is also criticism that the excessive burden of club activities encroaches on students' study time and rest time.

The challenges facing the Japanese education system are not few. School consolidations due to the declining birthrate, long working hours for teachers, bullying problems, and increases in school refusal can be cited. Additionally, voices questioning uniform education are growing louder, and a shift toward education that values individuality is being called for.

The examination system is also a major point of debate. Preparation for high school entrance exams and university entrance exams is a source of great stress for many students. The proportion of students attending cram schools is high, and the expression "examination war" is still sometimes used. In recent years, university entrance exam reform has been progressing, moving toward measuring not just memorized knowledge but also thinking ability and expressive power, but opinions are still divided on its effectiveness.

Education is the foundation of society. It can be said that the challenge going forward is to continue reforms that respond to the needs of a changing society while making use of the strengths of the Japanese education system.

## 27.3 Extended Reading Passage 2 — "An Earthquake Experience"

### 本文

あの日のことは、今でもはっきり覚えている。二〇一一年三月十一日、金曜日の午後二時四十六分だった。

私は当時、仙台市内の会社で働いていた。突然、机が激しく揺れ始めた。最初は「またいつもの地震か」と思ったが、揺れは止まらなかった。それどころか、どんどん強くなっていった。棚から書類が落ち、コップが床に落ちて割れた。立っていられなくなり、机の下にもぐった。

揺れがおさまった後、すぐに建物の外に出た。同僚たちの顔は青ざめていた。携帯電話はつながらない。テレビもネットも使えない。何が起きたのか、誰も正確には分からなかった。

しばらくして、ラジオからの情報で、宮城県沖で巨大な地震が発生したことを知った。マグニチュード九・〇。日本の観測史上最大の地震だった。そして、津波警報が出ているという。

会社から自宅まで歩いて帰った。普段は車で二十分の距離だが、その日は二時間以上かかった。道路は渋滞し、信号は消えていた。途中、建物にひびが入っているのが見えた。

自宅に着くと、幸い家族は全員無事だった。しかし、電気もガスも水道も止まっていた。その夜は暗い部屋の中で、毛布にくるまって過ごした。余震が何度も来て、そのたびに体が強張った。

あの経験を通じて、私は防災の大切さを痛感した。非常用の水や食料を備蓄すること、家族と避難場所を確認しておくこと、そして何より、日頃から地震への心構えを持っておくことが、命を守るために不可欠だと、あの日以来ずっと思い続けている。

### Translation

I still remember that day clearly. It was the afternoon of Friday, March 11, 2011, at 2:46 PM.

At the time, I was working at a company in Sendai. Suddenly, my desk began shaking violently. At first I thought, "Another ordinary earthquake?" But the shaking did not stop. On the contrary, it kept getting stronger. Documents fell from shelves, a cup fell to the floor and shattered. I could no longer stand and crawled under my desk.

After the shaking subsided, I immediately went outside the building. My colleagues' faces were pale. Cell phones could not connect. Neither TV nor internet was usable. No one knew exactly what had happened.

After a while, I learned from radio reports that a massive earthquake had occurred off the coast of Miyagi Prefecture. Magnitude 9.0. The largest earthquake in Japan's recorded history. And tsunami warnings had been issued.

I walked home from the office. Normally it is a twenty-minute drive, but that day it took more than two hours. Roads were congested and traffic lights were out. Along the way, I could see cracks in buildings.

When I arrived home, fortunately my entire family was safe. However, electricity, gas, and water were all cut off. That night, I spent huddled in blankets in a dark room. Aftershocks came again and again, and each time my body tensed up.

Through that experience, I keenly felt the importance of disaster preparedness. Stockpiling emergency water and food, confirming evacuation sites with family, and above all, maintaining a state of mental readiness for earthquakes at all times — I have continued to believe, ever since that day, that these things are essential for protecting lives.

## 27.5 Extended Reading Passage 4 — "A Restaurant Blog Review"

### 本文

先週、友達に教えてもらった代官山のイタリアンに行ってきた！ 前から気になってたんだけど、なかなか予約が取れなくて、やっと行けた〜。

まず前菜の盛り合わせを頼んだんだけど、これがもう最高。生ハムがとろっとしてて、チーズはこってり濃厚。パンもカリカリのふわふわで、何個でも食べられそうだった。

メインはパスタにした。私はトリュフのクリームパスタ、友達はボロネーゼ。一口食べた瞬間、「うまっ！」って声が出ちゃった。パスタのゆで加減が絶妙で、ソースとの絡みもばっちり。友達のボロネーゼも一口もらったけど、お肉がごろごろ入ってて、こっちもめちゃくちゃおいしかった。

デザートのティラミスもぺろっと完食。ほろ苦いコーヒーの味がしっかりしてて、甘すぎないのがよかった。

お会計は二人で一万二千円くらい。この内容でこの値段なら、コスパ最高だと思う。絶対また行く。予約は早めがおすすめです！

### Translation

Last week, I went to an Italian restaurant in Daikanyama that a friend told me about! I had been interested in it for a while, but I could never get a reservation, and I finally made it!

The moment I walked into the restaurant, a wonderful smell wafted over me, and that alone got my excitement up. The interior had a calm atmosphere, and the slightly dim lighting was stylish. There were counter and table seats, and we were shown to a table.

First, we ordered an appetizer platter, and it was absolutely amazing. The prosciutto was melt-in-your-mouth, and the cheese was rich and full-bodied. The bread was crunchy on the outside and fluffy on the inside — I felt like I could eat any number of pieces.

For the main course, we chose pasta. I had the truffle cream pasta and my friend had the Bolognese. The moment I took a bite, I blurted out "Delicious!" The pasta was cooked to perfection, and it was perfectly coated with sauce. I got a bite of my friend's Bolognese too, and it was loaded with chunks of meat — that one was incredibly delicious too.

I also polished off the tiramisu for dessert in no time. The bittersweet coffee flavor was strong, and I liked that it was not too sweet.

The bill came to about 12,000 yen for two people. For this quality at this price, I think the value is outstanding. I am definitely going again. I recommend making reservations early!

## 27.6 Extended Reading Passage 5 — "News Article: Social Issue"

### 本文

日本における「ヤングケアラー」の問題が、近年ようやく社会的な注目を集めるようになった。ヤングケアラーとは、家族の介護や世話を日常的に行っている十八歳未満の子どもを指す。病気の親の看病、障害のある兄弟の世話、幼い弟や妹の面倒など、本来は大人が担うべき責任を子どもが引き受けている状態である。

厚生労働省が実施した調査によると、中学生の約十七人に一人がヤングケアラーに該当するとされている。しかし、この数字は実態を反映しきれていない可能性がある。多くのヤングケアラーは、自分の状況を「普通のこと」と認識しており、支援を求めないケースが少なくない。周囲の大人も、その子どもが過度な負担を抱えていることに気づかない場合がある。

ヤングケアラーが直面する問題は深刻である。学業への影響が最も顕著であり、宿題をする時間がない、遅刻や欠席が増える、進学を諦めるといった事例が報告されている。加えて、同年代の友人との交流が減少し、孤立感を深める傾向がある。精神的な負担も大きく、うつ状態に陥るケースも確認されている。

この問題に対する取り組みは、まだ十分とは言えない。二〇二二年に「こども家庭庁」が設立され、ヤングケアラー支援の窓口が整備されつつあるが、実際にサービスにつながっている子どもの数は限られている。学校における早期発見の仕組みづくりや、地域の相談体制の充実が急務である。

ヤングケアラーの問題は、介護、福祉、教育が交差する複合的な課題であり、一つの制度だけでは解決できない。社会全体で子どもの権利を守るという視点に立ち、包括的な支援体制を構築していくことが求められている。

### Translation

The problem of "young carers" in Japan has finally begun to attract societal attention in recent years. A young carer refers to a child under the age of eighteen who routinely provides care or assistance to family members. Nursing a sick parent, caring for a sibling with a disability, looking after younger brothers or sisters — it is a state in which children are taking on responsibilities that adults should originally bear.

According to a survey conducted by the Ministry of Health, Labour and Welfare, approximately one in seventeen middle school students is estimated to qualify as a young carer. However, this figure may not fully reflect the actual situation. Many young carers perceive their situation as "normal," and cases where they do not seek support are not few. Adults around them may also fail to notice that these children are carrying an excessive burden.

The problems that young carers face are serious. The impact on academic performance is most conspicuous, with reported cases of having no time for homework, increasing tardiness and absences, and giving up on further education. In addition, there is a tendency for interaction with same-age friends to decrease, deepening feelings of isolation. The psychological burden is also great, and cases of falling into depression have been confirmed.

Efforts to address this problem cannot yet be called sufficient. In 2022, the Children and Families Agency was established, and support services for young carers are being developed, but the number of children actually connected to services is limited. Building systems for early detection in schools and enhancing community consultation structures are urgent priorities.

The young carer problem is a complex issue where caregiving, welfare, and education intersect, and it cannot be solved by a single system alone. Taking the perspective of protecting children's rights as a whole society, building a comprehensive support system is what is being called for.

## 27.7 Listening Comprehension Notes

At the end of Stage 3, your listening targets should include the following:

### Natural Speed

By now, you should be working with audio at or near natural speed. Textbook audio with clear enunciation and deliberate pauses is useful for studying specific grammar, but real comprehension requires exposure to natural speech patterns — contracted forms, swallowed syllables, and rapid topic shifts.

### What to Listen For

1. **Discourse connectors as signposts.** When you hear でも, しかし, ところが, you know a contrast is coming. When you hear それで, そのため, you know a consequence follows. Use these as anchors.

2. **Aizuchi patterns.** In natural conversation, the listener's reactions (うん, そうですか, へえ, なるほど) tell you how the speaker's message is landing. If the listener says えっ, something unexpected was just said. If they say なるほどね, the speaker made a point that was understood and accepted.

3. **Register shifts.** In a single conversation, speakers may shift between polite and casual forms depending on who they are addressing, how comfortable they feel, and what they are talking about. Track these shifts — they carry social meaning.

4. **Dialect awareness.** At minimum, recognize Kansai dialect features (や, へん, おる) so they do not block comprehension. You do not need to produce them.

5. **Implied meaning.** When someone says ちょっと... and trails off, the refusal is complete. When someone says 考えさせてください, the answer is probably no. Listen for what is not said as much as what is said.

### Recommended Practice

- NHK ニュース（やさしい日本語版）for news listening at controlled speed
- Podcasts aimed at intermediate learners for extended listening
- Variety shows and dramas (with Japanese subtitles) for natural conversation
- Audiobooks of graded readers for sustained listening practice

## 27.8 Self-Assessment Checklist

Use this checklist to evaluate your readiness. For each item, honestly assess whether you can do it consistently, sometimes, or not yet.

### Grammar Recognition

- [ ] I can identify は as contrastive vs topic-marking in context.
- [ ] I can distinguish は and が in most contexts.
- [ ] I can parse compound particles (について, に対して, において, etc.) without stopping.
- [ ] I can interpret わけだ, わけがない, わけにはいかない, わけではない correctly.
- [ ] I can distinguish the three uses of ものだ (general truth, nostalgia, obligation).
- [ ] I can interpret ことだ (advice) and ことはない (no need).
- [ ] I can understand べきだ, はずだ, and their negative/past forms.
- [ ] I can recognize and interpret ざるを得ない, かねる, かねない, がち, 気味, 得る.
- [ ] I can read である-style prose without converting it mentally to だ.
- [ ] I can recognize hedged assertions: と考えられる, と言える, と思われる.

### Reading

- [ ] I can read a 500-character passage in under 5 minutes with general comprehension.
- [ ] I can identify the text type (news, essay, blog, email, instruction) within the first few sentences.
- [ ] I can use discourse connectors to predict text structure.
- [ ] I can handle mixed-register texts without losing track of the overall meaning.
- [ ] I can read vertical text without significantly reduced speed.

### Vocabulary and Kanji

- [ ] I recognize approximately 600+ kanji in context.
- [ ] I can guess compound meanings from component kanji with reasonable accuracy.
- [ ] I know key vocabulary in the domains of education, work, technology, environment, and social relationships.

### Pragmatics and Communication

- [ ] I can recognize indirect refusals and hedging in Japanese.
- [ ] I understand the aizuchi system and can use basic back-channeling.
- [ ] I can identify formality level in written text (casual blog vs business email vs newspaper).
- [ ] I recognize common Kansai dialect features without being confused.

### What Comes Next

If most items are checked, you are ready for N3-level authentic materials and can begin preparing for Stage 4 (N2). If several items remain unchecked, review the relevant chapters and seek additional reading and listening practice in those areas. The transition from N3 to N2 is primarily a matter of vocabulary depth, reading speed, and exposure volume — the grammatical foundation built in this stage will serve you well.
