# Chapter 2 — Classical Residues: Temporal and Immediate Forms

Japanese has a remarkably rich inventory of expressions for describing the instant one event follows another. In English, we might say "the moment that," "as soon as," or "no sooner than" and leave it there. Japanese distinguishes these situations with far greater precision: Was the second event surprising? Was it expected? Does the pattern repeat itself? Was the speaker merely observing, or were they astonished?

This chapter covers five temporal expressions that convey immediate sequence, several of which trace their origins to classical grammar. Some, like ～なり and ～や否や, are literary and formal. Others, like ～たとたん(に), appear in everyday conversation. Understanding the differences between them will allow you to read and comprehend Japanese texts that describe rapid, unexpected, or frustrating sequences of events.

You already know several ways to express "when" and "after" from earlier stages: ～たら, ～と, ～時, ～てから, ～た後. The expressions in this chapter are more specific — they do not merely say "when X happened, Y happened" but add information about how quickly, how surprisingly, or how frustratingly Y followed X.

---

## 2.1 ～なり — "Immediately Upon"

～なり expresses that the instant someone does action A, they immediately do action B, with no pause or hesitation. It has a classical flavor and is used primarily in written narrative and formal speech. The subject of both clauses is typically the same person.

### Formation

| Element | Pattern |
|---|---|
| Verb (dictionary form) | 見る + なり |

～なり attaches to the dictionary form (終止形) of the verb.

### Examples

> 彼は　私の　顔を　見るなり、泣き出した。
> "The moment he saw my face, he burst into tears."

> 子どもは　家に　帰るなり、ランドセルを　投げ捨てて　外に　飛び出した。
> "The moment the child got home, he threw down his school bag and ran outside."

> 彼女は　手紙を　読むなり、顔色が　変わった。
> "The instant she read the letter, her expression changed."

> 部長は　報告を　聞くなり、立ち上がった。
> "The moment the department head heard the report, he stood up."

> 犬は　ドアが　開くなり、走って　出て行った。
> "The instant the door opened, the dog ran out."

### Key constraints

- The subject of both clauses is normally the same (or the second event is a direct, immediate reaction).
- The second action is **observed** and **past** — you do not use ～なり for habitual events or future plans.
- The second action is typically volitional and rapid.

---

## 2.2 ～や否や — "The Instant That"

～や否や (やいなや) literally means "whether or not" — the idea being that before you can even determine whether the first action has happened, the second action already begins. It expresses extreme immediacy and is used in literary and formal writing.

### Formation

| Element | Pattern |
|---|---|
| Verb (dictionary form) | 鳴る + や否や |

Like ～なり, this attaches to the dictionary form.

### Examples

> ベルが　鳴るや否や、学生たちは　教室を　飛び出した。
> "The instant the bell rang, the students rushed out of the classroom."

> 目が　覚めるや否や、彼は　携帯電話を　確認した。
> "The instant he woke up, he checked his phone."

> 試合終了の　笛が　鳴るや否や、観客は　歓声を　上げた。
> "The instant the final whistle blew, the spectators erupted in cheers."

> 扉が　開くや否や、冷たい　風が　吹き込んできた。
> "The instant the door opened, cold wind blew in."

> 社長が　入ってくるや否や、全員が　立ち上がった。
> "The instant the president walked in, everyone stood up."

### ～や否や vs ～なり

Both express extreme immediacy, and in many sentences they are nearly interchangeable. The differences are subtle:

| Feature | ～なり | ～や否や |
|---|---|---|
| Subject | Usually the same for both clauses | Can differ between clauses |
| Tone | Literary narrative | Literary, slightly more dramatic |
| Focus | Immediate action by the same agent | Extreme speed of the sequence |

---

## 2.3 ～そばから — "As Soon As... It Undoes Itself"

～そばから expresses a frustrating pattern: as fast as you do something, the result is undone or negated. You clean up, and it gets messy again. You teach something, and the student forgets immediately. The defining feature of ～そばから is **repetition** and **futility**.

### Formation

| Element | Pattern |
|---|---|
| Verb (dictionary form) | 教える + そばから |
| Verb (た form) | 教えた + そばから |

Both dictionary form and た form are used, with no significant difference in meaning.

### Examples

> 教えたそばから　忘れてしまう。
> "She forgets the moment I teach her."

> 片づけるそばから　子どもが　散らかす。
> "As fast as I clean up, the children make a mess."

> 稼ぐそばから　使ってしまう。
> "As fast as I earn money, I spend it."

> 覚えたそばから　忘れる。こんなことの　繰り返しだ。
> "I forget things the moment I learn them. It is this same cycle over and over."

> 注意したそばから　また　同じ　間違いを　する。
> "He makes the same mistake again the moment I correct him."

### The frustration element

～そばから always implies that the speaker finds the pattern annoying or discouraging. It would be unnatural to use it for something pleasant:

- Natural: 片づけるそばから散らかす (frustrating)
- Unnatural: 会うそばから楽しい (no futility or repetition)

---

## 2.4 ～が早いか — "No Sooner Than"

～が早いか expresses that the second action follows the first with almost impossible speed — so fast that it is as if the two events were simultaneous. It has a literary, dramatic tone and is used in narrative writing.

### Formation

| Element | Pattern |
|---|---|
| Verb (dictionary form) | 開ける + が早いか |

Attaches to the dictionary form.

### Examples

> ドアを　開けるが早いか、彼は　走り出した。
> "No sooner had he opened the door than he broke into a run."

> 合図が　出るが早いか、選手たちは　一斉に　スタートした。
> "No sooner was the signal given than the athletes all started at once."

> 母親の　姿を　見つけるが早いか、子どもは　泣きながら　駆け寄った。
> "No sooner had the child spotted his mother than he ran over crying."

> 電車が　止まるが早いか、乗客は　ドアに　殺到した。
> "No sooner had the train stopped than passengers rushed to the doors."

> 彼女は　目覚まし時計を　止めるが早いか、また　布団に　もぐり込んだ。
> "No sooner had she turned off the alarm clock than she burrowed back under the covers."

### Key features

- The second action is **instantaneous** and often **dramatic**.
- Like ～なり and ～や否や, this is a literary expression rarely used in casual conversation.
- It describes a single observed event, not a habitual pattern (contrast with ～そばから).

---

## 2.5 ～たとたん(に) — "The Moment Something Unexpected Happened"

～たとたん(に) expresses that at the exact moment the first action was completed, something unexpected happened. Unlike the literary expressions above, ～たとたん is common in everyday conversation and writing. Its distinguishing feature is the element of **surprise** — the second event was not anticipated.

### Formation

| Element | Pattern |
|---|---|
| Verb (た form) | 立ち上がった + とたん(に) |

～とたん always follows the た form (past tense) of the verb.

### Examples

> 立ち上がった　とたんに、めまいが　した。
> "The moment I stood up, I felt dizzy."

> 外に　出た　とたん、雨が　降り出した。
> "The moment I went outside, it started to rain."

> 薬を　飲んだ　とたんに、気分が　悪くなった。
> "The moment I took the medicine, I felt sick."

> ドアを　開けた　とたん、猫が　飛び出してきた。
> "The moment I opened the door, a cat jumped out."

> 彼の　名前を　聞いた　とたんに、全てを　思い出した。
> "The moment I heard his name, I remembered everything."

### Key constraints

- The second event must be **unintentional** or **unexpected**. You cannot use とたん if the second action was planned.
- The subject of the second clause is often different from the first, or the second event is something that happened to the speaker.

Compare:
> 家に帰ったとたん、電話が鳴った。 (Natural — the phone ringing was unexpected.)
> 家に帰ったとたん、テレビをつけた。 (Unnatural — turning on the TV was intentional. Use ～なり instead.)

---

## 2.6 How These Overlap and Differ

All five expressions in this chapter describe one event following another with little or no time gap. However, they are not interchangeable. The following table summarizes the key differences.

### Comparison Table

| Expression | Immediacy | Surprise | Repetition/Futility | Formality | Same subject required? |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| ～なり | Very high | Not required | No | Literary | Usually yes |
| ～や否や | Very high | Not required | No | Literary | No |
| ～そばから | Moderate | No | Yes (defining feature) | Neutral | Usually yes |
| ～が早いか | Extreme | Not required | No | Literary | Usually yes |
| ～たとたん(に) | High | Yes (defining feature) | No | Conversational/neutral | Often different |

### Choosing the right expression

**If the second event was surprising or unintended**, use ～たとたん(に):
> 席を　立った　とたんに、地震が　起きた。
> "The moment I stood up from my seat, an earthquake hit."

**If you are describing a dramatic, instantaneous sequence in narrative**, use ～なり, ～や否や, or ～が早いか:
> 合図を　聞くなり、飛び出した。
> "The instant he heard the signal, he dashed out."

**If the pattern repeats frustratingly**, use ～そばから:
> 洗った　そばから　汚れる。
> "They get dirty as fast as I wash them."

### What about ～たら / ～と?

The basic temporal connectors ～たら and ～と can also express "when X happened, Y happened," but they carry no special nuance of immediacy, surprise, or frustration. Compare:

> ドアを開けたら、猫がいた。 (When I opened the door, there was a cat. — Neutral discovery.)
> ドアを開けたとたん、猫が飛び出してきた。 (The moment I opened the door, a cat jumped out. — Surprise.)
> ドアを開けるなり、走り出した。 (The instant he opened the door, he broke into a run. — Immediate action, narrative.)

## 2.7 Reading Passage

次の文章は、ある小学校の先生が書いた日記の一部です。

---

四月十日（月）

新学期が始まった。教室に入るなり、子どもたちの元気な声が飛び込んできた。春休みの間、静かだった学校が、一日で嵐のごとく騒がしくなった。

一年生の担任になったのだが、これが大変だ。名前を教えたそばから忘れてしまう子がいる。「佐藤くん」と呼ぶと「僕は鈴木です」と言われる。その鈴木くんはチャイムが鳴るや否や教室を飛び出していくし、田中さんは席に着くが早いか隣の子とおしゃべりを始める。

今日、初めての授業で黒板に字を書いたとたんに、後ろの方から「先生、見えません」と声がした。振り向くなり、背の高い男の子が前の席に座っているのが目に入った。席順を決めたそばからもう勝手に移動しているのだ。

やれやれ、先が思いやられる。しかし、子どもたちの笑顔を見ていると、今年も頑張ろうという気持ちになる。

---

**Translation:**

April 10th (Monday)

The new semester has started. The moment I entered the classroom, the children's energetic voices came rushing in. During spring break, the school had been quiet, but in one day it became noisy like a storm.

I have become the homeroom teacher for the first graders, and this is quite a challenge. There are children who forget names the instant I teach them. When I call "Sato-kun," I am told "I'm Suzuki." That Suzuki-kun dashes out of the classroom the instant the bell rings, and Tanaka-san starts chatting with the child next to her no sooner than she sits down.

Today, the moment I wrote on the blackboard during the first class, a voice from the back called out, "Teacher, I can't see." The instant I turned around, I noticed a tall boy sitting in a front seat. I had just assigned the seating order, and they were already moving around on their own.

Good grief, I dread what lies ahead. But when I see the children's smiles, I feel motivated to do my best again this year.
