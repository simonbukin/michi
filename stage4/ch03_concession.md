# Chapter 3 — Concession and Counter-Expectation

Every language needs ways to say "although" and "despite." English manages with a handful of words: although, though, despite, even though, nevertheless. Japanese, characteristically, has a much larger inventory, and each expression carries its own shade of meaning. Some are neutral; some carry judgment. Some belong to formal writing; some appear only in conversation. Some express resigned acceptance; others carry reproach or regret.

You have already learned several concessive forms in earlier stages: ～ても/～でも (Stage 1), ～のに (Stage 2), and ～けれども (Stage 1). This chapter introduces the advanced concessive expressions that appear at the N2 level and beyond. These are not merely fancier ways of saying "although" — each one communicates something different about the speaker's attitude toward the gap between expectation and reality.

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to distinguish between eight concessive expressions and choose the one that fits the situation. More importantly, when you encounter these forms in reading, you will understand not just that the speaker is saying "although," but what they feel about it.

---

## 3.1 ～ものの — "Although" (Literary, Objective)

～ものの expresses concession in a relatively neutral, literary register. The speaker acknowledges that situation A exists, but notes that the expected consequence does not follow. It is close in meaning to ～けれども but sounds more formal and is more common in writing than in casual speech.

### Formation

| Preceding element | Pattern |
|---|---|
| Verb (plain form) | 買ったものの |
| い-adjective | 高いものの |
| な-adjective + な/である | 便利なものの / 便利であるものの |
| Noun + である | 学生であるものの |

### Examples

> 新しい　パソコンを　買ったものの、使い方が　よく　分からない。
> "Although I bought a new computer, I don't really understand how to use it."

> 努力した　ものの、結果は　出なかった。
> "Although I made an effort, the results did not come."

> 日本語が　読めるものの、話すのは　まだ　苦手だ。
> "Although I can read Japanese, speaking is still my weak point."

> 資格は　取ったものの、就職先が　見つからない。
> "Although I got the qualification, I cannot find a job."

> 引っ越しは　したものの、新しい　町に　まだ　慣れていない。
> "Although I moved, I still haven't gotten used to the new town."

### Tone

～ものの often implies a sense of being at a loss or slightly disappointed. The first clause describes something accomplished or true, but the expected benefit has not materialized. It does not carry the strong emotional charge of ～のに (which often sounds like a complaint).

## 3.2 ～ものを — "And Yet" (Regret or Reproach)

～ものを expresses that if only a certain condition had been met, the outcome would have been better — but it was not, and the speaker feels regret or reproach. It is close to のに but carries a stronger tone of "what a waste" or "you should have."

### Formation

| Preceding element | Pattern |
|---|---|
| Verb (plain form) | 言えばよかったものを |
| Conditional + ものを | 言ってくれれば良かったものを |

～ものを often appears after ～ば/～たら conditionals, creating the sense of "if only you had done X."

### Examples

> 早く　言ってくれれば　よかったものを。
> "If only you had told me sooner." (Reproach — why didn't you?)

> 黙っていれば　いいものを、余計な　ことを　言うから　怒られるんだ。
> "If you had just kept quiet it would have been fine, but you said something unnecessary, so you got scolded."

> もう少し　頑張れば　受かったものを、途中で　諦めてしまった。
> "If only he had tried a little harder he would have passed, but he gave up partway through."

> 素直に　謝れば　済んだものを。
> "If you had just honestly apologized, it would have been settled." (Implied: but you didn't, and now things are worse.)

> 相談してくれれば　助けられたものを。
> "If only you had consulted me, I could have helped."

### ～ものの vs ～ものを

These two look similar but function very differently:

| Expression | Meaning | Emotion |
|---|---|---|
| ～ものの | "although" — acknowledges a fact with mild disappointment | Neutral to mildly disappointed |
| ～ものを | "if only" / "and yet" — regrets an unrealized condition | Reproach, regret, frustration |

## 3.3 ～にもかかわらず — "Despite, Notwithstanding"

～にもかかわらず is the most formal and explicit of the concessive expressions. It directly states that despite condition A, result B occurred anyway. It is common in formal writing, news, and academic texts.

### Formation

| Preceding element | Pattern |
|---|---|
| Verb (plain form) | 注意したにもかかわらず |
| い-adjective | 高いにもかかわらず |
| な-adjective + (である) | 有名(である)にもかかわらず |
| Noun + (である) | 子ども(である)にもかかわらず |

### Examples

> 何度も　注意した　にもかかわらず、彼は　同じ　ミスを　繰り返した。
> "Despite being warned many times, he repeated the same mistake."

> 悪天候に　もかかわらず、試合は　予定通り　行われた。
> "Despite the bad weather, the match was held as scheduled."

> 多くの　反対が　あった　にもかかわらず、計画は　進められた。
> "Despite much opposition, the plan was carried forward."

> まだ　子どもである　にもかかわらず、大人顔負けの　演奏を　した。
> "Despite still being a child, she gave a performance that put adults to shame."

> 彼女は　疲れている　にもかかわらず、笑顔を　絶やさなかった。
> "Despite being tired, she never let her smile fade."

### Register

～にもかかわらず is too formal for casual conversation. In speech, ～のに or ～けど would be far more natural. Reserve this expression for writing and formal contexts.

---

## 3.4 ～くせに — "Despite" (Negative Judgment)

～くせに was introduced in Stage 2 as a way to express "even though" with a negative or critical judgment about the subject. This section expands on its usage and clarifies how it differs from other concessive forms.

### Formation

| Preceding element | Pattern |
|---|---|
| Verb (plain form) | 知っているくせに |
| い-adjective | 弱いくせに |
| な-adjective + な | 下手なくせに |
| Noun + の | 子どものくせに |

### Examples

> 知らない　くせに　偉そうに　言うな。
> "Don't talk so big when you don't even know."

> 自分だって　できない　くせに、人の　ことを　批判するなよ。
> "You can't even do it yourself, and yet you criticize others."

> お金が　ない　くせに　高い　ものばかり　買う。
> "Despite having no money, he only buys expensive things."

> まだ　新人の　くせに　先輩に　意見するなんて。
> "To talk back to seniors when you're still a newcomer..."

> 約束した　くせに　来なかった。
> "Despite having promised, he didn't come."

### The judgment element

～くせに always carries negative evaluation. The speaker is criticizing the subject for acting in a way that contradicts what should be expected. It is never used about oneself in polite contexts (it would sound self-deprecating in a harsh way), and using it about someone directly is rude. It is most natural when talking about a third party or when complaining.

---

## 3.5 ～といえども — "Even Though" (Formal/Literary)

～といえども is a formal, literary expression meaning "even though" or "even if." It appears in written discourse, speeches, and formal arguments. It derives from the classical concessive conditional and has a tone of solemnity.

### Formation

| Preceding element | Pattern |
|---|---|
| Verb (plain form) | 認めたといえども |
| Noun | 専門家といえども |
| いかに / たとえ + clause | たとえ困難であるといえども |

### Examples

> 専門家と　いえども、すべてを　知っている　わけでは　ない。
> "Even an expert does not know everything."

> たとえ　大統領と　いえども、法の　上に　立つことは　できない。
> "Even a president cannot stand above the law."

> いかに　才能が　あると　いえども、努力なしには　成功できない。
> "However talented one may be, one cannot succeed without effort."

> 親しい　間柄と　いえども、礼儀は　必要だ。
> "Even between close friends, manners are necessary."

> 子どもと　いえども、責任を　学ぶべきだ。
> "Even children should learn about responsibility."

---

## 3.6 ～とはいえ — "Having Said That, Although"

～とはいえ means "having said that" or "although it is true that." It acknowledges a fact and then introduces a qualification or counterpoint. It is less formal than ～といえども and appears in both writing and semi-formal speech.

### Formation

| Preceding element | Pattern |
|---|---|
| Verb (plain form) | 合格したとはいえ |
| い-adjective | 安いとはいえ |
| な-adjective + だ | 便利だとはいえ |
| Noun + (だ) | 夏(だ)とはいえ |

### Examples

> 合格した　とはいえ、ぎりぎりの　成績だった。
> "Although I passed, it was by the skin of my teeth."

> 春と　はいえ、まだ　寒い　日が　続いている。
> "Although it is spring, cold days continue."

> 安い　とはいえ、毎日　外食していたら　お金が　なくなる。
> "Although it is cheap, if you eat out every day you will run out of money."

> 大人と　はいえ、泣きたい　時も　ある。
> "Even though we are adults, there are times when we want to cry."

> 経験が　ある　とはいえ、油断は　できない。
> "Although I have experience, I cannot let my guard down."

### ～とはいえ vs ～といえども

| Expression | Formality | Tone |
|---|---|---|
| ～とはいえ | Semi-formal to formal | Concessive qualification ("but still") |
| ～といえども | Formal / literary | Emphatic concession ("even so") |

---

## 3.7 ～たところで — "Even If One Does" (Futility)

～たところで expresses that even if action A were performed, it would be pointless or would not change the outcome. It carries a strong nuance of **futility** — the speaker believes the action is useless.

### Formation

| Preceding element | Pattern |
|---|---|
| Verb (た form) | 謝ったところで |
| 疑問詞 + たところで | 何を言ったところで |

### Examples

> 今さら　謝った　ところで　遅い。
> "Even if you apologize now, it's too late."

> いくら　説明した　ところで、彼には　分からないだろう。
> "No matter how much I explain, he probably won't understand."

> 急いだ　ところで、もう　間に合わない。
> "Even if I hurry, I won't make it in time."

> 何を　言った　ところで、彼女の　決心は　変わらない。
> "No matter what I say, her decision won't change."

> 一人で　頑張った　ところで、限界が　ある。
> "Even if I try hard alone, there are limits."

### The futility nuance

～たところで is not a neutral "even if." It always implies that the speaker considers the action to be pointless or insufficient. Compare:

> 薬を飲んでも、治らないかもしれない。 (Even if I take medicine, I might not recover. — Uncertain but not dismissive.)
> 薬を飲んだところで、治らないだろう。 (Even if I take medicine, I probably won't recover. — Dismissive, futile.)

---

## 3.8 ～ながら(も) — "While, Although" (Concessive Use)

You learned ～ながら in Stage 2 as a way to express simultaneous actions (テレビを見ながら食べる, "eating while watching TV"). However, ～ながら(も) has a separate concessive use meaning "although" or "while being." This concessive ながら is distinct from the simultaneous ながら and is important to recognize.

### Formation

| Preceding element | Pattern |
|---|---|
| Verb (ます stem) | 知りながら(も) |
| い-adjective (full form, keeping い) | 狭いながら(も) |
| な-adjective stem | 不便ながら(も) |
| Noun | 少数ながら(も) |

Note: With い-adjectives, the full adjective form precedes ながら. With な-adjectives, the stem alone (without な) precedes ながら.

### Examples

> 残念ながら、ご期待に　沿えません。
> "Unfortunately (while it is regrettable), we cannot meet your expectations."

> 知っていながら、教えてくれなかった。
> "Although he knew, he didn't tell me."

> 狭いながらも、居心地の　いい　部屋だ。
> "Although it is small, it is a comfortable room."

> 彼は　学生ながら、すでに　会社を　経営している。
> "Although he is a student, he is already running a company."

> 微力ながら、お手伝いさせていただきます。
> "Though my ability is small, allow me to help." (Humble expression)

> 少数ながらも、賛成する　人は　いた。
> "Although few in number, there were people who agreed."

### Simultaneous ながら vs concessive ながら(も)

| Use | Example | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| Simultaneous | 音楽を聴きながら勉強する | "study while listening to music" |
| Concessive | 知っていながら黙っていた | "although I knew, I stayed silent" |

The concessive use often includes も (ながらも), which helps distinguish it from the simultaneous use. Context also makes the difference clear: if both actions happen at the same time, it is simultaneous; if there is a contradiction between the two clauses, it is concessive.

## 3.9 Formality Spectrum

The following table arranges this chapter's concessive expressions from most casual to most formal:

| Expression | Formality | Key nuance |
|---|---|---|
| ～くせに | Casual, critical | Negative judgment |
| ～ものの | Neutral-formal, written | Mild disappointment |
| ～ものを | Neutral-formal | Regret, reproach |
| ～ながら(も) | Neutral-formal | Contradiction, set phrases |
| ～たところで | Neutral-formal | Futility |
| ～とはいえ | Semi-formal | Qualification ("but still") |
| ～にもかかわらず | Formal, written | Objective concession |
| ～といえども | Formal, literary | Emphatic, solemn concession |

## 3.10 Reading Passage

次の文章は、あるフリーランスのデザイナーが自身のブログに書いた記事です。

---

フリーランスになって三年が経った。会社を辞めた時は不安だったものの、今ではこの働き方が自分に合っていると感じている。

とはいえ、すべてが順調というわけではない。収入は不安定だし、体調が悪くても休めない。会社員なら有給休暇を取ればいいものを、フリーランスにはそれがない。休んだところで、仕事は待ってくれない。

先月、大きなプロジェクトを引き受けた。経験があるとはいえ、今回のクライアントは要求が厳しく、修正を何度も求められた。直したそばから新しい修正が来る。正直、いらいらすることもあった。

それにもかかわらず、最終的に良い作品ができた時の達成感は何物にも代えがたい。会社員時代は安定した収入がありながらも、この充実感はなかった。少数ながらも、自分の仕事を本当に気に入ってくれるクライアントがいる。

専門家といえども完璧ではない。失敗もする。しかし、残念ながら後悔はしていない——いや、「残念ながら」ではなく、「幸いなことに」と言うべきだろう。

---

**Translation:**

Three years have passed since I became a freelancer. When I quit my company I was anxious, but now I feel this way of working suits me.

Having said that, everything is not going smoothly. My income is unstable, and even when I feel unwell I cannot take time off. If I were a company employee I could just take paid leave, but as a freelancer that does not exist. Even if I rest, the work does not wait.

Last month, I took on a large project. Although I have experience, this client's demands were strict, and I was asked for revisions many times. New corrections came as fast as I made the previous ones. Honestly, there were times I felt frustrated.

Despite all that, the sense of achievement when a good piece of work is completed in the end is irreplaceable. During my company employee days, although I had a stable income, I did not have this sense of fulfillment. Though few in number, there are clients who genuinely like my work.

Even an expert is not perfect. I make mistakes too. However, I regrettably have no regrets — no, I should say "fortunately" rather than "regrettably."
