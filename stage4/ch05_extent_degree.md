# Chapter 5 — Extent, Degree, and Limit

This chapter covers expressions that describe extent, degree, and the limits of what is possible or true. Some of these are sentence-level grammar patterns that hedge or qualify claims; others are suffixes that attach to nouns and adjectives to describe qualities, tendencies, and conditions.

The first half of the chapter deals with three expressions built around ない that create nuanced logical statements: "nothing is better than" (に越したことはない), "unless" (ないことには), and a double-negative hedge meaning "it is not impossible" (ないものでもない). These are tools for reasoning — they let you express qualified opinions and cautious claims, which are central to how Japanese adults communicate in both speech and writing.

The second half introduces four suffixes — っぽい, げ, だらけ, and まみれ — that describe how something looks, feels, or what it is covered in. These are some of the most useful descriptive tools in Japanese, appearing everywhere from casual conversation to literary prose. Understanding the differences between them, especially between だらけ and まみれ and between っぽい and げ, is essential for accurate reading comprehension.

---

## 5.1 ～に越したことはない — "Nothing Better Than, The More the Better"

～に越したことはない literally means "there is nothing that exceeds (this)" — in other words, this is the best option, or the more of it the better. It expresses a practical recommendation without strong insistence.

### Formation

| Preceding element | Pattern |
|---|---|
| Verb (dictionary form) | 早いに越したことはない |
| Verb (ない form) | 行かないに越したことはない |
| い-adjective | 安いに越したことはない |
| な-adjective + (である / な) | 静かであるに越したことはない |

The pattern can also appear as ～にこしたことはない (without kanji for 越す).

### Examples

> 準備は　早いに　越したことはない。
> "When it comes to preparation, the earlier the better."

> 健康の　ためには、運動する　に越した　ことはない。
> "For your health, nothing is better than exercising."

> お金は　あるに　越したことはない。
> "When it comes to money, you can never have too much."

> トラブルは　ないに　越したことはない。
> "When it comes to trouble, it is best if there is none."

> 薬は　飲まないに　越した　ことはない　が、必要な　時は　飲むべきだ。
> "It is best not to take medicine if you can avoid it, but you should take it when necessary."

### Nuance

This expression does not command or insist. It offers practical wisdom: "ideally, X is the best approach." It often implies "but I understand if the ideal is not always possible."

## 5.2 ～ないことには — "Unless"

～ないことには expresses that unless a certain condition is met, something else cannot happen or cannot be known. It is a conditional expression that emphasizes the indispensability of the condition.

### Formation

| Preceding element | Pattern |
|---|---|
| Verb (ない form) | 試してみないことには |
| い-adjective (～くない) | よくないことには |

The second clause typically expresses impossibility (分からない, できない, 始まらない).

### Examples

> 実際に　やってみない　ことには、分からない。
> "Unless you actually try it, you won't know."

> 本人に　聞かない　ことには、真相は　分からない。
> "Unless we ask the person themselves, we won't know the truth."

> データを　見ない　ことには、判断できない。
> "Unless I see the data, I cannot make a judgment."

> 現地に　行かない　ことには、状況は　つかめない。
> "Unless you go to the site, you cannot grasp the situation."

> まず　始めない　ことには、何も　進まない。
> "Unless you start first, nothing will progress."

### ～ないことには vs ～なければ

Both express "unless," but ～ないことには is more emphatic about the indispensability of the condition:

> 練習しなければ、上手にならない。 (If you don't practice, you won't improve. — Standard conditional.)
> 練習しないことには、上手にならない。 (Unless you practice, you won't improve. — Emphasizes that practice is absolutely essential.)

## 5.3 ～ないものでもない — "It's Not That One Can't" (Double Negative Hedging)

～ないものでもない is a cautious, hedging expression that uses a double negative to say "it is not the case that it is impossible" — meaning "it might be possible" or "one could, if pressed." The speaker avoids committing to a clear yes or no.

### Formation

| Preceding element | Pattern |
|---|---|
| Verb (ない form) | できないものでもない |
| Verb (ない form) | 行けないものでもない |

### Examples

> 頼まれれば、手伝えない　ものでもない。
> "If asked, it is not that I couldn't help." (= I could probably help.)

> この　値段なら、買えない　ものでもない。
> "At this price, it is not that I couldn't buy it." (= I could afford it.)

> 時間を　もらえれば、できない　ものでもない。
> "If I were given time, it is not that I couldn't do it."

> まったく　理解できない　ものでもない。
> "It is not that I completely fail to understand." (= I can understand to some degree.)

> 条件次第では、引き受けない　ものでもない。
> "Depending on the conditions, it is not that I wouldn't take it on."

### The hedging function

This is a quintessentially Japanese way of leaving room. Rather than saying はい、できます ("Yes, I can"), the speaker says できないものでもない ("Well, it is not as though I could not"). This saves face for both parties — the speaker has not firmly committed, and the listener has not been flatly refused. It is especially common in negotiations and when responding to requests.

## 5.4 ～っぽい — "Tendency, -ish" (Colloquial)

～っぽい is a versatile colloquial suffix that expresses "having the quality of," "tending toward," or "-ish." It attaches to nouns, verb stems, and adjective stems and creates い-adjectives.

### Formation

| Base | っぽい form | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| 忘れる (verb stem: 忘れ) | 忘れっぽい | forgetful, tends to forget |
| 怒る (verb stem: 怒り) | 怒りっぽい | quick-tempered, easily angered |
| 飽きる (verb stem: 飽き) | 飽きっぽい | easily bored, fickle |
| 子ども (noun) | 子どもっぽい | childish |
| 大人 (noun) | 大人っぽい | mature, adult-like |
| 男 (noun) | 男っぽい | mannish, masculine |
| 黒 (noun/color) | 黒っぽい | blackish, dark |
| 白 (noun/color) | 白っぽい | whitish, pale |
| 安 (adjective stem) | 安っぽい | cheap-looking |

### Examples

> 彼女は　忘れっぽい　から、メモを　取る　ようにしている。
> "She tends to be forgetful, so she makes a point of taking notes."

> あの人は　すぐ　怒りっぽくなる。
> "That person quickly becomes irritable."

> この　服は　安っぽく　見える。
> "These clothes look cheap."

> 彼女は　大人っぽい　雰囲気が　ある。
> "She has a mature aura about her."

> 空が　白っぽくなってきた。
> "The sky has become whitish."

> 子どもっぽい　ことを　言うなよ。
> "Don't say childish things."

### Nuance

When attached to nouns referring to people or qualities, っぽい often carries a **slightly negative** connotation — the quality is superficial, excessive, or unwanted. 子どもっぽい means "childish" (negative), not "childlike" (neutral/positive). 安っぽい means "cheap-looking" (negative), not simply "inexpensive." However, 大人っぽい can be positive (mature-looking).

When attached to colors, っぽい is neutral — it simply means "-ish" (黒っぽい = blackish).

## 5.5 ～げ — "Seeming, Appearing To" (Literary)

～げ is a suffix that creates な-adjectives meaning "seeming" or "appearing to have a quality." It is more literary and refined than っぽい and focuses on outward appearance — how something or someone looks to an observer.

### Formation

| Base | げ form | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| 悲しい (drop い) | 悲しげ | sad-looking, seeming sad |
| 楽しい (drop い) | 楽しげ | seeming happy, cheerful-looking |
| 寂しい (drop い) | 寂しげ | lonely-looking |
| 不安 (な-adj stem) | 不安げ | seeming anxious |
| 意味あり (noun + あり) | 意味ありげ | seeming meaningful, suggestive |
| 自信 (noun) | 自信げ | seeming confident |

げ forms function as な-adjectives: 悲しげ**な**顔, 楽しげ**に**話す.

### Examples

> 彼女は　悲しげな　顔を　していた。
> "She had a sad-looking expression."

> 子どもたちが　楽しげに　遊んでいる。
> "The children are playing happily (looking happy as they play)."

> 彼は　寂しげに　窓の　外を　見ていた。
> "He was looking out the window with a lonely expression."

> 彼女は　意味ありげに　微笑んだ。
> "She smiled suggestively (as if there were something behind it)."

> 不安げな　表情で　結果を　待っていた。
> "They were waiting for the results with anxious-looking expressions."

### っぽい vs げ

| Suffix | Type of word it creates | Focus | Register | Connotation |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| っぽい | い-adjective | Inherent tendency or quality | Colloquial | Often negative |
| げ | な-adjective | Outward appearance to observer | Literary/written | Neutral, descriptive |

Compare:
> 子どもっぽい — "childish" (the person actually has a childish quality)
> 子どもっぽい is about what someone **is**; there is no common form 子どもげ.

> 悲しげな顔 — "a face that looks sad" (what the observer sees)
> 悲しっぽい does not exist; っぽい does not attach to all adjective stems.

The two suffixes apply to different sets of words and create different kinds of descriptions. げ describes appearance; っぽい describes tendency.

## 5.6 ～だらけ — "Covered in, Full of" (Negative)

～だらけ is a suffix meaning "full of" or "covered in," and it almost always carries a negative connotation. The thing described is undesirably full of something.

### Formation

| Noun | だらけ form | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| 間違い | 間違いだらけ | full of mistakes |
| ほこり | ほこりだらけ | covered in dust |
| 傷 | 傷だらけ | covered in scratches/wounds |
| ゴミ | ゴミだらけ | littered with garbage |
| 嘘 | 嘘だらけ | full of lies |
| 借金 | 借金だらけ | deep in debt |
| 血 | 血だらけ | covered in blood |

だらけ attaches directly to nouns and functions as a の-adjective (間違いだらけ**の**作文) or predicate (部屋がほこりだらけだ).

### Examples

> この　作文は　間違いだらけだ。
> "This essay is full of mistakes."

> 部屋が　ほこりだらけで、くしゃみが　止まらない。
> "The room is covered in dust, and I cannot stop sneezing."

> 傷だらけの　猫が　庭に　いた。
> "A cat covered in scratches was in the garden."

> 嘘だらけの　言い訳を　するな。
> "Don't give me an excuse full of lies."

> 子どもたちが　泥だらけに　なって　帰ってきた。
> "The children came home covered in mud."

### Key feature

だらけ emphasizes that there is **too much** of something undesirable. It is not simply "there is some X" but "it is excessively full of X." You would not use だらけ for something positive:

- Natural: 間違いだらけ (full of mistakes — negative)
- Unnatural: 正解だらけ (full of correct answers — positive, not typical with だらけ)

## 5.7 ～まみれ — "Smeared With, Covered In" (Physical, Negative)

～まみれ is similar to だらけ but more specific: it describes being physically smeared or coated with a substance. The substance is something that clings to the surface — dirt, blood, sweat, oil. It is always negative and always physical.

### Formation

| Noun | まみれ form | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| 泥 | 泥まみれ | covered in mud |
| 汗 | 汗まみれ | drenched in sweat |
| 血 | 血まみれ | covered in blood |
| 油 | 油まみれ | covered in oil/grease |
| ほこり | ほこりまみれ | covered in dust |

Like だらけ, まみれ functions as a の-adjective or predicate.

### Examples

> 泥まみれに　なって　サッカーを　した。
> "I played soccer and got covered in mud."

> 汗まみれの　シャツを　洗濯した。
> "I washed the shirt that was drenched in sweat."

> 血まみれの　手で　助けを　求めた。
> "He called for help with blood-covered hands."

> 工場で　働いた　後は　いつも　油まみれだ。
> "After working in the factory, I am always covered in grease."

> 古い　倉庫は　ほこりまみれだった。
> "The old warehouse was covered in dust."

### だらけ vs まみれ — Contrastive Notes

| Feature | だらけ | まみれ |
|---|---|---|
| Type of thing | Abstract or physical (mistakes, lies, dust, scratches) | Physical substance only (mud, blood, sweat) |
| How it covers | Scattered throughout, full of | Smeared on, coating the surface |
| Connotation | Negative (too much) | Negative (dirty, messy) |
| Can be abstract? | Yes (嘘だらけ, 借金だらけ) | No (嘘まみれ is not standard) |

Some nouns can take both, but with different nuances:

> 血だらけ — "covered in blood" (blood is everywhere, possibly in pools or splatters)
> 血まみれ — "smeared with blood" (blood is coating the surface, clinging to skin or clothes)

> ほこりだらけ — "full of dust" (dust is everywhere in the room)
> ほこりまみれ — "coated in dust" (the surface is physically layered with dust)

The general rule: if the substance physically sticks to a surface, まみれ works. If the undesirable thing is scattered, spread, or abstractly present, だらけ is the right choice.

## 5.8 Reading Passage

次の文章は、ある引っ越し業者の若い社員が書いた体験記の一部です。

---

引っ越し業者で働き始めて半年が経った。体力には自信があったが、この仕事は想像以上にきつい。

初日のことは今でもよく覚えている。先輩に「まず荷物を見ないことには、見積もりはできないんだ」と言われて、お客様の家に向かった。玄関を開けたとたん、物であふれた部屋が目に入った。ほこりだらけの段ボール箱が山のように積まれていて、床は古い新聞紙だらけだった。

先輩は汗まみれになりながらも、てきぱきと作業を進めた。さすがベテランだけあって、大きな家具も一人で運べる。私はと言えば、箱を持ち上げたとたんに腰が痛くなり、情けないことに泥まみれの庭で転んでしまった。

「若いからといって無理をするな。腰は大事にするに越したことはない」と先輩に言われた。忘れっぽい私だが、この言葉だけは覚えている。

半年経った今、仕事にはだいぶ慣れた。できないものでもないと思えるようになった。まだ傷だらけの手は痛むが、不安げだった最初の頃を思い出すと、少しは成長したのだと感じる。

---

**Translation:**

Six months have passed since I started working at a moving company. I had confidence in my physical strength, but this job is tougher than I imagined.

I still remember the first day well. My senior told me, "Unless you see the luggage first, you can't make an estimate," and we headed to the customer's house. The moment I opened the front door, a room overflowing with things came into view. Dust-covered cardboard boxes were piled up like mountains, and the floor was littered with old newspapers.

My senior, while getting drenched in sweat, worked briskly and efficiently. As one would expect of a veteran, he could carry large furniture by himself. As for me, the moment I lifted a box my back started hurting, and pathetically, I fell over in the mud-covered garden.

"Just because you are young, don't overdo it. When it comes to your back, it is best to take care of it," my senior told me. I am forgetful, but this piece of advice I remember.

Now, six months later, I have gotten quite used to the job. I have come to feel that it is not something I cannot do. My hands are still covered in scratches and they hurt, but when I think back to the early days when I looked so anxious, I feel that I have grown at least a little.
