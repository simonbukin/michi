# Chapter 7 — Appearance, Manner, and Approximation

In Stage 2, you learned ようだ, みたいだ, and らしい as ways to express similarity, appearance, and hearsay. Those patterns cover most everyday comparison needs. The expressions in this chapter go further: they describe dramatic, literary, or nuanced ways of conveying how something appears, how someone behaves, or what quality a situation takes on. These are the patterns that make Japanese description vivid and precise.

Several of these expressions appear in literary writing, formal narration, and expressive speech. They allow the speaker or writer to paint a scene — describing someone who looks as though they are about to cry, someone who acts as if they know everything, or a day that feels as though spring has arrived. Understanding them is essential for reading novels, essays, and any Japanese text that goes beyond plain reporting.

All patterns in this chapter attach to plain forms unless otherwise noted.

---

## 7.1 ～んばかり(に) — "As If About To"

んばかり(に) describes an extreme state where something appears to be on the verge of happening, though it does not actually happen. The speaker is saying: "It was so intense that it seemed as if X were about to occur." It conveys dramatic emphasis and is common in literary and formal descriptive writing.

The ん in this pattern is the classical negative auxiliary ぬ in its prenominal form. Historically, ばかり meant "only" or "to the extent of." Together, んばかり conveys "to the extent that it almost seemed like it would."

### Formation

| Preceding element | Pattern |
|---|---|
| Verb (ない stem / 未然形) | 泣か + んばかり(に) |
| Verb (ない stem) | 溢れ + んばかり(に) |

Note: For 五段 verbs, use the あ-stem (the stem before ない): 泣く → 泣か, 叫ぶ → 叫ば. For 一段 verbs, drop る: 溢れる → 溢れ. The irregular verb する becomes せんばかり.

### Examples

> 彼女は　泣かんばかりの　顔で　頼んできた。
> "She pleaded with a face that looked as though she were about to cry."

> 会場は　溢れんばかりの　人で　埋め尽くされた。
> "The venue was packed with people to the point of overflowing."

> 観客は　割れんばかりの　拍手を　送った。
> "The audience gave applause so thunderous it seemed as though the place might split apart."

> 彼は　飛び上がらんばかりに　喜んだ。
> "He was so happy he looked as though he might jump for joy."

> 子どもたちは　声を　張り上げんばかりに　歌った。
> "The children sang as though they were about to strain their voices."

### Grammar note

んばかり can function as an adnominal modifier (before a noun, with の) or as an adverb (with に):

- 泣かんばかり**の**顔 — "a face as if about to cry" (modifying a noun)
- 飛び上がらんばかり**に**喜んだ — "rejoiced as if about to jump" (modifying a verb)

## 7.2 ～とばかりに — "As If to Say"

とばかりに describes an action performed with a manner that communicates an unspoken message. The person does not literally say the words, but their behavior conveys the sentiment so clearly that it is "as if they said" it. The と is the quotative particle, and ばかり adds the sense of "nothing but" or "exactly."

### Formation

| Pattern |
|---|
| Phrase (what the person seems to be saying) + とばかり(に) |

### Examples

> 「待っていました」とばかりに、彼は　話し始めた。
> "As if to say 'I've been waiting for this,' he began to speak."

> 「もう関係ない」とばかりに、彼女は　背を　向けた。
> "As if to say 'It's no longer my concern,' she turned her back."

> 「さあ来い」とばかりに、腕を　組んで　立っていた。
> "He stood with his arms crossed, as if to say 'Come on, bring it.'"

> 「知らない」とばかりに、首を　横に　振った。
> "She shook her head as if to say 'I don't know.'"

> ここぞとばかりに、彼は　自分の　手柄を　アピールした。
> "As if to say 'Now's my chance,' he promoted his own achievements."

### Set phrase: ここぞとばかりに

ここぞとばかりに is a common fixed expression meaning "seizing the moment" or "as if to say 'this is it.'" The ぞ is an emphatic classical particle.

## 7.3 ～かのように — "As Though"

かのように expresses a comparison to something that is not actually true. It is more literary and formal than みたいに or ように alone. The か adds a questioning nuance — "as if it were (but it is not)" — making the unreality of the comparison explicit.

### Formation

| Preceding element | Pattern |
|---|---|
| Verb (plain form) | 知っている + かのように |
| い-adjective | ないかのように |
| な-adjective + である | 自由であるかのように |
| Noun + である | 王様であるかのように |

Note: In casual speech, だ is sometimes used instead of である before かのように, but である is standard.

### Examples

> 彼は　何も　知らない　かのように　振る舞った。
> "He behaved as though he knew nothing."

> まるで　時間が　止まった　かのような　静けさだった。
> "It was a stillness as though time had stopped."

> 彼女は　自分が　女王である　かのように　命令した。
> "She gave orders as though she were a queen."

> 子どもたちは　世界に　何の　問題も　ない　かのように　遊んでいた。
> "The children played as though there were no problems in the world."

> 初めて　会った　のに、まるで　昔からの　友だちである　かのように　話した。
> "Even though they had just met, they talked as though they had been friends for years."

### かのように vs ように vs みたいに

| Expression | Formality | Nuance |
|---|---|---|
| みたいに | Casual | Simple resemblance |
| ように | Neutral | Resemblance; broad use |
| かのように | Formal / literary | Explicit unreality — "as if (but it is not)" |

All three can translate as "as if" or "as though," but かのように is chosen when the writer wants to emphasize that the comparison is counterfactual, and when a more elevated register is desired.

## 7.4 ～ぶる / ～ぶり — "Pretend to Be, Act Like"

ぶる is a suffix that attaches to nouns and adjective stems to create a verb meaning "to act as if one is X" or "to put on airs of X." It almost always carries a negative connotation — the person is pretending or being pretentious.

ぶり is the noun form derived from ぶる, and it appears in several important fixed expressions.

### Formation

| Base | + ぶる | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| 偉い (great) | 偉ぶる | to act important, be arrogant |
| 大人 (adult) | 大人ぶる | to act grown-up |
| 利口 (clever) | 利口ぶる | to act smart |
| 知った (knew) | 知ったかぶる | to pretend to know |
| 強い (strong) | 強がる* | to act tough |

*Note: 強がる uses がる rather than ぶる but conveys a similar meaning. The ぶる pattern is productive but not unlimited — some words conventionally take がる instead.

### Examples

> あの人は　いつも　偉ぶっている。
> "That person always acts self-important."

> 知らないのに　知ったかぶりを　するのは　やめなさい。
> "Stop pretending you know things when you don't."

> 彼女は　大人ぶっているけど、まだ　高校生だ。
> "She acts grown-up, but she's still a high school student."

> 学者ぶった　話し方が　鼻につく。
> "His scholarly way of speaking gets on my nerves."

### Etymology note: 久しぶり and ぶり

The ぶり in 久しぶり ("it's been a while") comes from the same root. ぶり originally meant "manner, way, style" — the way something presents itself. 久しぶり literally means "in a manner of long time" — that is, "after a long-time fashion." This same ぶり appears in:

- 三年ぶり — "for the first time in three years"
- 食べっぷり — "the way someone eats" (ぷり is a voiced variant)
- 仕事ぶり — "the way someone works, work performance"
- 飲みっぷり — "the way someone drinks"

> 十年ぶりに　故郷に　帰った。
> "I returned to my hometown for the first time in ten years."

> 彼の　仕事ぶりは　素晴らしい。
> "His work performance is outstanding."

### 知ったかぶり — a cultural note

知ったかぶり (pretending to know) is a frequently used word in Japanese that describes a universally recognized social behavior. It functions as both a noun (知ったかぶりをする — "to pretend to know") and as a modifier. The word is useful to know not only for its grammar but because the concept appears often in conversation:

> 知ったかぶりは　恥ずかしい。
> "Pretending to know is embarrassing."

---

## 7.5 ～めく — "To Seem Like, Take On the Quality of"

めく is a suffix that attaches to nouns to create a verb meaning "to take on the quality of X" or "to seem like X." Unlike ぶる, which implies pretense, めく is neutral or positive — it describes a genuine quality emerging or being sensed. It is literary and somewhat poetic.

### Formation

| Base | + めく | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| 春 (spring) | 春めく | to become spring-like |
| 皮肉 (irony) | 皮肉めく | to be ironic, sardonic |
| 謎 (mystery) | 謎めく | to be mysterious |
| 時 (time/occasion) | 時めく | to be in one's prime (archaic) |

めく creates 五段 verbs that conjugate normally: 春めく → 春めいた (past/attributive), 春めいて (te-form).

### Examples

> 三月に　入って、だいぶ　春めいてきた。
> "Now that we're in March, it's become quite spring-like."

> 皮肉めいた　笑みを　浮かべた。
> "He wore a sardonic smile."

> 彼女の　言葉には　謎めいた　ところが　あった。
> "There was something mysterious about her words."

> 冗談めいた　口調で　話したが、目は　笑っていなかった。
> "He spoke in a joking tone, but his eyes were not smiling."

> あの古い　屋敷は　どこか　謎めいている。
> "That old mansion has something mysterious about it."

### めく vs ぶる

| Suffix | Nuance | Example |
|---|---|---|
| ぶる | Pretending, putting on airs (negative) | 偉ぶる — "to act important" |
| めく | Taking on a genuine quality (neutral/positive) | 春めく — "to become spring-like" |

You cannot freely interchange them. 春ぶる would sound nonsensical, and 偉めく does not exist. Each suffix has its own set of words it naturally combines with.

### Relationship to ようだ / みたいだ

The patterns in this chapter are all more specific and expressive than the general comparison patterns ようだ and みたいだ from Stage 2. Here is how they relate:

| Expression | Register | What it conveys |
|---|---|---|
| ようだ / みたいだ | Neutral | General resemblance or appearance |
| んばかりに | Literary / formal | Extreme degree — "almost to the point of" |
| とばかりに | Literary / formal | Unspoken message conveyed through action |
| かのように | Literary / formal | Counterfactual comparison — "as if (but not)" |
| ぶる | Colloquial / neutral | Pretense, putting on airs |
| めく | Literary / poetic | Genuine quality emerging |

In practice, you will encounter ようだ and みたいだ far more often than any pattern in this chapter. But when Japanese writers and speakers want to be precise about the nature of a resemblance — whether it is dramatic, pretended, counterfactual, or genuinely emerging — they reach for these more specific tools.

## 7.6 Reading Passage

次の文章は、小説の冒頭部分です。主人公が久しぶりに故郷の町を訪れる場面です。

---

三月に入り、風はまだ冷たいものの、どこか春めいた空気が漂っていた。駅を出ると、見覚えのある商店街が広がっていた。十五年ぶりだった。

町は変わっていなかった。まるで時間が止まったかのように、同じ看板、同じ並木道、同じパン屋がそこにあった。私は何も感じていないかのように歩いた。しかし、胸の奥では懐かしさが溢れんばかりに膨らんでいた。

角を曲がると、小学校が見えた。校庭では子どもたちが声を張り上げんばかりに笑いながら走り回っていた。あの頃の自分もこうだったのだろうか。大人ぶっていた中学時代、知ったかぶりをしていた高校時代。皮肉めいた笑みが自然と浮かんだ。

「待っていましたよ」とばかりに、古い喫茶店の扉が開いた。中から、白髪の女性が顔を出した。

「あら、もしかして……太郎くん？」

私は首を縦に振った。十五年前、ここぞとばかりにこの町を飛び出した自分が、今こうして戻ってきている。謎めいた運命だと思った。

---

**Translation:**

The following is the opening passage of a novel. The protagonist visits their hometown for the first time in a long time.

---

March had begun, and though the wind was still cold, there was something spring-like in the air. Stepping out of the station, a familiar shopping street spread before me. It had been fifteen years.

The town had not changed. As though time had stopped, the same signs, the same tree-lined streets, the same bakery were all there. I walked as if I felt nothing. But deep in my chest, nostalgia was swelling to the point of overflowing.

Turning a corner, I could see the elementary school. In the schoolyard, children were running around, laughing so loudly it seemed their voices might burst. Had I been like that once too? The middle school years when I tried to act grown-up. The high school years when I pretended to know everything. A sardonic smile naturally formed on my face.

As if to say "I've been waiting for you," the door of the old coffee shop opened. From inside, a white-haired woman peeked out.

"Oh, could it be... Taro?"

I nodded. The person who had seized the moment to flee this town fifteen years ago was now back. I thought it was a mysterious twist of fate.
