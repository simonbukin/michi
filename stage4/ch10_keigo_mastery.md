# Chapter 10 — The Five-Part Keigo System: Full Mastery

In Stage 2, you learned the basics of keigo: the distinction between polite speech (丁寧語), respectful language (尊敬語), and humble language (謙譲語), along with a set of irregular verbs and productive patterns. That foundation was enough to follow most keigo you encountered in daily life. Now it is time to see the full picture.

The Japanese government's Council for Cultural Affairs (文化審議会) officially recognizes not three but five categories of keigo. The distinction between 謙譲語I and 謙譲語II (also called 丁重語) is one that even many native speakers cannot articulate clearly, yet it governs real patterns of usage. The fifth category, 美化語, covers the お and ご prefixes used to make speech sound refined rather than to show respect to any particular person.

This chapter systematizes everything. By the end, you will have a comprehensive reference for all special keigo verbs, a clear understanding of the productive patterns that let you generate keigo forms for any verb, and the ability to distinguish the five categories with precision. This is the chapter you will return to again and again.

---

## 10.1 The Five Categories of Keigo

Japanese keigo is not a binary system of "polite" and "rude." It is a five-part system, each category serving a different communicative function. Understanding the system as a whole is essential for producing correct keigo and, more importantly for the learner, for correctly interpreting keigo when reading or listening.

### The Five Categories

| Category | Japanese Name | Function | Direction |
|---|---|---|---|
| 尊敬語 | そんけいご | Raises the status of the subject (the person doing the action) | Upward — toward the person you are talking about |
| 謙譲語I | けんじょうごI | Lowers the speaker's action relative to the person affected by that action | Downward — toward the speaker, with a specific recipient |
| 謙譲語II (丁重語) | けんじょうごII (ていちょうご) | Makes the speaker's own actions sound formal and dignified, without reference to any specific recipient | Downward — toward the speaker, no specific recipient |
| 丁寧語 | ていねいご | Makes speech polite through sentence-ending forms | Toward the listener — です/ます |
| 美化語 | びかご | Beautifies vocabulary through prefixes, regardless of respect direction | No direction — general refinement |

### How the categories interact

In a single sentence, multiple categories can coexist. Consider:

> 先生が　お書きに　なった　論文を　拝読　いたしました。

This sentence contains:
- お書きになった — 尊敬語 (raising the teacher who wrote the paper)
- 拝読 — 謙譲語I (lowering the speaker's reading relative to the teacher)
- いたしました — 謙譲語II/丁重語 (making the speaker's action formal) + 丁寧語 (ました ending, polite to the listener)

The key insight is that 尊敬語 and 謙譲語I always involve a relationship between two people — someone whose status is raised and someone whose status is lowered. 謙譲語II, by contrast, is about the speaker's general tone of formality.

### Conjugation of いらっしゃる, おっしゃる, くださる, なさる

These four verbs are ラ行五段 verbs with an irregular ます form. The ます form drops the る, not the standard り:

| Verb | Dictionary | ます form | て form | た form |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| いらっしゃる | いらっしゃる | いらっしゃいます | いらっしゃって | いらっしゃった |
| おっしゃる | おっしゃる | おっしゃいます | おっしゃって | おっしゃった |
| くださる | くださる | くださいます | くださって | くださった |
| なさる | なさる | なさいます | なさって | なさった |

### Examples

> 社長は　もう　お帰りに　なりましたか。——　いいえ、まだ　いらっしゃいます。
> "Has the president left already?" — "No, he is still here."

> 先生が　そう　おっしゃったのであれば、間違いないでしょう。
> "If the professor said so, there must be no mistake."

> 部長、こちらの　資料を　ご覧に　なって　ください。
> "Director, please take a look at these materials."

> お客様が　お召し上がりに　なった　後、お茶を　お出し　してください。
> "After the guest finishes eating, please serve tea."

## 10.3 Productive Patterns

The irregular verbs in 10.2 cover a limited set of common verbs. For all other verbs, keigo forms are generated through productive patterns. These patterns are the backbone of the keigo system.

### 尊敬語: お/ご～になる

This is the standard way to create respectful forms for any verb.

### Formation

| Verb type | Pattern | Example |
|---|---|---|
| 和語 (native Japanese verb) | お + ます stem + になる | 読む → お読みになる |
| 漢語サ変 (Sino-Japanese する verb) | ご + noun + になる | 利用する → ご利用になる |

### Examples

> 先生は　もう　この　本を　お読みに　なりましたか。
> "Has the professor already read this book?"

> お客様が　ご利用に　なった　お部屋を　確認してください。
> "Please check the room that the guest used."

> 社長が　お決めに　なった　方針に　従います。
> "We will follow the policy that the president decided."

### 謙譲語I: お/ご～する

This is the standard way to create humble forms that lower the speaker relative to a specific recipient.

### Formation

| Verb type | Pattern | Example |
|---|---|---|
| 和語 | お + ます stem + する | 持つ → お持ちする |
| 漢語サ変 | ご + noun + する | 案内する → ご案内する |

### Examples

> お荷物を　お持ち　しましょうか。
> "Shall I carry your luggage?"

> 駅まで　ご案内　します。
> "I will guide you to the station."

> 後ほど　お電話　します。
> "I will call you later."

### 謙譲語II (丁重語): お/ご～いたす

By replacing する with いたす, the expression becomes even more formal. This adds a layer of 丁重語 (dignified formality) on top of the humble meaning.

### Formation

| Verb type | Pattern | Example |
|---|---|---|
| 和語 | お + ます stem + いたす | 待つ → お待ちいたす |
| 漢語サ変 | ご + noun + いたす | 連絡する → ご連絡いたす |

### Examples

> しばらく　お待ち　いたします。——　This is incorrect. お待ちいたす is humble, but 待つ here refers to the listener waiting.

Corrected: The speaker is the one waiting.

> 結果が　出ましたら、すぐに　ご連絡　いたします。
> "When the results come out, I will contact you immediately."

> 明日　改めて　ご説明　いたします。
> "I will explain again tomorrow."

> お忙しい　ところ、お時間を　いただきまして　ありがとう　ございます。
> "Thank you for giving me your time when you are busy."

### Which verbs take お and which take ご?

The general rule:

| Origin | Prefix | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| 和語 (native Japanese) | お | お話、お書き、お読み、お待ち |
| 漢語 (Sino-Japanese) | ご | ご連絡、ご案内、ご説明、ご報告 |

Exceptions exist. Some 漢語 words take お (お電話、お食事、お時間、お返事), usually because they have become deeply naturalized in daily speech. When in doubt, ご is safer for 漢語 and お is safer for 和語.

## 10.4 Distinguishing 謙譲語I from 謙譲語II

This is the most subtle distinction in the keigo system. Both 謙譲語I and 謙譲語II lower the speaker, but they differ in whether there is a specific person being elevated as a result.

### The distinction

| Category | What it does | Requires a recipient? | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| 謙譲語I | Lowers the speaker's action to elevate the person affected by that action | Yes — there must be a specific 相手 | 先生に　伺う (I humbly ask the teacher) |
| 謙譲語II (丁重語) | Makes the speaker's action sound formal and dignified | No — it is about the speaker's own bearing | 私は　明日　参ります (I will go tomorrow — formal) |

### Key verb pairs

Several verbs have both 謙譲語I and 謙譲語II forms:

| Plain | 謙譲語I (has recipient) | 謙譲語II (no recipient) |
|---|---|---|
| 行く・来る | 伺う (visit someone specific) | 参る (go/come — formal) |
| 言う | 申し上げる (say to someone specific) | 申す (say — formal) |
| 知る | 存じ上げる (know a person — respectful) | 存じる (know — formal) |
| いる | — | おる (be — formal) |
| する | — | いたす (do — formal) |
| 食べる・もらう | いただく (receive from someone) | いただく (eat — formal) |

### Examples showing the difference

**伺う (謙譲語I) vs 参る (謙譲語II):**

> 明日、先生の　お宅に　**伺います**。
> "I will visit the professor's home tomorrow." (Humble toward the professor — 謙譲語I)

> 明日、東京に　**参ります**。
> "I will go to Tokyo tomorrow." (Formal speech — no one is being elevated — 謙譲語II)

**申し上げる (謙譲語I) vs 申す (謙譲語II):**

> 社長に　お礼を　**申し上げました**。
> "I expressed my thanks to the company president." (Humble toward the president — 謙譲語I)

> 私は　田中と　**申します**。
> "My name is Tanaka." (Formal self-introduction — no one being elevated — 謙譲語II)

**存じ上げる (謙譲語I) vs 存じる (謙譲語II):**

> 山田先生のことは　よく　**存じ上げて**　おります。
> "I know Professor Yamada well." (Humble — elevating the professor — 謙譲語I)

> その件は　**存じて**　おります。
> "I am aware of that matter." (Formal — no person being elevated — 謙譲語II)

### Why this matters

Using 謙譲語I without a recipient sounds wrong to a careful listener. If you say 先生のお宅に参ります, it is not grammatically incorrect, but it fails to show the specific respect toward the professor that 伺います would convey. Conversely, using 謙譲語I when there is no recipient (東京に伺います, where Tokyo is not a person) sounds odd — 参ります is the appropriate choice.

In practice, the distinction is most important for 伺う/参る, 申し上げる/申す, and 存じ上げる/存じる. For other verbs, the productive pattern お～いたす covers most 丁重語 needs.

## 10.5 ございます — The Keigo Copula

ございます is the keigo form of あります. It serves as the polite copula in formal speech and is the foundation of extremely polite expressions.

### Formation

| Plain | 丁寧語 | Keigo (ございます) |
|---|---|---|
| ある | あります | ございます |
| ～だ | ～です | ～でございます |
| ～だろうか | ～でしょうか | ～でございましょうか |

### でございます as copula

でございます replaces です in highly formal contexts — hotel reception, department stores, ceremonial speeches, and formal business.

### Examples

> こちらが　会議室　**でございます**。
> "This is the conference room."

> お手洗いは　二階　**でございます**。
> "The restroom is on the second floor."

> 恐れ入りますが、ただいま　満席　**でございます**。
> "I am sorry, but we are currently fully booked."

> お名前は　何と　おっしゃいますか。——　田中　**でございます**。
> "What is your name?" — "It is Tanaka."

### ございましょうか — formal question

This form is used in extremely polite questions, typically in service contexts.

> 何か　ご質問は　**ございましょうか**。
> "Are there any questions?"

> ほかに　ご注文は　**ございますか**。
> "Are there any other orders?"

> お荷物は　こちらで　**よろしゅうございますか**。
> "Is this all right for your luggage?"

Note: よろしゅうございます is the keigo form of いいです. The adjective よい/よろしい undergoes sound change (ウ音便) before ございます: よろしい → よろしゅう.

### Adjective + ございます (ウ音便)

In very formal speech, い-adjectives change their ending before ございます:

| Adjective | ウ音便 form | + ございます |
|---|---|---|
| ありがたい | ありがとう | ありがとうございます |
| おはよい | おはよう | おはようございます |
| おめでたい | おめでとう | おめでとうございます |
| よろしい | よろしゅう | よろしゅうございます |
| うれしい | うれしゅう | うれしゅうございます |

Most of these are now fixed greetings. Only よろしゅうございます and a few others remain productive in modern usage. You do not need to generate these forms freely, but recognizing them is important.

## 10.6 Reading Passage

次の文章は、新入社員の佐藤さんが先輩の高橋さんから敬語について教わっている場面です。

---

高橋：佐藤さん、さっきの電話対応、ちょっと気になったところがあったんだけど。

佐藤：はい、何でしょうか。

高橋：お客様に「山田部長はいらっしゃいません」って言ってたよね。

佐藤：はい。部長は外出中でしたので。

高橋：社外の方に対しては、うちの部長に尊敬語を使うのは適切じゃないんだ。「山田は、ただいま外出しております」と言うのが正しい。社内の人間には、たとえ上司であっても、社外の人の前では謙譲語を使う。

佐藤：あ、そうなんですか。つまり、社外の方がいらっしゃる場合は、部長にも謙譲語IIを使うということですね。

高橋：その通り。「おります」「申しております」「参っております」——こういう丁重語を使う。相手を立てるために、身内を低くするわけだ。

佐藤：分かりました。では、「部長がそのようにおっしゃっていました」も間違いですか。

高橋：社外の方に言う場合はね。「部長の山田がそのように申しておりました」が正しい。ちなみに、社内同士の会話では「部長がおっしゃっていました」で問題ない。上司に対する尊敬語だから。

佐藤：なるほど。場面によって使い分けるんですね。ご説明いただきまして、ありがとうございます。

高橋：いいよ。最初は誰でも間違える。もう一つ。お客様に「ございます」を使えるようになると、ぐっと印象がよくなる。「担当は佐藤でございます」「お手洗いは奥でございます」。こういうのを自然に言えると、一人前だよ。

佐藤：はい、練習します。

---

**Translation:**

The following is a scene where new employee Sato is being taught about keigo by senior colleague Takahashi.

---

Takahashi: Sato, I noticed something about the phone call you handled earlier.

Sato: Yes, what is it?

Takahashi: You said to the client, "Director Yamada is not here" — using honorific language.

Sato: Yes. The director was out of the office.

Takahashi: When speaking to someone outside the company, it is not appropriate to use respectful language for our own director. The correct way is to say "Yamada is currently out of the office" using humble language. For people within our own company, even if they are our superiors, we use humble language in front of outsiders.

Sato: Oh, is that so? So when someone from outside the company is present, we use 謙譲語II even for the director?

Takahashi: Exactly. "Orimasu," "moushite orimasu," "maitte orimasu" — you use these dignified-formal forms. The idea is that you lower your own side in order to elevate the other party.

Sato: I understand. Then, is "The director said so" using おっしゃる also wrong?

Takahashi: When saying it to someone outside the company, yes. The correct form is "Our director Yamada said so" using 申す. By the way, in conversations between colleagues within the company, "The director said so" with おっしゃる is perfectly fine. That is respectful language directed at a superior.

Sato: I see. So you switch depending on the situation. Thank you for the explanation.

Takahashi: No problem. Everyone makes mistakes at first. One more thing — if you can use ございます naturally with clients, it makes a much better impression. "I am Sato, the person in charge." "The restroom is in the back." If you can say these things naturally, you will be considered a full professional.

Sato: Yes, I will practice.
