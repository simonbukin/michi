# Chapter 12 — Keigo Errors, Hypercorrection, and Real-World Usage

Keigo is difficult. It is difficult for learners, and it is difficult for native speakers. Surveys consistently show that a majority of Japanese adults feel uncertain about their own keigo usage, and errors are pervasive in everyday life — in convenience stores, on television, in business meetings, and in formal writing.

This chapter is not about perfecting your own keigo production, though understanding errors will certainly help with that. Its primary purpose is comprehension. When you encounter keigo in the wild, you will frequently meet forms that are technically incorrect but widely used. You need to be able to understand what the speaker intended, recognize why the form is considered an error, and decide for yourself whether the error matters in context.

We will examine four major categories of keigo errors: the overuse of させていただく, double honorifics (二重敬語), incorrect self-referential keigo, and the phenomenon known as バイト敬語 — the hypercorrect forms that emerged in the service industry and have spread into general speech.

---

## 12.1 させていただく — Correct Use vs Overuse

させていただく is the single most controversial keigo expression in modern Japanese. It is everywhere — in business, in speeches, on television, in announcements. It is also, in many of its uses, considered excessive or incorrect by language authorities.

### What させていただく actually means

させていただく is composed of:
- させる (causative) — "to be allowed to do"
- いただく (謙譲語I for もらう) — "to receive (humbly)"

The literal meaning is: "I humbly receive the favor of being allowed to do X." This implies two conditions:

1. **Permission**: Someone else has given permission or made the action possible
2. **Benefit**: The speaker benefits from this permission

### Formation

| Verb type | Pattern | Example |
|---|---|---|
| 五段 | あ-stem + せていただく | 休む → 休ませていただく |
| 一段 | stem + させていただく | 見る → 見させていただく |
| する | させていただく | 説明する → 説明させていただく |

### Correct uses

When both conditions (permission and benefit) are met, させていただく is appropriate:

> 本日は　お休みを　いただきまして、ありがとうございます。来週から　復帰　させていただきます。
> "Thank you for giving me time off. I will return to work from next week." (Permission: the company granted leave. Benefit: the speaker rested.)

> 先日の　会議で　発表　させていただきました。貴重な　機会を　ありがとうございました。
> "I was given the opportunity to present at the meeting the other day. Thank you for the valuable opportunity." (Permission: someone invited the speaker. Benefit: the speaker gained experience.)

### The overuse problem

In modern Japanese, させていただく is frequently used even when neither permission nor benefit is involved. This has become so widespread that linguists have given it a name: させていただく症候群 (させていただく syndrome).

### Examples of overuse

> ×　本日は　司会を　**させていただく**　山田です。
> (Common in practice, but technically problematic. The speaker is simply introducing themselves — no permission is being humbly received in the moment of speaking.)

A more natural alternative:

> ○　本日、司会を　**務めます**　山田です。
> "I am Yamada, and I will serve as today's moderator."

> ×　私は　東京に　**住まわせていただいて**　おります。
> (No one gave permission to live in Tokyo. This is excessive humility.)

> ○　私は　東京に　**住んで**　おります。
> "I live in Tokyo."

> ×　こちらの　商品を　**紹介させていただきます**。
> (The speaker is simply presenting a product. No permission was asked or granted.)

> ○　こちらの　商品を　**ご紹介いたします**。
> "I will introduce this product."

### Why it spreads

させていただく feels safe. In a culture where insufficient politeness can cause offense, using the most elaborate form available seems like the safest bet. The result is a kind of keigo inflation: as everyone uses させていただく, it becomes the expected baseline, and anything less sounds insufficiently polite.

For the learner, the key point is: understand what させていただく means when you encounter it, but in your own usage, prefer simpler alternatives like いたします or お～します unless the context genuinely involves receiving permission.

## 12.2 二重敬語 — Double Honorifics

二重敬語 (double honorifics) occurs when a speaker applies the same type of keigo twice to the same verb. The result is redundant and technically incorrect, though many double-honorific forms are so entrenched that they are accepted as idiomatic.

### The rule

One verb should receive only one layer of each keigo type. Applying 尊敬語 twice, or 謙譲語 twice, to the same verb is 二重敬語.

### Common errors

| Error | Analysis | Correct form |
|---|---|---|
| お読みになられる | お読みになる (尊敬語) + られる (尊敬語) = double | お読みになる |
| ご覧になられる | ご覧になる (尊敬語) + られる (尊敬語) = double | ご覧になる |
| おっしゃられる | おっしゃる (尊敬語) + られる (尊敬語) = double | おっしゃる |
| お召し上がりになる | 召し上がる (尊敬語) + お～になる (尊敬語) = double | 召し上がる |
| お伺いいたす | 伺う (謙譲語I) + お～いたす (謙譲語) = double | 伺う / お伺いする |

### Examples

> ×　先生が　**おっしゃられた**　通りです。
> ○　先生が　**おっしゃった**　通りです。
> "It is as the professor said."

> ×　社長は　もう　**お帰りに　なられました**。
> ○　社長は　もう　**お帰りに　なりました**。
> "The president has already left."

> ×　どうぞ　**お召し上がりに　なってください**。
> ○　どうぞ　**召し上がって　ください**。
> "Please help yourself (to food or drink)."

### Accepted exceptions

Some double-honorific forms are so widely used that they are now considered acceptable even by prescriptive authorities:

| Form | Status |
|---|---|
| お召し上がりになる | Widely accepted |
| お見えになる | Accepted (from 見える, already honorific) |
| お伺いする | Widely accepted |

The 文化審議会's 2007 report on keigo explicitly notes that certain double-honorific forms have become established through long use and need not be corrected. The learner should focus on recognizing double honorifics rather than anxiously avoiding every possible instance.

## 12.3 Incorrect Self-Referential Keigo

One of the most serious keigo errors is using 尊敬語 (respectful language) about oneself. Since 尊敬語 is designed to raise the status of the person you are talking about, using it for your own actions sounds as though you are elevating yourself — the opposite of the intended humility.

### Common errors

> ×　明日、三時に　**いらっしゃいます**。(Talking about oneself)
> ○　明日、三時に　**参ります**。
> "I will come at three o'clock tomorrow."

> ×　私が　**ご覧に　なりました**。(Using 尊敬語 for one's own looking)
> ○　私が　**拝見　いたしました**。
> "I looked at it."

> ×　私が　**召し上がりました**。
> ○　私が　**いただきました**。
> "I ate it."

### The ウチ/ソト error in companies

As discussed in Chapter 10, when speaking to people outside your company, you must use 謙譲語 for everyone inside your company — including your superiors. Failing to do this is a common error among new employees.

> ×　（社外の人に）弊社の　部長が　**おっしゃっていました**。
> ○　（社外の人に）弊社の　部長の　山田が　**申しておりました**。
> "Our director Yamada said (so)."

> ×　（社外の人に）社長は　**いらっしゃいません**。
> ○　（社外の人に）社長の　田中は　**おりません**。
> "President Tanaka is not here."

### Why this error is serious

This error reveals a fundamental misunderstanding of the keigo system. While double honorifics and させていただく overuse are stylistic issues, using 尊敬語 for yourself is a categorical error — it reverses the direction of respect. In business contexts, this can create an impression of arrogance or incompetence.

## 12.4 よろしかったでしょうか — バイト敬語

バイト敬語 (part-time-job keigo) refers to a set of expressions that emerged in convenience stores, fast-food restaurants, and other service-industry contexts. These forms spread through employee manuals and imitation, and while they are now extremely common, many of them are considered incorrect by language prescriptivists.

### Common バイト敬語 forms

| バイト敬語 (common but incorrect) | Correct form | Problem |
|---|---|---|
| よろしかったでしょうか | よろしいでしょうか | Past tense for a present question |
| ～のほうをお持ちしました | ～をお持ちしました | のほう is unnecessary |
| ～になります | ～でございます / ～です | なる implies change, but nothing is changing |
| ～円からお預かりします | ～円をお預かりします | から is illogical with お預かりする |
| ご注文は以上でよろしかったでしょうか | ご注文は以上でよろしいでしょうか | Same past-tense error |

### Examples with corrections

> ×　こちら、コーヒーに　**なります**。
> ○　こちら、コーヒー　**でございます**。
> "Here is your coffee."

The error: なる means "to become." The coffee did not become coffee — it always was coffee. でございます (or です) is the correct copula.

> ×　千円　**から**　お預かり　します。
> ○　千円　お預かり　いたします。
> "I will take your one thousand yen."

The error: から means "from," implying the money is a starting point. But the cashier is simply receiving money. The から is a meaningless insertion that has become habitual.

> ×　ご注文は　以上で　**よろしかった**　でしょうか。
> ○　ご注文は　以上で　**よろしい**　でしょうか。
> "Is that everything for your order?"

The error: The question is about the present situation, but よろしかった is past tense. The theory behind this usage is that the past tense sounds softer and less direct, but prescriptively it is incorrect.

### Why バイト敬語 exists

These forms arise from a genuine desire to be polite. Service workers, many of them young and without formal keigo training, construct what sounds like elaborate keigo by adding extra elements — past tense, のほう, から — that give the sentence a more complex, and therefore seemingly more polite, texture. The intention is good; the execution is flawed.

For the learner, the practical takeaway is simple: you will hear these forms constantly in daily life. Recognize them, understand what is intended, and in your own speech, use the correct alternatives.

## 12.5 Why Recognizing Errors Matters

You might wonder why a chapter on errors deserves space in a textbook. The answer is that real Japanese is full of imperfect keigo, and a learner who only knows textbook-correct forms will be confused by what they actually encounter.

### Three practical reasons

**First**, comprehension. If a store clerk says こちらコーヒーになります, you need to understand that they mean "Here is your coffee" — not that something is transforming into coffee. If a business partner writes させていただきたく存じます three times in one email, you need to parse each instance for its intended meaning rather than its literal one.

**Second**, social awareness. Understanding keigo errors lets you read the social situation. A person who uses バイト敬語 is probably young and working in service. A person who uses 二重敬語 is probably trying hard to be polite but lacks formal training. A person who uses させていただく excessively may be nervous or overly cautious. These are all social cues.

**Third**, self-monitoring. If you know the common errors, you can avoid them in your own speech and writing. This matters because keigo errors are one of the first things native speakers notice about non-native speech.

## 12.6 Reading Passage

次の文章は、ある会社の新入社員研修で使われた「敬語の間違い例」の資料です。各例文の誤りを指摘し、正しい表現に直しています。

---

**新入社員敬語研修資料：よくある間違い**

以下の会話には敬語の間違いが含まれています。正しい表現と比較してください。

**場面1：電話対応**

誤：「はい、山田部長はただいま外出されていらっしゃいます。」

問題点：二重敬語。「外出されて」（受身の尊敬語）と「いらっしゃいます」（尊敬語）が重なっている。さらに、社外の方への電話であれば、自社の上司に尊敬語を使うこと自体が誤り。

正（社外の方に対して）：「山田はただいま外出しております。」

正（社内の方に対して）：「山田部長はただいま外出されています。」

**場面2：会議の冒頭**

誤：「本日、司会をさせていただく佐藤です。ここで資料を配らせていただきたいと思います。それでは、会議を始めさせていただきます。」

問題点：一文に一回なら許容範囲だが、三回連続は明らかな過剰使用。資料を配ることに「許可」は不要。会議を始めることも司会者の役割であり、へりくだる必要はない。

正：「本日、司会を務めます佐藤です。ここで資料を配りたいと思います。それでは、会議を始めます。」

**場面3：コンビニでの会計**

誤：「お弁当のほう、温めますか。——はい。——では、温めさせていただきます。お会計は千二百円からお預かりいたします。レシートのほう、よろしかったでしょうか。」

問題点：「のほう」は不要。「させていただきます」は客が頼んだことなので許容範囲だが、「温めます」で十分。「から」は不適切。「よろしかったでしょうか」は過去形の誤用。

正：「お弁当、温めますか。——はい。——では、温めます。千二百円、お預かりいたします。レシートはよろしいですか。」

**場面4：メールの自己紹介**

誤：「先日のセミナーでお話しさせていただいた鈴木と申します。その節は大変お世話になられました。」

問題点：「お話しさせていただいた」はセミナーで話す機会を与えてもらったなら正しい。「お世話になられました」は、「お世話になる」は自分の側の表現（謙譲）であり、「られる」（尊敬）を付けるのは矛盾。

正：「先日のセミナーでお話しさせていただいた鈴木と申します。その節は大変お世話になりました。」

---

**Translation:**

The following is a document used in a new employee training session titled "Examples of Keigo Mistakes." Each example sentence's error is identified and corrected.

---

**New Employee Keigo Training Materials: Common Mistakes**

The following conversations contain keigo errors. Please compare them with the correct expressions.

**Scene 1: Phone Handling**

Error: "Yes, Director Yamada has gone out." (with double honorifics)

Problem: Double honorific. The passive-honorific "sarete" and "irasshaimasu" (honorific) overlap. Furthermore, if this is a call from someone outside the company, using honorific language for one's own superior is itself an error.

Correct (to someone outside the company): "Yamada is currently out of the office."

Correct (to someone inside the company): "Director Yamada is currently out of the office." (with single honorific)

**Scene 2: Opening of a Meeting**

Error: "I am Sato, who has been allowed to serve as moderator today. I would like to be allowed to distribute the materials. Now then, I will be allowed to begin the meeting." (three instances of させていただく)

Problem: Once per sentence is within acceptable range, but three times in a row is clearly excessive. No "permission" is needed to distribute materials. Starting the meeting is the moderator's role and does not require humility.

Correct: "I am Sato, and I will serve as today's moderator. I would like to distribute the materials. Now then, let us begin the meeting."

**Scene 3: Convenience Store Checkout**

Error: Contains のほう (unnecessary), させていただく (acceptable here but unnecessary), から (illogical), and よろしかったでしょうか (past tense error).

Problem: "no hou" is unnecessary. "sasete itadakimasu" is within acceptable range since the customer asked for it, but "atatamemasu" is sufficient. "kara" is inappropriate. "yoroshikatta deshou ka" is an incorrect use of past tense.

Correct: "Shall I heat the lunch box? — Yes. — I will heat it. I will take 1,200 yen. Would you like a receipt?"

**Scene 4: Email Self-Introduction**

Error: "I am Suzuki, who was allowed to speak at the seminar the other day. You were greatly taken care of at that time." (with honorific applied to a humble expression)

Problem: "osewani nararemashita" applies the honorific "rareru" to "osewa ni naru," which is itself a humble/self-lowering expression. The combination is contradictory.

Correct: "I am Suzuki, who spoke at the seminar the other day. Thank you very much for your help at that time."
