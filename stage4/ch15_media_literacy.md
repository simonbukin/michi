# Chapter 15 — Media Literacy: Reading Critically

Japanese news media maintains a reputation for factual accuracy, but every outlet makes choices about word selection, sentence structure, and emphasis that shape how readers interpret events. This chapter is not about grammar in the traditional sense — you already know the grammar patterns involved. Instead, it is about recognizing how those patterns function as signals of editorial stance, confidence, attribution, and opinion.

The ability to read critically means noticing when a journalist is reporting a fact, attributing a claim to a source, hedging a conclusion, or advancing an editorial position. Japanese has a rich set of grammatical tools for each of these functions, and skilled readers learn to pick up on them automatically. This chapter will train you to do the same.

All the examples in this chapter draw on patterns you have already studied in Stages 3 and 4 — passive constructions, modality markers, quotative structures, and formal written style. What is new here is the analytical framework for understanding why a writer chose one pattern over another.

## 15.1 Framing Through Word Choice

The same event can be described in Japanese using vocabulary that casts it in a positive, negative, or neutral light. Japanese newspapers do this constantly, and readers who are not attentive to word choice will absorb the framing without realizing it.

### Neutral vs loaded vocabulary

Consider an event: the government proposes a change to labor regulations. Different outlets might describe this with different verbs and nouns.

| Neutral | Positive framing | Negative framing |
|---|---|---|
| 変更する (change) | 改善する (improve) | 改悪する (worsen) |
| 見直す (review) | 改革する (reform) | 後退させる (set back) |
| 方針 (policy direction) | 取り組み (initiative, effort) | 強行 (forced push) |
| 決定する (decide) | 実現する (bring about) | 押し通す (force through) |

### Examples of framing in action

The same event — a bill passes the lower house — might appear as:

> 法案が衆議院で可決された。
> "The bill was passed in the House of Representatives." (Neutral)

> 法案が衆議院で可決され、改革が前進した。
> "The bill was passed in the House of Representatives, and reform moved forward." (Positive)

> 法案が衆議院で強行採決された。
> "The bill was forced through a vote in the House of Representatives." (Negative)

The word 強行採決 (forced vote) immediately signals editorial disapproval. A reader who does not recognize this framing will simply absorb the negative characterization as fact.

### Subject choice as framing

Who appears as the grammatical subject also shapes interpretation.

> 政府が新たな規制を導入した。
> "The government introduced new regulations." (Government as active agent — emphasizes responsibility)

> 新たな規制が導入された。
> "New regulations were introduced." (Passive — de-emphasizes who is responsible)

> 新たな規制の導入が決まった。
> "The introduction of new regulations was decided." (Nominalized — presents it as an impersonal process)

Japanese journalism frequently uses passive and nominalized constructions to create distance between the actor and the action. This is not accidental.

## 15.2 Editorial Stance Markers

Japanese editorial writing uses specific grammatical patterns to signal that the writer is expressing an opinion rather than reporting a fact. Learning to recognize these markers is essential for critical reading.

### べきだ — "should, ought to"

べきだ marks a clear normative claim. When you see it in a newspaper article, the writer has shifted from reporting to advocating.

> 政府はこの問題に早急に対応すべきだ。
> "The government should respond to this issue urgently."

> 国民は冷静に判断すべきである。
> "Citizens should judge calmly."

Note that べきである is the written form. In news editorials, you will almost always see べきだ or べきである rather than the spoken べきだ alone.

### ではないか — "isn't it the case that" (rhetorical)

This pattern, when used at the end of a statement in written style, functions as a rhetorical question that advances the writer's position. It is softer than a direct assertion but still clearly signals opinion.

> この政策は国民の利益に反するのではないか。
> "Does this policy not go against the interests of the people?" (= I think it does.)

> もう少し慎重に議論すべきではないか。
> "Shouldn't we discuss this more carefully?" (= I think we should.)

> 今こそ根本的な改革が必要なのではないだろうか。
> "Is it not precisely now that fundamental reform is needed?" (= I believe it is.)

The pattern ではないだろうか adds an additional layer of hedging but functions the same way — it is an opinion disguised as a question.

### と言わざるを得ない — "one cannot help but say"

This is one of the strongest editorial opinion markers. It frames the writer's judgment as something compelled by the evidence — "the facts force me to say this."

> 今回の対応は不十分だったと言わざるを得ない。
> "One cannot help but say that the response this time was insufficient."

> この結果は政策の失敗を示していると言わざるを得ない。
> "One cannot help but say that these results indicate a policy failure."

Despite the passive-sounding construction, this is a strong assertion of the writer's view. It carries more rhetorical weight than a simple statement because it implies reluctant but unavoidable judgment.

### と考える / と思われる — opinion with varying directness

| Pattern | Directness | Example |
|---|---|---|
| と考える | Direct personal opinion | この方針は正しいと考える |
| と思われる | Distanced (passive) | この方針は正しいと思われる |
| と言えよう | "One could say" (literary) | この方針は正しいと言えよう |
| と見るべきだ | "Should be seen as" | これは転換点と見るべきだ |

## 15.3 Hedging vs Asserting

Japanese writers signal how confident they are in a claim through specific grammatical choices. Recognizing the difference between a hedged claim and a direct assertion is a key reading skill.

### Confidence spectrum

From most hedged to most assertive:

| Pattern | Confidence level | Example |
|---|---|---|
| かもしれない | Possibility ("might") | 効果があるかもしれない |
| 可能性がある | Possibility exists | 影響を与える可能性がある |
| と見られている | "Is regarded as" | 原因と見られている |
| と考えられる | "Is thought to be" | 有効だと考えられる |
| とされている | "Is said to be" | 最大の課題とされている |
| と言える | "One can say" | 成功したと言える |
| に違いない | "No doubt" | 効果があるに違いない |
| である | Direct assertion | これは事実である |

### How hedging works in practice

A single paragraph might move between hedged and assertive claims. Notice the shifts:

> 今回の調査結果は、若者の政治離れが進んでいることを示している。投票率の低下は、民主主義の根幹に関わる問題**と言わざるを得ない**。原因は複合的**と考えられる**が、政治家の言葉が若者に届いていない**可能性がある**。教育現場での取り組みを強化**すべきだ**。

In this passage:
- 示している — presents the survey result as factual
- と言わざるを得ない — editorial judgment (strong)
- と考えられる — hedged assessment of cause
- 可能性がある — hedged hypothesis
- すべきだ — normative recommendation (opinion)

A critical reader registers each of these shifts. An uncritical reader absorbs all five statements as equally factual.

## 15.4 とされる vs である — Attributed Claim vs Stated Fact

This distinction is one of the most important in Japanese media literacy. The difference between とされている ("is said to be / is regarded as") and である ("is") marks the boundary between attributed information and asserted fact.

### とされる — "it is said that, it is considered to be"

とされる signals that the information comes from some external authority, consensus, or common understanding — but the writer is not personally vouching for it.

> この遺跡は約三千年前のものとされている。
> "This ruin is said to date to about three thousand years ago."

> 日本の食品ロスは年間六百万トン以上とされる。
> "Food waste in Japan is said to be over six million tons annually."

> 交渉は難航しているとされる。
> "Negotiations are said to be stalled."

### である — direct assertion

> この遺跡は約三千年前のものである。
> "This ruin dates to about three thousand years ago." (The writer states this as fact.)

### Related attribution patterns

| Pattern | Function |
|---|---|
| とされている | Widely regarded as (consensus attribution) |
| と見られている | Is seen as, is believed to (analytical attribution) |
| と報じられている | Has been reported as (media attribution) |
| との見方がある | There is a view that (attributed to unnamed analysts) |
| という指摘がある | There is a criticism/observation that (attributed to critics) |
| と言われている | It is said that (general knowledge attribution) |

### Why this matters

When a journalist writes とされている instead of である, they are creating distance from the claim. This might mean:
- The information has not been independently verified
- There is some disagreement about the facts
- The writer is protecting themselves from liability
- The information comes from a single source

A critical reader asks: "Who says so? Why is this attributed rather than stated directly?"

## 15.5 Reading Exercise: Two Editorial Treatments

The following two passages describe the same event — a proposed increase in the consumption tax — but with different editorial framing. Read both carefully and identify the stance markers, word choices, and hedging patterns in each.

### Context

政府は来年度からの消費税率引き上げを検討していることが明らかになった。

### Editorial A

---

政府は来年度からの消費税率引き上げを正式に検討していることが明らかになった。消費税の増税は、膨張する社会保障費を賄うために避けられない措置であり、先送りは将来世代への負担の転嫁に他ならない。

確かに、増税は家計に影響を与える。しかし、財政健全化なくして持続可能な社会保障制度の維持は不可能である。高齢化が加速する中、いま手を打たなければ、将来的にはより大幅な増税を余儀なくされることは明白だ。

国民に負担を求める以上、政府は歳出の無駄を徹底的に見直すべきであり、増税分の使途を明確に説明する責任がある。しかし、増税そのものを否定することは、現実から目を背けることに等しいと言わざるを得ない。

---

### Editorial B

---

政府が来年度からの消費税率引き上げを検討していることが明らかになった。景気回復の兆しがようやく見え始めた中での増税検討に、国民の間から不安の声が上がるのは当然ではないか。

消費税の増税は、家計を直撃し、個人消費を冷え込ませる恐れがある。前回の増税後、消費が長期にわたって低迷したことは記憶に新しい。同じ過ちを繰り返す可能性があるとの指摘は無視できない。

財源の確保が必要だとしても、消費税に頼る前に、歳出改革や法人税の見直しなど、他の選択肢を十分に検討すべきではないだろうか。増税ありきの議論は、国民の理解を得られないと言わざるを得ない。

---

**Translation:**

It was revealed that the government is officially considering an increase in the consumption tax rate starting next fiscal year.

### Editorial A

The government is officially considering an increase in the consumption tax rate starting next fiscal year. A consumption tax increase is an unavoidable measure to cover ballooning social security costs, and postponement is nothing other than shifting the burden to future generations.

Certainly, a tax increase will affect household finances. However, maintaining a sustainable social security system without fiscal consolidation is impossible. With the aging of the population accelerating, it is clear that if action is not taken now, even larger tax increases will be forced upon us in the future.

Since the government is asking citizens to bear a burden, it should thoroughly review wasteful spending, and it has a responsibility to clearly explain how the tax increase revenue will be used. However, one cannot help but say that denying the tax increase itself is tantamount to turning away from reality.

### Editorial B

It was revealed that the government is considering an increase in the consumption tax rate starting next fiscal year. With signs of economic recovery only just beginning to appear, is it not natural that voices of anxiety are rising among citizens?

A consumption tax increase risks directly hitting household finances and dampening personal consumption. It is still fresh in memory that consumption slumped for an extended period after the last tax increase. The observation that the same mistake could be repeated cannot be ignored.

Even if securing revenue sources is necessary, should we not sufficiently examine other options, such as spending reform and a review of corporate taxes, before relying on the consumption tax? One cannot help but say that a discussion premised on a tax increase will fail to win the understanding of the public.

---

### Analysis Guide

| Feature | Editorial A | Editorial B |
|---|---|---|
| Framing vocabulary | 避けられない措置 (unavoidable measure), 財政健全化 (fiscal consolidation) | 直撃する (hit directly), 冷え込ませる (chill), 過ち (mistake) |
| Stance on tax increase | Supports as necessary | Opposes as premature |
| Rhetorical questions | None | ではないか, べきではないだろうか |
| Attribution/hedging | 明白だ (clear — asserted) | 恐れがある (risk exists — hedged), 可能性がある (possibility) |
| と言わざるを得ない usage | Criticizes opponents of the tax | Criticizes proponents of the tax |
| Concessive structure | 確かに...しかし (concedes impact, argues for necessity) | としても (concedes need, argues for alternatives) |

Both editorials use the same grammatical tools — べき, と言わざるを得ない, ではないか — but deploy them to advance opposite positions. This is the core lesson of media literacy: grammar is not neutral. The patterns you have learned are rhetorical instruments, and their function depends on the argument they serve.

### Practice: Identifying stance in single sentences

For each of the following sentences, identify whether the writer is reporting a fact, attributing a claim, hedging, or expressing an opinion.

> （一）　政府は来年度予算案を国会に提出した。

Factual report. No attribution, hedging, or opinion markers.

> （二）　この政策は失敗だったと言わざるを得ない。

Opinion. The pattern と言わざるを得ない signals the writer's judgment.

> （三）　景気は回復しつつあると見られている。

Attributed claim. The pattern と見られている distances the writer from the assertion.

> （四）　国民の間では不安が広がっている可能性がある。

Hedged claim. The pattern 可能性がある marks this as a possibility, not a certainty.

> （五）　今こそ抜本的な改革に着手すべきではないだろうか。

Opinion disguised as a rhetorical question. べき and ではないだろうか together create a strong editorial recommendation.

> （六）　専門家の間では、効果は限定的だとの見方が多い。

Attributed claim. との見方が多い attributes the assessment to experts rather than asserting it directly.
