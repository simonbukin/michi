# Chapter 21 — Literature: First Encounters

Reading literature in Japanese is a different experience from reading newspapers or academic texts. The grammar is familiar, but the way it is deployed changes. Sentences grow longer or shorter for effect. The narrator's voice shifts between detached observation and intimate thought. Tense is used not to mark when something happened but to control narrative distance. Vocabulary draws on registers that journalism avoids: sensory detail, emotional nuance, and words chosen for sound as much as meaning.

This chapter introduces you to the conventions of Japanese literary prose. You will learn to recognize the differences between literary and non-literary style, understand how Japanese authors use tense as a narrative tool, encounter the literary copula forms that mark written fiction, and begin to read interior monologue — the technique of presenting a character's thoughts directly on the page.

The two reading passages at the end offer contrasting styles: one in the manner of early modern masters like Natsume Soseki or Akutagawa Ryunosuke, and one in a contemporary style reminiscent of Murakami Haruki. Both are original passages written in the spirit of these authors, not direct quotations.

## 21.1 Literary Vocabulary and Style Differences

Literary Japanese differs from journalistic or academic Japanese in several consistent ways. Recognizing these differences will prevent confusion when you encounter them for the first time in actual fiction.

### Word choice

Literary prose favors native Japanese words (和語) over Sino-Japanese compounds (漢語) in many contexts. Where a newspaper might write 開始する, a novel might write 始まる or 始める. Where a report says 移動する, a story says 歩く, 走る, or a more specific verb of motion.

> 男は黙って椅子から立ち上がり、窓のほうへ歩いていった。
> "The man silently stood up from his chair and walked toward the window."

Compare the journalistic equivalent:

> 男性は席を離れ、窓際に移動した。
> "The man left his seat and moved to the window side."

The literary version gives physical detail. The journalistic version reports an action.

### Sentence length and rhythm

Literary sentences vary in length deliberately. Short sentences create tension or emphasis. Long sentences with multiple clauses create a flowing, contemplative rhythm.

> 雨だった。一日中降り続いた。やむ気配はなかった。
> "It was raining. It continued to fall all day. There was no sign of it stopping."

> 彼女が何を考えているのか、その表情からは読み取ることができなかったが、少なくとも怒っているわけではなさそうだった。
> "He could not read from her expression what she was thinking, but at the very least she did not seem to be angry."

### Particles and omission

Literary prose sometimes drops particles that would be present in standard written Japanese, particularly は and を in fast-paced or emotionally intense passages.

> 声、聞こえた。遠くから。
> "A voice. He heard it. From far away."

## 21.2 Narrative Tense

Japanese fiction uses tense differently from English fiction. Understanding this system is essential for following the flow of a story.

### た-form as narrative past

The た-form is the default tense for narrating events that have occurred. This is the equivalent of English past tense narration ("He walked into the room. She looked up.").

> 太郎は部屋に入った。机の上に一通の手紙が置いてあった。彼はそれを手に取り、封を切った。
> "Taro entered the room. A letter had been placed on the desk. He picked it up and opened the seal."

### Present tense for vividness

Japanese authors frequently shift to present tense (dictionary form) within a past-tense narrative to create immediacy, vividness, or a sense of the character experiencing something in real time.

> 太郎は立ち止まった。目の前に広がるのは、見渡す限りの草原である。風が吹いている。草が揺れる。どこまでも続く緑の中に、一本の道が伸びていた。
> "Taro stopped. What spreads before his eyes is a grassland stretching as far as he can see. The wind is blowing. The grass sways. In the green that continues endlessly, a single road stretched out."

Notice how the passage shifts from た (立ち止まった) to present tense (である, 吹いている, 揺れる) and then back to た (伸びていた). This is not an error. The present-tense sentences place the reader inside the character's perception.

### How to read tense shifts

When you encounter a shift from た to dictionary form in narration, ask yourself:

| Tense | Function |
|-------|----------|
| た-form | Narrating events in sequence |
| Dictionary form | Describing what the character perceives in the moment |
| ている | Ongoing state or action that the character notices |
| であった | Formal literary past (see 21.3) |

> 電車が止まった。ドアが開く。人々が降りてくる。彼女の姿はなかった。
> "The train stopped. The doors open. People get off. She was not there."

The middle two sentences are in present tense: the narrator is showing us the scene as the character watches it unfold. The final sentence returns to past tense for the narrative judgment.

## 21.3 Literary Copula: であった and のであった

Modern literary prose uses copula forms that are distinct from both the spoken だった and the academic である.

### であった — literary past copula

であった is the past tense of である. It appears in narration where だった would feel too colloquial and でした would feel too polite.

> それは、彼にとって初めての経験であった。
> "That was, for him, a first experience."

> 彼女の瞳は、深い湖のような色であった。
> "Her eyes were a color like a deep lake."

### のであった — explanatory literary past

のであった adds an explanatory nuance, similar to のだった but more literary. It signals that the narrator is providing background information or explaining a reason.

> 彼がこの町を訪れたのは、三年ぶりのことであった。かつてここで暮らしていた日々が、遠い記憶のように思い出されるのであった。
> "His visiting this town was for the first time in three years. The days when he once lived here would be recalled like distant memories."

### のだった vs のであった

| Form | Register | Use |
|------|----------|-----|
| のだった | Casual literary, light novels | Character realization, casual narration |
| のであった | Formal literary, classic style | Authorial explanation, reflective narration |

> そうだ、約束があったのだった。
> "That's right — he had a promise." (Character remembering)

> この地方には、古くから独自の風習が伝えられていたのであった。
> "In this region, unique customs had been passed down since ancient times." (Narrator explaining)

## 21.4 内面描写 — Interior Monologue Conventions

Japanese fiction frequently presents a character's inner thoughts directly, without quotation marks or explicit markers like "he thought." Recognizing when the text shifts from narration to interior monologue is one of the most important skills for reading Japanese literature.

### Markers of interior monologue

Several linguistic features signal that the text has shifted to a character's thoughts:

**Casual sentence endings within formal narration:**

> 山田は窓の外を見つめていた。明日もまた同じ一日が始まるのだろうか。いつまでこんな生活が続くのか。彼は小さくため息をついた。
> "Yamada gazed out the window. Would tomorrow be the start of the same day again? How long would this kind of life continue? He let out a small sigh."

The middle two sentences are interior monologue. They use question forms (のだろうか, のか) that represent the character's thoughts, not the narrator's questions.

**Use of だろう and だろうか:**

> おそらく彼女は気づいていないだろう。いや、気づいていて、知らないふりをしているのかもしれない。
> "She probably hadn't noticed. No — perhaps she had noticed and was pretending not to know."

**Sudden shift to informal register:**

> 田中は提案書を読み返した。これでいいのか。本当にこの方向で間違いないのか。いや、今さら迷っても仕方がない。彼は書類を鞄に入れ、会議室へ向かった。
> "Tanaka reread the proposal. Is this really okay? Is this direction really right? No, there's no point hesitating now. He put the documents in his bag and headed for the conference room."

### Free indirect discourse

Japanese literature also uses free indirect discourse (自由間接話法), where the narrator's voice and the character's thoughts blend together without clear boundaries.

> 山本は家に帰ると、冷蔵庫を開けた。何もない。昨日買い物に行くはずだったのに、結局行かなかった。仕方ない。近くのコンビニで何か買ってこよう。
> "Yamamoto returned home and opened the refrigerator. Nothing. He was supposed to go shopping yesterday, but he didn't end up going. Oh well. He would go buy something at the nearby convenience store."

The narrator tells us Yamamoto opened the refrigerator. From 何もない onward, we are inside his perspective — but the narrator never says "he thought."

## 21.5 Reading Passage 1 — Classical Style

次の文章は、明治・大正期の文学を思わせる文体で書かれた短い物語の冒頭である。

---

秋の夕暮れであった。赤く染まった空が次第に暗さを増し、遠くの山々はすでに影となっていた。吾輩はこの町に着いたばかりであった。

駅前の通りには人影もまばらで、わずかに残る商店の明かりが、濡れた石畳の上にぼんやりと映っていた。雨は先ほどやんだらしいが、空気にはまだ湿り気が残っている。

宿を探さねばならぬ。しかし、こんな小さな町に、旅館などというものがあるのだろうか。吾輩は鞄を持ち直し、通りを歩き始めた。

しばらく行くと、古びた看板が目に入った。「松風旅館」とある。戸は閉まっていたが、中にはかすかに灯りが見えた。戸を叩くと、しばらくして中から老婆が顔を出した。

「お泊まりですか」

その声は低く、しわがれていたが、どこか温かみがあった。吾輩は頷き、中へ通された。狭い玄関に古い下駄が並んでいる。壁には色褪せた掛け軸がかかり、かすかに線香の匂いがした。

部屋に案内されると、そこは六畳ほどの小さな和室であった。窓からは何も見えない。暗闇の中に、虫の声だけが響いていた。吾輩は荷物を下ろし、畳の上に腰を下ろした。長い旅であった。疲れは体の隅々にまで染み込んでいるのであった。

---

**Translation:**

It was an autumn evening. The sky, dyed red, was gradually growing darker, and the distant mountains had already become shadows. I had just arrived in this town.

On the street in front of the station, there were few signs of people, and the light from the few remaining shops was faintly reflected on the wet cobblestones. The rain seemed to have stopped a short while ago, but there was still moisture in the air.

I must find lodging. But in such a small town, would there even be such a thing as an inn? I shifted my bag and began to walk down the street.

After walking for a while, an old sign caught my eye. It read "Matsukaze Inn." The door was closed, but a faint light could be seen inside. When I knocked, after a moment an old woman appeared from within.

"Are you staying the night?"

Her voice was low and hoarse, but it had a certain warmth. I nodded and was led inside. In the narrow entryway, old wooden clogs were lined up. On the wall hung a faded hanging scroll, and there was a faint smell of incense.

When I was shown to a room, it was a small Japanese-style room of about six tatami mats. Nothing could be seen from the window. In the darkness, only the sound of insects echoed. I set down my luggage and sat down on the tatami. It had been a long journey. The fatigue had seeped into every corner of my body.

## 21.6 Reading Passage 2 — Contemporary Style

次の文章は、現代の小説を思わせる文体で書かれた短い物語の一場面である。

---

僕がそのカフェに入ったのは、特に理由があったわけではない。ただ、歩き疲れていたし、少し喉が渇いていた。それだけのことだ。

店内は薄暗くて、古いジャズが小さな音量で流れていた。カウンターに座って、アイスコーヒーを注文した。隣の席には誰もいない。窓の外では、六月の雨が静かに降り続けている。

コーヒーが届くまでの間、僕は何をするでもなくぼんやりと外を眺めていた。向かいのビルの三階に明かりがついている。誰かが残業をしているのだろう。あるいは、僕と同じように、特に行く場所がないだけかもしれない。

「お待たせしました」

ウェイトレスがコーヒーを置いた。グラスの表面に水滴がついている。僕は一口飲んだ。悪くない。少なくとも、さっきまで歩いていた蒸し暑い通りよりは、ここのほうがずっとましだ。

ポケットから携帯を出して、画面を確認した。メッセージが一件。彼女からだった。「今日は遅くなるから、先にご飯食べてて」。短い文面を二回読んで、携帯をテーブルに置いた。

別に寂しいわけではない。ただ、こういう夜に一人でカフェにいると、なんとなく世界から取り残されたような気分になる。それは僕だけの感覚ではないと思う。たぶん。

雨はまだやみそうにない。僕はもう一口コーヒーを飲んで、窓の外を見た。

---

**Translation:**

It wasn't that I had any particular reason for entering that cafe. I was just tired from walking, and a little thirsty. That's all.

Inside the shop, it was dimly lit, and old jazz was playing at a low volume. I sat at the counter and ordered an iced coffee. No one was in the seat next to me. Outside the window, the June rain continued to fall quietly.

While waiting for the coffee to arrive, I was gazing absentmindedly outside without doing anything in particular. A light was on on the third floor of the building across the street. Someone was probably working overtime. Or perhaps, like me, they simply had nowhere particular to go.

"Sorry to keep you waiting."

The waitress set down the coffee. Water droplets clung to the surface of the glass. I took a sip. Not bad. At the very least, this place was much better than the humid street I had been walking along until a moment ago.

I took out my phone from my pocket and checked the screen. One message. It was from her. "I'll be late tonight, so go ahead and eat without me." I read the short message twice and placed the phone on the table.

It wasn't that I was lonely. It's just that when you're alone in a cafe on a night like this, you get this feeling of being left behind by the world. I don't think that feeling belongs to me alone. Probably.

The rain didn't seem like it would stop anytime soon. I took another sip of coffee and looked out the window.
