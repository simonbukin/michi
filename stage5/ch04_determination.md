# Chapter 4 — Determination, Inevitability, and Exclusivity

This chapter presents six expressions that convey determination in the face of difficulty, the inevitability of outcomes, and the exclusive or unique nature of something. These patterns are essential for advanced reading comprehension — they appear regularly in essays, editorials, literary prose, and formal speeches.

The first three patterns deal with what must happen, what cannot happen, and what is overcome despite opposition. The final three patterns identify something or someone as uniquely suited, irreplaceable, or so obvious that no further action is required. Together, they give the advanced learner powerful tools for expressing conviction and making emphatic judgments.

All patterns in this chapter belong to formal or written registers unless otherwise noted.

---

## 4.1 ～べくして — "Destined To"

べくして expresses inevitability — that something happened because it was destined or meant to happen. It conveys that the outcome was the natural, expected result of preceding circumstances. The pattern uses the classical auxiliary べし (expressing obligation or destiny) in its conjunctive form べく, plus して.

### Formation

| Pattern |
|---|
| Verb (dictionary form) + べくして + same verb (past tense) |

Note: This pattern almost always repeats the same verb: 起こるべくして起こった, なるべくしてなった, etc. The repetition emphasizes that the outcome was inevitable.

### Examples

> この事故は　起こるべくして　起こった。
> "This accident was destined to happen." (lit. "happened as it was meant to happen")

> 彼らは　出会うべくして　出会った　二人だ。
> "They are two people who were destined to meet."

> このチームは　勝つべくして　勝った。
> "This team won as they were meant to win."

> 彼は　成功すべくして　成功した　人物だ。
> "He is a person who succeeded because he was destined to succeed."

> この会社は　成長すべくして　成長してきた。
> "This company has grown as it was meant to grow."

### Usage note

べくして always implies that the result was not accidental — it was the logical, inevitable consequence of preparation, character, or circumstances. When used about accidents or failures, it suggests negligence or structural problems made the outcome unavoidable. When used about success, it suggests thorough preparation or natural talent.

---

## 4.2 ～べくもない — "Impossible To"

べくもない expresses that something is completely impossible — there is no way to do it. Like べくして, it uses the classical べく, but combined with もない to create a strong negation: "there is no possibility of doing."

### Formation

| Pattern |
|---|
| Verb (dictionary form) + べくもない |

### Examples

> 当時の　苦しみは　想像する　べくもない。
> "The suffering of that time is impossible to imagine."

> 彼の　才能は　凡人には　及ぶ　べくもない。
> "His talent is impossible for an ordinary person to match."

> あの頃の　輝きを　取り戻す　べくもない。
> "There is no way to recapture the brilliance of those days."

> 二十年前の　技術で、この問題を　解決する　べくもなかった。
> "With the technology of twenty years ago, it was impossible to solve this problem."

> その衝撃は　言葉で　表す　べくもない。
> "That shock is impossible to express in words."

### べくもない vs できない

| Expression | Nuance |
|---|---|
| べくもない | Emphatic impossibility — literary, implies it is utterly beyond reach |
| できない | Simple impossibility — neutral, everyday |

べくもない conveys a sense of awe or resignation before something that is fundamentally beyond one's capacity. It is much stronger and more literary than simple できない.

---

## 4.3 ～をものともせず(に) — "Undaunted By"

をものともせず(に) means "undaunted by" or "not deterred by." It expresses that someone faces a significant obstacle or difficulty but presses forward without being affected by it. ものともする means "to regard as something (worth worrying about)," so ものともせず means "without regarding it as anything."

### Formation

| Pattern |
|---|
| Noun + をものともせず(に) |

Note: The noun is always something negative or difficult — an obstacle, hardship, criticism, injury, etc.

### Examples

> 彼は　困難を　ものともせずに　前進し続けた。
> "He continued to advance, undaunted by difficulties."

> 選手は　怪我を　ものともせず、最後まで　戦い抜いた。
> "The athlete fought to the end, undaunted by injury."

> 批判を　ものともせずに、自分の　信念を　貫いた。
> "Undaunted by criticism, she held firm to her beliefs."

> 悪天候を　ものともせず、登山隊は　頂上を　目指した。
> "Undaunted by bad weather, the climbing party aimed for the summit."

> 周囲の　反対を　ものともせずに、彼女は　起業した。
> "Undaunted by the opposition of those around her, she started a business."

### Usage note

をものともせず always carries a tone of admiration or respect for the person who overcomes the obstacle. It is used to describe courageous, determined, or resilient behavior. The obstacle must be genuinely significant — you would not use this pattern for trivial inconveniences.

---

## 4.4 ～をおいて(他に)～ない — "No One / Nothing Other Than"

をおいて(他に)ない means "there is no one (or nothing) other than." It expresses that the stated person or thing is the only one suitable or qualified. The verb 置く (おく) here means "to set aside," so the literal sense is "if you set this aside, there is nothing else."

### Formation

| Pattern |
|---|
| Noun + をおいて(他に) + ない / いない |

### Examples

> この仕事を　任せられるのは、彼を　おいて　他にいない。
> "There is no one other than him to whom this job can be entrusted."

> この問題を　解決できるのは、あなたを　おいて　いない。
> "There is no one other than you who can solve this problem."

> 日本の　首都は、東京を　おいて　他にない。
> "There is no capital of Japan other than Tokyo."

> この役を　演じられるのは、彼女を　おいて　他にいないだろう。
> "There is probably no one other than her who can play this role."

> チームを　まとめられる　人材は、田中さんを　おいて　いない。
> "There is no talent who can bring the team together other than Mr. Tanaka."

### Usage note

をおいて他にない is always emphatic and exclusive. It declares that the named person or thing is irreplaceable and uniquely qualified. It is often used in recommendations, nominations, and decisive statements. The pattern is formal but can also appear in passionate spoken endorsements.

---

## 4.5 ～ならでは(の) — "Unique To"

ならでは(の) means "unique to" or "possible only with." It expresses that a quality, experience, or characteristic is exclusive to a particular person, place, or thing and cannot be found elsewhere. The expression comes from classical grammar: なら (conditional of だ) + では (negative conditional) = "if it were not."

### Formation

| Pattern |
|---|
| Noun + ならでは(の) + Noun |
| Noun + ならではだ |

### Examples

> これは　京都　ならではの　風景だ。
> "This is a landscape unique to Kyoto."

> 手作り　ならではの　温かみが　感じられる。
> "You can feel a warmth that is unique to handmade things."

> ベテラン　ならではの　技術が　光る　作品だ。
> "This is a work where the skill unique to a veteran shines."

> この味は　老舗　ならではだ。
> "This flavor is unique to a long-established shop."

> 地元の　人　ならではの　情報を　教えてもらった。
> "I was told information that only a local person would know."

### Usage note

ならでは always carries a positive connotation. It praises the unique, irreplaceable quality of something. You would not use it for negative uniqueness. It is versatile in register — it appears in both formal writing and conversational Japanese, making it one of the more accessible patterns in this chapter.

---

## 4.6 ～までもない / ～までもなく — "No Need To"

までもない means "there is no need to" — the action is unnecessary because the matter is already obvious or settled. までもなく is the conjunctive form, used mid-sentence to connect to a following clause.

### Formation

| Pattern |
|---|
| Verb (dictionary form) + までもない |
| Verb (dictionary form) + までもなく |

### Examples

> 言う　までもなく、健康は　最も　大切だ。
> "Needless to say, health is the most important thing."

> わざわざ　説明する　までもない　ことだ。
> "It is something that does not need to be explained."

> 確認する　までもなく、結果は　明らかだ。
> "Without even needing to confirm, the result is obvious."

> 彼の　実力は、今さら　証明する　までもない。
> "His ability does not need to be proven at this point."

> 議論する　までもなく、全員が　賛成した。
> "Without even needing to discuss it, everyone agreed."

### までもない vs までもなく

| Expression | Function | Position |
|---|---|---|
| までもない | Sentence-ending (predicate) | 説明するまでもない。 |
| までもなく | Mid-sentence (conjunctive) | 説明するまでもなく、分かる。 |

The fixed expression 言うまでもない / 言うまでもなく ("needless to say") is extremely common and should be memorized as a set phrase.

## 4.7 Reading Passage

次の文章は、ある登山家の伝記から抜粋したものです。

---

山岳写真家・高橋健一。彼の人生は、まさに困難をものともせずに歩み続けた道のりだった。

高橋は幼少期から体が弱く、医者からは激しい運動を禁じられていた。しかし、十五歳の時に父に連れられて登った富士山が、彼の運命を変えた。山頂から見た日の出の美しさは、言葉で表すべくもないものだった。その瞬間、彼は山と写真に人生を捧げることを決意した。

周囲の反対をものともせずに、高橋は山岳写真の道に進んだ。当時、山岳写真で生計を立てることは想像するべくもないほど困難だった。しかし、彼は諦めなかった。悪天候の中でも一瞬たりともシャッターチャンスを逃すまいと、何時間も山中で待ち続けた。

高橋の写真には、山を知り尽くした者ならではの視点がある。観光客が撮る写真とは根本的に異なる。何十年もの経験なくしては得られない構図、光の捉え方。それは努力の結果として起こるべくして起こった到達点である。

「山の本当の美しさを伝えられる写真家は、高橋さんをおいて他にいない」と、ある評論家は語る。言うまでもなく、高橋自身はそのような評価を気にしていない。彼にとって大切なのは、山と向き合う時間そのものだ。

完璧な一枚を撮るために、高橋は今日も山に入る。七十歳を過ぎてなお、年齢をものともせず、カメラを担いで険しい山道を登り続けている。彼の姿は、何かに人生を捧げるとはどういうことかを、言葉を用いるまでもなく、私たちに教えてくれる。

---

**Translation:**

The following is an excerpt from the biography of a mountaineer.

---

Mountain photographer Kenichi Takahashi. His life was truly a journey of continuing to walk forward, undaunted by difficulties.

Takahashi had a weak body from childhood and was forbidden by doctors from engaging in strenuous exercise. However, when he was fifteen, climbing Mount Fuji with his father changed his destiny. The beauty of the sunrise seen from the summit was something impossible to express in words. In that moment, he resolved to devote his life to mountains and photography.

Undaunted by the opposition of those around him, Takahashi pursued the path of mountain photography. At the time, making a living from mountain photography was unimaginably difficult. But he did not give up. Determined not to miss a shutter opportunity for even a single moment, he would wait for hours in the mountains even in bad weather.

Takahashi's photographs have a perspective unique to someone who knows mountains inside and out. They are fundamentally different from photographs taken by tourists. The composition and the way of capturing light that cannot be obtained without decades of experience. That is a destination that was reached as it was destined to be reached, as a result of effort.

"There is no photographer other than Mr. Takahashi who can convey the true beauty of mountains," says one critic. Needless to say, Takahashi himself does not concern himself with such evaluations. What matters to him is the time spent facing the mountains.

In pursuit of the perfect shot, Takahashi heads into the mountains again today. Even past the age of seventy, undaunted by age, he continues to climb steep mountain paths carrying his camera. His figure teaches us, without the need for words, what it means to devote one's life to something.
