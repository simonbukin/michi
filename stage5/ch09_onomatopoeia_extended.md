# Chapter 9 — Extended Onomatopoeia

Japanese onomatopoeia — 擬音語 (giongo, sound-mimicking words) and 擬態語 (gitaigo, state-mimicking words) — form one of the language's most distinctive features. Stage 4 introduced the foundational system and several dozen common items. This chapter expands your repertoire with over fifty additional expressions that appear regularly in N1-level texts, literature, and daily conversation. These are words that native speakers use constantly but that textbooks often treat as an afterthought.

The expressions in this chapter go beyond simple sound effects. Many describe subtle emotional states, social dynamics, physical sensations, and textures with a precision that ordinary adjectives and adverbs cannot match. The difference between じわじわ and すんなり, between むっとする and つんとする, between もちもち and ねばねば — these distinctions are the texture of fluent Japanese.

Each entry below includes the word, its grammatical behavior (whether it takes と, する, な, or some combination), its meaning, and example sentences showing natural usage.

---

## 9.1 Grammatical Patterns — Quick Review

Before diving in, recall the three main grammatical patterns for onomatopoeia:

| Pattern | Function | Example |
|---|---|---|
| ～と + verb | Adverbial (describes how an action is done) | じっと見る |
| ～する | Verbal (the state or action itself) | どきどきする |
| ～な/の + noun | Adjectival (modifies a noun) | ふわふわな布団 |

Some words use all three patterns; others are restricted. The entries below note which patterns each word takes.

## 9.7 Reading Passage

次の文章は、ある料理ブロガーが書いた記事です。

---

先日、友人に誘われて人気のパン屋に行ってきた。開店前から行列ができていて、そわそわしながら待つこと三十分。ようやく店に入ると、焼きたてのパンの香りがふわっと広がった。

一番人気はもちもちの食パン。一口食べてみると、外はさくさく、中はしっとりとして、思わずじーんときた。こんなパンが近所にあったとは——まさに灯台下暗し。

友人はクロワッサンを選んだが、ぱさぱさしているかと思いきや、中はとろとろのバターが層になっていて絶品だった。二人でにやにやしながら食べ比べた。

帰り道、友人がしみじみと言った。「毎日こつこつ焼いている職人さんって、すごいよね。」本当にその通りだ。じわじわと人気が広がっているのも納得できる。

ただ、食べすぎてお腹がいっぱいになり、午後はぐったり。夕食はあっさりとしたおかゆにした。

---

**Translation:**

The other day, a friend invited me to a popular bakery. A line had already formed before opening, and I waited for thirty minutes, fidgeting restlessly. When we finally entered, the scent of freshly baked bread wafted softly through the air.

The most popular item was the chewy shokupan. When I took a bite, the outside was crispy, the inside was moist, and I was involuntarily moved. To think that a bread like this was in my neighborhood — truly, it's darkest under the lighthouse.

My friend chose a croissant, and though I expected it might be dry, inside it had layers of melting butter and was exquisite. We ate and compared, grinning at each other.

On the way home, my friend said with deep feeling, "The craftspeople who bake steadily every day are really amazing, aren't they?" Indeed, that is exactly right. It makes sense that their popularity is gradually spreading.

However, having eaten too much, my stomach was full and I was exhausted all afternoon. For dinner, I had a simple, light rice porridge.
