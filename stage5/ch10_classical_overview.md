# Chapter 10 — Classical Japanese: What You Need to Know

Classical Japanese is not a foreign language. It is an earlier form of the same language you have been studying, and traces of it survive everywhere in modern usage. Legal documents, the preamble to the Japanese Constitution, proverbs, formal set phrases, poetry, and literary allusion all draw on classical grammar and vocabulary. At the N1 level and beyond, you will encounter classical forms not as museum pieces but as living fragments embedded in contemporary text.

This chapter does not aim to make you a reader of 源氏物語 in the original. Its goal is more practical: to give you enough understanding of classical Japanese that you can recognize its forms when they appear in modern contexts, parse set phrases that preserve classical grammar, and appreciate the historical depth beneath the modern language you already know.

---

## 10.1 Why Classical Japanese Matters for Modern Reading

Classical Japanese (文語, ぶんご) is not confined to literature courses. It appears in contexts that every advanced reader will encounter.

### The Constitution

The preamble to the Japanese Constitution (日本国憲法前文) uses grammatical structures inherited from classical Japanese. Legal and governmental language in general preserves older forms.

> 日本国民は、正当に選挙された国会における代表者を通じて行動し、われらとわれらの子孫のために、諸国民との協和による成果と、わが国全土にわたって自由のもたらす恵沢を確保し...
> "We, the Japanese people, acting through our duly elected representatives in the National Diet, determined that we shall secure for ourselves and our posterity the fruits of peaceful cooperation with all nations and the blessings of liberty throughout this land..."

Notice わが (classical form of 我が, "our"), もたらす used in a relative clause modifying 恵沢, and the overall sentence architecture with multiple clauses suspended before a final verb — a hallmark of classical-influenced legal prose.

### Legal language

Contracts and laws use classical negative forms (ざる, ず), classical conditionals (ば in its older sense), and set endings like ものとする and べからず.

> 何人も、法律の定める手続によらなければ、その生命若しくは自由を奪われ、又はその他の刑罰を科せられない。
> "No person shall be deprived of life or liberty, nor shall any other criminal penalty be imposed, except according to procedure established by law."

### Proverbs and set phrases

Everyday proverbs preserve classical grammar in fossilized form:

> 聞くは一時の恥、聞かぬは一生の恥。
> "Asking is a moment's shame; not asking is a lifetime's shame."

Here 聞かぬ uses the classical negative ぬ (= modern ない). The proverb would sound wrong in fully modern grammar.

### Literary allusion

Educated writing frequently alludes to classical texts. Newspaper editorials quote from the 万葉集, politicians cite Confucian classics, and novelists echo the cadences of Heian prose. Without any classical awareness, these references pass unnoticed.

### Formal set phrases

Expressions used in ceremonies, announcements, and formal correspondence retain classical elements:

> 本日はお日柄もよく... (formal wedding opening)
> 恐れながら申し上げます (humble address, preserving ながら in its classical concessive sense)

## 10.2 Classical vs Modern: Overview of Key Differences

The shift from classical to modern Japanese was gradual, spanning centuries, but several systematic changes can be identified. Understanding these broad patterns makes individual classical forms easier to recognize.

### Verb classes

Classical Japanese had more verb conjugation classes than modern Japanese. The most important mapping:

| Classical | Modern | Notes |
|-----------|--------|-------|
| 四段活用 (よだんかつよう) | 五段活用 | Added one row; vowel patterns shifted |
| 上二段活用 (かみにだんかつよう) | 上一段活用 | Collapsed into single-vowel pattern |
| 下二段活用 (しもにだんかつよう) | 下一段活用 | Collapsed into single-vowel pattern |
| 上一段活用 | 上一段活用 | Small class; survived intact |
| 下一段活用 | 下一段活用 | Very small class; survived intact |
| カ行変格 (カへん) | カ行変格 | 来る — irregular in both systems |
| サ行変格 (サへん) | サ行変格 | する — irregular in both systems |
| ナ行変格 (ナへん) | (absorbed) | 死ぬ, 去ぬ — merged into 五段 |
| ラ行変格 (ラへん) | (absorbed) | あり, をり, はべり, いまそかり |

### Auxiliary verbs

Classical Japanese expressed negation, tense, aspect, mood, and evidentiality through a rich system of auxiliary verbs (助動詞) that attached to verb stems. Many of these have no direct modern equivalent.

| Classical auxiliary | Function | Modern trace |
|--------------------|----------|-------------|
| ず | Negation | ～ず in set phrases (絶えず, やむを得ず) |
| き, けり | Past tense | ～けり in poetry and literary endings |
| たり | Perfective | ～たる in 堂々たる, 悠々たる |
| べし | Obligation, conjecture | ～べき, ～べし in formal writing |
| まじ | Negative of べし | あるまじき in set phrases |
| なり | Copula / hearsay | ～なり in formal assertions |
| む | Volitional, conjecture | Evolved into modern ～う/～よう |

### Particles

Several classical particles survive in modern Japanese but with restricted or shifted usage:

| Particle | Classical function | Modern trace |
|----------|-------------------|-------------|
| ぞ | Emphasis (triggers 連体形) | ぞ in emphatic speech (これこそ問題だ) |
| なむ | Emphasis (triggers 連体形) | Mostly extinct; rare literary use |
| や | Question (triggers 連体形) | や in rhetorical questions |
| か | Question (triggers 連体形) | か as question marker (function preserved) |
| こそ | Strong emphasis (triggers 已然形) | こそ still used for emphasis |
| は | Topic (same) | Same function preserved |

## 10.3 The Classical Copula なり and Its Modern Descendants

The classical copula system differed significantly from the modern だ/です/である system. Understanding its evolution clarifies many forms you encounter in formal and literary Japanese.

### Classical なり

In classical Japanese, なり served as the primary copula (connecting subject to predicate noun or adjective). It conjugated as follows:

### Formation

| 活用形 | なり |
|--------|------|
| 未然形 | なら |
| 連用形 | なり / に |
| 終止形 | なり |
| 連体形 | なる |
| 已然形 | なれ |
| 命令形 | なれ |

### Examples of classical なり

> 春なり。
> "It is spring."

> これは夢なるか。
> "Is this a dream?" (なる = 連体形, triggered by か)

### Modern descendants

The classical copula なり did not simply become だ. It branched into several modern forms:

| Modern form | Origin | Register |
|-------------|--------|----------|
| だ | Contraction of である (で + ある) | Colloquial |
| です | Politeness layer added to だ | Polite |
| である | で (連用形 of classical にてあり) + ある | Written, formal |
| なり | Direct survival | Very formal, literary |
| なる | Attributive form survival | Set phrases: 多大なる, 偉大なる |

### なり and なる in modern set phrases

> 我が社の多大なる貢献
> "Our company's great contribution"

> 事実なり。
> "It is a fact." (Formal declaration, legal register)

> 以上のとおりに相違なきことを誓います。
> "I swear that the above is without discrepancy." (なき = classical negative attributive of なし)

### Two kinds of なり in classical Japanese

Be aware that classical Japanese has two homophonous なり:

| なり | Meaning | Attached to |
|------|---------|------------|
| 断定のなり | Copula ("is") | Nouns, 連体形 |
| 伝聞推定のなり | Hearsay ("I hear that...") | 終止形 |

> 男なり。 → "He is a man." (copula)
> 男来るなり。 → "I hear that a man is coming." (hearsay)

You do not need to produce these distinctions, but recognizing that なり can mean "I hear that..." explains certain literary passages.

## 10.4 Classical Particles: ぞ, なむ, や, か — 係り結び

The most distinctive feature of classical Japanese grammar is 係り結び (かかりむすび), a system where certain emphatic or interrogative particles in the middle of a sentence force the final verb into a specific conjugation form. This system broke down during the medieval period, but its traces survive in modern Japanese.

### What is 係り結び?

In modern Japanese, a sentence typically ends in 終止形 (terminal form):

> 花が咲く。 "Flowers bloom."

In classical Japanese, if an emphatic particle appeared in the sentence, the final verb shifted to 連体形 (attributive form) or 已然形 (realis form) instead:

### Formation

| 係助詞 | Meaning | Final verb form |
|--------|---------|----------------|
| ぞ | Emphasis | 連体形 |
| なむ | Emphasis | 連体形 |
| や | Question | 連体形 |
| か | Question | 連体形 |
| こそ | Strong emphasis | 已然形 |

### Examples

> 花ぞ咲ける。
> "The flowers ARE blooming." (ぞ emphasizes; 咲ける is 已然形 of 咲く... but note: ぞ normally triggers 連体形. In this example, 咲ける is the 連体形 of the auxiliary けり → 咲きけり → 咲ける)

A clearer example:

> 我こそ知れ。
> "It is I who knows." (こそ triggers 已然形 知れ)

> いづれの御時にか、女御、更衣あまた候ひ給ひける。(源氏物語冒頭)
> "In which emperor's reign was it — many imperial consorts and attendants served [at court]."

Here か triggers 連体形 on the final ける (連体形 of けり).

### Why you need to recognize this

You will not use 係り結び in your own writing. But you need to recognize it for three reasons:

**1. Literary quotation and allusion**

Educated Japanese writing quotes classical texts. If you see ぞ followed by a verb that does not look like 終止形, 係り結び is the explanation.

**2. Modern こそ**

Modern Japanese preserves one piece of 係り結び: the pattern ～こそ...が / ～こそ...け(れ)ど. In modern grammar, こそ is followed by a concessive clause:

> 彼こそが適任だと思う。
> "I think HE is the right person."

The emphasis function of こそ is directly inherited from classical usage.

**3. Fossilized phrases**

> いかがなものか。 "How about that?" / "Is that really acceptable?"
> なんぞ。 "What?" (classical ぞ as emphasis)

### Modern traces of classical particles

| Expression | Classical origin | Modern meaning |
|------------|-----------------|----------------|
| まさか | まさ + か (interrogative) | "No way, surely not" |
| いかが | いかが (interrogative) | "How about...?" |
| なぜ | 何ぞ + え → なぜ | "Why?" |
| だからこそ | こそ emphasis | "Precisely because..." |
| ～てこそ | こそ emphasis | "Only by doing ~" |

## 10.5 Reading Passage — Classical Forms in Modern Contexts

次の文章は、現代文の中に現れる文語的表現を集めたものである。

---

日本国憲法の前文には、「われらは、平和を維持し、専制と隷従、圧迫と偏狭を地上から永遠に除去しようと努めている国際社会において、名誉ある地位を占めたいと思う」とある。この一文を読むだけでも、現代の日本語話者が文語と無縁ではいられないことがわかるだろう。

日常においても、文語の痕跡は至るところに見られる。「やむを得ず」と言うとき、私たちは古典の否定助動詞「ず」を使っている。「知るべし」と書くとき、古典の推量助動詞「べし」を使っている。「堂々たる態度」の「たる」は、完了の助動詞「たり」の連体形である。

こうした表現は、化石のように固まったまま現代語に残っているのではない。むしろ、格調高い文章を書こうとするとき、あるいは力強い主張をしようとするとき、書き手は意識的に文語的表現を選ぶのである。古典文法は死んだのではなく、特別な場面で生き続けているのだ。

ことわざもまた、古典文法の宝庫である。「急がば回れ」の「ば」は已然形接続の古典的条件表現であり、現代語の「急ぐなら」とは微妙に響きが異なる。「聞くは一時の恥、聞かぬは一生の恥」の「ぬ」は、打消の助動詞「ず」の連体形である。これらの表現を「古い」と片付けてしまえば、日本語の深い層を見逃すことになる。

---

**Translation:**

The preamble to the Japanese Constitution states, "We desire to occupy an honored place in an international society striving for the preservation of peace, and the banishment of tyranny and slavery, oppression and intolerance for all time from the earth." Reading even this single sentence, one can understand that modern Japanese speakers cannot remain unrelated to classical language.

In daily life as well, traces of classical language can be seen everywhere. When we say やむを得ず ("unavoidably"), we are using the classical negative auxiliary ず. When we write 知るべし ("one should know"), we are using the classical auxiliary of conjecture べし. The たる in 堂々たる態度 ("a dignified bearing") is the attributive form of the perfective auxiliary たり.

These expressions are not fossilized remains frozen in the modern language. Rather, when one seeks to write with dignity, or when one seeks to make a powerful assertion, the writer consciously chooses classical expressions. Classical grammar has not died — it continues to live in special contexts.

Proverbs, too, are a treasure house of classical grammar. The ば in 急がば回れ ("if you hurry, take the long way around") is a classical conditional using the realis form, and its resonance differs subtly from the modern 急ぐなら. The ぬ in 聞くは一時の恥、聞かぬは一生の恥 is the attributive form of the negative auxiliary ず. If we dismiss these expressions as simply "old," we will miss the deep layers of the Japanese language.
