# Chapter 11 — Classical Verb System (For Recognition)

The classical Japanese verb system is more complex than the modern one, with more conjugation classes and a richer set of auxiliary verbs. You do not need to memorize these conjugation tables for production. The goal of this chapter is recognition: when you encounter a classical verb form in a proverb, a legal phrase, a literary allusion, or a formal expression, you should be able to identify what it is and understand its function.

The first half of this chapter maps classical verb classes onto their modern descendants, showing you the historical logic behind forms that might otherwise seem arbitrary. The second half focuses on classical auxiliary verbs that survive in modern set phrases — forms like ず, べし, たり, and けり that you will encounter regularly in advanced reading.

---

## 11.1 四段活用 — Precursor to Modern 五段

The largest class of classical verbs was 四段活用 (よだんかつよう, "four-level conjugation"). These verbs used four vowels across their conjugation — a, i, u, e — compared to the five vowels (a, i, u, e, o) of modern 五段活用.

### Formation

Using 書く as an example:

| 活用形 | 四段 (classical) | 五段 (modern) |
|--------|-----------------|--------------|
| 未然形 | 書か (a) | 書か (a) |
| 連用形 | 書き (i) | 書き (i) |
| 終止形 | 書く (u) | 書く (u) |
| 連体形 | 書く (u) | 書く (u) |
| 已然形 | 書け (e) | — |
| 仮定形 | — | 書け (e) |
| 命令形 | 書け (e) | 書け (e) |
| 未然形② | — | 書こ (o) |

The key difference: classical 四段 did not have the お-row (未然形 + う/よう for volitional). Modern 五段 added this row, giving the "five levels."

### The vowel shift

Classical 已然形 and modern 仮定形 use the same form (書け) but have different grammatical functions:

> Classical: 書けば = "since/because one writes" (已然形 + ば = established fact)
> Modern: 書けば = "if one writes" (仮定形 + ば = conditional)

This shift is important: in classical Japanese, 已然形 + ば expressed a reason or established condition ("since..."), while 未然形 + ば expressed a hypothetical ("if..."). Modern Japanese merged both into the conditional 仮定形 + ば.

### Examples of classical 四段 in proverbs

> 急がば回れ。
> "If you are in a hurry, take the roundabout way."

急が is the 未然形 of 急ぐ (四段). In classical grammar, 未然形 + ば = hypothetical condition ("if one hurries..."). This is different from the modern pattern where ば attaches to 仮定形.

> 聞くは一時の恥。
> "Asking is a moment's shame."

聞く is the 連体形 of 聞く (四段), functioning as a noun ("the act of asking"). This nominalizing use of 連体形 survives in modern Japanese.

## 11.2 上二段 and 下二段 — Precursors That Collapsed into Modern 一段

Two major classical verb classes — 上二段活用 (かみにだんかつよう) and 下二段活用 (しもにだんかつよう) — each used two vowels in their conjugation. Over time, both simplified into the single-vowel patterns of modern 上一段 and 下一段.

### 上二段活用

"Upper two-level" verbs alternated between the い-row and う-row.

Using 起く (おく, "to get up"; modern 起きる) as an example:

| 活用形 | 上二段 | Modern 上一段 |
|--------|-------|-------------|
| 未然形 | 起き (i) | 起き (i) |
| 連用形 | 起き (i) | 起き (i) |
| 終止形 | 起く (u) | 起きる (iru) |
| 連体形 | 起くる (uru) | 起きる (iru) |
| 已然形 | 起くれ (ure) | — |
| 命令形 | 起きよ (iyo) | 起きろ / 起きよ |

The critical difference: classical 上二段 終止形 ended in -u (起く), while the 連体形 ended in -uru (起くる). When the 終止形/連体形 distinction collapsed in late medieval Japanese, the 連体形 won — and then simplified to the modern -iru pattern.

### 下二段活用

"Lower two-level" verbs alternated between the え-row and う-row.

Using 受く (うく, "to receive"; modern 受ける) as an example:

| 活用形 | 下二段 | Modern 下一段 |
|--------|-------|-------------|
| 未然形 | 受け (e) | 受け (e) |
| 連用形 | 受け (e) | 受け (e) |
| 終止形 | 受く (u) | 受ける (eru) |
| 連体形 | 受くる (uru) | 受ける (eru) |
| 已然形 | 受くれ (ure) | — |
| 命令形 | 受けよ (eyo) | 受けろ / 受けよ |

The same pattern: 連体形 replaced 終止形, then simplified to -eru.

### Why this matters

When you see a verb ending in -u in a classical text where you expect a modern -iru or -eru verb, you are looking at a 二段 verb in its 終止形. For instance:

> 落つ (おつ, "to fall") → modern 落ちる
> 見ゆ (みゆ, "to be visible") → modern 見える
> 聞こゆ (きこゆ, "to be audible") → modern 聞こえる

## 11.3 上一段, 下一段, カ変, サ変, ナ変, ラ変 — Brief Overview

Several smaller verb classes existed in classical Japanese. Here is a brief overview for recognition purposes.

### 上一段活用

A very small class that conjugated using only the い-row, just like modern 上一段. The most important members:

- 見る (みる, "to see")
- 着る (きる, "to wear")
- 似る (にる, "to resemble")
- 射る (いる, "to shoot")
- 居る (ゐる, "to be/sit")

These verbs survived essentially unchanged into modern Japanese.

### 下一段活用

In classical Japanese, this class had only one member:

- 蹴る (ける, "to kick")

It conjugated with え-row throughout: 蹴 (け), 蹴 (け), 蹴る (ける), 蹴る (ける), 蹴れ (けれ), 蹴よ (けよ). In modern Japanese, 蹴る has been absorbed into 五段 conjugation.

### カ行変格活用 (カ変)

The verb 来 (く, "to come") was irregular in classical Japanese, just as 来る is irregular in modern Japanese.

| 活用形 | Classical | Modern |
|--------|-----------|--------|
| 未然形 | こ | こ |
| 連用形 | き | き |
| 終止形 | く | くる |
| 連体形 | くる | くる |
| 已然形 | くれ | くれ |
| 命令形 | こ / こよ | こい |

### サ行変格活用 (サ変)

The verb す ("to do"; modern する) was irregular:

| 活用形 | Classical | Modern |
|--------|-----------|--------|
| 未然形 | せ | し / せ / さ |
| 連用形 | し | し |
| 終止形 | す | する |
| 連体形 | する | する |
| 已然形 | すれ | すれ |
| 命令形 | せよ | しろ / せよ |

The modern formal command form せよ is directly inherited from classical Japanese.

### ナ行変格活用 (ナ変)

Two verbs: 死ぬ (しぬ, "to die") and 去ぬ/往ぬ (いぬ, "to go away"). These conjugated irregularly in classical Japanese but have been absorbed into modern 五段 (死ぬ) or become archaic (去ぬ).

### ラ行変格活用 (ラ変)

Four important verbs: あり ("to be"), をり ("to be/sit"), はべり ("to serve/be [humble]"), いまそかり ("to be [honorific]"). Their key peculiarity: the 終止形 ended in -り instead of -る.

> あり → 終止形: あり (not ある)

This is why the classical copula なり ends in -り: it derives from に + あり. Modern ある regularized the 終止形 to match the 連体形.

## 11.4 Key Classical Auxiliary Verbs in Modern Set Phrases

This section is the most practically important part of the chapter. These auxiliary verbs appear constantly in modern Japanese within set phrases, formal writing, and literary contexts.

### ず — Negative

The classical negative auxiliary ず attached to the 未然形 of verbs. Its conjugation:

### Formation

| 活用形 | ず |
|--------|---|
| 未然形 | ず |
| 連用形 | ず |
| 終止形 | ず |
| 連体形 | ぬ / ざる |
| 已然形 | ね / ざれ |

The 連体形 ざる is extremely common in modern formal Japanese: ～ざるを得ない ("cannot help but...").

### Examples in modern usage

> やむを得ず、計画を変更した。
> "Unavoidably, we changed the plan."

> 知らず知らずのうちに、彼は涙を流していた。
> "Without realizing it, he was shedding tears."

> 絶えず努力を続けることが大切だ。
> "It is important to keep making efforts without cease."

> 我々は、この問題を看過せざるを得ない状況に置かれている。
> "We are placed in a situation where we cannot help but overlook this problem."

> 予期せぬ事態が発生した。
> "An unexpected situation has occurred." (せぬ = ぬ is 連体形 of ず)

### たり — Perfective

The classical perfective たり indicated a completed action or a resulting state. Its 連体形 たる survives prominently in modern Japanese as a way to create dignified adjective-like expressions.

### Formation (of surviving forms)

| Form | Usage | Example |
|------|-------|---------|
| たる (連体形) | Modifies nouns | 堂々たる態度 |
| たり (終止形) | Ends sentences (rare, literary) | 堂々たり |
| たれ (已然形) | Classical only | — |

### Examples in modern usage

> 堂々たる態度で演説した。
> "He gave a speech with a dignified bearing."

> 泰然たる表情を崩さなかった。
> "He did not break his composed expression."

> 悠々たる流れの大河。
> "A great river with a leisurely flow."

> 彼は厳然たる事実を突きつけた。
> "He confronted them with a stark fact."

The pattern is: 漢語 + たる + noun. This creates a formal, elevated tone that 漢語 + な cannot achieve.

### べし — Obligation / Conjecture

The classical auxiliary べし expressed obligation ("should"), strong conjecture ("must be"), or fitness ("it is fitting to"). Its modern descendants — べき (連体形), べく (連用形), べからず (べし + ず) — are extremely common in formal Japanese.

### Formation (of surviving forms)

| Form | Function | Example |
|------|----------|---------|
| べき | Modifies nouns ("should") | すべきこと |
| べく | Adverbial ("in order to") | 知るべく |
| べし | Sentence-final (literary) | 知るべし |
| べからず | Prohibition ("must not") | 入るべからず |

### Examples in modern usage

> これは知るべき事実である。
> "This is a fact one should know."

> あるべき姿に戻さなければならない。
> "We must restore it to the form it should have."

> なすべきことをなせ。
> "Do what ought to be done."

> 芝生に入るべからず。
> "Keep off the grass." (Literally: "One must not enter the lawn.")

> 問題を解決すべく、会議を開いた。
> "We held a meeting in order to resolve the problem."

### まじ — Negative of べし

まじ was the negative counterpart of べし: "should not," "must not," "probably not." Its 連体形 まじき survives in a common set phrase.

### Examples in modern usage

> あるまじき行為として非難された。
> "It was condemned as behavior that should not exist."

> 教師にあるまじき発言だ。
> "It is a remark unbecoming of a teacher."

> 許すまじき犯罪。
> "An unforgivable crime."

### なり — Assertion / Hearsay

As discussed in Chapter 10, the copula なり survives in formal assertions. The hearsay なり (attached to 終止形) is rarer but appears in literary contexts.

### Examples in modern usage

> 彼の決意は固きなり。
> "His determination is firm." (Literary, elevated)

> これぞ武士の道なり。
> "This is the way of the warrior." (Archaic declaration)

### けり — Exclamatory Past

The classical past tense auxiliary けり had two functions: narrating past events and expressing emotional exclamation (a sudden realization or deeply felt awareness). The exclamatory use survives in poetry and literary prose.

### Examples

> 春が来にけり。
> "Ah, spring has come." (Emotional realization, not mere statement of fact)

> 昔々、あるところにおじいさんとおばあさんが住んでおりけり。
> "Long, long ago, in a certain place, there lived an old man and an old woman." (Narrative past — fairy-tale convention)

> ～けり as a literary ending is a marker of the classical storytelling register.

## 11.5 Reading Practice — Proverbs and Set Phrases Parsed with Classical Grammar

The following proverbs and set phrases use classical grammar. Each is parsed to show the classical forms at work.

---

**1. 急がば回れ**

> "If you hurry, go around — haste makes waste."

| Element | Analysis |
|---------|----------|
| 急が | 未然形 of 急ぐ (四段) |
| ば | Classical hypothetical conditional (未然形 + ば = "if") |
| 回れ | 命令形 of 回る (四段) |

---

**2. 聞くは一時の恥、聞かぬは一生の恥**

> "Asking is a moment's shame; not asking is a lifetime's shame."

| Element | Analysis |
|---------|----------|
| 聞く | 連体形 of 聞く (四段), nominalized |
| 聞かぬ | 未然形 聞か + ぬ (連体形 of negative ず), nominalized |

---

**3. 去る者は日々に疎し**

> "Those who leave grow distant day by day."

| Element | Analysis |
|---------|----------|
| 去る | 連体形 of 去る (四段), modifying 者 |
| 疎し | 終止形 of classical adjective 疎し (modern 疎い) |

---

**4. 知らぬが仏**

> "Not knowing is Buddha — ignorance is bliss."

| Element | Analysis |
|---------|----------|
| 知らぬ | 未然形 知ら + ぬ (連体形 of ず), nominalized |
| が | Subject marker |
| 仏 | Buddha (predicate) — copula omitted |

---

**5. 備えあれば憂いなし**

> "If there is preparation, there is no worry."

| Element | Analysis |
|---------|----------|
| 備え | 連用形 of 備える, used as noun |
| あれ | 已然形 of あり (ラ変) |
| ば | 已然形 + ば = "since/when there is" (established condition) |
| 憂い | Worry, concern (noun) |
| なし | Classical adjective "there is not" (modern ない) |

---

**6. 触らぬ神に祟りなし**

> "A god you do not touch brings no curse — let sleeping dogs lie."

| Element | Analysis |
|---------|----------|
| 触らぬ | 未然形 触ら + ぬ (連体形 of ず), modifying 神 |
| 祟り | Curse (noun) |
| なし | Classical adjective "there is not" |

---

**7. やむを得ず**

> "Unavoidably."

| Element | Analysis |
|---------|----------|
| やむ | 連体形 of やむ (四段, "to cease") |
| を | Object marker |
| 得 | 連用形 of 得 (下二段, "to be able to obtain") |
| ず | 終止形 of negative ず |

Literally: "Not being able to obtain cessation" → "cannot be helped."

## 11.6 Reading Practice — Constitution Preamble Excerpts

The following excerpts from the preamble of the Japanese Constitution (日本国憲法前文) demonstrate classical-influenced grammar in a modern legal context.

---

日本国民は、正当に選挙された国会における代表者を通じて行動し、われらとわれらの子孫のために、諸国民との協和による成果と、わが国全土にわたって自由のもたらす恵沢を確保し、政府の行為によって再び戦争の惨禍が起こることのないようにすることを決意し、ここに主権が国民に存することを宣言し、この憲法を確定する。

---

### Parsing key phrases

| Phrase | Analysis |
|--------|----------|
| われら | Classical first-person plural (modern 我々) |
| わが国 | わが = classical 我が ("our"); surviving in formal usage |
| もたらす恵沢 | もたらす (連体形) modifying 恵沢 = "blessings that [liberty] brings" |
| 惨禍 | さんか = "disaster, calamity" — formal Sino-Japanese compound |
| ここに | "Hereby" — formal locative, classical flavor |
| 存する | そんする = "to reside in, to exist in" — literary する verb |

---

そもそも国政は、国民の厳粛な信託によるものであって、その権威は国民に由来し、その権力は国民の代表者がこれを行使し、その福利は国民がこれを享受する。これは人類普遍の原理であり、この憲法は、かかる原理に基づくものである。

---

### Parsing key phrases

| Phrase | Analysis |
|--------|----------|
| そもそも | "In the first place, fundamentally" — classical discourse marker |
| 信託 | しんたく = "trust, mandate" |
| であって | Classical-influenced copula in te-form |
| 由来し | ゆらいし = 連用形 of 由来する ("to originate from") |
| これを行使し | Classical object fronting with これ; 行使する = "to exercise" |
| かかる | Classical 連体形 of かかり ("such, this kind of") — modern そのような |
| 基づく | もとづく = "to be based on" |

### Notes on the constitutional style

The Constitution preamble exemplifies a register where classical and modern grammar coexist. The sentence structure — with multiple clauses linked by 連用形 (し) before a final verb — follows the classical pattern of long, suspended sentences. Vocabulary is heavily Sino-Japanese (漢語), appropriate for legal solemnity. Pronouns like われら and determiners like わが and かかる are classical survivals chosen for their formal weight.

---

**Translation:**

"We, the Japanese people, acting through our duly elected representatives in the National Diet, determined that we shall secure for ourselves and our posterity the fruits of peaceful cooperation with all nations and the blessings of liberty throughout this land, and resolved that never again shall we be visited with the horrors of war through the action of government, do proclaim that sovereign power resides with the people and do firmly establish this Constitution."

"Government is a sacred trust of the people, the authority for which is derived from the people, the powers of which are exercised by the representatives of the people, and the benefits of which are enjoyed by the people. This is a universal principle of mankind upon which this Constitution is founded."
