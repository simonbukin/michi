# Chapter 13 — Literary Reading: Short Stories

Japanese literature has a tradition of short fiction stretching back centuries. The short story — 短編小説 (たんぺんしょうせつ) — occupies a central place in this tradition, and many of Japan's most celebrated authors are known primarily for their short works. For the advanced learner, short stories offer an ideal entry point into literary reading: they are long enough to develop style and theme but short enough to read in a single sitting without the stamina demands of a full novel.

This chapter introduces the conventions of Japanese literary prose and then presents two adapted reading passages in the styles of two of Japan's most important modern authors: 芥川龍之介 (あくたがわりゅうのすけ, 1892-1927) and 太宰治 (だざいおさむ, 1909-1948). These passages are original compositions written in the spirit of these authors' distinctive styles — they are not direct quotations from copyrighted works. After each passage, comprehension questions and stylistic analysis guide your reading.

---

## 13.1 Literary Style Features

Literary Japanese differs from expository or journalistic Japanese in several systematic ways. Understanding these features before you encounter them in actual texts prevents confusion and allows you to appreciate what the author is doing rather than simply struggling with what the author is saying.

### Narrative tense shifts

As introduced in Chapter 21 of Stage 4, Japanese fiction moves between past tense (た-form) and present tense (dictionary form) within a single passage. The shift is deliberate and meaningful:

- **た-form:** Narrates events in sequence; creates distance between narrator and event
- **Dictionary form:** Creates immediacy; places the reader inside the character's perception
- **ている:** Describes ongoing states the character observes
- **であった:** Formal literary past; signals an authorial, elevated narrative voice

> 男は歩いていた。突然、足が止まる。目の前に、見覚えのある建物が立っている。三年前に出たはずの町だった。
> "The man had been walking. Suddenly, his feet stop. Before his eyes, a familiar building stands. It was the town he was supposed to have left three years ago."

The shift from た to dictionary form (止まる, 立っている) pulls the reader into the character's present-moment experience. The return to た (だった) reasserts the narrator's retrospective perspective.

### Interior monologue

Japanese fiction frequently presents characters' thoughts without quotation marks or explicit attribution (see Stage 4, Chapter 21.4). The shift from narration to thought is signaled by changes in register, question forms, and sentence-final particles:

> 鈴木は時計を見た。もう七時か。急がなければ。彼は鞄を掴んで立ち上がった。
> "Suzuki looked at the clock. Seven o'clock already? He had to hurry. He grabbed his bag and stood up."

The middle two sentences are Suzuki's thoughts, not the narrator's commentary.

### Figurative language

Literary prose uses metaphor, simile, and personification more freely than expository writing:

> 沈黙が部屋を満たした。それは冬の湖のように冷たく、重かった。
> "Silence filled the room. It was cold and heavy, like a winter lake."

> 記憶は水のように指の間からこぼれていった。
> "Memories spilled from between his fingers like water."

### Deliberate ambiguity

Unlike academic or journalistic writing, which aims for clarity, literary prose sometimes cultivates ambiguity. Subjects are omitted not because they are obvious but because the author wants the reader to feel uncertain about who is speaking, thinking, or acting. Sentence-final expressions like のかもしれない, のだろうか, and ような気がする create a haze of uncertainty that is part of the literary effect.

## 13.2 Author Voice and Style

Japanese literary criticism pays close attention to 文体 (ぶんたい, "style" or "voice"). Each major author has a distinctive way of constructing sentences, choosing vocabulary, and positioning the narrator relative to the characters. Here are three contrasting examples:

### 夏目漱石 (なつめそうせき, 1867-1916)

Soseki's prose is characterized by intellectual precision, ironic distance, and a narrator who observes human behavior with detached amusement. His sentences tend to be well-structured, his vocabulary balanced between 和語 and 漢語, and his narrators self-aware.

> Style markers: measured sentence rhythm, ironic commentary, psychological observation, classical-influenced grammar

### 太宰治 (だざいおさむ, 1909-1948)

Dazai's prose is intensely personal, confessional, and emotionally raw. His narrators speak directly to the reader, shifting between self-deprecation and desperate sincerity. Sentences can be short and staccato or long and breathless. The boundary between author and narrator is deliberately blurred.

> Style markers: first-person confession, direct reader address, emotional intensity, self-conscious humor, colloquial tone in literary contexts

### 村上春樹 (むらかみはるき, 1949-)

Murakami's prose is understated, rhythmic, and influenced by American fiction. His narrators are detached and slightly melancholic. Descriptions are precise and concrete. Metaphors are unexpected but specific. The emotional content is conveyed through what is left unsaid.

> Style markers: simple sentence structure, concrete sensory detail, understatement, detached first-person narration, Western cultural references

## 13.3 Reading: Adapted Excerpt in the Style of 芥川龍之介

次の文章は、芥川龍之介の文体を模した創作短編の一部である。芥川は、古典や歴史を題材にとり、人間の利己心や道徳の脆さを冷徹な筆致で描いた作家として知られる。

---

ある日の暮れ方のことである。一人の男が、山寺の石段を登っていた。男は旅の途中であった。笠を深くかぶり、杖をつき、疲れた足を一段ずつ運んでいる。

石段の数は百を超えていた。男は何度か立ち止まり、息をついた。見上げれば、杉の木の間から夕空がわずかに覗いている。寺はまだ見えない。ただ、どこからか鐘の音が、かすかに聞こえてくるばかりであった。

ようやく石段を登りきったとき、男の目の前に現れたのは、予想していたものとはまるで違う光景であった。本堂は半ば崩れ、屋根には草が生い茂り、境内には人の気配がない。荒れ果てた寺であった。

男は落胆した。しかし、日はすでに暮れかけている。他に泊まる場所もない。男は本堂の軒下に腰を下ろし、背負っていた荷物を解いた。

そのとき、本堂の奥から、かすかな灯りが漏れているのに気づいた。

人がいる。男は立ち上がり、慎重に近づいた。板戸の隙間から覗くと、中には一人の老僧が座っていた。老僧は背を丸め、何かを一心に書いている。その手元には、紙が幾重にも積まれていた。

男は戸を叩いた。

「旅の者でございます。一晩の宿をお借りできれば幸いに存じます」

老僧は顔を上げた。その顔は、蝋のように白く、頬はこけ、眼窩の奥に小さな目が光っていた。しかし、その目には、狂気とも執念ともつかぬ、異様な力が宿っていた。

「宿か。構わぬ。だが、一つだけ約束してくれ」

老僧はそう言うと、手元の紙の束を指さした。

「わしが死んだら、これをすべて焼いてくれ。一枚残らず」

男は紙の束を見た。そこには、びっしりと文字が書き連ねてあった。何年、あるいは何十年もかけて書かれたものであろう。男は不思議に思ったが、頷いた。

その夜、男は隣の部屋で眠った。夜中にふと目が覚めると、隣からはまだ筆を走らせる音が聞こえていた。

翌朝、男が老僧の部屋を訪ねると、老僧は机に向かったまま、すでに息絶えていた。その手にはまだ筆が握られている。最後の一枚には、途中で終わった文が一行だけ残されていた。

男は約束を思い出した。紙の束を見つめた。焼くべきか。しかし、これほどの分量を、読みもせずに焼いてよいものだろうか。男の中で、約束を守ろうとする心と、その紙に書かれたものへの好奇心とが、せめぎ合っていた。

男は一枚目を手に取った。

---

**Translation:**

It was toward the end of a certain day. A man was climbing the stone steps of a mountain temple. The man was in the middle of a journey. Wearing a deep sedge hat, leaning on a staff, he carried his tired feet one step at a time.

The stone steps numbered over a hundred. The man stopped several times and caught his breath. Looking up, he could see the evening sky peeking faintly through the gaps between cedar trees. The temple was not yet visible. Only the sound of a bell could be heard faintly coming from somewhere.

When he finally finished climbing the steps, what appeared before the man's eyes was a sight entirely different from what he had expected. The main hall was half-collapsed, grass grew thickly on the roof, and in the temple grounds there was no sign of human presence. It was a ruined temple.

The man was disappointed. However, the sun was already beginning to set. There was nowhere else to stay. The man sat down under the eaves of the main hall and undid the luggage he had been carrying on his back.

At that moment, he noticed a faint light leaking from the depths of the main hall.

Someone was here. The man stood up and approached cautiously. Peering through the gap in the wooden door, inside sat a single elderly monk. The monk was hunched over, writing something with intense concentration. At his hands, sheets of paper were stacked in many layers.

The man knocked on the door.

"I am a traveler. I would be most grateful if I could borrow lodging for one night."

The old monk raised his face. That face was white as wax, the cheeks sunken, and small eyes gleamed from deep within the eye sockets. Yet in those eyes dwelt a strange power that could be called neither madness nor obsession.

"Lodging? I don't mind. But promise me just one thing."

Saying this, the old monk pointed to the bundle of papers at his hands.

"When I die, burn all of these. Every last page."

The man looked at the bundle of papers. Characters were written there in dense rows. They must have been written over years, perhaps decades. The man found it strange, but he nodded.

That night, the man slept in the next room. When he happened to wake in the middle of the night, the sound of a brush being moved could still be heard from next door.

The following morning, when the man visited the old monk's room, the monk was still facing his desk, but had already breathed his last. A brush was still gripped in his hand. On the final page, a single line of an unfinished sentence remained.

The man remembered his promise. He stared at the bundle of papers. Should he burn them? But was it truly right to burn such a volume without even reading it? Within the man, the desire to keep his promise and his curiosity about what was written on those pages were locked in struggle.

The man picked up the first page.

## 13.4 Reading: Adapted Excerpt in the Style of 太宰治

次の文章は、太宰治の文体を模した創作短編の一部である。太宰は、一人称の告白体で、自意識の苦しみや人間の弱さを赤裸々に描いた作家として知られる。

---

私は、嘘をつくのが下手だった。いや、正確に言えば、嘘をつくこと自体は上手だったのかもしれない。問題は、嘘をついた後に来る罪悪感に耐えられないということであった。嘘をつくたびに、私は自分が少しずつ腐っていくような気がした。

友人の田村に金を借りた話をしよう。

あれは秋のことだった。私は原稿料の入る見込みが外れ、月末の家賃が払えなくなっていた。情けない話である。三十を過ぎた男が、家賃すら払えない。世間の人々は、毎月きちんと家賃を払い、電気代を払い、時には貯金さえしているのだろう。なぜ私にはそれができないのか。才能がないからではない。いや、才能がないからかもしれないが、それ以上に、生きることの根本的な部分で何かが欠落しているのだと思う。

田村に電話をした。田村は大学時代の友人で、今は銀行に勤めている。堅実な男である。私とは正反対だ。

「少し相談があるんだが」

私はそう切り出した。相談、という言葉を使った時点で、すでに嘘が始まっている。相談ではない。金を借りたいのである。しかし、「金を貸してくれ」とは、どうしても言えなかった。

田村は言った。「どうした。珍しいな、お前から電話なんて」

その声は温かかった。私は、その温かさに触れた瞬間、自分がこれからやろうとしていることの卑しさに気づいた。この男は、私を友人だと思っている。私は、その友情を利用して金を引き出そうとしている。

しかし、家賃は払わなければならない。

「実は、少し困っていてね」

「困っている？ 何に」

「金だよ」

言ってしまった。言ってしまえば、不思議と楽になった。もう取り繕う必要がない。私は正直に、原稿料が入らなかったこと、家賃が払えないこと、来月には必ず返すことを話した。来月には必ず返す、という部分だけは、嘘かもしれなかった。

田村は笑った。怒るでもなく、呆れるでもなく、ただ穏やかに笑った。

「いくら要る」

「三万でいい。いや、五万あると助かる」

三万で足りるのに五万と言った。この瞬間、私はまた腐った。ほんの少しだが、確実に腐った。

田村は翌日、封筒に五万円を入れて持ってきてくれた。「返さなくていい」と言った。私はそれを受け取りながら、泣きそうになった。嬉しかったのではない。自分が情けなかったのだ。

あれから三年が経つ。私はあの五万円を、まだ返していない。

---

**Translation:**

I was bad at lying. No, to be precise, I may have been good at the act of lying itself. The problem was that I could not endure the guilt that came after telling a lie. Every time I lied, I felt as though I were rotting away, little by little.

Let me tell you about the time I borrowed money from my friend Tamura.

It was autumn. My expected manuscript fee had fallen through, and I was unable to pay the rent at the end of the month. A pathetic story. A man past thirty who cannot even pay his rent. The people of the world probably pay their rent properly every month, pay their electric bills, and sometimes even save money. Why can I not do that? It is not because I lack talent. Well, it may be because I lack talent, but more than that, I think something is fundamentally missing in the basic part of living.

I called Tamura. Tamura was a friend from university who now works at a bank. A solid man. The exact opposite of me.

"There's something I'd like to discuss."

I began with those words. The moment I used the word "discuss," the lie had already begun. It was not a discussion I wanted. I wanted to borrow money. But I simply could not bring myself to say "lend me money."

Tamura said, "What's up? That's unusual, a call from you."

His voice was warm. The moment I was touched by that warmth, I realized the baseness of what I was about to do. This man thinks of me as a friend. I am trying to use that friendship to extract money.

But the rent had to be paid.

"The truth is, I'm in a bit of trouble."

"Trouble? With what?"

"Money."

I said it. Once I said it, I felt strangely relieved. There was no longer any need to keep up appearances. I honestly told him that the manuscript fee had not come in, that I could not pay the rent, that I would definitely pay him back next month. The part about definitely paying back next month may have been a lie.

Tamura laughed. Neither angry nor exasperated — he simply laughed gently.

"How much do you need?"

"Thirty thousand would be enough. No — fifty thousand would really help."

Thirty thousand would have been enough, but I said fifty thousand. In that instant, I rotted again. Just a little, but certainly, I rotted.

Tamura brought fifty thousand yen the next day in an envelope. He said, "You don't have to pay it back." As I accepted it, I nearly cried. It was not that I was happy. It was that I felt pathetic.

Three years have passed since then. I still have not returned that fifty thousand yen.

## 13.5 Comprehension and Discussion

### Passage 1 — The Mountain Temple

Consider the following questions about the adapted Akutagawa-style passage:

**1. Structure and theme**

> この物語の中心的な葛藤は何か。
> "What is the central conflict of this story?"

The man faces a moral dilemma: keep his promise to the dead monk and burn the papers unread, or satisfy his curiosity and read them. This is a classic Akutagawa theme — the tension between moral duty and human desire, between what one should do and what one wants to do.

**2. Narrative technique**

> 語り手はどのような視点で物語を語っているか。
> "From what perspective does the narrator tell the story?"

The narrator is third-person omniscient but restrained — we learn the man's feelings (落胆した, 不思議に思った) but the narrator does not editorialize. This detached, observational style is characteristic of Akutagawa.

**3. Classical elements**

> 文体における文語的要素を指摘せよ。
> "Identify classical elements in the style."

The passage uses であった (literary past copula), ばかりであった (emphatic limit), ぬ in 構わぬ (classical negative), and sentence structures with suspended clauses (連用形 chaining). The opening ある日の暮れ方のことである follows the classical tale-opening formula.

### Passage 2 — The Borrowed Money

**1. Voice and confession**

> 語り手の自己認識はどのようなものか。
> "What is the narrator's self-awareness like?"

The narrator is painfully self-aware. He recognizes his own weakness in real time — he knows he is manipulating his friend even as he does it. This gap between self-knowledge and self-control is a defining feature of Dazai's characters.

**2. The lie structure**

> 「嘘」はこの文章でどのように展開されているか。
> "How does the concept of 'lies' develop in this passage?"

The passage traces a cascade of small lies: using "discuss" instead of "lend me money," saying fifty thousand when thirty thousand would suffice, promising to repay next month. Each lie is small, but together they form a pattern of self-corruption — the "rotting" metaphor the narrator uses.

**3. Style markers**

> 太宰的な文体の特徴を三つ挙げよ。
> "Name three characteristics of Dazai's style in this passage."

First, direct reader address (話をしよう, "let me tell you"). Second, self-deprecating commentary embedded in narration (情けない話である). Third, the confessional first person that blurs the line between author and character — a hallmark of the 私小説 (しょうせつ, "I-novel") tradition that Dazai both exemplified and subverted.
