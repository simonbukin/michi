# Chapter 2 — Near-Synonym Grammar: Cause, Reason, Purpose

Japanese has an extraordinary number of ways to express why something happened. English relies heavily on "because," "since," "due to," and "in order to," but Japanese distributes these meanings across more than a dozen distinct patterns, each encoding information about formality, objectivity, the speaker's emotional stance, and whether the cause is viewed positively or negatively.

You have learned all of these individually across Stages 1 through 5. This chapter places them side by side. The goal is not to learn new forms but to sharpen your ability to choose the right one — and to understand why a Japanese writer or speaker chose one over another.

---

## 2.1 から vs ので vs ため(に) vs によって vs につき

These five expressions all mark cause or reason, but they differ sharply in register, subjectivity, and the relationship between speaker and listener.

### Comparison Table

| Expression | Register | Subjectivity | Typical context |
|---|---|---|---|
| から | Casual to neutral | Subjective — speaker's personal reasoning | Conversation, personal writing |
| ので | Neutral to polite | Softer, more considerate | Polite conversation, general writing |
| ため(に) | Formal | Objective — presents cause as fact | Reports, news, academic writing |
| によって | Formal | Objective — identifies agent or method | Academic, legal, news |
| につき | Very formal | Impersonal — institutional notice | Signs, official announcements |

### から — Personal, assertive reasoning

から presents the reason as the speaker's own judgment. It can sound assertive or even pushy in certain contexts.

> 暑いから、窓を　開けてもいい？
> "It's hot, so can I open the window?"

> 危ないから、やめなさい。
> "It's dangerous, so stop it."

### ので — Softened, considerate reasoning

ので presents the reason as an observable circumstance rather than a personal assertion. It sounds more polite and less imposing.

> 電車が　遅れましたので、遅刻してしまいました。
> "Because the train was delayed, I ended up being late."

> 明日は　用事があるので、参加できません。
> "I have plans tomorrow, so I cannot participate."

### ため(に) — Objective, formal cause

ため(に) treats the cause as an established fact. It does not convey the speaker's personal feelings about the reason.

> 台風の　ため、本日の　便は　すべて　欠航と　なりました。
> "Due to the typhoon, all flights today have been canceled."

> 人口増加の　ため、住宅不足が　深刻化している。
> "Due to population growth, the housing shortage is becoming severe."

### によって — Agent or cause in formal contexts

によって identifies the cause, agent, or means. It is common in passive constructions and formal analysis.

> この建物は　地震によって　損壊した。
> "This building was damaged by the earthquake."

> 技術革新によって、生活は　大きく　変わった。
> "Life has changed dramatically through technological innovation."

### につき — Institutional notice

につき is restricted to signs, notices, and official announcements. It is never used in conversation.

> 工事中につき、通行止め。
> "Closed to traffic due to construction."

> 本日定休日につき、お休みさせていただきます。
> "Closed today as it is our regular day off."

### Substitution test

"Because of rain, the event was canceled."

| Version | Effect |
|---|---|
| 雨だから、イベントは中止になった。 | Casual, personal report. |
| 雨なので、イベントは中止になりました。 | Polite, considerate explanation. |
| 雨のため、イベントは中止となりました。 | Formal, objective announcement. |
| 雨につき、イベントは中止。 | Sign or notice — terse, impersonal. |

## 2.2 ために vs ように — Purpose with volitionality

Both ために and ように express purpose ("in order to"), but they differ based on whether the subject can control the outcome.

### The core distinction

| Expression | Subject control | Verb type before it |
|---|---|---|
| ために | Volitional — the subject intentionally acts | Volitional verb (dictionary form) |
| ように | Non-volitional — the subject hopes for a result | Non-volitional verb, potential form, or ない form |

### Clear cases

> 日本語を　学ぶために、東京に　引っ越した。
> "I moved to Tokyo in order to study Japanese." (Deliberate action)

> 日本語が　話せるように、毎日　練習している。
> "I practice every day so that I can speak Japanese." (Hoping for the ability to develop)

> 忘れないように、メモを　取った。
> "I took notes so that I wouldn't forget." (Preventing an involuntary event)

### Edge cases and common errors

**Potential verbs** take ように, not ために, because the ability itself is not directly controllable.

> ○ 読めるように練習する。
> × 読めるために練習する。

**Negative purpose** uses ように, because "not doing something" is framed as a state to maintain rather than a volitional act.

> ○ 遅れないように早く出た。
> × 遅れないために早く出た。 (Grammatically possible in formal writing, but ように is far more natural.)

**When both are possible:** With volitional verbs, both can sometimes work, but the nuance shifts.

> 合格するために勉強する。 — "I study in order to pass." (Direct purpose, strong intention)
> 合格するように勉強する。 — "I study so that I will pass." (Hoping for the outcome, slightly less direct)

> 子どもが　安心して　遊べるように、公園を　整備した。
> "The park was maintained so that children could play with peace of mind." (The children's feeling of safety is not directly controllable by the maintainers.)

## 2.3 おかげで vs せいで vs ばかりに vs だけに

These four expressions all identify a cause, but they differ in whether the speaker views that cause positively, negatively, or with a special sense of irony.

### Comparison Table

| Expression | Cause valence | Speaker's stance | Nuance |
|---|---|---|---|
| おかげで | Positive (usually) | Gratitude | "Thanks to" |
| せいで | Negative | Blame | "Because of (something bad)" |
| ばかりに | Negative | Regret, irony | "Just because of this one thing, everything went wrong" |
| だけに | Neutral to emphatic | Proportional emphasis | "Precisely because / given that" |

### おかげで — Grateful cause

> 先生の　おかげで、合格できました。
> "Thanks to my teacher, I was able to pass."

> 天気が　良かった　おかげで、ピクニックを　楽しめた。
> "Thanks to the good weather, we enjoyed the picnic."

Ironic use: おかげで can be used sarcastically to blame someone while using the form of gratitude.

> あなたの　おかげで、大変な　目に　遭った。
> "Thanks to you, I had a terrible experience." (Sarcastic)

### せいで — Blaming cause

> 彼の　せいで、計画が　台無しに　なった。
> "Because of him, the plan was ruined."

> 寝不足の　せいで、頭が　ぼんやりする。
> "Because of lack of sleep, my head feels foggy."

### ばかりに — Ironic, regrettable cause

ばかりに highlights that one single factor led to an unfortunate result. It carries a strong sense of "if only that one thing had been different."

> 正直に　言った　ばかりに、みんなに　嫌われた。
> "Just because I spoke honestly, everyone came to dislike me."

> ちょっと　油断した　ばかりに、大きな　事故に　なった。
> "Just because I let my guard down for a moment, it became a major accident."

> お金が　ない　ばかりに、進学を　諦めなければならなかった。
> "Just because I had no money, I had to give up going to college."

### だけに — Proportional cause

だけに states that precisely because condition A is true, result B is natural or especially notable. It can be positive, negative, or neutral.

> 人気がある　だけに、予約が　取りにくい。
> "Precisely because it is popular, reservations are hard to get."

> 期待が　大きかった　だけに、失望も　大きかった。
> "Precisely because expectations were high, the disappointment was also great."

> さすが　プロだけに、仕上がりが　違う。
> "As expected of a professional — precisely because he is one, the finish is different."

## 2.4 からこそ vs だけあって vs だけに — "Precisely Because"

These three all emphasize the cause-effect relationship, but they do so from different angles.

### Comparison Table

| Expression | Focus | Emotional tone | Register |
|---|---|---|---|
| からこそ | The cause is the essential reason | Emphatic, sometimes passionate | Casual to formal |
| だけあって | The result is worthy of / consistent with the cause | Admiration, "as expected" | Neutral to formal |
| だけに | The cause makes the result proportionally natural | Objective emphasis | Neutral to formal |

### からこそ — "It is precisely because"

からこそ insists that the stated reason is the crucial factor. It is often used to argue a point or express strong conviction.

> 好きだからこそ、厳しいことも　言うんだ。
> "It is precisely because I care about you that I say harsh things."

> 失敗した　からこそ、学べたことが　ある。
> "It is precisely because I failed that there were things I could learn."

> 日本語が　難しいからこそ、やりがいがある。
> "It is precisely because Japanese is difficult that it is rewarding."

### だけあって — "As you would expect from"

だけあって evaluates the result as living up to or being consistent with the cause. It typically carries admiration or acknowledgment.

> さすが　ベテランだけあって、対応が　速い。
> "As you'd expect from a veteran, his response is fast."

> 有名な　レストランだけあって、料理が　おいしかった。
> "As you'd expect from a famous restaurant, the food was delicious."

> 10年　住んでいた　だけあって、この町のことを　よく　知っている。
> "As you'd expect from someone who lived here for 10 years, she knows this town well."

### The three compared

"She trained for years, so her performance is outstanding."

| Version | Nuance |
|---|---|
| 何年も訓練したからこそ、あの演技ができるのだ。 | Insistence — "it is specifically because of the training." |
| 何年も訓練しただけあって、演技がすばらしい。 | Admiration — "as you'd expect given the training." |
| 何年も訓練しただけに、演技のレベルが高い。 | Objective observation — "given the training, the level is naturally high." |

### Exercise B — Explain the difference

For each pair, explain how the meaning or impression changes.

**1a.** 先生が厳しいおかげで、成長できた。
**1b.** 先生が厳しいせいで、つらかった。

**2a.** 日本語を話すために、毎日練習する。
**2b.** 日本語が話せるように、毎日練習する。

**3a.** 有名だからこそ、批判も多い。
**3b.** 有名なだけに、批判も多い。

### Exercise C — Register correction

The following sentences use cause/reason expressions at the wrong register. Correct them.

**1.** 本日、設備点検から、エレベーターは使用できません。(building notice)

**2.** お母さんが手伝ってくれたため、宿題が早く終わったよ。(child speaking to friend)

**3.** 雨につき、今日は出かけたくないな。(casual conversation)

---

## 2.6 Answer Key

### Exercise A

1. **b) ため** — Formal context (reporting lateness to a meeting). から is too casual; につき is for signs and notices.

2. **b) だけあって** — Admiration for someone's composure being consistent with their experience. からこそ would insist on the cause; ばかりに is for negative results.

3. **b) ように** — "Not catching a cold" is an involuntary state; ように is required. ために would require a volitional verb.

4. **c) ばかりに** — Emphasizes that one small cause (a single unnecessary remark) led to a regrettable result. せいで would simply blame; ばかりに adds the ironic "just because of that one thing."

5. **b) からこそ** — Insists that the painful experience was the essential reason for personal growth. A positive framing of difficulty. ばかりに and せいで would frame it negatively.

6. **b) につき** — Institutional notice. のため is possible but につき is the standard phrasing for signs. なので is too conversational.

### Exercise B

1. Both identify the teacher's strictness as a cause. おかげで frames it with gratitude (the strictness helped growth). せいで frames it as blame (the strictness caused suffering). Same fact, opposite evaluation.

2. ために treats speaking Japanese as a volitional goal pursued directly. ように treats the ability to speak as a hoped-for result that develops over time. The practice is the same; the framing of the goal differs.

3. からこそ argues passionately that fame is the essential reason criticism exists. だけに observes proportionally that given the fame, criticism naturally follows. からこそ is more argumentative; だけに is more analytical.

### Exercise C

1. から → **につき** or **のため**: 本日、設備点検につき、エレベーターは使用できません。

2. ため → **おかげで** or **から**: お母さんが手伝ってくれたから、宿題が早く終わったよ。(ため is too formal for a child's speech.)

3. につき → **から** or **ので**: 雨だから、今日は出かけたくないな。(につき is for institutional notices, not casual speech.)
