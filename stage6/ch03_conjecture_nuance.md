# Chapter 3 — Near-Synonym Grammar: Conjecture and Evidence

How do you know what you claim to know? In English, the difference between "it seems," "it looks like," "apparently," and "I hear that" is often blurred in casual speech. Japanese forces you to be precise. Each conjecture expression encodes the speaker's evidence basis: did you see it yourself, infer it from clues, hear it from someone, or deduce it logically? Did you observe the surface or reach a conclusion through reasoning?

This chapter examines three clusters of near-synonyms that all deal with conjecture, evidence, and certainty. You have already learned each of these forms individually. Now it is time to understand the full system and the choices it requires.

---

## 3.1 ようだ vs みたいだ vs らしい vs そうだ (Appearance) vs そうだ (Hearsay)

This is perhaps the most important five-way distinction in Japanese grammar. All five can translate as "it seems" in English, but they are based on fundamentally different kinds of evidence.

### The Five-Way Distinction

| Expression | Evidence basis | Register | Formation |
|---|---|---|---|
| ようだ | Speaker's direct observation + inference | Neutral to formal | V plain / い-adj / な-adj な / N の + ようだ |
| みたいだ | Same as ようだ, but casual | Casual | V plain / い-adj / な-adj / N + みたいだ |
| らしい | Indirect evidence — hearsay or external clues | Neutral | V plain / い-adj / な-adj / N + らしい |
| そうだ (様態) | Appearance — how something looks right now | Neutral | V ます stem / い-adj stem / な-adj stem + そうだ |
| そうだ (伝聞) | Hearsay — someone told you | Neutral | V plain / い-adj / な-adj だ / N だ + そうだ |

### ようだ — Inference from direct observation

The speaker has personally observed something and draws a conclusion. The evidence is firsthand.

> 彼は　怒っている　ようだ。
> "He seems to be angry." (I can see his face, his tone — I'm inferring from what I observe.)

> 外は　雨が　降っている　ようだ。
> "It seems to be raining outside." (I see wet umbrellas, hear sounds on the roof.)

### みたいだ — Casual version of ようだ

Functionally identical to ようだ but used in casual speech. Avoid in formal writing.

> 彼、怒ってる　みたいだよ。
> "He seems angry." (Same inference, casual delivery.)

> この店、人気　みたいだね。
> "This place seems popular, doesn't it." (Looking at the long line.)

### らしい — Inference from indirect evidence

The speaker did not directly observe the situation but has gathered information — from rumors, news, contextual clues, or what others said.

> 彼は　来月　結婚する　らしい。
> "Apparently he is getting married next month." (I heard from someone / read somewhere.)

> あの店は　おいしい　らしい。
> "That restaurant is apparently good." (Based on reviews, reputation — I haven't been there.)

> 隣の部屋で　何か　あった　らしい。
> "Something apparently happened in the next room." (I hear noise but did not see it.)

### そうだ (様態) — Visual impression of imminent state

This そうだ is based on the immediate visual appearance of something. It often implies the situation is about to happen or looks a certain way on the surface.

> 雨が　降りそうだ。
> "It looks like it's going to rain." (Looking at the sky right now.)

> このケーキは　おいしそうだ。
> "This cake looks delicious." (Looking at it — I haven't tasted it.)

> 彼は　泣きそうだ。
> "He looks like he's about to cry." (His face right now.)

Note the formation difference: おいしい → おいしそう (stem), not おいしいそう.

### そうだ (伝聞) — Pure hearsay

This そうだ reports what the speaker heard from another source. It attaches to the plain form of the sentence with no modification.

> 明日は　雨だそうだ。
> "I hear it will rain tomorrow." (Weather report, someone told me.)

> あの映画は　面白いそうだ。
> "I hear that movie is interesting." (Someone's report.)

Note the formation difference: おいしい → おいしいそうだ (full form), not おいしそうだ.

### Critical contrasts

| Sentence | Meaning |
|---|---|
| おいしそうだ | "It looks delicious." (Appearance — looking at the food.) |
| おいしいそうだ | "I hear it's delicious." (Hearsay — someone told me.) |
| おいしいようだ | "It seems delicious." (Inference — I see people's reactions.) |
| おいしいらしい | "Apparently it's delicious." (Indirect evidence — reviews, reputation.) |

| Sentence | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 雨が降りそうだ | "It looks like it'll rain." (Looking at the sky.) |
| 雨が降るそうだ | "I hear it'll rain." (Weather forecast.) |
| 雨が降っているようだ | "It seems to be raining." (Hearing sounds, seeing wet pavement.) |
| 雨が降っているらしい | "Apparently it's raining." (Someone mentioned it, or I see clues.) |

## 3.2 はずだ vs わけだ vs に違いない vs に相違ない vs にほかならない

These expressions all convey logical certainty or conclusion, but they differ in strength, subjectivity, and what kind of reasoning supports them.

### Comparison Table

| Expression | Certainty level | Reasoning type | Register |
|---|---|---|---|
| はずだ | Strong expectation | Based on known facts or schedule | Neutral |
| わけだ | Logical conclusion | "So that's why" — connects evidence to explanation | Neutral |
| に違いない | Strong conviction | Speaker's personal certainty | Neutral to formal |
| に相違ない | Same as above | Same as above, but formal/literary | Formal, written |
| にほかならない | Definitive identification | "Is nothing other than" | Formal, written |

### はずだ — "Should be" (expectation based on information)

はずだ expresses that something should be the case based on prior knowledge, schedule, or logic. It can be contradicted by reality.

> 彼は　もう　着いている　はずだ。
> "He should have arrived by now." (He left on time; logically, he should be there.)

> この書類は　昨日　提出した　はずだ。
> "I should have submitted this document yesterday." (I believe I did.)

> 予約した　はずなのに、名前が　ない。
> "I should have made a reservation, but my name isn't there." (Expectation contradicted.)

### わけだ — "So that explains it"

わけだ marks a conclusion reached by connecting pieces of information. It often has an "aha" quality — the speaker has figured something out.

> 彼は　アメリカに　5年　住んでいた。英語が　上手な　わけだ。
> "He lived in America for five years. No wonder his English is good."

> 毎日　3時間　練習している。上達する　わけだ。
> "She practices three hours every day. No wonder she's improving."

> 道理で　寒い　わけだ。窓が　開いていた。
> "No wonder it's cold. The window was open."

### に違いない — "There is no doubt that"

に違いない expresses the speaker's strong personal conviction. Unlike はずだ, which is based on external information, に違いない reflects the speaker's internal certainty.

> 彼が　犯人に　違いない。
> "He must be the culprit." (I'm convinced.)

> このまま　行けば、成功する　に違いない。
> "If things continue like this, it will surely succeed."

### に相違ない — Formal variant of に違いない

に相違ない has the same meaning but is restricted to formal writing, legal documents, and literary contexts.

> これは　本物に　相違ない。
> "This is undoubtedly genuine."

> 彼の　証言は　事実に　相違ない。
> "His testimony is without doubt factual."

### にほかならない — "Is nothing other than"

にほかならない identifies something definitively. It is used to assert that X is the essential nature or true identity of something.

> 彼の　成功は、努力の　結果に　ほかならない。
> "His success is nothing other than the result of hard work."

> これは　差別に　ほかならない。
> "This is nothing other than discrimination."

> 彼が　辞任したのは、責任を　取った　ということに　ほかならない。
> "His resignation is nothing other than taking responsibility."

### Choosing between them

| Situation | Best choice |
|---|---|
| Expectation based on schedule or known facts | はずだ |
| Connecting evidence to reach an explanation | わけだ |
| Strong personal conviction about a fact | に違いない |
| Same conviction in formal/legal writing | に相違ない |
| Defining the essential nature of something | にほかならない |

## 3.3 と思われる vs と考えられる vs とされている vs と見られている

These hedging expressions are essential in academic writing, news, and formal discourse. They all create distance between the speaker and the claim, but the type of distance varies.

### Comparison Table

| Expression | Literal sense | Distance type | Common in |
|---|---|---|---|
| と思われる | "is thought" | General impression or tentative judgment | Essays, editorials |
| と考えられる | "is considered" | Reasoned analysis | Academic papers, research |
| とされている | "is regarded as" | Established consensus | Reference works, textbooks |
| と見られている | "is viewed as / seen as" | Current assessment by observers | News, media analysis |

### と思われる — "It is thought that"

A general hedging expression. The speaker presents an opinion without claiming it as absolute fact.

> この問題の　解決には　時間が　かかると　思われる。
> "It is thought that solving this problem will take time."

> 原因は　ストレスであると　思われる。
> "The cause is thought to be stress."

### と考えられる — "It is considered that"

Implies that reasoning or analysis supports the claim. More intellectual than と思われる.

> この遺跡は　約3000年前のものと　考えられている。
> "These ruins are considered to be from approximately 3,000 years ago."

> 温暖化が　原因であると　考えられる。
> "It is considered that global warming is the cause."

### とされている — "It is regarded as"

Presents established consensus or conventional understanding. Something that "everyone knows" or that authorities have determined.

> 日本の　人口は　約1億2000万人と　されている。
> "Japan's population is regarded as being approximately 120 million."

> この薬は　安全と　されている。
> "This medicine is regarded as safe."

### と見られている — "It is viewed as / seen as"

Often used in news to report current assessments by analysts, authorities, or the public. Implies ongoing observation.

> 容疑者は　すでに　国外に　逃亡したと　見られている。
> "The suspect is seen as having already fled the country."

> 来月の　選挙では　与党が　有利と　見られている。
> "The ruling party is seen as having the advantage in next month's election."

### Register and interchangeability

These four are sometimes interchangeable, but switching them shifts the impression:

"This temple is believed to have been built in the 8th century."

| Version | Impression |
|---|---|
| 8世紀に建てられたと思われる。 | Tentative assessment. |
| 8世紀に建てられたと考えられている。 | Reasoned scholarly conclusion. |
| 8世紀に建てられたとされている。 | Established historical consensus. |
| 8世紀に建てられたと見られている。 | Current view based on recent findings. |

## 3.5 Answer Key

### Exercise A

1. **a) おいしそうだ** — The speaker is looking at the food and judging by appearance. おいしいそうだ (hearsay) would mean "I hear it's delicious." おいしいようだ would require inference from others' reactions.

2. **b) そうだ（伝聞）** — The information source is explicitly stated (天気予報によると). Hearsay そうだ is the natural match. らしい is possible but less precise when the source is named.

3. **b) わけだ** — The speaker has learned new information (daily jogging) and connects it to an observed result (weight loss). "No wonder." はずだ would express expectation; に違いない would express conviction without the "aha" realization.

4. **b) 考えられている** — Scientific/archaeological consensus based on analysis. 思われる is too tentative; 見られている implies current ongoing assessment rather than established scholarly view.

5. **b) ようだ** — The speaker directly observes her facial expression and infers. らしい would suggest indirect evidence. 伝聞そうだ would mean someone told the speaker.

6. **b) ほかならない** — Identifies the essential nature: "this accident is nothing other than the result of human error." に違いない would express personal conviction; に相違ない would be a formal variant of conviction but not identification.

### Exercise B

- **と見られている** ("is seen as relating to the Nankai Trough") — Current expert assessment; the connection has not been definitively confirmed.
- **と思われる** ("multiple buildings are thought to have been damaged") — Tentative report; full damage assessment is not yet complete.
- **とのことだ** ("according to experts, aftershocks are possible") — Reports what experts said; a formal hearsay marker.
- **と考えられる** ("it is considered advisable to evacuate early") — Reasoned recommendation based on the situation.
- **とされている** ("it is regarded that there is no tsunami risk") — Official determination by authorities; established assessment.

### Exercise C

1. あの人は日本人のようだ。 — Changes from indirect evidence (I heard, I read, contextual clues) to direct observation (I see how they behave, dress, speak and infer they are Japanese).

2. 明日は暑くなるそうだ。 — Changes from visual impression (looking at the sky, feeling humidity) to hearsay (I heard from the forecast or someone else). Note the formation change: 暑くなりそうだ (stem + そう) → 暑くなるそうだ (plain form + そう).

3. 彼は来ないはずだ。 — Changes from personal conviction ("I'm sure he won't come") to expectation based on known facts ("He shouldn't be coming" — perhaps he said he was busy, or his schedule conflicts). はずだ can be proven wrong; に違いない expresses unshakeable belief.
