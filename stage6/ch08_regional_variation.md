# Chapter 8 — Regional Variation (Expanded)

Japan is often described as a linguistically homogeneous country. This is a myth. The Japanese archipelago hosts a range of regional varieties so diverse that speakers from opposite ends of the country can struggle to understand each other when speaking in full dialect. The standardization of 標準語 (ひょうじゅんご, standard language) — based primarily on the Tokyo dialect and promoted aggressively through education and media since the Meiji period — has created a shared national register, but it has not eliminated regional variation. It has simply added a layer on top of it.

For the advanced learner, regional variation matters for three reasons. First, you will encounter it: in conversation with speakers outside Tokyo, in media that uses dialect for characterization, and in literature set in specific regions. Second, understanding dialect features sharpens your grasp of standard Japanese by showing you which features are universal and which are Tokyo-specific conventions you have been treating as the default. Third, regional variation is a window into sociolinguistic competence — the ability to recognize and interpret the social meaning of linguistic choices.

This chapter covers the four most commonly encountered regional varieties — Kansai, Tohoku, Kyushu, and Hokkaido — then examines the phenomenon of code-switching and the role of dialect in media.

## 8.1 関西弁 Expanded

Kansai dialect (関西弁, かんさいべん) — particularly the Osaka-Kyoto-Kobe variety — is by far the most widely recognized and culturally influential regional variety in Japan. Thanks to the dominance of Kansai-based comedians (芸人, げいにん) on national television, many Kansai features are now understood nationwide, and some have entered standard casual Japanese (see Chapter 7, Section 7.3).

However, "Kansai-ben" is itself an umbrella term covering significant internal variation. Osaka speech (大阪弁), Kyoto speech (京都弁), and Kobe speech (神戸弁) differ from each other in vocabulary, intonation, and even some grammatical features. What follows focuses primarily on the Osaka variety, which is the most widely known, with notes on Kyoto features where they differ significantly.

### Verb Conjugations

The most immediately noticeable features of Kansai verb conjugation are the negative form, the honorific ～はる, and the past progressive ～てん.

### Formation Table: Kansai Negative Forms

| Verb class | Standard negative | Kansai negative | Example (standard) | Example (Kansai) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 五段 (u-verbs) | ～ない | ～へん / ～ん | 行かない | 行かへん / 行かん |
| 一段 (ru-verbs) | ～ない | ～へん / ～ん | 食べない | 食べへん / 食べん |
| する | しない | せえへん / せん | しない | せえへん / せん |
| 来る | 来ない（こない） | けえへん / こん | 来ない | けえへん / こん |
| ある | ない | あらへん / ない | ない | あらへん / ない |

**Important variation:** The negative conjugation differs even within Kansai. Osaka tends toward ～へん, while parts of Kyoto and Shiga may use ～ひん (食べひん instead of 食べへん). Kobe sometimes uses ～ん exclusively.

> 標準語：「明日、行かないの？」
> Standard: "You're not going tomorrow?"

> 大阪弁：「明日、行かへんの？」
> Osaka: "You're not going tomorrow?"

> 京都弁：「明日、行かひんの？」
> Kyoto: "You're not going tomorrow?"

### Formation Table: The Honorific ～はる

Kansai has its own honorific system built around the suffix ～はる (attached to the verb stem). This is lighter than standard 尊敬語 — it conveys respect and social distance without the full weight of お～になる or いらっしゃる.

| Standard keigo | Kansai ～はる | Standard plain | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| いらっしゃる / お行きになる | 行かはる / 行きはる | 行く | to go |
| 召し上がる / お食べになる | 食べはる | 食べる | to eat |
| なさる / おする | しはる | する | to do |
| いらっしゃる / おいでになる | いてはる / いはる | いる | to be |
| おっしゃる / お言いになる | 言わはる / 言いはる | 言う | to say |

> 標準語：「先生はもうお帰りになりました。」
> Standard: "The teacher has already gone home."

> 関西弁：「先生、もう帰らはったわ。」
> Kansai: "The teacher already went home."

The ～はる form is ubiquitous in Kyoto, where it is used even for strangers and in moderately formal situations. In Osaka, it is used but somewhat less pervasively — Osaka speakers may mix ～はる with standard keigo forms depending on the formality of the situation.

**An important cultural note:** In Kyoto, ～はる can be applied to animals and even inanimate subjects (rain, for instance) as a general softener. In Osaka, applying ～はる to animals or things would sound unusual.

> 京都弁：「雨、降ってはるわ。」
> Kyoto: "It's raining." (lit: The rain is graciously falling.)

> 京都弁：「お猫さん、寝てはるわ。」
> Kyoto: "The cat is sleeping." (lit: The honorable cat is graciously sleeping.)

These uses would strike an Osaka speaker as distinctly Kyoto.

### Formation Table: Past Progressive ～てん / ～とった

| Standard | Kansai (Osaka) | Kansai (Kobe) | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| ～ていた | ～てん / ～ててん | ～とった / ～とう | was doing |
| 食べていた | 食べてん / 食べててん | 食べとった / 食べとう | was eating |
| 見ていた | 見てん / 見ててん | 見とった / 見とう | was watching |
| 知っていた | 知っててん | 知っとった / 知っとう | knew |
| 行っていた | 行っててん | 行っとった | had been going |

> 標準語：「昨日、何してたの？」
> Standard: "What were you doing yesterday?"

> 大阪弁：「昨日、何してたん？」/ 「何しててん？」
> Osaka: "What were you doing yesterday?"

> 神戸弁：「昨日、何しとったん？」
> Kobe: "What were you doing yesterday?"

### Adjective Forms

### Comparison Table: Adjective Differences

| Standard | Kansai | Meaning | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| おもしろい | おもろい | interesting, funny | Shortened; very common |
| つまらない | おもんない / しょうもない | boring, worthless | おもんない = おもしろくない contracted |
| すごい | えらい / すごい | amazing, terrible | えらい has broader meaning in Kansai |
| だめ | あかん | no good | Kansai's most iconic word |
| こわい | こわい / おっかない | scary | おっかない is more Tohoku/Kanto |
| ありがたい | ありがたい / おおきに | thankful | おおきに = thank you |

> 標準語：「この映画、面白くない。つまらない。」
> Standard: "This movie isn't interesting. It's boring."

> 関西弁：「この映画、おもんない。しょうもないわ。」
> Kansai: "This movie isn't funny. It's worthless."

### The Copula: や vs だ

Perhaps the single most recognizable feature of Kansai speech is the copula や replacing standard だ.

### Formation Table: Copula Comparison

| Standard | Kansai | Context |
|---|---|---|
| だ | や | Plain assertion |
| だった | やった | Past |
| だろう | やろう | Conjecture |
| だから | やから | Because |
| だけど | やけど | But |
| なの？ | なん？ | Question (explanatory) |
| でしょう？ | やろ？ | Seeking agreement |
| ではない / じゃない | やない / ちゃう | Negative |

> 標準語：「これは本当だろう？」
> Standard: "This is true, isn't it?"

> 関西弁：「これほんまやろ？」
> Kansai: "This is true, right?"

Note: ほんま (= 本当) is another Kansai staple. ほんまに = 本当に (really).

> 標準語：「違うよ。そうじゃないよ。」
> Standard: "That's wrong. It's not like that."

> 関西弁：「ちゃうで。そうちゃうで。」
> Kansai: "Nope. That ain't it."

ちゃう (= 違う, and also the Kansai negative copula) is another high-frequency form.

### Particles

### Comparison Table: Particle Differences

| Standard | Kansai | Function | Example (Kansai) |
|---|---|---|---|
| ～から | ～さかい / ～から | Reason | 暑いさかい窓開けよう |
| ～ので | ～よって / ～さかい | Reason (softer) | 遅れるよって先行ってて |
| ～している | ～しとる / ～してる | Progressive | 何しとるん？ |
| ～てしまう | ～てまう / ～てもうた | Completion/regret | 食べてもうた |
| ～なければ | ～なあかん | Must do | 行かなあかん |
| ～ではないか | ～やんか | Isn't it? | ええやんか |

> 標準語：「食べてしまった。もう行かなければならない。」
> Standard: "I've eaten it all. I have to go now."

> 関西弁：「食べてもうた。もう行かなあかん。」
> Kansai: "Ate it all up. Gotta go now."

### Pitch Accent Differences

Kansai Japanese uses a different pitch accent system from Tokyo Japanese. This is one of the most difficult features to master but also one of the most immediately identifiable.

### Comparison Table: Pitch Accent — Tokyo vs Kansai

| Word | Tokyo pitch | Kansai pitch | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| 橋 | は↑し（①） | は↓し（②） | bridge |
| 箸 | は↓し（②） | は↑し（①） | chopsticks |
| 雨 | あ↑め（①） | あ↓め（②） | rain |
| 飴 | あ↓め（②） | あ↑め（①） | candy |
| 川 | か↓わ（②） | か↑わ（①） | river |
| 赤い | あ↑かい | あ↓かい | red |
| 高い | た↑かい | た↓かい | expensive/tall |

This is not random variation — Kansai pitch accent follows its own systematic rules. In many cases, the Kansai pattern is actually closer to historical Japanese pitch accent, and the Tokyo pattern represents the innovation.

### Extended Kansai Dialogue

**Setting:** Two Osaka friends, Yuki (female, 30s) and Mika (female, 30s), discussing weekend plans.

> ゆき：「なあ、今週末ひま？」
> Yuki: "Hey, are you free this weekend?"
>
> みか：「ん？土曜はバイトやけど、日曜は空いてるで。なんで？」
> Mika: "Hm? I'm working Saturday, but Sunday I'm free. Why?"
>
> ゆき：「天王寺に新しいカフェできてん。めっちゃおしゃれらしいわ。行かへん？」
> Yuki: "A new cafe opened in Tennoji. It's apparently super stylish. Wanna go?"
>
> みか：「ええやん。行こ行こ。何時くらいがいい？」
> Mika: "Nice. Let's go. What time works?"
>
> ゆき：「うーん、昼くらい？ランチもそこで食べよか。」
> Yuki: "Hmm, around noon? We could eat lunch there too."
>
> みか：「おっけー。でも人気のとこやったら並ぶんちゃう？予約した方がええんちゃう？」
> Mika: "Okay. But if it's popular, won't there be a line? Shouldn't we make a reservation?"
>
> ゆき：「あー、確かに。ほんなら予約しとくわ。あとで店の名前送るな。」
> Yuki: "Ah, good point. Then I'll make a reservation. I'll send you the shop name later."
>
> みか：「りょーかい。楽しみやわ。あ、そういえば、この前の合コンどうやった？」
> Mika: "Got it. I'm excited. Oh, by the way, how was that mixer the other day?"
>
> ゆき：「あかんかったわ。全然あかん。おもんない人ばっかりやってん。」
> Yuki: "No good. Totally no good. Everyone was boring."
>
> みか：「うそやん。かわいそう。まあ、ええ人見つかるよ、そのうち。」
> Mika: "No way. Poor you. Well, you'll find someone good eventually."
>
> ゆき：「やろ？まあ、焦ってもしゃあないしな。」
> Yuki: "Right? Well, there's no point in rushing."

## 8.2 東北弁 Features for Recognition

Tohoku dialect (東北弁, とうほくべん) refers to the varieties spoken in Japan's northeastern region (Aomori, Iwate, Miyagi, Akita, Yamagata, Fukushima). Tohoku dialects are among the most difficult for standard Japanese speakers to understand, and they carry strong stereotypical associations — rural, agricultural, warm but unsophisticated. These stereotypes are unfair but culturally pervasive, and understanding them helps you interpret how dialect is used in media.

For the learner, the goal is recognition rather than production. You are unlikely to need to speak Tohoku dialect, but you should be able to recognize its major features when you hear them.

### Phonological Features

The most striking feature of Tohoku speech is vowel merging. Standard Japanese has five vowels (あ い う え お), but in strong Tohoku dialect, い and え frequently merge, as do し and す, ち and つ, じ and ず.

### Comparison Table: Tohoku Vowel and Consonant Merging

| Standard pronunciation | Tohoku pronunciation | Meaning | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| いち (ichi) | えち / えづ (echi / edzu) | one | い → え |
| すし (sushi) | すす (susu) | sushi | し → す |
| 知事 (chiji) | つず (tsuzu) | governor | ち → つ, じ → ず |
| 七 (shichi) | すつ (sutsu) | seven | し → す, ち → つ |
| 寿司 (sushi) と 煤 (susu) | Both すす | sushi / soot | Merged |

This merging is what makes Tohoku speech so difficult for outsiders. The distinction between し/す, ち/つ, じ/ず — which are separate phonemes in standard Japanese — collapses.

### Grammatical Features

### Formation Table: Tohoku Grammatical Markers

| Feature | Standard | Tohoku | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| Copula (assertion) | ～だ | ～だべ / ～だっぺ | そうだべ = そうだろう |
| Tag question | ～でしょう / ～だろう | ～べ？/ ～っぺ？ | 行くべ？ = 行くだろう？ |
| Completion | ～てしまう | ～ちゃう / ～ちまう | 食っちまった = 食べてしまった |
| Progressive | ～ている | ～でる / ～てら | 何してら？= 何してる？ |
| Emphasis | ～のだ | ～のっしゃ / ～んだべ | 行くんだべ = 行くんだろう |
| Prohibition | ～てはいけない | ～ちゃなんね | やっちゃなんねぞ = やってはいけないぞ |
| Let's | ～よう | ～べ | 行くべ = 行こう |

> 標準語：「明日は天気がいいだろう。海に行こうよ。」
> Standard: "The weather will probably be good tomorrow. Let's go to the beach."

> 東北弁：「明日はてんき、いいべ。海さ行くべ。」
> Tohoku: "Weather'll be good tomorrow. Let's go to the beach."

Note: さ replacing に/へ as a directional particle is a characteristic Tohoku feature. 海さ行く = 海に行く.

### Sentence-Final ～っちゃ

The particle ～っちゃ (a contraction of ～というのは or a general emphatic) is associated with some Tohoku and northern dialects.

> 「そんなこと言ったっちゃ、しょうがないっちゃ。」
> "Even if you say that, it can't be helped."

### Vocabulary

### Comparison Table: Tohoku Vocabulary

| Tohoku | Standard | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| めんこい | かわいい | cute |
| しょす / しょっぱい | 恥ずかしい | embarrassed |
| おばんです | こんばんは | good evening |
| け | 食べなさい / 来なさい | eat / come (imperative) |
| く | 食べる | to eat |
| さ | に / へ | directional particle |
| んだ | そうだ | that's right |
| んだがら | だから | so, therefore |

> 東北弁での短い会話：
>
> A：「おばんです。寒いべ。」
> A: "Good evening. It's cold, isn't it."
>
> B：「んだ、んだ。今年は雪多いべな。」
> B: "Yeah, yeah. There'll be a lot of snow this year, right."
>
> A：「まず、中さ入って、あったまっていけ。」
> A: "Well, come inside and warm up."
>
> B：「んだな。ありがとな。」
> B: "Yeah. Thanks."

## 8.3 九州弁 Features for Recognition

Kyushu dialect (九州弁, きゅうしゅうべん) encompasses the varieties spoken across Kyushu's seven prefectures. Like Tohoku, there is enormous internal variation — Hakata-ben (Fukuoka), Kumamoto-ben, Kagoshima-ben, and Nagasaki-ben differ significantly from each other. The following focuses on features most commonly encountered, with emphasis on Hakata/Fukuoka forms that have the widest media presence.

### Sentence-Final Particles

Kyushu dialects are known for distinctive sentence-final particles.

### Formation Table: Kyushu Sentence-Final Particles

| Particle | Region | Function | Example | Standard equivalent |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| ～ばい | Fukuoka/Hakata | Assertion (mild) | うまかばい | うまいよ |
| ～たい | Fukuoka/Hakata | Assertion/explanation | そうたい | そうだよ |
| ～と | Fukuoka/Kumamoto | Question / assertion | 何しよると？ | 何してるの？ |
| ～けん | Fukuoka/broad Kyushu | Reason (= から) | 暑いけん窓開けて | 暑いから窓開けて |
| ～ちゃ | Kitakyushu | Emphasis | 知っとっちゃ | 知ってるよ |
| ～もん | Kumamoto | Emphasis | 行くもん | 行くもん (similar) |
| ～ど / ～ぞ | Kagoshima | Strong assertion | 行くど | 行くぞ |

> 標準語：「何をしているの？おいしいよ、これ。暑いから窓を開けて。」
> Standard: "What are you doing? This is delicious. Open the window, it's hot."

> 博多弁：「何しよると？うまかばい、これ。暑いけん窓開けて。」
> Hakata: "What are you doing? This is tasty. Open the window, it's hot."

### Verb and Adjective Forms

### Comparison Table: Kyushu Verb/Adjective Forms

| Standard | Kyushu (Hakata) | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| ～ている | ～とる / ～よる | ～よる = ongoing action; ～とる = resultant state |
| ～ない | ～ん | 食べん = 食べない |
| ～ではない | ～やない / ～じゃない | Similar to Kansai |
| おいしい | うまか | い-adjective → か (from classical し → か) |
| すごい | すごか | Same pattern |
| 寒い | さむか | Same pattern |
| 大きい | おおきか | Same pattern |
| ～だろう | ～やろう / ～やろ | Similar to Kansai |

**The ～よる vs ～とる distinction** is a feature of Kyushu (and some western) dialects that standard Japanese lacks. Standard ～ている covers both ongoing actions and resultant states, but Kyushu splits these:

> 雨が降りよる。 (It is currently raining — ongoing action)
> 雨が降っとる。 (It has rained / it is in a state of having rained — resultant state)

> 標準語では両方「雨が降っている」
> In standard Japanese, both are 「雨が降っている」

This distinction is grammatically precise and expressive — it is one case where dialect provides a tool that standard Japanese lacks.

### Kyushu Dialogue

**Setting:** Two Hakata friends, Taku (male, 20s) and Ken (male, 20s), at a ramen shop.

> たく：「ここのラーメン、うまかばい。初めて来たと？」
> Taku: "The ramen here is great. Is this your first time?"
>
> けん：「いや、前にも来たことあるばい。でも久しぶりやけん、楽しみやったっちゃんね。」
> Ken: "Nah, I've been before. But it's been a while, so I was looking forward to it."
>
> たく：「やっぱ博多のラーメンが一番たい。東京のは全然ちがうけんね。」
> Taku: "Hakata ramen is the best after all. Tokyo's is totally different."
>
> けん：「わかる。東京におった時、ラーメン屋行っても何か違うと思いよった。」
> Ken: "I know. When I was in Tokyo, even when I went to ramen shops, I kept thinking something was off."
>
> たく：「そうやろ？味噌とか醤油とかもうまかけど、やっぱ豚骨やないと。」
> Taku: "Right? Miso and soy sauce are good too, but it's gotta be tonkotsu."
>
> けん：「ほんなこつ、そうたい。」
> Ken: "That's the truth."

| Kyushu form | Standard | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| うまか | おいしい / うまい | delicious |
| ～と？ | ～の？ | question marker |
| ～ばい | ～よ | assertion |
| ～やけん | ～だから | because |
| ～たい | ～だよ | assertion/explanation |

## 8.4 北海道弁 Features

Hokkaido dialect (北海道弁, ほっかいどうべん) is one of the "younger" dialects of Japanese, having developed primarily since the Meiji-era colonization of Hokkaido. It blends features from various mainland dialects brought by settlers (particularly from Tohoku and northern Honshu) with local innovations.

### Key Features

### Formation Table: Hokkaido Dialect Features

| Feature | Hokkaido | Standard | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| ～しょ | Tag question / confirmation | ～でしょう | いいしょ？= いいでしょう？ |
| なまら | Very, extremely | すごく / とても | なまらうまい = すごくおいしい |
| ～っしょ | Agreement-seeking | ～でしょう | そうっしょ？= そうでしょう？ |
| したっけ | Then / goodbye | それじゃ / そしたら | したっけ、また明日 = じゃ、また明日 |
| ～かい | Question | ～か / ～の | 行くかい？= 行くの？ |
| わや | Terrible, messed up | めちゃくちゃ / ひどい | わやだ = ひどい状態だ |
| ばくる | To exchange | 交換する | ばくって = 交換して |
| こわい | Tired | 疲れた | こわい = 疲れた (NOT 怖い) |
| ゴミを投げる | To throw away trash | ゴミを捨てる | ゴミ投げといて = ゴミ捨てておいて |

**Warning:** こわい in Hokkaido means "tired," not "scary." This is one of the most famous false friends within Japanese dialects.

> 標準語：「すごく疲れた。ゴミ捨てといてくれる？」
> Standard: "I'm really tired. Can you throw out the trash?"

> 北海道弁：「なまらこわい。ゴミ投げといてくれる？」
> Hokkaido: "I'm really tired. Can you throw out the trash?"

A non-Hokkaido speaker hearing なまらこわい might interpret it as "really scary."

### Hokkaido Dialogue

**Setting:** Two Hokkaido friends chatting after work.

> A：「今日、なまらこわかった。残業3時間もしたっしょ。」
> A: "I'm so tired today. I did three hours of overtime."
>
> B：「わや。大変だったっしょ。」
> B: "That's rough. Must have been tough."
>
> A：「したっけさ、帰ったらまた雪かきしなきゃなんないべ。」
> A: "And then when I get home I gotta shovel snow again."
>
> B：「まあ、しょうがないしょ。北海道だもん。」
> B: "Well, can't be helped. It's Hokkaido."
>
> A：「そうっしょ。まあ、ビール飲んでから頑張るわ。」
> A: "Right. Well, I'll have a beer first and then get to it."
>
> B：「いいね。じゃ、したっけね。」
> B: "Good plan. Well, see you."

## 8.5 Code-Switching: When and Why Speakers Shift

Most dialect speakers in Japan are bidialectal — they can speak both their regional variety and standard Japanese, and they switch between them depending on the context. This code-switching (コードスイッチング, or 方言切り替え, ほうげんきりかえ) is a rich area of sociolinguistic study and one that advanced learners should understand.

### When Speakers Use Standard Japanese

### Formation Table: Contexts Favoring Standard Japanese

| Context | Reason | Example situation |
|---|---|---|
| Formal speeches | Standard is the prestige variety | Wedding speeches, presentations |
| Speaking with non-locals | Mutual intelligibility | Talking to someone from another region |
| Business/professional | National workplace norms | Phone calls with Tokyo headquarters |
| Writing | Written standard is universal | Business emails, reports |
| Media appearances | Broadcasting norms | News reading, formal interviews |
| Speaking to foreigners | Assumption that foreigners learned standard | Tourist interactions |

### When Speakers Use Dialect

### Formation Table: Contexts Favoring Dialect

| Context | Reason | Example situation |
|---|---|---|
| Family at home | Intimacy, identity | Dinner conversation |
| Friends from same region | Solidarity, naturalness | Casual hangout |
| Emotional moments | Dialect is the "heart language" | Anger, grief, joy |
| Humor | Dialect has comedic/warm associations | Telling jokes, banter |
| Regional identity display | Pride, cultural marking | Local festivals, community events |
| Deliberate characterization | Social signaling | Establishing in-group status |

### The Emotional Dimension

A widely observed phenomenon is that speakers who normally use standard Japanese may revert to dialect in moments of strong emotion. This is because dialect is typically the language of childhood and intimate relationships — the language closest to the emotional core.

> 普段（標準語）：「すみません、ちょっと困ります。」
> Normally (standard): "Excuse me, that's a bit of a problem."
>
> 怒った時（関西弁が出る）：「ちょっと待てや！なにしとんねん！」
> When angry (Kansai emerges): "Hold on! What do you think you're doing!"

> 普段（標準語）：「ありがとうございます。とても嬉しいです。」
> Normally (standard): "Thank you very much. I'm very happy."
>
> 感動した時（東北弁が出る）：「ありがどな……ほんとにうれしいんだ……。」
> When moved (Tohoku emerges): "Thank you... I'm really happy..."

### Code-Switching Dialogue

**Setting:** Sato Yumi, originally from Osaka, works at a Tokyo company. We follow her through different interactions.

**Scene 1: Business call with a client (standard Japanese)**

> 佐藤：「はい、株式会社〇〇の佐藤でございます。いつもお世話になっております。はい、承知いたしました。それでは、来週の火曜日にお伺いいたします。」
> Sato: "Yes, this is Sato from ○○ Corporation. Thank you for your continued support. Yes, understood. I will visit you next Tuesday."

No trace of Kansai dialect. Pure standard keigo.

**Scene 2: Chat with Tokyo coworker (standard casual)**

> 佐藤：「ねえ、今日のランチどうする？新しいイタリアンできたらしいよ。」
> Sato: "Hey, what are we doing for lunch? Apparently a new Italian place opened."

Still standard Japanese, but casual. She fits in with her Tokyo colleagues.

**Scene 3: Phone call with her mother in Osaka (full Kansai)**

> 佐藤：「あ、お母さん？うん、元気やで。仕事？まあまあやな。忙しいけど、なんとかやっとるわ。え、お父さん風邪？あかんやん。ちゃんと病院行かせてよ。うん、うん。ほんなら、また電話するわな。」
> Sato: "Oh, Mom? Yeah, I'm fine. Work? So-so. Busy, but I'm managing. What, Dad has a cold? That's no good. Make him go to the hospital. Yeah, yeah. Well then, I'll call again."

Complete switch to Kansai. The や copula, やで, やな, あかん, やっとる — all the features from Section 8.1 are present.

**Scene 4: After-work drinks with another Osaka transplant (Kansai resurfaces)**

> 佐藤：「なあ、東京の人ってさ、なんでこんなに冷たいん？大阪やったらもっとフレンドリーやのに。」
> Sato: "You know, why are Tokyo people so cold? In Osaka people would be friendlier."
>
> 同僚（大阪出身）：「わかるわ〜。おれも最初びっくりしたもん。電車で誰も話さへんやん。」
> Colleague (from Osaka): "I know, right. I was surprised at first too. Nobody talks on the train."
>
> 佐藤：「ほんまにな。まあ、慣れたけど。たまに関西弁出ると、みんな『え、関西の人？』って聞いてくるのおもろいけど。」
> Sato: "For real. Well, I'm used to it now. But it's funny when my Kansai slips out and everyone goes 'Oh, you're from Kansai?'"

### Key Observations About Code-Switching

1. **The switch is not random.** It follows clear social logic: formality, audience, emotional state, and topic all influence the choice.

2. **It is often unconscious.** Speakers may not realize they have switched until someone points it out. The phone call to Mom triggers automatic dialect use.

3. **It is a sign of competence, not confusion.** Bidialectal speakers are deploying two linguistic systems strategically. This is a skill, not a deficit.

4. **Partial switching is common.** Speakers may use standard grammar with dialect vocabulary (or vice versa), creating a blended register rather than cleanly switching between two systems.

5. **Direction matters.** Switching from standard to dialect signals intimacy, emotion, or solidarity. Switching from dialect to standard signals formality, distance, or accommodation to outsiders.

## 8.6 Dialect in Media — Characterization Through Regional Speech

Japanese media uses dialect as a powerful characterization tool. Knowing the conventions helps you interpret character traits that are immediately obvious to Japanese audiences but invisible to learners who have not studied regional variation.

### Common Dialect-Character Associations in Media

### Comparison Table: Dialect Stereotypes in Media

| Dialect | Character type | Stereotype | Example characters |
|---|---|---|---|
| 関西弁 (Osaka) | Comedian, merchant, loud friend | Funny, shrewd, energetic, pushy | Countless comedy characters |
| 関西弁 (Kyoto) | Refined woman, scheming politician | Elegant, indirect, potentially two-faced | Maiko/geisha characters, political dramas |
| 東北弁 | Rural character, honest farmer | Simple, honest, warm, unsophisticated | Many NHK drama characters |
| 九州弁 (Hakata) | Tough but warm man/woman | Direct, passionate, loyal | Action/drama characters from Kyushu |
| 広島弁 | Yakuza, tough guy | Aggressive, intimidating | Yakuza film characters |
| 北海道弁 | Down-to-earth character | Easygoing, hardworking, plain | Characters in Hokkaido-set dramas |
| 沖縄弁 | Free-spirited character | Laid-back, warm, spiritual | Characters in Okinawa-set stories |
| 名古屋弁 | Comic relief, eccentric | Quirky, distinctive, sometimes provincial | Often played for laughs |

### The Hiroshima-Yakuza Connection

One of the strongest dialect-character associations in Japanese media is the use of Hiroshima dialect (広島弁, ひろしまべん) for yakuza characters. This stems from the real-world historical presence of yakuza organizations in the Hiroshima area and was cemented by the hugely influential 仁義なき戦い (Battles Without Honor and Humanity) film series of the 1970s.

Key Hiroshima features used in yakuza portrayals:

> 「わりゃあ、なに言うとるんじゃ。」(= お前は何を言っているんだ)
> "What do you think you're saying?"

> 「殺すぞ、こりゃあ。」(= 殺すぞ、こいつ)
> "I'll kill you."

The じゃ copula, the rough contracted pronouns (わりゃあ = われは, こりゃあ = こいつは), and the aggressive intonation have become so associated with yakuza characters that using Hiroshima dialect in fiction almost automatically signals violence or criminal activity — regardless of whether the actual character is from Hiroshima.

### Kyoto Dialect and Indirect Communication

Kyoto speech in media often signals refinement, tradition, and — in some portrayals — hidden meaning or passive aggression. The stereotype of Kyoto people saying one thing and meaning another (京都人の本音と建前, きょうとじんのほんねとたてまえ) is heavily referenced in media.

> テレビドラマの京都の女将：
> 「まあ、元気なお子さんやこと。」
> TV drama, Kyoto innkeeper: "My, what an energetic child."

Surface meaning: a compliment about the child's vitality.
Implied meaning (according to the stereotype): "Your child is too noisy. Control them."

> 「ええ時計してはりますなあ。」
> "What a nice watch you have."

Surface meaning: admiring the watch.
Implied meaning (stereotype): "You've been here too long. Look at the time and leave."

**Important caveat:** These are stereotypes amplified by media. Real Kyoto residents do not constantly speak in code. But the trope is so well-established that any media portrayal of a Kyoto character is likely to reference it.

### Kansai Dialect in Comedy

The association between Kansai dialect and comedy (お笑い, おわらい) is deep. Many of Japan's most famous comedians are from Kansai, and the manzai (漫才, まんざい) tradition of paired comedy is rooted in Osaka. As a result, Kansai dialect in media often signals humor, even when the character is not a comedian.

Key comedy-related Kansai features:

> ツッコミ（突っ込み）: The "straight man" response
> 「なんでやねん！」 "Why the heck is that!" (Classic tsukkomi phrase)
>
> ボケ: The "funny man" setup
> 「ほら、あの有名な人おるやん。えーっと、誰やっけ……オバマ大統領？」
> "You know that famous person? Um, who was it... President Obama?"
>
> ツッコミ：「ちゃうわ！」
> Tsukkomi: "No it's not!"

### How to Use This Knowledge

1. **When watching media,** pay attention to which characters speak in dialect and which speak standard. The choice is almost never random — it encodes information about the character's background, personality, and role in the story.

2. **Be aware of the stereotypes** so you can recognize them, but do not internalize them as facts about real people. Not all Osaka people are funny; not all Kyoto people are passive-aggressive; not all Hiroshima people are threatening.

3. **Notice when media subverts expectations.** A yakuza character who speaks elegant Kyoto dialect, or a refined professor who speaks broad Tohoku, creates interest precisely because it violates the expected associations. These subversions are sophisticated storytelling devices.

## 8.7 Listening Practice Notes — Recommended Media for Dialect Exposure

The best way to develop recognition of regional variation is through exposure to natural or naturalistic dialect use in media. The following recommendations are organized by dialect.

### 関西弁

**Television:**
- お笑い番組 (comedy shows): Any show featuring Kansai comedians provides extensive exposure. Look for 吉本新喜劇 (Yoshimoto Shin-Kigeki) for broad Osaka comedy with heavy dialect.
- ケンミンSHOW (Kenmin Show): A variety show specifically about regional differences across Japan.

**Film:**
- Movies set in Osaka or Kobe naturally feature Kansai speech. Look for works by directors from the region.

**Podcasts/YouTube:**
- Channels run by Kansai-based creators. The casual format means dialect appears naturally.

### 東北弁

**Television:**
- NHK連続テレビ小説 (morning dramas) set in Tohoku regions. These are carefully researched and use coached dialect.
- Documentaries about the 2011 earthquake and its aftermath often feature Tohoku speakers.

**Film:**
- Films set in rural Tohoku. The natural speech of local actors provides authentic exposure.

### 九州弁

**Television:**
- Dramas set in Fukuoka or Kyushu. Look for local broadcast station content.

**Film:**
- Films by Kyushu-based directors or set in Kyushu locations.

### 北海道弁

**Television:**
- 北の国から (Kita no Kuni kara / From the North Country): A classic drama series set in rural Hokkaido.
- Any content set in Hokkaido will feature some dialect elements.

### 広島弁

**Film:**
- 仁義なき戦い (Battles Without Honor and Humanity): The classic yakuza film series. Heavy Hiroshima dialect throughout.
- この世界の片隅に (In This Corner of the World): An anime film set in wartime Hiroshima with careful dialect representation.

### General Advice for Dialect Listening

1. **Start with Kansai.** It is the most commonly encountered dialect, and exposure is easiest to find. Once you can recognize Kansai features, other dialects become easier to parse by contrast.

2. **Use subtitles strategically.** Watch first with Japanese subtitles (which are usually in standard Japanese — note the gap between what you hear and what you read). Then rewatch without subtitles.

3. **Do not try to learn all dialects actively.** Your goal is recognition, not production. Focus on being able to identify which dialect a speaker is using and understanding the general meaning, even if you miss some words.

4. **Listen for the features you have studied.** When you hear ～へん, ～はる, or や as a copula, note it mentally. When you hear ～ばい or ～けん, register it as Kyushu. This active recognition practice builds the pattern-matching ability you need.

## 8.10 Historical Context: Dialect and Standardization

Understanding why Japan has a "standard language" and how it was created sheds light on the current sociolinguistic situation.

### The Meiji Standardization Project

Before the Meiji period (1868-1912), there was no single "standard Japanese." People spoke their regional varieties, and written Japanese was based on classical grammar that did not reflect any spoken dialect. The Meiji government, in its drive to create a modern nation-state, recognized the need for a unified national language and launched the 言文一致 (げんぶんいっち, "unification of speech and writing") movement.

The variety chosen as the basis for standard Japanese was the educated speech of Tokyo — specifically, the speech of the 山の手 (やまのて, the upscale western hillside areas of Tokyo). This was a political decision, not a linguistic one: Tokyo speech was not "better" or "more correct" than other varieties, but Tokyo was the capital and the seat of power.

### 方言札 — Dialect Punishment Tags

In the most extreme phase of standardization (particularly during the early twentieth century and the wartime period), some schools in dialect-heavy regions used 方言札 (ほうげんふだ) — punishment tags that students had to wear if caught speaking dialect in school. This practice was particularly widespread in Okinawa and Tohoku. It caused significant psychological harm and contributed to dialect shame (方言コンプレックス, ほうげんコンプレックス) that persists in some communities to this day.

> 「学校では標準語を話しなさい。方言は恥ずかしいものです。」
> "Speak standard Japanese at school. Dialect is shameful."

This attitude is now widely recognized as harmful and linguistically unfounded, but its legacy shapes how some older speakers feel about their own dialect.

### The Modern Reversal

Since the late twentieth century, attitudes toward dialect have shifted dramatically. Dialect is now often seen as charming, warm, and authentic — a mark of regional identity to be proud of. Television shows celebrate regional differences, and young people sometimes adopt dialect features from regions they have never lived in (see Chapter 7 on the Kansai pipeline). The term 方言萌え (ほうげんもえ) describes the phenomenon of finding dialect speech attractive or endearing.

> 「方言っていいよね。なんか温かい感じがする。」
> "Dialect is nice, isn't it. It feels kinda warm."

This reversal is incomplete — dialect shame persists in some contexts, and speakers may still switch to standard when they want to be taken seriously in professional settings. But the overall trend is toward greater acceptance and even celebration of regional variation.

## 8.11 Summary

### Key Takeaways

1. **Japan has enormous regional linguistic diversity.** The standard-language dominance in education and media can obscure this, but dialect is alive and actively used throughout the country.

2. **Kansai dialect** has the highest cultural visibility and is the most important variety for learners to recognize. Its features — the や copula, ～へん negation, ～はる honorifics, distinctive pitch accent — are the most commonly encountered non-standard forms.

3. **Tohoku, Kyushu, and Hokkaido dialects** have distinctive features that are worth knowing for recognition. You do not need to produce these forms, but you should be able to identify them and understand their general meaning.

4. **Code-switching is normal and strategic.** Most dialect speakers switch between dialect and standard depending on context, audience, and emotional state. This is a sign of linguistic competence.

5. **Media uses dialect as a characterization tool,** and the associations (Kansai = comedy, Hiroshima = yakuza, Kyoto = refinement) are powerful stereotypes that you should recognize but not internalize as facts.

6. **Pitch accent varies by region,** and Kansai pitch accent in particular systematically differs from Tokyo pitch accent. This is one of the most immediately identifiable dialect features.

7. **Your goal as a learner is recognition, not production.** Unless you live in a specific region and want to integrate linguistically, your focus should be on understanding dialect when you encounter it, not on speaking it yourself.
