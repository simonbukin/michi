# Chapter 17 — Pre-Modern and Early Modern Literature

Japanese literature did not begin in the twentieth century, but for the language learner approaching novels, the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries represent a practical starting point. The writers of the Meiji era (1868–1912) and Taisho era (1912–1926) created modern Japanese literary prose — the ancestor of the language you read today. Their work is challenging but not impenetrable, and the effort of reading them yields a depth of understanding that no amount of textbook study can provide.

This chapter focuses on two giants of the pre-modern and early modern period: 夏目漱石 (Natsume Soseki, 1867–1916) and 芥川龍之介 (Akutagawa Ryunosuke, 1892–1927). For each author, we examine the defining features of their style, then work through an extended original passage composed in that style, with full annotation and translation.

## 17.1 夏目漱石: Style Features, Vocabulary, the Meiji Intellectual Voice

### The Writer

Natsume Soseki is arguably the most important figure in modern Japanese literature. A scholar of English literature who studied in London, he returned to Japan and produced a body of work that defined what it meant to write a novel in Japanese. His major works include 吾輩は猫である (I Am a Cat, 1905), 坊っちゃん (Botchan, 1906), こころ (Kokoro, 1914), and 明暗 (Light and Darkness, 1916, unfinished).

### Defining Style Features

| Feature | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Intellectual narration | Narrators are educated men who think in abstractions | Frequent use of terms like 意識, 自覚, 矛盾, 人間 |
| Sino-Japanese compound density | Heavy use of 漢語 (kango) for precision and formality | 苦悶, 煩悶, 懊悩 — multiple words for mental anguish |
| Psychological interiority | Long passages of self-analysis and moral reasoning | Extended interior monologues about duty, love, guilt |
| Classical grammar remnants | Occasional use of classical verb forms and particles | ～ざるを得ない, ～たるもの, ～べし |
| Measured sentence rhythm | Balanced, architectonic sentences; parallel structures | Often builds to a moral or philosophical observation |
| Ironic distance | The narrator observes himself and others with analytical detachment | Self-aware commentary on his own feelings |

### The Meiji Intellectual Voice

Soseki's narrators are typically educated men caught between tradition and modernity, between Japanese and Western values, between social obligation and personal desire. They speak — and think — in a register that combines the precision of academic Sino-Japanese compounds with the intimacy of first-person reflection.

This voice has several characteristics that the reader must learn to recognize:

**1. Abstract vocabulary for concrete emotions**

Where a modern writer might say 悲しかった (I was sad), Soseki's narrator might write:

> 言いようのない寂寥の感が胸の底に沈殿していた。
> "An inexpressible feeling of desolation had settled at the bottom of my chest."

The emotion is named not with a simple adjective but with a Sino-Japanese compound (寂寥, "loneliness/desolation") wrapped in a metaphor (沈殿する, "to precipitate, to settle").

**2. Self-conscious narration**

Soseki's narrators frequently comment on their own inability to understand their feelings:

> 自分でも何故そう感じたのかわからなかった。ただ、そう感じたという事実だけが、動かしがたい確実さをもって、私の前に横たわっていた。
> "I myself did not understand why I felt that way. Only the fact that I did feel it lay before me with an immovable certainty."

**3. Moral gravity**

Soseki's prose carries an ethical weight. His characters are not merely unhappy — they are grappling with questions of right and wrong, sincerity and deception, duty and selfishness.

> 人間の心ほど当てにならないものはない。
> "Nothing is less reliable than the human heart."

### Soseki's Sentence Architecture

Soseki builds sentences in layers. A typical Soseki sentence might follow this pattern:

1. Establish a situation or observation
2. Qualify it with a concessive or conditional clause
3. Add an introspective comment
4. Land on a philosophical generalization

> 彼がそう言ったとき、私はすぐには答えなかった。答えたくなかったのではない。答えるべき言葉が、どうしても見つからなかったのである。人は時として、最も切実なことほど、言葉にすることができないものだ。
> "When he said that, I did not answer immediately. It was not that I did not want to answer. I simply could not find the words I should have said. At times, the more urgent a matter is, the less able a person is to put it into words."

Notice the architecture: situation (彼がそう言ったとき) → action (答えなかった) → negation of the obvious explanation (答えたくなかったのではない) → the real explanation (見つからなかったのである) → philosophical generalization (人は時として…).

The final sentence uses のである (explanatory copula, literary register) and ものだ (general truth), both characteristic of Soseki's essayistic voice.

### Key Grammatical Patterns in Soseki

| Pattern | Function | Example |
|---|---|---|
| のである / のだ | Explanatory; signals the narrator is explaining something to the reader | そう思ったのである |
| ものだ | General truth or nostalgic recollection | 人間とはそういうものだ |
| ざるを得ない | "Cannot help but..." (classical negative + 得る) | 認めざるを得なかった |
| ～たるもの | "One who is..." (classical attributive) | 学者たるもの |
| に相違ない | "There is no doubt that..." | 何かあったに相違ない |
| やむを得ず | "Having no choice but to..." | やむを得ず席を立った |
| ～して然るべきだ | "It is only right/natural that..." | 謝罪して然るべきだ |
| いかに…とはいえ | "However much...even so..." | いかに親しいとはいえ |

### Soseki's Use of である vs. だ vs. です

Soseki primarily writes in the である体 (dearu-tai) — the literary plain style that uses である as the copula. This choice itself is significant. In Soseki's time, three styles competed for dominance in Japanese fiction:

| Style | Copula | Register | Effect |
|---|---|---|---|
| である体 | である | Literary, authoritative | The narrator speaks as an essayist; measured, intellectual |
| だ体 | だ | Conversational plain | The narrator speaks casually; modern, direct |
| です・ます体 | です | Polite | The narrator addresses the reader as a social equal; intimate but formal |

Soseki's use of である creates a particular distance — the narrator is not chatting with you (だ) or being polite to you (です), but speaking with the authority of someone who has considered what they are about to say. When Soseki occasionally drops from である into だ, the shift signals a moment of emotional immediacy — the intellectual armor has slipped.

> 私はそれを聞いて黙っていた。何と答えていいかわからなかったのである。いや、わからなかったのではない。答えたくなかったのだ。
> "I heard this and remained silent. I did not know how to answer. No — it was not that I did not know. I did not want to answer."

Notice: のである (measured, explanatory) → のだ (raw, immediate). The shift from である to だ marks the moment the narrator stops explaining and starts feeling.

### Soseki's Prose Rhythm: The Long Sentence

Soseki's sentences often build through a pattern of qualification and extension. The following template captures a common structure:

**Template:** [Situation] + [Initial reaction] + [Qualification of reaction] + [Deeper reflection] + [Philosophical observation]

> 私は窓の外を眺めていた。何を考えていたわけでもない。ただ、何も考えていないということが、それ自体一つの思考の形態であるように感じられて、その空白の中に自分の存在の輪郭のようなものがぼんやりと浮かんでくるのを、不思議な心持ちで眺めていたのである。

> I was gazing out the window. I was not thinking about anything in particular. And yet, the very fact of thinking about nothing felt itself like a form of thought, and I watched with a strange feeling as something like the outline of my own existence floated vaguely into view within that blankness.

This sentence begins with a simple action (gazing), negates the obvious interpretation (I wasn't thinking about anything), discovers a paradox (not-thinking as a form of thinking), and ends with a metaphysical observation. This escalating structure is quintessentially Soseki.

## 17.2 Reading: Extended Passage in Soseki's Style

The following passage is an original composition written in a style inspired by Soseki's later novels — particularly the introspective, psychologically dense voice of works like こころ and 行人. It is not a copy or paraphrase of any existing work.

### 『沈黙の重さ』（原文）

> 　私が初めて彼に会ったのは、もう二十年以上も前のことである。場所は確か、本郷の古い下宿屋の二階であった。秋の終わりの、まだ冬というには早い、しかし既に夏の記憶が薄れてしまったような、中途半端な季節の午後のことである。
>
> 　彼は窓際に腰を掛けて、煙草を吸っていた。私がその部屋に通されたとき、彼はこちらを見もせずに、ただ煙の行方を目で追っているようであった。私は入口に立ったまま、挨拶の言葉を探した。が、どういうわけか、口が動かなかった。彼の周囲には、何か人を寄せ付けない空気が漂っていて、私はその空気に押し返されるような心持ちがしたのである。
>
> 　やがて彼は、吸いかけの煙草を灰皿に押しつけ、ゆっくりとこちらを向いた。
>
> 「あなたが田中君の紹介の方ですか」
>
> 　声は思いのほか穏やかであった。さきほどまで感じていた威圧感が、その一言で嘘のように消えた。私は慌てて頭を下げた。
>
> 「はい。先日お手紙を差し上げた者です。お忙しいところ恐れ入ります」
>
> 「忙しくなどありません。ご覧の通り、暇を持て余しているだけです」
>
> 　そう言って彼は微かに笑った。が、その笑いの奥に、私は一種の哀しみのようなものを感じ取った。それが何であるか、そのときの私にはわからなかった。ただ、この人には何かがある、何か深い、容易には触れさせてもらえない何かがある、という直感だけが、漠然と、しかし確かに、私の中に生じたのであった。
>
> 　後になって考えれば、あの直感は正しかった。彼が抱えていたもの——それは罪と呼ぶには重すぎ、悲しみと呼ぶには冷たすぎる、何か名前のない感情であった。彼はそれを二十年近く一人で抱えてきたのだ。誰にも打ち明けず、誰にも悟られないように、日々の表面を取り繕いながら、その重さを支え続けてきたのだ。
>
> 　人間は秘密を持つことによって孤独になる。だが同時に、秘密を持たない人間には、本当の意味での深さがないとも言える。この矛盾を、私はあの日以来、繰り返し考え続けている。答えは今もって出ない。おそらく、出ないまま私は死ぬのであろう。
>
> 　あの午後、彼が最初に見せた微笑みの意味を、私がようやく理解したのは、彼がこの世を去った後のことであった。あの笑いは、孤独な人間が、久しぶりに他者の存在を感じたときに、思わず漏らしてしまう、安堵と諦めの入り混じった笑いであったのだ。

### 『沈黙の重さ』（Translation: "The Weight of Silence"）

> It was more than twenty years ago that I first met him. The place, if I remember correctly, was the second floor of an old boarding house in Hongo. It was an afternoon in a season caught between — the end of autumn, too early to be called winter, yet summer's memory had already faded. A half-hearted sort of season.
>
> He was sitting by the window, smoking. When I was shown into the room, he did not so much as glance my way — he merely seemed to be following the smoke with his eyes. I stood in the doorway, searching for words of greeting. But for some reason, I could not make my mouth move. There was something in the air around him that kept people at a distance, and I felt as though I were being pushed back by that air.
>
> Presently he pressed his half-smoked cigarette into the ashtray and slowly turned to face me.
>
> "You are the one recommended by Tanaka, I take it?"
>
> His voice was unexpectedly gentle. The oppressive feeling I had sensed moments before vanished as if it had been a lie. I bowed my head hastily.
>
> "Yes. I am the one who wrote to you the other day. I apologize for intruding on your time."
>
> "I am not busy in the least. As you can see, I am merely a man with time to spare."
>
> Saying this, he smiled faintly. But behind that smile, I sensed something like a species of sorrow. What it was, I could not tell at the time. Only an intuition arose in me — vague, yet certain — that this man carried something: something deep, something that would not easily be revealed.
>
> Looking back, that intuition was correct. What he carried — it was too heavy to call guilt, too cold to call sadness. It was a nameless emotion. He had carried it alone for nearly twenty years. Without confiding in anyone, without letting anyone perceive it, maintaining the surface of daily life while bearing that weight.
>
> A person becomes lonely by keeping secrets. And yet one might also say that a person without secrets lacks true depth. I have thought about this contradiction repeatedly since that day. The answer has still not come. In all likelihood, I will die without finding it.
>
> It was only after he had departed this world that I finally understood the meaning of that first smile he showed me that afternoon. It was a smile that escapes involuntarily from a lonely person who, after a long absence, senses the presence of another human being — a smile mingling relief and resignation.

| 通された | とおされた | Was shown in, was led to | Passive of 通す, polite/formal |
| 寄せ付けない | よせつけない | Does not let approach; keeps at bay | |

| 恐れ入ります | おそれいります | I am sorry to trouble you (humble) | Formal set phrase |

### Style Analysis

This passage demonstrates several key Soseki features:

1. **The retrospective frame**: The narrator tells us from the start that this happened "more than twenty years ago," establishing temporal distance that allows for reflection.

2. **Layered sentence structure**: Sentences build through qualification: 「罪と呼ぶには重すぎ、悲しみと呼ぶには冷たすぎる、何か名前のない感情」 — three layers of description before the noun arrives.

3. **The のである ending**: Used five times, marking the narrator's explanatory, essayistic voice.

4. **Philosophical generalization**: The paragraph beginning 「人間は秘密を持つことによって孤独になる」moves from narrative into universal reflection — a characteristic Soseki gesture.

5. **The precision of emotional vocabulary**: Rather than simple emotion words, the passage uses compounds and circumlocutions: 哀しみのようなもの ("something like sadness"), 安堵と諦めの入り混じった ("mingling relief and resignation").

## 17.3 芥川龍之介: Style Features, the Ironic/Philosophical Narrator

### The Writer

Akutagawa Ryunosuke (1892–1927) is best known for short stories that reinterpret classical Japanese and Chinese tales through a modern, psychologically acute lens. His works include 羅生門 (Rashomon, 1915), 鼻 (The Nose, 1916), 藪の中 (In a Grove, 1922), and 河童 (Kappa, 1927). Japan's most prestigious literary prize, the 芥川賞, bears his name.

### Defining Style Features

| Feature | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Ironic distance | The narrator stands apart from characters, observing with cool detachment | Characters' self-deceptions are laid bare |
| Philosophical compression | Complex ideas packed into short, precise sentences | A single sentence may contain an entire moral argument |
| Classical framing | Stories set in historical periods, using period-appropriate language | Heian, Muromachi, and Edo settings are common |
| Psychological precision | Exact naming of mental states, often with clinical detachment | 彼は自分の虚栄心を意識した ("He became aware of his own vanity") |
| Structural elegance | Stories built around a single ironic reversal or revelation | The ending reframes everything that came before |
| Economy of description | Physical descriptions are sparse but telling | One detail stands for an entire character |

### The Ironic Narrator

Akutagawa's narrators are characterized by what might be called omniscient irony. They know more than the characters, and they let the reader know it — not through overt commentary, but through the careful selection of detail and the precise deployment of words that reveal more than the characters themselves realize.

Consider the difference between a neutral and an ironic narration of the same event:

**Neutral:**
> 彼は自分が正しいと信じて、門を出た。
> "Believing he was right, he left through the gate."

**Akutagawa-style ironic:**
> 彼は自分が正しいと信じていた。——少なくとも、彼自身はそう信じていた。
> "He believed he was right. — At least, he himself believed so."

The addition of 少なくとも彼自身は ("at least he himself") opens a gap between the character's self-perception and reality. The reader immediately understands that the character is wrong, without the narrator ever saying so directly. The dash (——) creates a pause, a moment of reconsideration, that is characteristic of Akutagawa's rhythm.

### Akutagawa's Philosophical Method

Akutagawa embeds philosophical observations within narrative, often through a technique of stating a general principle and then demonstrating it through a character's behavior.

> 人間の心には互いに矛盾した二つの感情が、同時に存在し得る。
> "In the human heart, two mutually contradictory emotions can exist simultaneously."

This kind of statement — abstract, authoritative, slightly clinical — is Akutagawa's signature. It positions the narrator not as a storyteller but as a moral philosopher who happens to be telling a story.

### Akutagawa's Characteristic Vocabulary

| Word | Reading | Meaning | Usage in Akutagawa |
|---|---|---|---|
| 嘲笑 | ちょうしょう | Mockery, ridicule | Characters mock each other — and are mocked by fate |
| 虚栄心 | きょえいしん | Vanity | A central theme: human self-deception |
| 利己主義 | りこしゅぎ | Egoism, selfishness | Characters act from self-interest while professing virtue |
| 偽善 | ぎぜん | Hypocrisy | The gap between appearance and reality |
| 良心 | りょうしん | Conscience | Often appears as something characters try to ignore |
| 漠然と | ばくぜんと | Vaguely | Characters' moral feelings are often vague, half-formed |
| 云う | いう | To say (archaic kanji for 言う) | Period-appropriate orthography |
| 唯 | ただ | Merely, only | Akutagawa frequently uses 唯 in kanji |
| 勿論 | もちろん | Of course | Formal register |
| 憐れ | あわれ | Pity, pathos | Classical aesthetic term |

### Key Grammatical Patterns in Akutagawa

| Pattern | Function | Example |
|---|---|---|
| ——（ダッシュ） | Pause for ironic reconsideration | 正しいと思った。——が、本当にそうだろうか。 |
| のみならず | "Not only...but also" (literary) | 彼のみならず、周囲の者もまた |
| ～とは限らない | "It is not necessarily the case that..." | 善人とは限らない |
| ～に過ぎない | "Is nothing more than..." | 口実に過ぎなかった |
| ～と言っても過言ではない | "It would not be an exaggeration to say..." | 偽善者と言っても過言ではない |
| いわば | "So to speak" | いわば、彼は自分自身の囚人であった |
| ～ずにはいられない | "Cannot help but..." | 笑わずにはいられなかった |
| 果たして | "As expected; sure enough" | 果たして、彼の予感は的中した |

## 17.4 Reading: Extended Passage in Akutagawa's Style

The following is an original passage composed in a style inspired by Akutagawa's short fiction — particularly the ironic, psychologically precise voice of works like 鼻, 羅生門, and 藪の中. It is not a copy or paraphrase of any existing work.

### 『善人の条件』（原文）

> 　或る寺の住職に、慈円という僧があった。この僧は近在の人々から「生き仏」とまで呼ばれるほど、慈悲深い人物として知られていた。貧しい者には食を施し、病む者には薬を与え、悩める者には耳を傾けた。実際、慈円ほど善良な僧は、この辺りには他に一人もいないと、誰もが口を揃えて言ったものである。
>
> 　しかし、慈円自身がそのような評判をどう受け止めていたかといえば、事情は些か複雑であった。彼は確かに善行を為した。だがその善行を為すたびに、彼の胸の中には、微かな、しかし消しがたい満足感が湧いてくるのを、彼自身、はっきりと自覚していたのである。
>
> 　施しをした後、相手が涙を流して感謝する姿を見たとき、慈円の心に去来したものは、果たして純粋な慈悲であったか。——それとも、善人である自分に対する、一種の陶酔であったか。この問いが、夜ごと彼を苦しめた。
>
> 　或る冬の夕暮れ、一人の旅の僧が寺を訪れた。痩せ衰えた姿で、数日間何も食べていないという。慈円は直ちに粥を作らせ、寝所を用意させた。旅の僧は黙って食事を摂り、黙って横になった。礼の一言もなかった。
>
> 　その夜、慈円は眠れなかった。原因は明白であった。感謝されなかったことが、彼には不満だったのだ。自分の善行が認められなかったことが、これほどまでに彼の心を乱すとは。——彼は暗闇の中で、自分自身の姿を、初めて正視した。
>
> 　善人とは何か。慈円は長い夜の間、繰り返しこの問いを自分に向けた。善行を為して満足する者は、果たして善人と呼べるのか。いや、そもそも、善行を為して満足を覚えない人間が、この世に存在し得るのだろうか。
>
> 　翌朝、旅の僧は何も言わずに去った。慈円は門前に立って、その後ろ姿を見送った。冬の朝の空気は冷たく、息が白かった。旅の僧の姿が杉並木の向こうに消えたとき、慈円は呟いた。
>
> 「あの男のほうが、私よりもよほど正直だ」
>
> 　その日から、慈円の善行は以前にも増して精勤なものとなった。だが彼の目には、かつてのような穏やかな光はもうなかった。その代わりに、何か鋭い、痛みを伴った光が宿るようになっていた。近在の人々は相変わらず彼を「生き仏」と呼んだ。慈円はそれを聞くたびに、微かに、しかし確かに、顔を歪ませた。——もっとも、それに気づいた者は、一人もいなかったが。

### 『善人の条件』（Translation: "The Conditions of a Good Man"）

> At a certain temple, there was a head priest named Jien. This monk was known as a man of such deep compassion that the people of the surrounding area went so far as to call him a "living Buddha." He gave food to the poor, medicine to the sick, and lent his ear to the troubled. Indeed, everyone agreed that there was not another monk in the area as virtuous as Jien.
>
> However, how Jien himself received this reputation was a somewhat more complicated matter. He did, to be sure, perform good deeds. But each time he performed them, he was clearly aware of a faint yet indelible sense of satisfaction welling up within his breast.
>
> When, after giving alms, he saw the recipient weep with gratitude — was what crossed Jien's mind truly pure compassion? — Or was it a species of intoxication with himself as a good man? This question tormented him nightly.
>
> One winter evening, a traveling monk visited the temple. His body was wasted and thin; he said he had eaten nothing for several days. Jien immediately had rice porridge prepared and sleeping quarters arranged. The traveling monk ate in silence and lay down in silence. He did not offer a single word of thanks.
>
> That night, Jien could not sleep. The reason was obvious. He was displeased that he had not been thanked. That his good deed had gone unacknowledged disturbed his heart to this extent — in the darkness, he looked squarely at himself for the first time.
>
> What is a good man? Throughout the long night, Jien posed this question to himself again and again. Can a person who takes satisfaction in good deeds truly be called a good man? No — to begin with, can a person who performs good deeds without feeling satisfaction even exist in this world?
>
> The next morning, the traveling monk left without a word. Jien stood at the temple gate, watching him go. The morning winter air was cold, and his breath was white. When the traveling monk's figure disappeared beyond the row of cedar trees, Jien murmured:
>
> "That man is far more honest than I."
>
> From that day, Jien's good works became even more diligent than before. But in his eyes, the gentle light that had once been there was gone. In its place, something sharp had come to dwell — a light tinged with pain. The people of the area continued to call him a "living Buddha." Each time Jien heard it, his face twisted — faintly, but unmistakably. — Though, of course, not a single person noticed.

### Annotation Notes

### Style Analysis

This passage demonstrates several key Akutagawa features:

1. **The classical setting**: The story is set in a temple, in an unspecified past, with a monk as protagonist — a typical Akutagawa framing that allows universal themes to be explored through historical distance.

2. **Ironic structure**: The "living Buddha" turns out to be a man tormented by his own vanity. The gap between reputation and reality is the engine of the story.

3. **The philosophical question embedded in narrative**: 「善人とは何か」is posed not as an essay question but as a character's midnight torment.

4. **The dash (——) as ironic pause**: Used twice — once to introduce the disturbing alternative interpretation, once to undercut the ending with the final ironic observation.

5. **The final sentence reversal**: Everything the villagers believe is shown to be wrong in the final paragraph, but the narrator does not condemn them — he simply notes their blindness with clinical precision.

6. **Moral ambiguity**: Akutagawa does not resolve the philosophical question. The story ends not with an answer but with a changed man whose change goes unnoticed.

### Akutagawa's Ironic Reversal: Structure in Detail

Almost every Akutagawa story contains a moment of reversal — a point where the reader's understanding of the character or situation is overturned. This structure can be mapped as follows:

### The Akutagawa Reversal Pattern

| Stage | Function | In 『善人の条件』 |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Establishment | A character is presented with a clear identity | Jien is a "living Buddha," universally admired |
| 2. Complication | A detail introduces doubt about that identity | Jien is secretly aware of his own satisfaction |
| 3. Test | An event forces the character to confront the doubt | The thankless traveling monk |
| 4. Revelation | The character sees himself clearly | Jien realizes his displeasure at not being thanked |
| 5. Changed state | The character is transformed, but the world does not notice | Jien works harder, but with pain; villagers see nothing |

This five-stage structure appears in many Akutagawa stories. In 鼻 (The Nose), a monk is embarrassed by his long nose → gets it shortened → discovers people mock him more → realizes human nature enjoys others' misfortune → returns to his original nose and feels oddly relieved. In 羅生門 (Rashomon), a dismissed servant hesitates to steal → encounters an old woman stealing from corpses → rationalizes that survival justifies theft → steals from the old woman → disappears into the darkness.

The reversal is never simple. The character does not move from good to bad or from ignorance to knowledge. Rather, they move from a comfortable illusion to an uncomfortable truth — and the reader, who has been watching from the narrator's ironic vantage point, arrives at the truth slightly before the character does.

### Reading Akutagawa: A Checklist

When reading an Akutagawa story, ask yourself at each stage:

1. What does the character believe about himself?
2. What does the narrator's word choice reveal that the character does not see?
3. Where is the gap between self-image and reality?
4. What event will force that gap open?
5. What does the character learn — and what does he fail to learn?

## 17.6 Comparative Reading Exercise

### Soseki vs. Akutagawa: The Same Scene, Two Voices

To sharpen your ability to distinguish authorial voice, read the following two short passages. Both describe the same situation — a man sitting alone in a room at dusk — but in the style of different authors.

### Soseki-style:

> 　日が暮れかけていた。部屋の中は次第に薄暗くなり、畳の目もそろそろ見分けがつかなくなってきた。私は立ち上がって電灯をつけようかとも思ったが、何となくそのまま暗がりの中に坐っていたい気がした。別に理由があったわけではない。ただ、明るい中で自分の姿を見たくなかったのである。自分の姿と言っても、鏡の中の顔のことではない。明るい部屋に一人で坐っている自分という存在の、その手持ち無沙汰な有様を、第三者の目で眺めるのが厭だったのだ。

> Day was fading. The room grew gradually dim, and the lines of the tatami were becoming difficult to distinguish. I thought of getting up to turn on the light, but somehow I felt like remaining in the darkness. There was no particular reason. I simply did not want to see myself in the light. By "see myself," I do not mean my face in a mirror. I found it distasteful to observe, as if through a third person's eyes, the idle, purposeless spectacle of my own existence sitting alone in a bright room.

### Akutagawa-style:

> 　部屋は暗かった。男は電灯をつけなかった。——正確に言えば、つけたくなかったのではない。つける必要を感じなかったのだ。暗闇の中にいることが、彼にはむしろ自然に思えた。光の中にいる人間は、否応なしに自分の輪郭を持たなければならない。暗闇は、その義務から人を解放する。彼は今、輪郭のない存在でいたかった。

> The room was dark. The man did not turn on the light. — To be precise, it was not that he did not want to. He simply felt no need. Being in darkness seemed to him, rather, the more natural state. A person in the light must, whether they wish it or not, possess an outline. Darkness liberates one from that obligation. At this moment, he wanted to be an existence without outlines.

### Analysis

| Feature | Soseki passage | Akutagawa passage |
|---|---|---|
| Person | First person (私) | Third person (男/彼) |
| Sentence length | Long, qualifying, self-correcting | Short, precise, building to a philosophical point |
| Self-analysis | Gradual, exploratory ("I thought... but... not that... rather...") | Immediate and surgical ("To be precise...") |
| The core insight | Revealed through layers of qualification | Delivered as a crystalline aphorism |
| Tone | Uncomfortable intimacy | Cool detachment |
| Key sentence | 明るい部屋に一人で坐っている自分という存在の、その手持ち無沙汰な有様を、第三者の目で眺めるのが厭だった | 光の中にいる人間は、否応なしに自分の輪郭を持たなければならない |

Both passages deal with the same theme — the discomfort of self-awareness — but Soseki approaches it through the narrator's fumbling attempt to explain his own feelings, while Akutagawa distills it into an abstract principle about light and darkness.

## 17.7 Practice: Parsing Complex Literary Sentences

The following sentences are written in the style of Meiji/Taisho literary prose. For each, identify the main clause, subordinate clauses, and the function of key grammatical elements.

### Sentence 1 (Soseki-style)

> 彼女がそう言ったとき、私は何も答えなかったが、答えなかったのは、答える言葉がなかったからではなく、答えるべき言葉が多すぎて、そのどれを選んでいいかわからなかったからである。

**Structure breakdown:**

| Element | Function |
|---|---|
| 彼女がそう言ったとき | Temporal clause: "When she said that" |
| 私は何も答えなかったが | Main clause 1: "I did not answer" + concessive が |
| 答えなかったのは | Topic: "The reason I did not answer" (nominalized with の + は) |
| 答える言葉がなかったからではなく | Negated reason: "was not because I had no words" |
| 答えるべき言葉が多すぎて | Real reason, part 1: "the words I should say were too many" |
| そのどれを選んでいいかわからなかったからである | Real reason, part 2: "because I did not know which to choose" |

**Key grammatical features:**
- のは…からである: Explanatory structure (the reason for X is Y)
- ではなく…からである: "Not because A, but because B" — a Soseki favorite for self-analysis
- べき: Obligation/propriety ("words I ought to say")
- どれを選んでいいか: Embedded question ("which to choose")

### Sentence 2 (Akutagawa-style)

> 彼は善良な人間であった。——少なくとも、世間がそう呼ぶところの善良な人間であった。が、世間の定義する善良と、彼自身が密かに希求していた善良とは、必ずしも同じものではなかったのである。

**Structure breakdown:**

| Element | Function |
|---|---|
| 彼は善良な人間であった | Simple assertion: "He was a good person" |
| ——少なくとも | Ironic dash + qualification: "At least" |
| 世間がそう呼ぶところの善良な人間であった | Revision: "He was what the world calls a good person" |
| が | Adversative: "However" |
| 世間の定義する善良と | Subject A: "The goodness the world defines" |
| 彼自身が密かに希求していた善良とは | Subject B: "The goodness he himself secretly aspired to" |
| 必ずしも同じものではなかったのである | Predicate: "were not necessarily the same" |

**Key grammatical features:**
- ——（ダッシュ）: Akutagawa's ironic pause
- ところの: Literary relative clause marker ("that which the world calls")
- 密かに希求する: "To secretly aspire to" — elevated Sino-Japanese compound
- 必ずしも…ではない: "Not necessarily" — qualification, nuance
- のである: Explanatory finality

### Sentence 3 (Transition exercise)

> 私はそのとき初めて、自分が今まで信じてきたものが、果たして信じるに足るものであったかどうかという疑問を、明確な形で意識せざるを得なくなったのであった。

**Parse this sentence yourself, then check the analysis below.**

| Element | Function |
|---|---|
| 私はそのとき初めて | Time/topic: "At that moment, for the first time, I" |
| 自分が今まで信じてきたものが | Embedded subject: "what I had believed until now" |
| 果たして | Adverb: "truly, as expected" — adds doubt |
| 信じるに足るものであったかどうか | Embedded question: "whether it was something worthy of belief" |
| という疑問を | Object: "the question of whether..." |
| 明確な形で | Adverbial: "in a clear form" |
| 意識せざるを得なくなった | Predicate: "could not help but become aware of" |
| のであった | Explanatory past: "that was the case" |

**Key grammatical features:**
- V てきた: "Had been V-ing up to now" — accumulated past action
- V るに足る: Classical pattern — "worthy of V-ing" (足る = to suffice)
- かどうかという: Embedded yes/no question turned into a noun phrase
- V ざるを得ない: "Cannot help but V" — classical negative ざる + 得ない
- のであった: Emphatic explanatory past — stronger than のだった

## 17.9 Summary

This chapter has introduced two foundational authors of modern Japanese literature:

1. **Natsume Soseki** writes in a voice characterized by intellectual introspection, heavy Sino-Japanese compounds, the explanatory のである, and a habit of building from personal experience toward philosophical generalization. His sentences are long, layered, and self-correcting.

2. **Akutagawa Ryunosuke** writes with ironic precision, using classical settings to explore universal moral questions. His narrators are detached observers who embed philosophical insights in narrative. His sentences are shorter, sharper, and often punctuated by the ironic dash.

Both authors reward careful reading. Soseki teaches you to think in Japanese — to follow an argument through its qualifications and arrive at an insight that could only be reached through that particular chain of reasoning. Akutagawa teaches you to see in Japanese — to perceive the gap between what people say and what they are, and to name that gap with clinical exactness.

The next chapter moves forward in time to the modern and contemporary period, where new voices — confessional, detached, surreal — await.
