# Chapter 18 — Modern and Contemporary Literature

The writers of the Meiji and Taisho eras established modern Japanese prose. The writers who followed — from the turbulent Showa period through the Heisei era and into the present — transformed it. The confessional intensity of 太宰治, the cool detachment of 村上春樹, the lyrical minimalism of 吉本ばなな, the quiet surrealism of 小川洋子, the comedic literary voice of 又吉直樹 — each represents a distinct approach to the question of what a Japanese novel can be and how its language should sound.

This chapter works through these voices one by one. For Dazai and Murakami, we examine style features in detail and work through extended original passages. For the contemporary authors, we offer brief introductions with short excerpts that demonstrate each writer's distinctive qualities.

## 18.1 太宰治: Confessional Voice, Self-Deprecation, Unreliable Narration

### The Writer

Dazai Osamu (1909–1948) is one of the most distinctive and immediately recognizable voices in Japanese literature. His major works include 人間失格 (No Longer Human, 1948), 走れメロス (Run, Melos!, 1940), 斜陽 (The Setting Sun, 1947), and numerous short stories and autobiographical essays. His life — marked by suicide attempts, substance abuse, and a restless inability to fit into society — is inseparable from his literary voice.

### Defining Style Features

| Feature | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Confessional first person | The narrator speaks directly to the reader, as if making a confession | 「私は恥ずかしい人間です」 |
| Self-deprecation as strategy | The narrator constantly belittles himself, but this humility is itself a performance | Self-abasement that paradoxically demands sympathy |
| Unreliable narration | The narrator's account of events may be distorted by shame, self-pity, or outright deception | Events are filtered through an unstable consciousness |
| Cascading clauses | Sentences that accumulate through て-form and continuative connections, often breathless | Long runs of clause after clause, mimicking uncontrolled thought |
| Direct address to the reader | Breaking the fourth wall; speaking as if the reader is present | 「あなたは笑うかもしれません」 |
| Colloquial intimacy | Despite dealing with serious themes, the tone is conversational, almost chatty | Formal literary vocabulary mixed with casual speech |

### The Confessional Voice

Dazai's narrators speak as if they are sitting across from you, confessing everything. The tone is intimate, often uncomfortably so. The reader is made complicit — pulled into the narrator's world of shame and self-analysis.

> 私には、人間の営みというものが、どうしても理解できなかったのです。
> "I simply could not understand the workings of human life."

This sentence — or variations of it — could serve as the epigraph for Dazai's entire body of work. The narrator positions himself as fundamentally different from other people, unable to grasp what everyone else seems to understand naturally. This outsider position is both genuine pain and literary strategy: it wins the reader's sympathy while keeping the narrator at the center of attention.

### Self-Deprecation as Performance

Dazai's self-deprecation is not simple modesty. It is a complex rhetorical strategy.

> 私のような人間に、あなたの気持ちがわかるはずがありません。いえ、わかるはずがないと言うよりも、わかる資格がないと言ったほうが正確でしょう。
> "A person like me could not possibly understand your feelings. No — rather than saying I could not possibly understand, it would be more accurate to say I have no right to understand."

Notice the self-correction: 「わかるはずがない」is replaced by the even more self-abasing 「わかる資格がない」. Each revision deepens the performance of humility. And yet the very act of making this correction demonstrates a sophisticated self-awareness that contradicts the claimed helplessness.

This is the core paradox of Dazai's unreliable narration: the narrator claims to be weak, foolish, and incapable, but the prose itself — witty, precisely calibrated, rhetorically effective — reveals a mind of considerable power. The reader must learn to hold both truths simultaneously.

### Cascading Clauses

Dazai's sentences often build through accumulation. Where Soseki constructs architecturally balanced periods, Dazai piles clause upon clause in a rush that mimics the narrator's inability to stop talking — or thinking.

> 朝が来て、目が覚めて、また一日が始まるのだと思うと、もうそれだけで胸が重くなり、布団から出る気力もなく、ただ天井を見つめて、何も考えたくないのに考えが止まらず、結局また同じことの繰り返しなのだと気づいて、ますます憂鬱になるのでした。
> "When morning came, and I woke, and I thought that yet another day was beginning, just that alone made my chest heavy, and I had no energy to leave the futon, and I merely stared at the ceiling, and though I wanted to think of nothing, my thoughts would not stop, and I realized it was the same thing all over again, and I became even more depressed."

This single sentence contains eight clauses linked by て-form, continuative forms, and のに. The effect is breathless, relentless — a mind that cannot stop circling around its own misery.

### Key Grammatical Patterns in Dazai

| Pattern | Function | Example |
|---|---|---|
| のです / のでした | Confessional explanation; "you see, the thing is..." | 怖かったのです (The thing is, I was afraid) |
| ～かもしれません | Hedging; the narrator is uncertain even about his own experience | 嘘だったかもしれません |
| ～はずがありません | Strong denial, often about one's own ability | 私にできるはずがありません |
| ～と言ったほうが | Self-correction: "it would be better to say..." | 寂しいと言ったほうが正確です |
| ～てしまう | Regrettable completion; things happen despite one's will | また嘘をついてしまいました |
| ～たところで | "Even if I did..., it would be pointless" | 謝ったところで、許されるはずがない |
| ～ないではいられない | "Cannot help but..." (compulsive) | 泣かないではいられなかった |
| いわゆる | "So-called" — used with ironic distance | いわゆる「正常な」人間 |

## 18.2 Reading: Extended Passage in Dazai's Style

The following passage is an original composition written in a style inspired by Dazai's confessional first-person narratives — particularly the voice of 人間失格 and 津軽. It is not a copy or paraphrase of any existing work.

### 『道化の朝』（原文）

> 　私は子供の頃から、人を笑わせることだけが取り柄でした。取り柄と言えば聞こえはいいのですが、正直に申し上げれば、それは取り柄などではなく、一種の病気のようなものでした。人が怒っている場面、悲しんでいる場面、真剣に何かを議論している場面——そういう場面に出くわすたびに、私はどうしても可笑しくなってしまうのです。いえ、可笑しいのではありません。怖いのです。人間が本気になっている姿が、私にはたまらなく怖いのでした。
>
> 　その恐怖を隠すために、私は笑いました。自分から冗談を言い、馬鹿な真似をし、周囲の緊張を和らげようとしました。すると不思議なことに、人々は私を好きになるのです。面白い奴だ、愉快な奴だ、一緒にいると退屈しない。そう言って、皆が私の傍に集まってきました。私はそれが嬉しいのか悲しいのか、自分でもわかりませんでした。たぶん、両方だったのでしょう。
>
> 　中学に入ってからも、この癖は直りませんでした。授業中、先生が黒板に向かって真剣な顔をして数式を書いているのを見ると、私はつい隣の生徒にくだらない絵を描いて見せてしまうのです。先生の似顔絵です。鼻を大きく描いて、頭に花を生やして、手に団扇を持たせる。隣の生徒は声を殺して笑い、その笑いが教室の後ろまで伝染して、やがて先生が振り返ります。
>
> 「何がおかしい」
>
> 　誰も答えません。私も知らん顔をしています。先生は怪訝な顔をして、また黒板に向かいます。私はそのとき、安堵するのと同時に、言いようのない罪悪感に襲われるのでした。——この人は真剣なのだ。真剣に教えようとしているのだ。それを茶化している自分は、一体何なのだ。
>
> 　あなたは笑うかもしれません。大袈裟だと思うかもしれません。たかが授業中の悪戯ではないか、と。しかし私にとって、あの瞬間は深刻でした。自分が道化でしかいられないということ、人間の真剣さに正面から向き合うことができないということ、それがどれほど孤独なことか、あなたにはおわかりにならないでしょう。いえ、おわかりにならないほうがいいのです。わかってしまったら、あなたもまた、私と同じ場所に立つことになるのですから。
>
> 　あの頃の私は、毎朝、鏡の前で笑顔の練習をしていました。口角の上げ方、目の細め方、声を出すタイミング。全部、研究して、練習して、完璧にしたのです。その結果、私の笑顔はとても自然に見えるようになりました。自然に見える笑顔ほど不自然なものはないということを、当時の私は知りませんでした。いえ、知っていたのかもしれません。知っていて、知らないふりをしていたのかもしれません。
>
> 　人間は、自分について、どこまで正直になれるものなのでしょう。私はこうしてあなたに告白しているつもりですが、この告白自体が、ひょっとすると、もう一つの道化なのかもしれないのです。

### 『道化の朝』（Translation: "A Clown's Morning"）

> Since childhood, my only talent was making people laugh. "Talent" sounds too flattering — to be honest, it was not a talent at all, but rather a kind of illness. Scenes where people were angry, scenes where they were sad, scenes where they were seriously discussing something — every time I encountered such a scene, I could not help finding it funny. No — it was not that I found it funny. I was frightened. The sight of human beings being earnest was unbearably frightening to me.
>
> To hide that fear, I laughed. I cracked jokes of my own initiative, played the fool, tried to ease the tension around me. Then, strangely enough, people grew fond of me. He's a funny fellow, an amusing fellow, you're never bored when he's around. Saying such things, everyone gathered near me. Whether this made me happy or sad, I could not tell. Probably it was both.
>
> Even after I entered middle school, this habit did not go away. In class, when I saw the teacher facing the blackboard with a serious expression, writing mathematical formulas, I could not resist drawing a ridiculous picture and showing it to the student beside me. A caricature of the teacher. The nose drawn large, flowers sprouting from the head, a fan placed in one hand. The student next to me stifled a laugh, the laughter spread to the back of the classroom, and presently the teacher turned around.
>
> "What is so amusing?"
>
> No one answered. I too played innocent. The teacher made a puzzled face and turned back to the blackboard. At that moment, I was seized simultaneously by relief and by an inexpressible guilt. — This person is serious. He is earnestly trying to teach. What, then, am I — I who mock him?
>
> You may laugh. You may think I am exaggerating. A mere classroom prank, you might say. But for me, that moment was grave. That I could only exist as a clown, that I could not face human earnestness head-on — you cannot imagine how lonely that is. No — it is better that you cannot imagine it. For if you could, you would find yourself standing in the same place as I.
>
> In those days, I practiced smiling in front of the mirror every morning. The angle of the corners of my mouth, the narrowing of my eyes, the timing of my voice. I studied it all, practiced it all, perfected it all. As a result, my smile came to look very natural. That there is nothing more unnatural than a natural-looking smile — this, I did not know at the time. No — perhaps I did know. Perhaps I knew, and pretended not to.
>
> How honest can a human being be about himself? I intend to be confessing to you here, but this confession itself may, quite possibly, be yet another act of clowning.

### Style Analysis

1. **The confessional のです**: The passage uses のです/のでした repeatedly, creating the effect of someone explaining themselves — "you see, the thing is..."

2. **Self-correction as structure**: The narrator constantly revises his own statements: 「可笑しいのではありません。怖いのです」, 「知りませんでした。いえ、知っていたのかもしれません」. Each revision reveals another layer of self-awareness.

3. **Direct address**: 「あなたは笑うかもしれません」breaks the fourth wall, making the reader complicit in the narrator's confession.

4. **The paradox ending**: The final paragraph undermines the entire confession by suggesting it might itself be a performance. This is the quintessential Dazai move — the unreliable narrator who tells you he is unreliable, which paradoxically makes him seem more honest, which is perhaps the deepest form of manipulation.

5. **Colloquial-literary mixture**: Words like くだらない, 茶化す, and ひょっとすると sit alongside 罪悪感, 安堵, and 道化, creating the tonal instability that defines Dazai's voice.

### Dazai's Influence on Contemporary Japanese

Dazai's literary voice has permeated Japanese culture far beyond literature. The confessional, self-deprecating tone that he perfected can be heard in:

- **Blog and social media writing**: The practice of prefacing statements with すみません or 恐縮ですが, then revealing personal weakness or failure, owes something to Dazai's confessional mode.
- **Comedic self-deprecation**: The manzai tradition of 自虐ネタ (self-deprecating humor) echoes Dazai's strategy of making one's own weakness the subject of entertainment.
- **The "useless adult" (ダメ人間) archetype**: A character type in anime, manga, and light novels that traces directly back to Dazai's narrators.

Understanding Dazai's voice helps you recognize these echoes in contemporary Japanese culture — and helps you understand why 人間失格 remains one of the best-selling novels in Japan decades after its publication.

### Dazai's です・ます Style: The Polite Confession

A crucial technical point: Dazai often writes in です・ます form rather than the literary である or the casual だ. This choice is significant. The polite form creates a paradoxical intimacy — the narrator is being formally polite to the reader while confessing the most shameful details of his life. The effect is as if someone were bowing deeply while telling you they have committed a terrible crime.

> 私は、人間が怖いのです。人間を愛しているのかもしれません。しかし、怖いのです。ですから、私はいつも笑っているのです。笑っていないと、怖くてたまらないのです。
> "I am afraid of human beings. Perhaps I love them. But I am afraid of them. And so I am always smiling. If I were not smiling, I would be unbearably afraid."

The です at the end of each sentence sounds like a polite explanation — but the content is raw confession. This mismatch between form and content is one of Dazai's most powerful tools.

Compare this to the same content in plain form:

> 俺は人間が怖い。愛してるのかもしれない。でも怖い。だからいつも笑ってる。笑ってないと怖くてたまらない。

The plain-form version sounds tough, blunt, almost angry. Dazai's です・ます version sounds vulnerable, apologetic, painfully honest. The politeness form becomes a form of emotional exposure.

## 18.3 村上春樹: Detached First Person, Surreal Imagery, Western-Influenced Syntax

### The Writer

Murakami Haruki (born 1949) is the most internationally recognized Japanese writer of the contemporary period. His major works include ノルウェイの森 (Norwegian Wood, 1987), 世界の終りとハードボイルド・ワンダーランド (Hard-Boiled Wonderland and the End of the World, 1985), ねじまき鳥クロニクル (The Wind-Up Bird Chronicle, 1994–1995), and 1Q84 (2009–2010).

### Defining Style Features

| Feature | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Detached first person | The narrator observes events with emotional distance, often reporting feelings as if they belong to someone else | 「悲しみのようなものが胸にあった」 |
| Western-influenced syntax | Longer sentences with embedded clauses, relative clause-heavy structures influenced by English | Sentences that feel "translated" — deliberately so |
| Surreal intrusions | Mundane, realistic settings invaded by unexplained strange events | A well at the bottom of which another world exists |
| Pop culture references | Music, food, brand names — precise cataloguing of the material world | Specific jazz albums, beer brands, cooking details |
| Flat, precise description | Physical actions described with almost clinical precision | Step-by-step narration of cooking, cleaning, walking |
| Emotional understatement | Strong emotions delivered in a tone that refuses to dramatize them | 「それは悲しいことだった。でも仕方がなかった。」 |
| Repetitive phrasing | Key phrases repeated at intervals, creating a rhythmic, almost hypnotic effect | やれやれ, ～のようなもの, ～ということ |

### The Murakami Voice

Murakami's narrators are typically men in their twenties or thirties — alone (often recently separated from a partner), living simply, cooking for themselves, listening to music, and gradually drawn into events they do not fully understand. Their defining characteristic is detachment: they experience strange things and report them without alarm.

> 電話が鳴った。午前二時だった。こんな時間に電話をかけてくる人間は限られている。限られてはいるが、だからと言って出たくなるわけではなかった。
> "The phone rang. It was two in the morning. The number of people who would call at this hour was limited. Limited, yes — but that didn't mean I wanted to answer."

This is characteristic Murakami: a statement, a qualification of that statement, and a further qualification of the qualification. The narrator thinks in careful logical steps, each one slightly undermining the last. The effect is a voice that seems perpetually in the process of measuring its own reactions.

### Western-Influenced Syntax

Murakami has been open about the influence of American writers — particularly Raymond Chandler, F. Scott Fitzgerald, and Kurt Vonnegut — on his prose style. His Japanese often reads as if it were conceived in English and rendered into Japanese, with sentence structures that feel different from traditional Japanese literary prose.

**Traditional Japanese structure:**
> 窓から見える海は青く、風が気持ちよかった。
> "The sea visible from the window was blue, and the breeze felt good."

**Murakami-style structure:**
> 窓の外には海が見えた。青い海だった。風が吹いていた。気持ちのいい風だった。僕はしばらくその風に吹かれていた。
> "Outside the window, the sea was visible. It was a blue sea. Wind was blowing. It was a pleasant wind. I stood in that wind for a while."

The Murakami version breaks the complex sentence into short declarative units, each standing alone. This staccato rhythm — statement, pause, statement, pause — creates the detached, almost trance-like quality of his narration.

### Surreal Imagery in Mundane Settings

Murakami's surrealism does not announce itself. Strange things happen in the middle of ordinary life, and the narrator accepts them with the same flat equanimity he brings to everything else.

> 猫が話しかけてきたとき、僕は特に驚かなかった。そういうこともあるだろう、と思った。
> "When the cat spoke to me, I was not particularly surprised. I figured things like that probably happen."

This is not magical realism in the García Márquez sense — there is no mythic framework, no cultural tradition justifying the strangeness. It is private surrealism: the world simply becomes strange, and the narrator's inability to be surprised by it becomes itself a source of unease.

### Key Grammatical Patterns in Murakami

| Pattern | Function | Example |
|---|---|---|
| ～のようなもの | "Something like..." — hedging, imprecision | 悲しみのようなもの ("something like sadness") |
| ～ということだ | Reporting; maintaining distance from direct experience | 彼女は来ないということだった |
| ～わけではない | "It's not that..." — negating an assumption | 怒っていたわけではない |
| ～としても | "Even assuming that..." | それが本当だとしても |
| やれやれ | "Good grief" — Murakami's signature interjection | やれやれ、と僕は思った |
| ～かもしれない | "Maybe..." — perpetual uncertainty | 正しかったのかもしれない |
| ～みたいに | "Like..." — colloquial simile | 眠っているみたいに静かだった |
| 仕方がない | "It can't be helped" — resignation | 仕方がない、と僕は思った |

## 18.4 Reading: Extended Passage in Murakami's Style

The following passage is an original composition written in a style inspired by Murakami's first-person narratives. It is not a copy or paraphrase of any existing work.

### 『井戸のある庭』（原文）

> 　その家に引っ越したのは三月の終わりだった。不動産屋の女性が、庭に古い井戸がありますけど気にしないでくださいねと言った。僕は気にしなかった。井戸があろうがなかろうが、家賃が安いことのほうがよほど重要だった。
>
> 　引っ越しの荷物を片づけ終わると、僕はビールを開けて庭に出た。四月の頭にしては暖かい日で、風もなかった。庭の隅に井戸があった。コンクリートの蓋がしてあって、その上に植木鉢が三つ載っていた。植木鉢の中身はとっくに枯れていたが、土だけが黒々と残っていた。
>
> 　僕はビールを飲みながら、その井戸を眺めた。特に何かを考えていたわけではない。ただ眺めていた。井戸というものは、眺めているだけで不思議な気持ちにさせられる。地面に穴が開いていて、その穴がどこまで続いているのかわからない。もしかしたらどこにも続いていないのかもしれない。ただ暗いだけなのかもしれない。でも、その「ただ暗いだけ」というのが、妙に気になるのだった。
>
> 　夜になって、台所でパスタを茹でていると、庭のほうから微かな音が聞こえた。風の音ではなかった。もっと規則的で、もっと低い音だった。何かが、井戸の底で、ゆっくりと動いているような音。僕はパスタの火を止めて、窓から庭を見た。月明かりの中に、井戸の蓋が見えた。植木鉢はそのままだった。何も変わっていないように見えた。
>
> 　音は止んでいた。
>
> 　僕はパスタを茹で直した。アーリオ・オーリオにして、ビールの残りと一緒に食べた。テレビのニュースを見ながら食べた。ニュースキャスターが経済指標について何か言っていたが、僕は聞いていなかった。井戸の音のことを考えていた。
>
> 　考えていたというのは正確ではないかもしれない。考えるというほど積極的な行為ではなかった。ただ、井戸の音というものが、頭の片隅にぼんやりと居座っていた。邪魔ではなかった。でも、消えもしなかった。
>
> 　その夜、夢を見た。井戸の底に僕がいた。暗くて、湿っていて、上を見上げると丸い空が見えた。小さな丸い空。昼なのか夜なのかわからなかった。空は灰色だった。僕はそこに座って、誰かが来るのを待っていた。誰が来るのかはわからなかった。でも、誰かが来ることだけはわかっていた。
>
> 　朝、目が覚めたとき、枕が濡れていた。泣いていたのかもしれない。でも悲しかった記憶はなかった。やれやれ、と僕は思った。

### 『井戸のある庭』（Translation: "The Garden with a Well"）

> I moved into that house at the end of March. The real estate agent, a woman, told me there was an old well in the garden but not to worry about it. I didn't worry about it. Whether there was a well or not, the cheap rent was far more important.
>
> When I finished unpacking, I opened a beer and went out into the garden. For the beginning of April, it was a warm day, and there was no wind. The well was in a corner of the garden. It had a concrete lid, and on top of the lid sat three flower pots. Whatever had been in the flower pots had long since withered, but the soil remained, dark and black.
>
> I looked at the well while drinking my beer. I wasn't thinking about anything in particular. I was just looking. A well is the kind of thing that makes you feel strange just by looking at it. There is a hole in the ground, and you don't know how far it goes. Maybe it doesn't go anywhere at all. Maybe it's just dark. But that "just dark" part is what bothered me in a strange way.
>
> At night, while I was boiling pasta in the kitchen, I heard a faint sound from the direction of the garden. It wasn't the wind. It was more regular, lower. The kind of sound something might make moving slowly at the bottom of a well. I turned off the burner under the pasta and looked at the garden from the window. In the moonlight, I could see the lid of the well. The flower pots were still there. Nothing appeared to have changed.
>
> The sound had stopped.
>
> I boiled the pasta again. Made it aglio e olio and ate it with the rest of the beer. Ate while watching the news on television. The newscaster was saying something about economic indicators, but I wasn't listening. I was thinking about the sound from the well.
>
> "Thinking" may not be the right word. It wasn't an act as deliberate as thinking. The sound of the well simply squatted vaguely in a corner of my mind. It wasn't in the way. But it wouldn't go away, either.
>
> That night, I had a dream. I was at the bottom of the well. It was dark and damp, and when I looked up, I could see a round piece of sky. A small, round sky. I couldn't tell if it was day or night. The sky was gray. I sat there, waiting for someone to come. I didn't know who was coming. But I knew that someone would.
>
> When I woke in the morning, my pillow was wet. Maybe I had been crying. But I had no memory of being sad. Good grief, I thought.

| Japanese | Reading | Meaning | Style note |
|---|---|---|---|
| 不動産屋 | ふどうさんや | Real estate agency/agent | Mundane detail |
| 荷物を片づける | にもつをかたづける | To unpack/put away luggage | |
| とっくに | とっくに | Long ago, ages ago | Colloquial |
| 黒々と | くろぐろと | Dark and black, blackly | Emphatic doubling |
| 妙に | みょうに | Strangely, oddly | Murakami keyword |
| 規則的 | きそくてき | Regular, rhythmic | |
| 止む | やむ | To stop, to cease (of sounds, rain) | |
| 茹で直す | ゆでなおす | To reboil, to boil again | Mundane precision |
| 居座る | いすわる | To settle in, to squat (and refuse to leave) | Used metaphorically for a thought |

| 湿る | しめる | To be damp | |
| やれやれ | やれやれ | Good grief, oh well | Murakami signature |

### Style Analysis

1. **Flat, precise narration of mundane actions**: Boiling pasta, drinking beer, watching news — described with the same attention as the mysterious well sounds.

2. **The short declarative sentence**: 「音は止んでいた。」stands as its own paragraph, creating a rhythmic pause that controls pacing.

3. **Self-correcting narration**: 「考えていたというのは正確ではないかもしれない」— the narrator qualifies his own words, a Murakami habit.

4. **Surreal intrusion**: The well sounds and the dream emerge naturally from the mundane setting. The narrator does not dramatize them.

5. **Emotional understatement**: The wet pillow suggests crying, but the narrator cannot confirm even his own emotions. やれやれ dismisses what might be a profound experience.

6. **Specific brand/food references**: Aglio e olio, beer — the material texture of daily life.

## 18.5 Contemporary Voices

### Murakami's Kitchen Scenes and Material Precision

One of the most distinctive features of Murakami's prose is the precise, almost ritualistic description of cooking and eating. These scenes serve a narrative function: they ground the surreal in the physical, giving the reader something tangible to hold onto when the story ventures into strange territory.

> 僕はフライパンにオリーブオイルを入れて、ニンニクを二片、薄く切って炒めた。ニンニクがきつね色になったところで、ベーコンを加えた。ベーコンは三枚。それぞれ三センチくらいの幅に切ってある。ベーコンの脂が出てきたところで、茹でたてのスパゲティを入れ、塩と黒胡椒で味をつけた。最後に刻んだパセリを散らした。

> I put olive oil in the frying pan and sliced two cloves of garlic thin and fried them. When the garlic turned golden, I added bacon. Three slices. Each cut to about three-centimeter widths. When the fat from the bacon began to render, I added the freshly boiled spaghetti and seasoned it with salt and black pepper. Finally, I scattered chopped parsley on top.

Notice the precision: two cloves, three slices, three centimeters. These numbers are not random — they signal that the narrator pays attention to the physical world with a meticulousness that contrasts sharply with his vagueness about emotional matters. The cooking scene is the one place where this narrator is fully competent, fully present. It is his anchor to reality.

### Murakami's Simile Patterns

Murakami uses similes frequently, but they are of a particular kind — often slightly odd, slightly off, creating a momentary defamiliarization.

| Murakami-style simile | Effect |
|---|---|
| まるで百年前の手紙のように黄ばんでいた | "Yellowed like a letter from a hundred years ago" — temporal distance made visual |
| 記憶の底に沈んだ石のように | "Like a stone sunk to the bottom of memory" — abstract made physical |
| 誰かが世界のスイッチを切ったみたいに静かだった | "Quiet as if someone had flipped off the world's switch" — mechanical metaphor for absence |
| 何かの予感のようなものが体の中を通り過ぎた | "Something like a premonition passed through my body" — emotion as physical transit |

These similes share a common quality: they translate intangible experiences (memory, silence, foreboding) into physical images. They are Murakami's method of making the vague concrete — or at least of trying to, and acknowledging the attempt through のようなもの ("something like").

### 吉本ばなな — Lyrical Minimalism

**Yoshimoto Banana** (born 1964) broke onto the literary scene with キッチン (Kitchen, 1988), a novella about grief, food, and found family. Her style is characterized by emotional directness, short sentences, and a gentle, almost translucent prose that deals with loss, healing, and the small comforts of daily life.

### Style Features

| Feature | Description |
|---|---|
| Simple, luminous sentences | Short declarative sentences with a gentle emotional charge |
| Sensory focus | Light, warmth, food, and sleep — the body's basic comforts |
| Grief as background | Characters are often recovering from loss; sadness pervades without overwhelming |
| Hiragana-rich prose | Fewer kanji, softer visual texture on the page |
| Present-tense intimacy | Frequent use of present tense for emotional immediacy |

### Excerpt in Yoshimoto Banana's Style

> 　台所の明かりだけがついている部屋で、あたしはひとりでスープを飲んでいた。夜中の三時だった。外はしんとしていて、たまに車の音がする。スープはたまねぎとじゃがいもだけで作った、シンプルなやつだ。おばあちゃんがよく作ってくれたのと同じレシピ。
>
> 　おばあちゃんが死んでから、もう半年になる。悲しいかと聞かれたら、うん、悲しいよと答える。でもそれは、泣くような悲しさじゃなくて、もっと静かなもの。空気みたいなもの。いつもそこにあるけど、普段は気づかない。ふとした瞬間に、ああ、あるなって思う。そういう悲しさ。

> In a room lit only by the kitchen light, I was drinking soup alone. It was three in the morning. Outside, it was perfectly still — only the occasional sound of a car. The soup was a simple one, made with just onion and potato. The same recipe grandma used to make.
>
> It's been half a year since grandma died. If someone asked me whether I'm sad, I'd say yeah, I'm sad. But it's not the kind of sadness that makes you cry. It's quieter than that. Like air. It's always there, but you don't usually notice it. Then, in an unguarded moment, you think — oh, there it is. That kind of sadness.

### 小川洋子 — Quiet Surrealism

**Ogawa Yoko** (born 1962) is known for novels that combine meticulous realism with quietly impossible premises. Her major works include 博士の愛した数式 (The Professor and His Housekeeper, 2003) and 密やかな結晶 (The Memory Police, 1994). Her prose is calm, observational, and precise — the strangeness of her worlds emerges not through dramatic revelation but through the steady accumulation of impossible details treated as ordinary.

### Style Features

| Feature | Description |
|---|---|
| Calm, observational tone | The narrator describes impossible things with the same precision as ordinary ones |
| Disappearance as metaphor | Things vanish — memories, objects, concepts — and the world adjusts |
| Institutional settings | Museums, laboratories, apartments — enclosed, ordered spaces |
| Emotional restraint | Characters feel deeply but express little; emotion emerges through image and action |
| Meticulous physical detail | Objects are described with care; their materiality matters |

### Excerpt in Ogawa's Style

> 　ある朝、目が覚めると、鳥がいなくなっていた。空にも、木の枝にも、電線の上にも、一羽も見当たらなかった。鳴き声もしなかった。世界から、鳥に関するすべてが消えたようだった。
>
> 　私はいつものように朝食を作り、いつものように食べた。トーストとゆで卵と紅茶。食べながら窓の外を見た。空はいつもより広く見えた。何かが足りないと、空間はこんなにも広がるものなのか。
>
> 　「鳥がいませんね」と、隣の老人が言った。
> 　「ええ」と私は答えた。
> 　「仕方がないですね」
> 　「ええ」
>
> 　それだけだった。私たちはそれ以上、鳥のことを話さなかった。話す必要がなかったのかもしれない。あるいは、もう話せなくなっていたのかもしれない。

> One morning, when I woke, the birds were gone. Not in the sky, not on the tree branches, not on the power lines — not a single one. No birdsong, either. It was as though everything related to birds had vanished from the world.
>
> I made breakfast as usual and ate it as usual. Toast, a boiled egg, and tea. I looked out the window while eating. The sky seemed wider than before. When something is missing, does space expand this much?
>
> "There are no birds," the old man next door said.
> "No," I answered.
> "It can't be helped."
> "No."
>
> That was all. We did not speak of the birds again. Perhaps there was no need. Or perhaps we were already unable to.

### 又吉直樹 — Comedic Literary Voice

**Matayoshi Naoki** (born 1980) won the 芥川賞 in 2015 for 火花 (Spark), a novel about two struggling comedians in Tokyo. A comedian himself, Matayoshi brings a unique voice to literary fiction — one that is funny, self-aware, linguistically playful, and deeply serious beneath its comic surface.

### Style Features

| Feature | Description |
|---|---|
| Humor as structural element | Comedy is not decoration but the mechanism through which truth is revealed |
| Osaka/comedy-world language | Kansai inflections, manzai terminology, performer slang |
| Passionate, run-on narration | Sentences that build with enthusiasm, stacking observations |
| Self-conscious artistry | Characters and narrator are aware of language as craft |
| Youth-culture specificity | Precise observation of Tokyo/Osaka subcultural details |

### Excerpt in Matayoshi's Style

> 　先輩は漫才について語り出すと止まらない人だった。居酒屋のテーブルに肘をついて、焼酎のお湯割りを飲みながら、ツッコミの間について三時間ぶっ通しで喋ったことがある。三時間だ。ツッコミの間だけで三時間。普通の人間にはできない。というか、普通の人間はしない。
>
> 　「いいか、ツッコミっていうのはな、タイミングが全てや。早すぎたら客が追いつかへん。遅すぎたら空気が死ぬ。ちょうどええところ、客が『あれ？ これおかしいぞ？』って思った瞬間、そこにバシッと言葉を入れるんや。それが快感やねん」
>
> 　僕はうんうんと頷きながら聞いていた。正直に言うと、先輩の話の半分くらいは理解できていなかった。でも、理解できないということと、感動するということは、矛盾しないのだと僕はそのとき知った。先輩の目が輝いていた。焼酎で赤くなった顔が輝いていた。言葉の意味はよくわからなくても、この人が何かとんでもないことを考えているのだということだけは、はっきりとわかった。

> My senpai was the kind of person who, once he started talking about manzai, could not stop. Elbows on the izakaya table, drinking hot-water-diluted shochu, he once talked for three hours straight about the timing of tsukkomi. Three hours. On tsukkomi timing alone. A normal person couldn't do that. Or rather, a normal person wouldn't.
>
> "Listen, tsukkomi is all about timing, right? Too fast and the audience can't keep up. Too slow and the air dies. Right at the sweet spot — the instant the audience thinks 'huh, something's off here' — that's where you slam in the words. That's the rush."
>
> I listened, nodding along. Honestly, I understood maybe half of what senpai was saying. But I learned then that not understanding something and being moved by it are not contradictory. Senpai's eyes were shining. His face, flushed red from shochu, was shining. Even if I didn't quite grasp the meaning of his words, I understood with perfect clarity that this person was thinking about something extraordinary.

## 18.7 Reading Strategies for Modern Japanese Fiction

### Practical Advice for the N1-Level Reader

**Starting points by difficulty:**

| Difficulty | Author | Recommended first work | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| Accessible | 吉本ばなな | キッチン | Short, simple vocabulary, emotionally direct |
| Accessible | 村上春樹 | ノルウェイの森 | Realistic, relatively simple syntax, widely available with translations |
| Moderate | 又吉直樹 | 火花 | Contemporary language, engaging story, moderate length |
| Moderate | 小川洋子 | 博士の愛した数式 | Gentle pacing, clear prose, satisfying narrative |
| Challenging | 太宰治 | 走れメロス | Shorter than 人間失格, more accessible vocabulary |
| Very challenging | 太宰治 | 人間失格 | Long cascading sentences, archaic vocabulary, psychologically demanding |

### The Translation Comparison Method

For your first novel in Japanese, consider reading a work for which you have already read the English translation. This is not cheating — it is strategic. Knowing the story frees your attention to focus on how the author writes rather than what happens.

1. Read the English translation first (or a chapter summary).
2. Read the Japanese text, chapter by chapter.
3. When you encounter a passage you cannot parse, check the translation.
4. Return to the Japanese and reread the passage with understanding.
5. Note the Japanese expressions that the translation could not fully capture.

This fifth step is where the real learning happens. Every translation loses something, and what it loses is precisely the literary texture you are trying to acquire.

### Building Stamina

Novel-length reading requires physical and mental stamina. Build it gradually:

| Week | Daily reading goal | Approximate pages |
|---|---|---|
| 1–2 | 15 minutes | 3–5 pages |
| 3–4 | 30 minutes | 7–10 pages |
| 5–8 | 45 minutes | 12–18 pages |
| 9+ | 60 minutes | 20–30 pages |

Do not increase speed at the expense of comprehension. If you find yourself skimming, slow down. If you find yourself looking up every other word, speed up and trust context. The goal is a sustainable pace that allows both comprehension and pleasure.

## 18.8 Practice: Identifying Authorial Voice

Read each of the following short passages and identify which author's style it most closely resembles. Explain your reasoning by pointing to specific features of vocabulary, sentence structure, and tone.

### Passage A

> 　冷蔵庫の中には牛乳とバターとレタスの残りが入っていた。牛乳は明日が賞味期限だった。僕はグラスに牛乳を注ぎ、パンを一枚焼いた。バターを塗って食べた。特に美味しいわけではなかったが、特にまずいわけでもなかった。僕はそのパンを食べながら、昨日の出来事について考えようとした。考えようとしたが、うまくまとまらなかった。やれやれ。

> In the refrigerator were milk, butter, and the remains of a head of lettuce. The milk's expiration date was tomorrow. I poured milk into a glass and toasted a slice of bread. Spread butter on it and ate. It wasn't particularly delicious, but it wasn't particularly bad either. While eating the bread, I tried to think about what had happened yesterday. I tried, but my thoughts wouldn't come together. Good grief.

**Identification:** Murakami Haruki

**Evidence:**
- Flat, precise narration of mundane actions (opening the refrigerator, making toast)
- Specific material details (milk, butter, lettuce, expiration date)
- ～わけではない / ～わけでもない pattern — hedging, neither-here-nor-there
- やれやれ — Murakami's signature
- Attempt to think about something emotional, followed by inability — characteristic detachment
- Short declarative sentences with minimal ornamentation

### Passage B

> 　私は嘘をつきました。また嘘をつきました。いえ、嘘というのは正確ではないかもしれません。嘘というほど明確な意志があったわけではないのです。ただ、本当のことを言う勇気がなかっただけです。勇気がなかったと言うのも、少し違うかもしれません。本当のことが何なのか、自分でもよくわかっていなかったのですから。

> I lied. I lied again. No — "lied" may not be the right word. It was not as though I had the clear intent that the word "lie" implies. I simply lacked the courage to tell the truth. Though saying I "lacked the courage" may not be quite right either. For I did not clearly understand, myself, what the truth was.

**Identification:** Dazai Osamu

**Evidence:**
- Confessional first person in です/ます — formal politeness creating paradoxical intimacy
- Self-correction as structure: each statement is revised by the next
- のです pattern — explanatory, confiding
- Self-deprecation (lacking courage, not understanding truth)
- The escalating honesty that loops back into uncertainty
- かもしれません — habitual hedging about one's own experience

### Passage C

> 　あたしは目を閉じて、太陽の光を顔に感じていた。まぶたの裏がオレンジ色に透けて見えた。風が髪を揺らした。どこかでラジオの音が聞こえていた。知らない歌だったけれど、メロディが優しかった。こういう午後がずっと続けばいいのにと思った。続かないと知っているから、余計にそう思った。

> I closed my eyes and felt the sunlight on my face. Behind my eyelids, the light showed through as orange. The wind moved my hair. Somewhere a radio was playing. I didn't know the song, but the melody was gentle. I wished this kind of afternoon could last forever. Knowing it wouldn't is exactly why I wished it all the more.

**Identification:** Yoshimoto Banana

**Evidence:**
- あたし — casual feminine first person
- Sensory focus: sunlight, color behind eyelids, wind, sound
- Short, luminous sentences
- Present-tense feeling embedded in past narration
- The final reflection: knowing something won't last makes you wish it would — a characteristic Yoshimoto move
- Gentle sadness without despair
- Hiragana-rich feel (fewer heavy kanji compounds)

### Passage D

> 　火曜日の朝、目が覚めると、色が一つ減っていた。何色が消えたのかはすぐにはわからなかった。部屋を見回した。棚の上の花瓶。カーテン。本の背表紙。どれも以前と同じに見えた。だが、何かが違った。やがて気づいた。紫がなかった。部屋の中から、紫色がすべて消えていた。本の背表紙に印刷されていたはずの紫は灰色になり、花瓶の模様から紫の部分だけが抜け落ちていた。

> When I woke on Tuesday morning, one color was gone. I couldn't immediately tell which one had disappeared. I looked around the room. The vase on the shelf. The curtains. The spines of books. Everything appeared the same as before. But something was different. Eventually I noticed. There was no purple. Every trace of purple had vanished from the room. The purple that should have been printed on book spines had turned gray, and only the purple portions of the vase's pattern had fallen away.

**Identification:** Ogawa Yoko

**Evidence:**
- Calm, matter-of-fact reporting of an impossible event
- Disappearance as central motif
- Meticulous inventory of physical objects (vase, curtains, book spines)
- No emotional reaction from the narrator — quiet acceptance
- The impossible is treated as merely puzzling, not terrifying
- Precise description of how the impossible manifests in material detail

## 18.9 Comparative Grammar: How Authors Use the Same Patterns Differently

The same grammatical pattern can serve entirely different purposes depending on the author's voice. The following table demonstrates how five common patterns are used by different writers.

### ～のだ / ～のです (Explanatory)

| Author | Typical usage | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| Soseki | 彼にはそれがわからなかったのである | Essayistic explanation to the reader |
| Dazai | 怖かったのです | Confession; "you see, the truth is..." |
| Murakami | そういうことだったのだ | Flat realization; accepting a fact |
| Yoshimoto | さみしかったのだ | Simple emotional truth, stated gently |

### ～かもしれない (Uncertainty)

| Author | Typical usage | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| Soseki | 彼は来ないかもしれない | Rational assessment of probability |
| Dazai | 私が間違っていたのかもしれません | Self-doubt; undermining one's own narrative |
| Murakami | そうだったのかもしれない | Detached — even the narrator's own memories are uncertain |
| Ogawa | もう話せなくなっていたのかもしれない | Eerie suggestion; the impossible creeping in |

### ～ていた (Past progressive/state)

| Author | Typical usage | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| Soseki | 私は長い間そのことを考えていた | Extended contemplation; intellectual process |
| Dazai | ずっと怯えていたのです | Chronic emotional state; ongoing suffering |
| Murakami | 彼女は窓の外を見ていた | Snapshot; flat description of another person's action |
| Yoshimoto | 光が部屋に差し込んでいた | Sensory scene-setting; warmth, comfort |

### Short sentence / 体言止め

| Author | Typical usage | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| Akutagawa | 暗闇。沈黙。 | Dramatic compression; philosophical weight |
| Murakami | 午前二時。電話。 | Cinematic; deadpan rhythm |
| Yoshimoto | 台所。明かり。温かさ。 | Sensory inventory; gentle accumulation |
| Matayoshi | 三時間。ツッコミだけで三時間。 | Comic emphasis; incredulity |

## 18.11 Extended Practice: Voice Matching

### Exercise: Complete the Passage

Each of the following passages has a final sentence missing. Write a sentence that completes the passage in a way consistent with the author's voice. Then compare with the suggested completions below.

### Passage 1 (Dazai-style)

> 私は彼女に嘘をつきました。愛していると言いました。愛していなかったわけではありません。たぶん、愛していたのだと思います。ただ、その愛が、彼女の求めていた愛と同じものであったかどうかは、____

**Suggested completion:**
> 正直に申し上げて、私にはわからないのです。いえ、わからないのではなく、わかりたくなかったのかもしれません。

(The self-correction, the hedging かもしれません, the のです — all Dazai markers.)

### Passage 2 (Murakami-style)

> 猫は毎朝同じ時間にやってきた。僕がトーストを焼いている匂いに引き寄せられるのかもしれないし、単に散歩のルートにこの家が含まれているだけかもしれない。理由はわからなかった。____

**Suggested completion:**
> でも、理由がわからないということは、特に気にならなかった。猫にはきっと猫なりの理由があるのだろう。僕にはトーストを焼く理由がある。それで十分だった。

(The acceptance of not-knowing, the parallel structure, the flat concluding それで十分だった — all Murakami markers.)

### Passage 3 (Yoshimoto Banana-style)

> 引っ越したばかりの部屋は、まだ何もなくてがらんとしていた。でも、窓からの光がすごくきれいで、____

**Suggested completion:**
> それだけで、ここに住んでよかったなって思えた。光のある場所で眠れるって、それだけで幸せなことなのだと思う。

(The simple emotional realization, the って-form casual quotation, the sensory comfort — all Yoshimoto markers.)

### Passage 4 (Ogawa-style)

> その日から、街の中の時計がすべて止まった。デジタル時計もアナログ時計も、腕時計も壁掛け時計も、すべてが同じ時刻を指したまま動かなくなった。____

**Suggested completion:**
> 人々は最初のうち不便がっていたが、やがて慣れた。時間というものがなくなっても、朝は来たし、夜も来た。ただ、それが何時であるかを、誰も知らなくなっただけだった。

(The calm acceptance of the impossible, the "ただ…だけだった" structure, the matter-of-fact last sentence — all Ogawa markers.)

## 18.12 The私小説 (I-Novel) Tradition

### Understanding Japan's Most Distinctive Literary Genre

The 私小説 (ししょうせつ, or わたくししょうせつ) — the "I-novel" — is a genre unique to Japanese literature. It is fiction that is transparently autobiographical: the narrator is understood to be the author, and the events described are understood to be (largely) true.

This genre is central to understanding Dazai, but it also shapes the work of many other Japanese writers. When you read a Japanese novel narrated in the first person, the question "Is this the author speaking?" is almost always relevant — even when the answer is complicated.

### Key Features of the 私小説

| Feature | Description |
|---|---|
| First-person narration | The narrator is an author or writer figure |
| Confessional content | Personal failings, romantic entanglements, family conflicts |
| Minimal plot | Events are drawn from life, not constructed for dramatic effect |
| Psychological focus | The interest is in the narrator's inner experience, not external action |
| Ambiguous fictionality | The boundary between truth and fiction is deliberately blurred |

### The 私小説 and the Problem of Truth

The 私小説 creates a reading experience different from Western autobiographical fiction. In the West, the reader generally assumes that a novel is fiction unless told otherwise. In the 私小説 tradition, the reader assumes that the novel is substantially true — and the interest lies in how the author transforms life into art, what they choose to include and exclude, and what the act of writing reveals about their character.

This has practical implications for reading. When you encounter a first-person Japanese novel, ask:

1. Is this a 私小説? (Is the narrator a writer? Do the life details match the author's biography?)
2. If so, what is the author choosing to confess — and what might they be hiding?
3. How does the act of writing function within the story? Is the narration itself part of the drama?

### Major 私小説 Authors

| Author | Key 私小説 work | Subject matter |
|---|---|---|
| 田山花袋 | 蒲団 (The Quilt, 1907) | Unrequited desire for a student; founding text of the genre |
| 志賀直哉 | 暗夜行路 (A Dark Night's Passing, 1921–37) | Spiritual and artistic growth |
| 太宰治 | 人間失格 (No Longer Human, 1948) | Self-destruction, alienation, shame |
| 檀一雄 | 火宅の人 (House on Fire, 1975) | Marital infidelity, alcoholism |
| 西村賢太 | 苦役列車 (Suffering Train, 2010) | Poverty, manual labor, literary ambition |

## 18.13 Literary Prizes: Understanding the Japanese Literary Landscape

When you read reviews, bookstore displays, or author profiles in Japanese, you will encounter references to literary prizes. Knowing the major prizes helps you understand where an author fits in the literary ecosystem.

### The Two Major Prizes

| Prize | 芥川賞 (Akutagawa Prize) | 直木賞 (Naoki Prize) |
|---|---|---|
| Named after | 芥川龍之介 | 直木三十五 (novelist, 1891–1934) |
| Awarded for | Best "pure literature" (純文学) work by a new or emerging writer | Best "popular literature" (大衆文学) work by a new or emerging writer |
| Genre | Literary fiction, experimental prose | Genre fiction: mystery, historical, entertainment |
| Typical winner | Serious, often challenging prose | Accessible, plot-driven, often widely read |
| Frequency | Twice yearly (January and July) | Twice yearly (January and July) |
| Prestige | Considered Japan's highest literary honor for new writers | Equally prestigious but for a different kind of excellence |

### Other Notable Prizes

| Prize | Focus | Notable winners |
|---|---|---|
| 谷崎潤一郎賞 | Established literary fiction | 村上春樹, 小川洋子 |
| 三島由紀夫賞 | Bold, innovative work by newer writers | Various experimental writers |
| 読売文学賞 | Broad literary achievement | Major established authors |
| 本屋大賞 | Voted by bookstore staff; accessible, beloved books | Often overlaps with widely-read titles |
| 野間文芸賞 | Established literary excellence | Senior literary figures |

### 純文学 vs. 大衆文学

The distinction between 純文学 (pure/literary fiction) and 大衆文学 (popular/mass fiction) is more prominent in Japan than the "literary vs. genre" distinction in English-language publishing. Understanding this division helps you navigate bookstores, reviews, and conversations about books.

| 純文学 | 大衆文学 |
|---|---|
| Emphasis on style, language, psychological depth | Emphasis on plot, entertainment, readability |
| Often short; may lack conventional plot | Often long; strong narrative structure |
| Characters are psychologically complex | Characters serve the story |
| Authors: 太宰治, 村上春樹, 小川洋子 | Authors: 東野圭吾, 宮部みゆき, 伊坂幸太郎 |
| Sold in the 文芸 section | Sold in the 小説 or genre-specific sections |

For the language learner, 大衆文学 is often easier to read — the vocabulary is more contemporary, the sentences are more straightforward, and the plots provide motivation to keep reading. 純文学 is more challenging but offers greater linguistic reward.

## 18.14 Summary

This chapter has explored five distinctive modern and contemporary voices:

1. **Dazai Osamu** — the confessional voice that uses self-deprecation, cascading clauses, and direct address to create an intimacy that is simultaneously genuine and performed.

2. **Murakami Haruki** — the detached observer who describes mundane life and surreal intrusions with equal flatness, using short declarative sentences and emotional understatement.

3. **Yoshimoto Banana** — the lyrical minimalist who writes about grief and daily comfort in luminous, hiragana-rich prose.

4. **Ogawa Yoko** — the quietly surrealist who treats impossible premises with meticulous calm, creating unease through the very ordinariness of her prose.

5. **Matayoshi Naoki** — the comedic literary voice that uses humor, passion, and subcultural specificity to explore questions of art and devotion.

Each author represents not just a different set of vocabulary and grammar preferences, but a different relationship to language itself — a different answer to the question of what Japanese prose can do and be. Reading them is not just a language exercise; it is an encounter with the full expressive range of modern Japanese.
