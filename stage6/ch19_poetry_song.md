# Chapter 19 — Poetry, Song, and Compressed Expression

Everything we have studied so far — novels, essays, literary prose — operates in the mode of expansion. A novelist takes an idea and unfolds it across hundreds of pages. A poet does the opposite: a poet takes the world and compresses it into seventeen syllables, or thirty-one, or a single image that holds more than any paragraph could contain.

This chapter enters the world of compressed expression in Japanese: 俳句 (haiku), 短歌 (tanka), modern free verse (現代詩), and song lyrics. These forms demand a different kind of reading — one that listens for what is not said, that dwells in the space between images, and that understands how the constraints of form create meaning rather than limiting it.

## 19.1 俳句 and 短歌 — Structure, Seasonal Words, Cutting Words

### The Architecture of Haiku

A haiku consists of three phrases arranged in a 5-7-5 pattern of 音 (on, or mora — the rhythmic units of Japanese, roughly equivalent to one kana character each).

### Haiku Structure

| Element | Mora count | Function |
|---|---|---|
| 上の句 (first phrase) | 5 mora | Sets the scene, introduces an image |
| 中の句 (middle phrase) | 7 mora | Develops or pivots |
| 下の句 (final phrase) | 5 mora | Completes, resolves, or opens outward |

Total: 17 mora.

Important: mora are not the same as syllables. In Japanese, each kana character (including small kana in combinations) generally counts as one mora, with a few special rules:

### Mora Counting Rules

| Element | Count | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Regular kana | 1 mora each | か = 1, き = 1 |
| Long vowels | 2 mora | おう = 2 (お + う) |
| っ (geminate) | 1 mora | きっと = 3 (き + っ + と) |
| ん (moraic nasal) | 1 mora | さんぽ = 3 (さ + ん + ぽ) |
| Diphthongs (きゃ etc.) | 1 mora | きゃく = 2 (きゃ + く) |

A note on counting: the particle は, when used grammatically, is pronounced わ but still counts as one mora. The particle へ, pronounced え, likewise counts as one mora. In poetry, pronunciation governs the count, so be attentive to actual spoken rhythm rather than written appearance.

### The Three Pillars of Traditional Haiku

**1. 季語 (kigo) — Seasonal Words**

Every traditional haiku contains a 季語 — a word or phrase that evokes a particular season. These are codified in reference books called 歳時記 (saijiki). The seasonal word is not merely a label; it carries an entire world of associations. A single word like 蛍 (firefly) does not just indicate summer — it brings with it the darkness of a summer evening, the nearness of water, the faintness and brevity of light, and centuries of poems that have used the same word.

### Seasonal Words by Season

| Season | 季語 | Reading | Meaning | Associations |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 春 (Spring) | 花 | はな | Cherry blossoms | Beauty, transience, celebration |
| 春 | 蛙 | かわず | Frog | Sound, water, Basho's famous poem |
| 春 | 霞 | かすみ | Haze, mist | Soft landscape, gentle warmth |
| 春 | 雛 | ひな | Hina dolls | Girls' Day (March 3), femininity |
| 春 | 蝶 | ちょう | Butterfly | Transformation, lightness |
| 春 | 梅 | うめ | Plum blossom | Early spring, fragrance, perseverance |
| 春 | 燕 | つばめ | Swallow | Arrival, renewal, eaves of houses |
| 夏 (Summer) | 蝉 | せみ | Cicada | Heat, noise, impermanence |
| 夏 | 夕立 | ゆうだち | Sudden evening rain | Drama, refreshment, surprise |
| 夏 | 蛍 | ほたる | Firefly | Darkness, faint light, romance |
| 夏 | 向日葵 | ひまわり | Sunflower | Brightness, energy, peak summer |
| 夏 | 風鈴 | ふうりん | Wind chime | Sound, coolness, nostalgia |
| 夏 | 雷 | かみなり | Thunder | Power, sudden change, summer storms |
| 秋 (Autumn) | 月 | つき | Moon | Contemplation, loneliness, beauty |
| 秋 | 紅葉 | もみじ | Autumn leaves | Color, change, Buddhist impermanence |
| 秋 | 虫 | むし | Insects (singing) | Night sounds, melancholy |
| 秋 | 露 | つゆ | Dew | Ephemerality, morning freshness |
| 秋 | 秋風 | あきかぜ | Autumn wind | Chill, loneliness, change |
| 秋 | 柿 | かき | Persimmon | Ripeness, rural autumn, Shiki's famous poem |
| 秋 | 鹿 | しか | Deer | Calling, loneliness, mountain autumn |
| 冬 (Winter) | 雪 | ゆき | Snow | Silence, purity, cold |
| 冬 | 枯野 | かれの | Withered field | Desolation, Basho's death poem |
| 冬 | 炬燵 | こたつ | Heated table | Warmth, home, domestic comfort |
| 冬 | 霜 | しも | Frost | Cold, early morning, crystal |
| 冬 | 冬木立 | ふゆこだち | Winter trees (bare) | Starkness, skeletal beauty |
| 冬 | 木枯 | こがらし | Cold winter wind | Biting cold, desolation, barren landscape |

Some seasonal words belong to the 新年 (New Year) category, which is treated as its own micro-season in the 歳時記 tradition. Words such as 初日の出 (first sunrise), 松飾り (pine decorations), and 年賀状 (New Year's cards) fall into this category.

**2. 切れ字 (kireji) — Cutting Words**

A 切れ字 is a word or particle that creates a pause — a cut — within the haiku. This cut divides the poem into two parts that resonate against each other. The most common cutting words are:

### Cutting Words

| 切れ字 | Function | Position | Effect |
|---|---|---|---|
| や | Exclamatory cut; creates a pause after the first or second phrase | Usually after 5 or 12 mora | Opens a space between two images; the strongest cut |
| かな | Emotional emphasis; wonder, admiration | Usually at the end (final 5) | Softens the ending with a note of feeling; "how...!" |
| けり | Realization, completion; "indeed, it is so" | Usually at the end | Marks a moment of sudden awareness |
| ぞ | Emphatic assertion | Various | Strong, declarative |
| なり | Copula with a sense of being present to the moment | Various | "It is..." — freshness of perception |
| らん | Conjecture; "perhaps," "I wonder if" | Usually at the end | Adds uncertainty, dreamlike quality |
| よ | Emphatic; calling attention | Various | Direct address, assertion |

The cut is the most important structural element in haiku. Without it, a haiku is merely a description. With it, a haiku becomes a juxtaposition — two images or ideas placed side by side with a gap between them that the reader must fill.

It is essential to understand that the cut is not equivalent to a comma or period in English. It is closer to a musical rest — a silence that is part of the composition. In performance (haiku are traditionally read aloud), the cut is realized as a pause of variable length, during which the listener holds the first image while anticipating the second.

**3. 取り合わせ (toriawase) — Juxtaposition**

The essence of haiku is the pairing of two images or ideas that are related not by logic but by feeling. The cut separates them; the reader's imagination connects them.

Consider the structure:

> [Image A] — [cut] — [Image B]

The haiku does not explain the relationship between A and B. The reader perceives it intuitively. This is why haiku, despite their brevity, can be inexhaustibly rich: the relationship between the images is not stated but evoked, and different readers may perceive it differently.

The quality of a haiku's juxtaposition depends on the distance between the two images. If they are too close (rain and umbrella), the poem is predictable. If they are too far apart (rain and algebra), the poem is incoherent. The art lies in finding the right distance — close enough that a connection can be felt, far enough that the connection surprises.

### The Architecture of Tanka

Tanka (短歌) is an older form — the dominant poetic form of classical Japanese literature (万葉集, 古今和歌集). It consists of five phrases in a 5-7-5-7-7 pattern, totaling 31 mora.

### Tanka Structure

| Element | Mora | Traditional name | Function |
|---|---|---|---|
| First phrase | 5 | 初句 (しょく) | Opens the poem |
| Second phrase | 7 | 二句 (にく) | Develops |
| Third phrase | 5 | 三句 (さんく) | Pivots (often the turning point) |
| Fourth phrase | 7 | 四句 (よんく) | Deepens or shifts perspective |
| Fifth phrase | 7 | 結句 (けっく) | Closes the poem |

The tanka is sometimes divided at the third phrase into an 上の句 (5-7-5, upper verse) and a 下の句 (7-7, lower verse). The pivot between these two halves is crucial — it is where the poem turns from observation to feeling, from scene to reflection, or from one image to a contrasting one.

This division is not merely analytical. In the 連歌 (linked verse) tradition, one poet would compose the upper verse and another would respond with a lower verse, creating a collaborative poem. This practice eventually gave birth to haiku: the upper verse (5-7-5) was extracted and allowed to stand on its own.

### Tanka vs. Haiku

| Feature | 俳句 | 短歌 |
|---|---|---|
| Length | 17 mora (5-7-5) | 31 mora (5-7-5-7-7) |
| 季語 required | Yes (in traditional haiku) | No (season may be present but is not required) |
| Emotional directness | Indirect; emotion evoked through image | Direct; emotion can be stated openly |
| Subject matter | Nature, seasons, observation | Love, grief, longing, personal emotion |
| Typical voice | Impersonal or observational | Personal, often first person |
| The cut | Central structural element | Less prominent; the pivot at line 3 serves a similar function |
| Historical origin | Developed from 連歌 in the Edo period | Ancient form, dominant in Heian literature |
| Modern practice | Both traditional and experimental schools | Revived in the modern era; active community today |

## 19.2 How to Read a Haiku: What Is Said vs. What Is Evoked

### The Principle of 余白 (Yohaku) — Empty Space

In visual art, 余白 refers to the empty space in a painting — the unpainted areas that give shape to what is painted. In haiku, the same principle applies: what the poem does not say is as important as what it does say.

A haiku presents an image. That image evokes a world of associations, feelings, and memories that the poem itself never mentions. The reader's task is to dwell in that evoked world — to feel what the image implies without trying to translate it into explicit statement.

This is the fundamental difference between reading haiku and reading prose. In prose, meaning is built through accumulation — more words, more detail, more explanation. In haiku, meaning is built through reduction — fewer words, fewer details, until only the essential image remains, and everything else is implied.

### Reading Method: Three Passes

**Pass 1: The Image**
What does the haiku literally describe? Read the words and see the scene. Do not interpret yet. Simply observe.

**Pass 2: The Season and Setting**
What does the 季語 tell you about the time, place, and emotional atmosphere? What associations does it carry? What other poems have used this seasonal word?

**Pass 3: The Space Between**
What is not said? What feelings arise from the juxtaposition? What does the cut open up? Where does your mind go after the poem ends?

### Analysis: Basho (松尾芭蕉, 1644-1694)

Let us apply this method to the most famous haiku in the Japanese language:

> 古池や蛙飛びこむ水の音
> ふるいけ　や　かわず　とびこむ　みずのおと

**Pass 1: The Image**
An old pond. A frog jumps in. The sound of water.

**Pass 2: The Season**
蛙 (frog) is a spring seasonal word. The setting is still, quiet — an old pond implies a place largely undisturbed.

**Pass 3: The Space Between**
The cut (や) comes after 古池 — "old pond!" — creating a pause. Before the frog jumps, there is silence. After the sound, there is silence again. The poem captures a single moment of disruption in an otherwise timeless stillness. The "old" pond suggests deep time; the frog's jump is instantaneous. The sound proves the silence that surrounds it.

What is not said: the poet's solitude; the quality of the silence before and after; the ripples spreading and fading; the return to stillness. All of this exists in the 余白 — the empty space of the poem.

Now let us examine another Basho haiku:

> 夏草や兵どもが夢の跡
> なつくさや　つわものどもが　ゆめのあと

> "Summer grasses — where warriors once dreamed"

**季語:** 夏草 (summer grasses) — summer

**切れ字:** や after the first phrase

**Analysis:** Basho composed this at 平泉 (Hiraizumi), the site of a twelfth-century fortress where the Fujiwara clan met its end. The cut separates the present (summer grasses growing thickly) from the past (warriors and their dreams). The grasses have swallowed all trace of the battles. Where armies clashed, only grass grows now. The word 夢 (dream) is double: the warriors' ambitions were themselves dreams — insubstantial, now vanished. The poem is often read as a meditation on impermanence (無常), but it is also about the relationship between nature's persistence and human ephemerality. The grasses are alive; the warriors are gone.

### Analysis: Yosa Buson (与謝蕪村, 1716-1784)

Buson was a painter as well as a poet, and his haiku often have a visual quality — precise colors, careful composition, a sense of landscape seen from a specific vantage point.

> 菜の花や月は東に日は西に
> なのはなや　つきはひがしに　ひはにしに

> "Rapeseed flowers — the moon in the east, the sun in the west"

**季語:** 菜の花 (rapeseed/canola flowers) — late spring

**切れ字:** や after the first phrase

**Analysis:** This is a poem of vast scale. The first phrase presents a field of yellow rapeseed flowers. The cut opens outward to the sky: the moon is rising in the east while the sun is setting in the west. The three images — yellow flowers below, pale moon to the left, red sun to the right — form a panoramic composition worthy of a painted screen. The poem captures the specific moment of twilight when both celestial bodies are visible simultaneously. The flowers are below, connecting earth to sky. The painter's eye is evident: this is haiku as landscape painting.

> 春の海ひねもすのたりのたりかな
> はるのうみ　ひねもすのたり　のたりかな

> "The spring sea — all day long, gently rising, gently falling"

**季語:** 春の海 (the spring sea) — spring

**切れ字:** かな at the end

**Analysis:** The onomatopoeia のたりのたり (a slow, lazy undulation) is the heart of this poem. It captures the quality of the spring sea — not the violent waves of winter, not the glassy calm of summer, but a gentle, continuous, almost sleepy motion. ひねもす (all day long) extends this motion through time: the sea has been doing this all day, will continue doing it. The repetition of のたり mirrors the sea's own repetition. The かな at the end adds a quality of absorbed contemplation — "how peaceful, this endless gentle motion."

### Analysis: Kobayashi Issa (小林一茶, 1763-1828)

Issa's haiku are known for their warmth, humor, and sympathy for small creatures. Where Basho seeks the cosmic in the particular, and Buson paints landscapes, Issa finds tenderness in the overlooked.

> やせ蛙負けるな一茶これにあり
> やせがえる　まけるないっさ　これにあり

> "Lean frog, don't give up! Issa is here for you!"

**季語:** 蛙 (frog) — spring

**Analysis:** This poem breaks several conventions. It names the poet himself (一茶). It addresses the frog directly. It takes sides in a frog-wrestling match (a rural pastime). The lean, underdog frog has Issa's support. The humor is real, but so is the sympathy — Issa, who suffered poverty and loss throughout his life, identifies with the underdog. The poem is at once comic and quietly autobiographical.

> 我と来て遊べや親のない雀
> われときて　あそべやおやの　ないすずめ

> "Come with me, come play — little sparrow without a mother"

**季語:** 雀 (sparrow) — spring (when fledglings appear)

**切れ字:** や embedded within the phrase

**Analysis:** Issa lost his mother as a child and was raised by a stepmother who treated him harshly. This haiku is addressed to a young sparrow that has lost its parent. The invitation to play together is both tender and heartbreaking — the poet recognizes himself in the orphaned bird. The simplicity of the language (come, play, no mother) is the simplicity of a child speaking to a child. No artifice, no distance.

### Analysis: Masaoka Shiki (正岡子規, 1867-1902)

Shiki revolutionized haiku in the Meiji era with his principle of 写生 (shasei) — sketching from life. He argued that haiku should observe reality directly rather than relying on literary convention.

> 柿くへば鐘が鳴るなり法隆寺
> かきくへば　かねがなるなり　ほうりゅうじ

> "Eating a persimmon — the bell sounds at Horyuji"

**季語:** 柿 (persimmon) — autumn

**切れ字:** なり (copula of direct perception)

**Analysis:** Two sensory experiences occur simultaneously: the taste of a persimmon and the sound of a temple bell. Neither is more important than the other. The poem does not say "while eating a persimmon, I heard a bell" — it presents the two experiences in parallel, connected by the conditional ば but not subordinated. The persimmon's autumn sweetness and the ancient bell's resonance merge into a single moment. This is 写生 in action: direct, precise, present-tense observation without commentary.

### Common Misreadings

| Misreading | Why it fails |
|---|---|
| "This poem is about nature" | Too general. A haiku is about a specific moment — one sound in one silence, one frog in one pond. |
| "The poet is describing a scene" | A haiku does not describe; it presents. The difference matters. Description implies distance; presentation implies presence. |
| "The frog symbolizes something" | Haiku generally avoid symbolism. The frog is a frog. Its meaning comes from the juxtaposition with the old pond, not from standing for something else. |
| "This is simple" | The simplicity is the point. Haiku compresses complexity into apparent simplicity. |
| "I need to find the hidden meaning" | There is no hidden meaning. The meaning is on the surface. The depth comes from the resonance of the surface image. |

### The Art of 切れ (Kire) — The Cut in Practice

The cut is not merely a grammatical pause. It is a conceptual gap — a space where two parts of the poem face each other without the bridge of explanation.

Consider the difference between:

> 古池に蛙飛びこむ水の音
> (An old pond — into which a frog jumps — the sound of water)

and

> 古池や蛙飛びこむ水の音
> (Old pond! A frog jumps in — the sound of water)

The first version (に) connects the images logically: the pond is a destination. The second version (や) separates them: "Old pond!" stands as its own exclamation — a world unto itself — and then the frog event arrives as a separate perception. The や opens a gap that the reader's mind must leap across, just as the frog leaps into the water.

## 19.3 Modern Poetry (現代詩) — Conventions, Free Verse, Concrete Poetry

### The Shift from Traditional to Modern

Modern Japanese poetry (現代詩, gendaishi) broke free from the mora-counting constraints of haiku and tanka in the early twentieth century, influenced by Western poetry (particularly French symbolism and American modernism). Key figures include:

### Major Modern Poets

| Poet | Period | Style | Key works |
|---|---|---|---|
| 萩原朔太郎 | 1886-1942 | Symbolist, psychological, urban anxiety | 月に吠える (Howling at the Moon) |
| 中原中也 | 1907-1937 | Lyrical, musical, deeply personal | 山羊の歌 (Goat Songs) |
| 宮沢賢治 | 1896-1933 | Visionary, nature-mystical, cosmic | 春と修羅 (Spring and Asura) |
| 谷川俊太郎 | 1931-2024 | Accessible, philosophical, playful | 二十億光年の孤独 (Loneliness of Two Billion Light Years) |
| 吉増剛造 | 1939- | Experimental, deconstructive, visual | Avant-garde visual poetry |
| 石垣りん | 1920-2004 | Spare, daily-life focused, feminist | 表札 (Nameplate) |
| 茨木のり子 | 1926-2006 | Clear, direct, ethically engaged | 自分の感受性くらい (At Least Your Own Sensitivity) |

### Features of Modern Japanese Poetry

| Feature | Description |
|---|---|
| Free verse (自由詩) | No fixed mora count; line breaks determined by rhythm and meaning |
| Visual arrangement | Line breaks, spacing, and typography become meaningful |
| Personal voice | Often first person; direct expression of individual perception |
| Imagery over statement | Images juxtaposed without logical connectives |
| Linguistic play | Unusual kanji usage, invented compounds, grammatical disruption |
| Influence of translation | Many modern poets were also translators; their Japanese absorbs foreign rhythms |
| Kanji-kana contrast | Deliberate manipulation of the visual texture of the writing system |

### How Free Verse Works in Japanese

In English, free verse is defined against a background of stressed and unstressed syllables. In Japanese, free verse is defined against the background of the 5-7 mora rhythm that pervades the language naturally. Even "free" verse in Japanese often falls into 5-mora and 7-mora phrases unconsciously — and when it deliberately breaks those rhythms, the break is felt.

The following is an original poem demonstrating this principle:

> 世界が終わるとき
> 最後に聞こえるのは
> きっと
> 風の音だろう

> "When the world ends / the last thing you'll hear / surely / will be the sound of wind"

The first line has 9 mora (irregular). The second has 9. The third has 3. The fourth has 7. The compression of the third line — きっと, just three mora standing alone — creates a pause that mimics the certainty and hesitation of the word itself. This is how free verse in Japanese creates rhythm: not through regularity, but through the tension between expected and actual line lengths.

### Original Poem: 『名前のない季節』 (A Season Without a Name)

The following is an original composition in the style of modern Japanese free verse, demonstrating how contemporary poets handle imagery, line breaks, and the interplay between abstract thought and concrete observation.

> 四月でも五月でもない
> 暦が落とした
> 名前のない季節がある
>
> 朝の光が
> テーブルの上の
> 水のコップを通り抜けて
> 壁に小さな虹を作る
>
> それだけのことを
> 誰かに伝えたいと思う
> その「誰か」がいないことを
> 知りながら
>
> 言葉は
> 光に似ている
> 何かを通り抜けるとき
> 色が分かれる

> "There is a season that belongs to neither April nor May / one the calendar dropped / a season without a name // Morning light / passes through / a glass of water on the table / and makes a small rainbow on the wall // I want to tell someone / about just that / knowing / that there is no such 'someone' // Words / are like light / when they pass through something / the colors separate"

**Analysis of structure:** The poem is built on a single extended metaphor — light passing through glass — that is introduced as a literal observation (stanza 2) and then applied to language itself (stanza 4). The pivot occurs at stanza 3, where the desire to communicate meets the absence of an audience. The final stanza resolves the tension: words, like light, reveal their spectrum only when they pass through something — a poem, a reader, an experience. The line breaks isolate key phrases: 知りながら (knowing) stands alone, forcing the reader to pause on the weight of that knowledge before continuing.

### Concrete Poetry (具体詩)

Japanese concrete poetry exploits the visual properties of the writing system — the physical shapes of kanji, the contrast between kanji and kana, the spatial arrangement of characters on the page.

A concrete poem might arrange the kanji 雨 (rain) in a pattern that looks like falling rain on the page, or stack the kanji 山 (mountain) to create a visual mountain. The meaning is not just in the words but in their visual arrangement.

This is an area where Japanese has unique advantages. The visual density of kanji, the contrast between angular kanji and flowing hiragana, and the spatial flexibility of vertical and horizontal writing all offer possibilities unavailable to purely alphabetic languages.

Consider how kanji themselves contain visual meaning: 森 (forest) is three 木 (tree) characters stacked together. 品 (goods) is three 口 (mouth/opening) characters. This inherent visual logic means that Japanese concrete poetry can play with meaning at the level of individual characters in ways that alphabetic writing cannot.

### Reading Modern Poetry: Approach

1. **Read aloud.** Modern Japanese poetry is written to be heard. The sounds — the rhythms, the pauses, the texture of consonants and vowels — are integral to the meaning.

2. **Follow the line breaks.** Each line break is a choice. Ask yourself: why did the poet break the line here? What is gained by the pause?

3. **Let images accumulate.** Modern poetry often presents a series of images without explicit connection. Do not search for a logical thread. Let the images accumulate and see what feeling emerges.

4. **Notice the kanji-kana balance.** A line dense with kanji feels different from a line of flowing hiragana. Poets manipulate this texture deliberately. A line written entirely in hiragana feels soft, childlike, or dreamy. A line heavy with kanji feels formal, intellectual, or weighty.

5. **Attend to particles.** In modern poetry, the choice of particle can carry enormous weight. The difference between を and に, between は and が, can shift the entire meaning of a line. Poets sometimes omit particles entirely, creating an ambiguity that prose would not allow.

## 19.4 Song Lyrics as Literary Text

### How Musical Meter Interacts with Japanese Mora Timing

Japanese song lyrics occupy a unique space between poetry and speech. They are constrained by musical rhythm — each note must align with a mora — and this constraint produces linguistic effects that differ from both prose and traditional poetry.

### Mora and Musical Notes

In Japanese song, the general principle is: one mora = one note. This creates a fundamentally different relationship between text and music than in English (where one syllable may stretch across multiple notes, or multiple syllables may crowd into a single beat).

### Examples of Mora-Note Alignment

| Mora count | Musical implication |
|---|---|
| A 4-mora word (さくらばな) | Takes 4 notes |
| A 2-mora word (空) | Takes 2 notes |
| A word with っ (きっと) | The っ takes its own note/beat — often a rest or held note |
| A word with ん (さんぽ) | The ん takes its own note |
| A long vowel (おおきい) | Each mora gets a note; the word stretches across 4 notes |

This one-to-one relationship means that:

1. **Short words** fit naturally into fast passages.
2. **Long compound words** require slower melodies or longer phrases.
3. **The small tsu (っ)** creates natural rhythmic pauses.
4. **The nasal ん** creates a humming or sustained quality.

### The Tension Between Pitch Accent and Melody

In spoken Japanese, words have pitch accent patterns that distinguish meaning (雨 あめ① "rain" vs. 飴 あめ⓪ "candy"). In song, the melody overrides these patterns — the musical pitch may contradict the word's natural pitch accent. This is generally accepted in Japanese song culture, though skilled lyricists try to align melody and pitch accent when possible, especially in the emotional climax of a song.

This means that song lyrics are designed to be understood through context and visual text (many Japanese listeners follow along with lyrics on screen) rather than through pitch accent alone.

### Literary Qualities of Song Lyrics

Song lyrics in Japanese often exhibit poetic techniques that connect them to the literary tradition:

| Technique | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| 掛詞 (kakekotoba) | Pivot words with double meanings | Classical technique revived in modern lyrics |
| 体言止め | Ending a phrase with a noun (no verb) | Creates an open, unresolved feeling |
| 倒置法 | Inversion of normal word order | Emphasis through unexpected placement |
| 反復 | Repetition of phrases or structures | Chorus structure; emotional intensification |
| 比喩 | Metaphor and simile | Rain as tears, wind as change, seasons as life stages |
| 擬人法 | Personification | Giving human qualities to seasons, cities, time |
| 省略 | Omission of subjects, particles, verbs | Creates ambiguity and universality |

### How Songs Teach Literary Japanese

For the language learner, Japanese songs offer several advantages:

1. **Repetition.** A chorus repeats multiple times, drilling vocabulary and grammar naturally.
2. **Emotional context.** Music provides emotional scaffolding that aids comprehension and retention.
3. **Pronunciation.** Singing forces attention to mora timing — you cannot fudge the rhythm.
4. **Colloquial-literary blend.** Songs mix registers freely, bridging the gap between textbook Japanese and literary Japanese.
5. **Cultural vocabulary.** Songs introduce seasonal, emotional, and place-related vocabulary in memorable contexts.

### Reading Song Lyrics Critically

When analyzing song lyrics as literary text, consider:

| Dimension | Questions to ask |
|---|---|
| Imagery | What images recur? What season is evoked? |
| Register | Is the language formal, casual, poetic, colloquial? Does it shift? |
| Person | Who is speaking? To whom? Is there a narrative? |
| Sound | How do the sounds of the words contribute to the mood? |
| Structure | How does the verse-chorus structure mirror the emotional arc? |
| Untranslatability | What elements work only in Japanese? (Word play, kanji-kana contrast, mora rhythm) |
| Ambiguity | Where is the meaning deliberately left open? Why? |

## 19.5 Reading Practice — Classical and Modern Haiku with Analysis

The following section presents ten haiku — a mix of well-known classical haiku and original compositions in the classical and modern styles. Each is followed by detailed analysis including season word identification, cutting word analysis, and interpretation of implied meaning.

### Haiku 1: Spring (Basho)

> 古池や蛙飛びこむ水の音
> ふるいけや　かわずとびこむ　みずのおと

**Translation:** "Old pond! A frog jumps in — the sound of water"

**Mora count:** ふ・る・い・け・や (5) / か・わ・ず・と・び・こ・む (7) / み・ず・の・お・と (5)

**季語:** 蛙 (frog) — spring

**切れ字:** や — cuts after the first phrase, establishing the old pond as a complete world before the action intrudes

**Analysis:** This poem has been discussed in Section 19.2 above. It is included here for completeness as the foundational example of haiku reading. The key insight: the poem is not about the frog. It is about the sound. And it is not about the sound. It is about the silence that the sound reveals.

---

### Haiku 2: Summer (Basho)

> 閑さや岩にしみ入る蝉の声
> しずかさや　いわにしみいる　せみのこえ

**Translation:** "Stillness — the cicada's cry seeps into the rocks"

**Mora count:** し・ず・か・さ・や (5) / い・わ・に・し・み・い・る (7) / せ・み・の・こ・え (5)

**季語:** 蝉 (cicada) — summer

**切れ字:** や after the first phrase

**Analysis:** Basho composed this at 立石寺 (Risshakuji), a mountain temple in Yamagata prefecture. The paradox is immediate: stillness, and then the sound of cicadas, which is one of the loudest sounds in the Japanese natural world. But the stillness is not broken by the sound — instead, the sound "seeps into" (しみ入る) the rocks, as if the rocks are absorbing it, as if the silence is so deep that even noise becomes part of it. The verb しみ入る (to soak into, to permeate) usually describes liquids. Applying it to sound is synesthetic — it makes the cicada cry feel like something that soaks into stone over centuries. The rocks, the temple, the mountain — all are saturated with this ancient sound.

---

### Haiku 3: Autumn (Buson)

> 秋風や書きかけにして文机
> あきかぜや　かきかけにして　ふみづくえ

**Translation:** "Autumn wind — left half-written, the writing desk"

**Mora count:** あ・き・か・ぜ・や (5) / か・き・か・け・に・し・て (7) / ふ・み・づ・く・え (5)

**季語:** 秋風 (autumn wind) — autumn

**切れ字:** や after the first phrase

**Analysis:** The autumn wind blows. A letter or manuscript lies half-written on a desk. The poet does not tell us why the writing stopped. Was the writer distracted by the wind? Called away? Overcome by the melancholy that autumn wind traditionally carries? The unfinished writing is the poem's emotional center — something begun but not completed, like the season itself, which is always in the process of passing. The desk is empty of its writer. The wind enters through an unseen window. The image is perfectly still, but the stillness suggests departure.

---

### Haiku 4: Winter (Issa)

> これがまあ終の栖か雪五尺
> これがまあ　ついのすみかか　ゆきごしゃく

**Translation:** "So this is it — my final dwelling? Five feet of snow"

**Mora count:** こ・れ・が・ま・あ (5) / つ・い・の・す・み・か・か (7) / ゆ・き・ご・しゃ・く (5)

**季語:** 雪 (snow) — winter

**Analysis:** Issa returned to his hometown in the snow country of Shinano (present-day Nagano) after years of wandering. This haiku captures the moment of arrival — or perhaps resignation. これがまあ ("So this is...") is colloquial, almost comic in its understatement. 終の栖 (final dwelling) is literary and grave. The contrast between the casual opening and the weighty phrase creates Issa's characteristic blend of humor and pathos. And then: 雪五尺 — five shaku (about 1.5 meters) of snow. The house is half-buried. This is where he will spend his remaining years. The snow is both literal (the harsh reality of his rural home) and figurative (the weight of a life lived).

---

### Haiku 5: Autumn (Shiki)

> 柿くへば鐘が鳴るなり法隆寺
> かきくへば　かねがなるなり　ほうりゅうじ

**Translation:** "Eating a persimmon — the bell sounds at Horyuji"

**Mora count:** か・き・く・へ・ば (5) / か・ね・が・な・る・な・り (7) / ほ・う・りゅ・う・じ (5)

**季語:** 柿 (persimmon) — autumn

**切れ字:** なり (copula of direct perception)

**Analysis:** Two sensory experiences occur simultaneously: the taste of a persimmon and the sound of a temple bell. Neither is more important than the other. The poem does not say "while eating a persimmon, I heard a bell" — it presents the two experiences in parallel, connected by the conditional ば but not subordinated. The persimmon's autumn sweetness and the ancient bell's resonance merge into a single moment. This is 写生 in action: direct, precise, present-tense observation without commentary.

### Haiku 6: Spring (Original — Classical Style)

> 花散るや踏みゆく人の足音も
> はなちるや　ふみゆくひとの　あしおとも

**Translation:** "Cherry petals scatter — even the footsteps of those who walk through them..."

**Mora count:** は・な・ち・る・や (5) / ふ・み・ゆ・く・ひ・と・の (7) / あ・し・お・と・も (5)

**季語:** 花散る (cherry blossoms scattering) — late spring

**切れ字:** や — cuts after the first phrase, separating the visual image of falling petals from the auditory image of footsteps

**Analysis:** The も at the end is crucial — "even the footsteps." It implies that everything is softened, hushed, absorbed by the carpet of fallen petals. The sound of walking is muffled. The も suggests that the petals' silence has spread to encompass the human world. What is not said: the beauty of the scene, the transience of the blossoms, the way even human activity becomes gentle in the presence of natural beauty.

---

### Haiku 7: Summer (Original — Classical Style)

> 蝉しぐれ石の階段照りかえし
> せみしぐれ　いしのかいだん　てりかえし

**Translation:** "Cicada chorus — the stone stairway throws back the glare"

**Mora count:** せ・み・し・ぐ・れ (5) / い・し・の・か・い・だ・ん (7) / て・り・か・え・し (5)

**季語:** 蝉しぐれ (cicada chorus) — high summer

**切れ字:** None explicitly marked; the cut falls naturally after the first phrase through the juxtaposition of sound (cicadas) and sight (glaring stone)

**Analysis:** Two sensory experiences juxtaposed: the overwhelming sound of cicadas and the blinding glare of sun reflected off stone stairs. Both are excessive — too much sound, too much light. Together they create an almost physical sensation of midsummer heat. The word 照りかえし (throwing back light, reflecting glare) suggests the heat bouncing back and forth between sky and stone, just as the cicada sound bounces between trees and walls. Neither sound nor light can escape.

---

### Haiku 8: Autumn (Original — Classical Style)

> 月見酒盃に映りし雲一つ
> つきみざけ　さかずきにうつりし　くもひとつ

**Translation:** "Moon-viewing sake — reflected in the cup, a single cloud"

**Mora count:** つ・き・み・ざ・け (5) / さ・か・ず・き・に・う・つ・り・し (special — this runs slightly long at 9, a permissible variation in classical style with the archaic し) / く・も・ひ・と・つ (5)

**季語:** 月見 (moon-viewing) — autumn

**Analysis:** The scene is elegant: drinking sake while viewing the autumn moon. But the poem focuses not on the moon itself but on what appears in the sake cup — a single cloud. The diminutive scale (the whole sky reduced to a cup's surface) creates a sense of intimacy. The archaic past-tense し (classical equivalent of た) gives the poem a formal, traditional feel. The cloud is 一つ — just one. It is alone in the cup, as the drinker may be alone under the moon. What is not said: the loneliness that moon-viewing traditionally implies; the impermanence of the cloud's reflection; the moment when the drinker raises the cup and the image vanishes.

---

### Haiku 9: Modern (Original — Domestic)

> 冷蔵庫ひらけば夜の光かな
> れいぞうこ　ひらけばよるの　ひかりかな

**Translation:** "The refrigerator — when I open it, the light of night"

**Mora count:** れ・い・ぞ・う・こ (5) / ひ・ら・け・ば・よ・る・の (7) / ひ・か・り・か・な (5)

**季語:** None traditional — this is a 無季俳句 (seasonless haiku), common in modern practice

**切れ字:** かな at the end — lending a note of wonder to the ordinary image

**Analysis:** A completely modern, domestic scene: opening a refrigerator at night, the light spilling out into the dark kitchen. Yet the language treats it with the same attention a classical haiku would give to moonlight or fireflies. 夜の光 (the light of night) elevates the refrigerator's glow to something almost beautiful. The かな at the end adds quiet wonder — "how strange, this light." The poem finds the poetic within the mundane, which is the central achievement of modern haiku.

---

### Haiku 10: Modern (Original — Surreal)

> 階段を降りても降りても春の底
> かいだんを　おりてもおりても　はるのそこ

**Translation:** "Down the stairs, still down the stairs — the bottom of spring"

**Mora count:** か・い・だ・ん・を (5) / お・り・て・も・お・り・て・も (8 — deliberately long, mimicking the endless descent) / は・る・の・そ・こ (5)

**季語:** 春 (spring)

**Analysis:** This haiku breaks the 5-7-5 form in the middle phrase (8 mora instead of 7), and the excess is meaningful: the extra mora makes the descent feel longer, more relentless. The repetition of 降りても降りても ("even descending, still descending") creates a dream-like, surreal quality. And the destination — 春の底 ("the bottom of spring") — is impossible. Spring has no bottom. Yet the image is immediately vivid: a sense of descending deeper and deeper into the season, into its warmth and heaviness and dream-quality. The poem hovers between observation and hallucination.

### Tanka Practice

### Tanka 1: Classical Style (Original)

> あかねさす夕べの空を見上げつつ帰らぬ人を待つ心かな
> あかねさす　ゆうべのそらを　みあげつつ　かえらぬひとを　まつこころかな

**Translation:** "Looking up at the madder-glowing evening sky, the heart that waits for one who will not return"

**Mora count:** 5-7-5-7-7 (31 mora)

**Analysis:**

- あかねさす is a 枕詞 (makurakotoba, "pillow word") — a classical fixed epithet traditionally associated with 日 (sun) or 昼 (day), here repurposed for the evening sky's red glow.
- つつ (continuative) indicates ongoing action — the looking-up is sustained, habitual.
- 帰らぬ人 — "one who will not return" — is deliberately ambiguous. Has the person died? Left permanently? Simply not yet returned? The ambiguity is the poem's emotional center.
- The final かな adds an exhale of emotion: "oh, this heart..."

The tanka moves from external image (sky) to internal state (waiting heart), with the pivot at the third phrase. The upper verse (5-7-5) gives us the scene; the lower verse (7-7) gives us the feeling.

### Tanka 2: Modern Style (Original)

> コンビニの光のなかに立ちつくし買うものもなく夜が明けてゆく
> コンビニの　ひかりのなかに　たちつくし　かうものもなく　よがあけてゆく

**Translation:** "Standing transfixed in the light of a convenience store, with nothing to buy, the night giving way to dawn"

**Mora count:** 5-7-5-7-7 (31 mora)

**Analysis:** A thoroughly modern tanka that uses the traditional 5-7-5-7-7 structure for a contemporary scene. The convenience store — that most mundane of modern Japanese spaces — becomes a site of existential pause. The character stands in the artificial light with 買うものもなく — "nothing to buy," which implies also "no reason to be here." The final phrase, 夜が明けてゆく, moves from the human to the cosmic: while this person stands purposelessly in fluorescent light, dawn is arriving. The contrast between the static human figure and the moving sky is the poem's emotional core.

## 19.7 Extended Reading: A Modern Poem

The following is an original poem written in the style of modern Japanese free verse, followed by analysis.

### 『窓のない部屋で』

> 窓のない部屋で
> 私は窓の絵を描いた
>
> 窓の向こうには
> 海があった
> 青い、深い、知らない海
>
> 波の音が聞こえるはずはなかった
> でも私は聞いた
> あるいは
> 聞こうとした
>
> 描いた窓を開けることはできない
> それは知っている
> 描いた海に溺れることもできない
> それも知っている
>
> けれど
>
> 知っていることと
> 信じていることは
> 違う
>
> 私は窓を描き続ける
> いつか
> 風が吹くまで

### Translation: "In a Room Without Windows"

> In a room without windows
> I drew a picture of a window
>
> Beyond the window
> there was a sea
> Blue, deep, a sea I did not know
>
> There was no way I could hear the sound of waves
> But I heard them
> Or rather
> I tried to hear them
>
> I cannot open a window I drew
> I know that
> I cannot drown in a sea I drew
> I know that too
>
> And yet
>
> Knowing
> and believing
> are different
>
> I keep drawing windows
> Until someday
> the wind blows

### Analysis

This poem operates on the tension between impossibility and desire. The room without windows is a condition — perhaps isolation, perhaps limitation, perhaps the condition of being human. The drawn window is art itself: an image of what is absent, which cannot replace reality but which the speaker insists on making anyway.

**Structural features:**
- Line breaks create pauses that control rhythm and emphasis. The single word けれど standing alone as a line turns the entire poem.
- The repetition of 知っている (I know) followed by the distinction between knowing and believing creates a logical structure that the poem then transcends.
- The final three lines move from ongoing action (描き続ける) to temporal hope (いつか) to an image (風が吹くまで) — ending on a condition that may or may not be met.

**Language features:**
- Simple vocabulary throughout. The poem's power comes from structure and juxtaposition, not from rare words.
- The self-correction あるいは (or rather) is a prose technique borrowed into poetry, giving the speaker a halting, honest quality.
- 溺れる (to drown) introduces danger into what has been a gentle meditation — the drawn sea is not merely comforting but also potentially consuming.

### 『雨あがり』(Original Song Lyric)

>
> 傘をたたんで歩き出す
> 濡れた靴が光ってる
> こんな小さなことでいい
> こんな小さなことがいい
>
> 雨あがりの匂いがする
> 土と草とアスファルト
> 混ざり合って名前のない
> 懐かしい匂いがする

### Translation: "After the Rain"

> I walk through the town after the rain
> The sky reflected in a puddle
> is upside down but still blue
> so somehow I start to smile
>
> Since the day you disappeared
> more mornings than I can count have come
> more nights than I can count have gone
> only the seasons change honestly
>
> I fold my umbrella and start walking
> my wet shoes are shining
> this kind of small thing is enough
> this kind of small thing is good
>
> I can smell the after-rain
> soil and grass and asphalt
> all mixed together, a nameless
> nostalgic smell

### Analysis

**Mora-music alignment:** Each line is designed to fit a musical phrase. Notice the parallel structure of the second verse: 数えきれないほどの朝が来て / 数えきれないほどの夜が去って — the repetition creates a musical pattern where the melody would repeat with variation.

**Poetic techniques:**
- **体言止め:** The second and fourth verses end on nouns (匂いがする is technically a verb phrase, but the emphasis falls on 匂い). This creates an open, unresolved quality.
- **でいい / がいい contrast:** The shift from で (sufficiency) to が (preference) in こんな小さなことでいい / こんな小さなことがいい is subtle but crucial. The first line says "this is enough." The second says "this is what I want." The shift from resignation to affirmation is the emotional core of the verse.
- **Namelessness:** 名前のない匂い (a nameless smell) echoes the Japanese poetic tradition of finding beauty in what cannot be categorized or named.

**Register:** The language is casual-poetic — colloquial enough for a pop song (なんだか, こんな), but with enough literary texture (数えきれないほどの, 混ざり合って) to feel crafted rather than conversational.

**Structure:** This lyric uses shorter verses than "After the Rain," creating a more fragmented, contemplative feel. The final two lines stand alone as a coda — the emotional resolution compressed into the simplest possible statement.

**Key literary features:**
- **Personification of the timetable:** 時刻表だけが明日を知っている — "only the timetable knows about tomorrow." The inanimate object possesses knowledge that the speaker lacks. This is a gentle surrealism common in Japanese song lyrics.
- **The unsaid:** 言葉にならなかった想い (feelings that never became words) is the thematic center. The song is itself an attempt to give form to what resisted language — a meta-poetic gesture.
- **名前のない時間 (time without a name):** Echoes the 名前のない匂い from the previous song, suggesting a thematic preoccupation with what exists beyond categorization. This unnamed time between night and morning is liminal — neither one thing nor another — and the speaker wants to inhabit that in-between space.
- **The wind:** 誰も乗らない風 (wind carrying no one) is free in a way the speaker is not. The envy is quiet but present.

## 19.9 Comparative Table: Compressed Forms

| Form | Length | Constraints | Freedom | Core skill |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 俳句 | 17 mora | 5-7-5; 季語; 切れ字 | Image choice, juxtaposition | Seeing |
| 短歌 | 31 mora | 5-7-5-7-7 | Emotion, narrative, reflection | Feeling |
| 現代詩 | Variable | None formal; self-imposed | Line break, visual, conceptual | Thinking |
| 歌詞 | Determined by melody | Musical meter, singability | Emotional directness, repetition | Resonating |

### What These Forms Share

Despite their differences, all four compressed forms share certain principles:

1. **Economy.** Every word must earn its place. There is no room for filler, decoration, or unnecessary explanation.
2. **Sensory primacy.** Concrete images are preferred over abstract statements. The reader should see, hear, smell, taste, or touch before thinking.
3. **Resonance (余韻).** The poem or song does not end when the words stop. The best compressed expression leaves a vibration in the reader's mind that continues after the final word.
4. **The unsaid.** What is omitted is as carefully chosen as what is included. The spaces, silences, and gaps are part of the composition.
5. **Musical awareness.** Even poems that are not songs are shaped by sound — the texture of consonants and vowels, the rhythm of mora, the rise and fall of pitch.

## 19.10 Summary

This chapter has explored the world of compressed expression in Japanese:

1. **Haiku** operates through the juxtaposition of images separated by a cut. The seasonal word anchors the poem in the natural world, and the empty space around and between the images is where meaning lives. We examined haiku by Basho, Buson, Issa, and Shiki, and saw how each poet brings a distinct sensibility to the form.

2. **Tanka** extends the haiku's compression to allow direct expression of emotion, pivoting at the third phrase from observation to feeling. Its 31-mora structure provides enough space for both scene and sentiment, outer world and inner world.

3. **Modern poetry** breaks free from mora-counting constraints but retains the Japanese language's natural 5-7 rhythms as a background against which variation is felt. Line breaks, visual arrangement, and the interplay of kanji and kana become new tools for creating meaning.

4. **Song lyrics** demonstrate how musical constraints shape literary expression, forcing compression and repetition that can achieve poetic effects. The one-to-one relationship between mora and musical notes creates unique possibilities and limitations.

5. **All compressed forms** share a common principle: what is not said matters as much as what is said. The reader's task is not merely to decode the words but to inhabit the space they create.

The ability to read compressed expression is not a separate skill from reading prose. It is the same skill — attention to language — taken to its highest intensity. A reader who can dwell in the silence after a haiku, who can feel the turn of a tanka, who can follow the breath of a free verse line, will bring that same sensitivity to every sentence they read in Japanese.
