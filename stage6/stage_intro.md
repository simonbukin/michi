# Stage 6: N1 Mastery

## What This Stage Covers

Stage 6 exists because passing N1 and mastering Japanese are not the same thing. You have the grammar, the kanji foundation, the classical reading competence, and the cultural literacy. Now the work changes again: you stop acquiring new structural knowledge and start refining what you already have — discriminating nuance, shifting registers fluidly, catching errors that trip up even advanced learners, and building the specialized vocabulary that lets you operate in professional and academic domains without hesitation.

This stage covers:

- **Nuance discrimination** — Systematic comparison of near-synonyms and overlapping grammar: when にとって and に対して diverge, why ようやく and やっと feel different, how ことにする and ことになる encode agency, and the subtle register differences between expressions that dictionaries translate identically
- **Register fluidity** — Moving between formal written prose, casual spoken Japanese, literary narration, business communication, academic writing, and internet-inflected speech within a single conversation or text without stumbling or sounding mismatched
- **Error recognition and self-correction** — Identifying common fossilized errors, hypercorrection patterns, unnatural collocations, and particle misuse that persist even at advanced levels — not just in your own output but in texts produced by other non-native speakers
- **Specialized domain vocabulary** — Building vocabulary clusters for specific fields: law (判例, 訴訟, 損害賠償), medicine (診断, 処方, 副作用), technology (実装, 仕様, 互換性), finance (利回り, 減価償却, 時価総額), and academia (論旨, 査読, 帰結), reaching 10,000-12,000+ total vocabulary
- **Novel-length literary reading** — Sustained reading of full novels, not excerpts: 夏目漱石, 谷崎潤一郎, 川端康成, 三島由紀夫 through 村上春樹, 小川洋子, and contemporary prizewinners, maintaining comprehension and engagement across hundreds of pages
- **Sociolinguistic variation** — Dialect awareness (関西弁, 東北弁, 博多弁), gendered speech patterns, generational differences, professional jargon, and the way social context reshapes grammar choices in real time
- **Humor, irony, and indirectness** — Understanding what is not said: 皮肉, understatement, in-group jokes, 漫才 structure, literary irony, passive-aggressive politeness, and the pragmatic meaning behind phrases like ちょっと… and 考えさせてください
- **Contrastive analysis and 常用漢字 completion** — Explicit comparison of Japanese structures against English-language transfer errors, completion of the full 常用漢字 set (2,136 characters), and metalinguistic awareness of how Japanese organizes information differently from English

## What Changes from Stage 5

- **The focus shifts from "acquiring knowledge" to "refining judgment"** — Stage 5 gave you the full N1 inventory, classical grammar, 四字熟語, and literary reading skills. Stage 6 assumes all of that is in place and works on the harder problem: knowing which of three grammatically correct options sounds natural, which register fits the moment, and which word carries the right connotation
- **Reading targets shift from stamina-building to interpretive depth** — You are no longer proving you can sustain attention across ten pages; you are reading entire novels and engaging with literary style, authorial voice, unreliable narration, and cultural subtext that requires rereading and reflection
- **Listening targets shift from comprehension to full participation** — Stage 5 asked you to follow unscripted media; Stage 6 asks you to catch the irony in a 漫才 routine, understand why a comment on a variety show got a laugh, and notice when a speaker is being deliberately indirect or evasive
- **Vocabulary growth becomes domain-driven rather than frequency-driven** — The most common 8,000 words are behind you. The next 2,000-4,000 come from specific fields you choose to engage with — your profession, your reading interests, your daily life in Japan. No two learners at this stage have the same vocabulary
- **Error correction replaces error tolerance** — At earlier stages, making yourself understood was the goal. At this stage, the goal is sounding natural — eliminating the fossilized mistakes, unnatural collocations, and register mismatches that mark even fluent non-native speakers
- **No furigana for any 常用漢字** — Furigana appears only for characters outside the 常用漢字 set

## Prerequisites Checklist

Before beginning Stage 6, you should be able to:

- [ ] Use the full N1 grammar inventory fluently in recognition and produce the most common patterns in appropriate contexts
- [ ] Read and understand 四字熟語 (50-100) and proverbs (30-50) as they appear in editorials, speeches, and everyday argumentation
- [ ] Parse classical Japanese verb forms and auxiliary verbs (ず, たり, べし, まじ, なり, けり) in literary and legal contexts without modern glosses
- [ ] Sustain reading across 10+ pages of unsimplified Japanese text — editorials, short stories, reports — without comprehension collapse
- [ ] Follow fully unscripted native-speed media: podcasts, variety shows, 漫才, documentaries, and interviews with natural elision, false starts, and dialect
- [ ] Navigate manga, light novel, and internet Japanese registers alongside formal written and spoken styles
- [ ] Demonstrate historical and cultural literacy sufficient to understand allusions to 明治維新, 戦後, バブル崩壊, and standard debate rhetoric
- [ ] Recognize approximately 1,500 kanji in context
- [ ] Know approximately 8,000 vocabulary items

If any of these feel shaky, review the relevant Stage 5 chapters before proceeding.

## What Mastery Means

Mastery is not knowing everything. No native speaker knows every word in their language, reads every register with equal ease, or never mishears a sentence. Mastery is the ability to handle anything — to encounter an unfamiliar word and infer its meaning from kanji components and context, to hear an unfamiliar dialect form and map it to standard Japanese, to read a dense legal paragraph and extract its argument even when half the vocabulary is domain-specific.

What separates a learner who passed N1 from a learner who has mastered Japanese is not a larger grammar list or a higher kanji count. It is the depth of processing behind every sentence. The N1 passer reads ご検討いただければ幸いです and understands the literal meaning. The master reads the same phrase and hears the social calculation behind it — the careful distance, the implied request, the pressure wrapped in politeness — and knows exactly how to respond in kind.

This stage cannot give you that depth directly. No textbook can. What it can do is name the dimensions you need to develop — nuance, register, error awareness, domain range, literary interpretation, sociolinguistic sensitivity — and provide structured practice in each one. The depth itself comes from the same place it has always come from: thousands of hours of reading, listening, speaking, and living in the language.

The learners who reach mastery are not the ones who studied the most textbooks. They are the ones who used Japanese the most — who read novels for pleasure, argued about politics in Japanese, laughed at jokes they once would have missed, and kept going long after the textbook ran out of pages.

This is the last stage. After this, the textbook ends and the language continues.
