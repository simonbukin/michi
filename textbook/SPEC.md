## For use by Claude Code or any agent generating this textbook.

# 1. MISSION

We are writing a complete, modern Japanese-language textbook series that takes a learner from absolute zero to JLPT N1. This document concerns **Stage 1 (N5)**, the first volume.

The book is **comprehension-focused**. The learner is never asked to produce output — no writing exercises, no speaking drills, no "now conjugate this verb from memory." Every structure is taught so the learner can **recognize, parse, and understand** it in reading and listening. However, the grammar is taught with the same rigor and depth as a four-skills course, because comprehension of Japanese requires understanding *why* a speaker chose one form over another.

**Handwriting is excluded entirely.** Kanji are taught for recognition. Stroke order is not covered.

---

# 2. PEDAGOGICAL GROUNDING

## 2.1 Why Comprehension-First?

The dominant approach in language pedagogy since the 1980s has been "communicative" — emphasizing output from early stages. This works well in classroom settings with native-speaker interaction. But for self-study and for learners whose primary goal is to *understand* Japanese (reading novels, following media, comprehending workplace communication), a comprehension-first approach is more efficient and produces deeper structural understanding.

The theoretical basis draws from:

- **Krashen's Input Hypothesis** — acquisition occurs through comprehensible input, not through output practice. Output is a *result* of acquisition, not a cause.
- **Nation's Four Strands** — meaning-focused input should be the largest strand in a balanced program. For a comprehension-focused course, it is the dominant strand.
- **Extensive Reading research (Tadoku)** — massive amounts of level-appropriate reading input produce vocabulary acquisition, grammatical intuition, and reading fluency that cannot be achieved through grammar study alone.

We are not dogmatic about this. We simply believe that for this book's target audience, comprehension is the right primary channel. Learners who want to develop output skills can layer speaking/writing practice on top of the structural knowledge this book provides.

## 2.2 Why Teach Grammar at Full Depth?

Some comprehension-focused approaches de-emphasize explicit grammar ("just read a lot and you'll figure it out"). We reject this for Japanese because:

1. **Japanese is agglutinative and head-final.** A single verb can encode tense, polarity, aspect, voice, causation, and politeness level through stacked suffixes. Without explicit morphological knowledge, the learner cannot segment these suffixes and will misparse sentences.

2. **The writing system requires explicit instruction.** Unlike European languages, where a learner can start reading immediately, Japanese requires learning two phonetic scripts and a logographic system before *any* reading is possible.

3. **Social register is grammaticalized.** Politeness is not a matter of word choice (as in English) but of verb morphology, pronoun selection, and an entire honorific conjugation system. A learner who doesn't understand this system will systematically misread social dynamics in everything they encounter.

4. **The は/が distinction, aspect system, giving/receiving verbs, and conditional forms are not acquirable through input alone at reasonable timescales.** These require explicit contrastive instruction.

## 2.3 Core Pedagogical Principles

### Principle 1: Spiral Curriculum
Key structures are introduced early at a basic level and revisited at increasing depth in later stages. The は/が distinction, for example, gets a gentle first encounter in Stage 1, a full treatment in Stage 3, and nuance refinement in Stage 6. The learner is told explicitly when a topic is being simplified and will be revisited.

### Principle 2: Pronunciation Is a Comprehension Skill
Pitch accent, mora timing, devoicing, and intonation are taught from Chapter 1 — not for production, but because they are **essential for listening comprehension**. A learner who doesn't know that 雨 and 飴 differ only in pitch will mishear both. A learner unaware of devoicing will fail to segment natural speech. Every vocabulary item in the book has its pitch accent marked.

### Principle 3: Cultural Knowledge Is Comprehension Infrastructure
Culture is not in sidebar boxes. It is woven into grammar explanations (why 授受表現 encodes social perspective), into thematic content (understanding 本音と建前 to parse indirect refusals), and into pragmatic instruction (why a sentence ending in けど... with no conclusion is a polite refusal, not an unfinished thought). Without cultural knowledge, a learner will parse every word correctly and still misunderstand the message.

### Principle 4: Authentic Context from Day One
Even at N5, example sentences and reading passages should feel like things a real person might actually say or write. No "this is a pen" unless it occurs in a plausible context. Reading passages simulate real genres: self-introductions, emails, restaurant reviews, daily routine narratives, directions, online profiles.

### Principle 5: No Romaji After Chapter 2
Romaji is used in Chapters 1 and 2 only, as a bridge while the learner is acquiring kana. From Chapter 3 onward, all Japanese is written in Japanese script. This is non-negotiable. Romaji dependency is the single most common cause of stalled progress in Japanese learners.

### Principle 6: Kanji as a System, Not a List
Kanji are never taught as isolated characters to memorize. Each kanji is introduced with:
- Its radical and what meaning category the radical suggests
- At least one 音読み and one 訓読み reading (where applicable)
- At least two vocabulary words that use it
- A note on any phonetic component

From Stage 3 onward (not applicable to this volume, but the groundwork is laid here), kanji are studied in semantic clusters.

### Principle 7: Explicit Verb Classification
The 五段/一段/irregular distinction is taught explicitly and early, because it governs all conjugation. The learner learns to identify verb class from dictionary form, including the tricky cases where a verb *looks* like 一段 but is actually 五段 (かえる, はしる, しる, etc.). Every verb in the vocabulary lists is marked for class.

# 3. WHAT THIS BOOK COVERS (Stage 1 / N5 Scope)

## 3.1 Structural / Grammatical Goals

**Writing systems:**
- Full recognition of hiragana and katakana (all 46 basic + dakuten + handakuten + combinations + small っ + long vowels)
- Katakana extensions for foreign sounds (ティ, ファ, etc.)
- ~100 kanji for recognition (25 taught explicitly with full analysis, ~75 encountered through vocabulary)
- Radical awareness and the logic of kanji composition

**Pronunciation and prosody (for listening comprehension):**
- Mora timing and minimal pairs
- The five vowels (no reduction, no diphthongization)
- Devoicing of い/う between voiceless consonants
- Pitch accent: the four Tokyo noun patterns, accent notation, minimal pairs
- Basic intonation contours: questions, confirmation, surprise, trailing off
- The Japanese r, ふ, moraic nasal ん and its allophonic variation

**Sentence-level basics:**
- X は Y です / じゃありません (topic-comment predication)
- Existence: いる (animate) / ある (inanimate) with locative に
- Demonstratives: こ・そ・あ・ど system (things, modifiers, places, directions)
- Particles: は, が, を, に, で, へ, と, も, の, から, まで, か, よ, ね

**Verb system:**
- Three-class identification: 五段, 一段, irregular (する, くる)
- ます-form: four variants (affirmative/negative × non-past/past)
- て-form: full formation including 五段 sound changes
- て-form applications: ている (progressive/resultative), てください, てもいい, てはいけない, て-chaining
- ない-form (plain negative)
- た-form (plain past)
- The full plain-form paradigm (4 forms per verb)
- する compound verbs (べんきょうする, りょうりする, etc.)

**Adjectives:**
- い-adjectives: all four forms + prenominal + て-form; irregular いい/よい
- な-adjectives: all four forms + prenominal (な) + て-form (で)
- すき/きらい/じょうず/へた with が

**Grammar patterns:**
- たい (want to — first person), たがる (third person)
- ほしい (want a thing)
- つもり (intention)
- なければならない / なくてはいけない (must)
- ほうがいい (had better)
- でしょう (probably)
- と思う (I think — plain form embedding)
- ましょう (let's), ませんか (invitation)
- から/ので (because)
- けど/けれども (but, although, softener)
- んです/のです (explanatory)
- 前に, 後で, 時, ながら (time clauses)

**Counters:**
- つ, 人, 本, 枚, 台, 匹, 杯, 冊, 階 — taught as a system with all sound changes

**Social/pragmatic foundations:**
- The うち/そと principle — kinship terms (ちち vs おとうさん), preview of keigo logic
- Giving/receiving verbs: あげる, もらう, くれる as a perspective system
- Sentence-final particles: よ, ね, よね + recognition of な, ぞ, ぜ, わ, かな, っけ
- Aizuchi (back-channeling): うん, ええ, そうですか, なるほど, へえ
- Honorifics preview: recognition of いらっしゃる, おっしゃる, お/ご prefixes

## 3.2 Thematic / Situational Content

Reading passages and examples cover:
1. Self-introduction and personal background (family, うち/そと kinship)
2. Daily routine and schedule
3. Shopping and prices
4. Food, restaurants, ordering
5. Asking for and understanding directions
6. Describing people, places, objects, weather
7. Making plans and invitations
8. Hobbies and interests
9. Travel and transport basics
10. Giving advice and expressing obligations

## 3.3 Quantitative Targets

- ~800 vocabulary items (all with pitch accent marked)
- ~100 kanji (25 explicit + 75 contextual)
- 26 chapters across 7 parts
- 6 appendices

# 4. TONE AND STYLE

## 4.1 Voice

The textbook is written in a **direct, clear, intellectually honest** voice. It respects the learner's intelligence. It does not talk down, use excessive exclamation marks, or pretend Japanese is easy. It does not use empty encouragement ("Great job! You're doing amazing!"). It does explain *why* things work the way they do.

When something is genuinely difficult or confusing (the は/が distinction, て-form sound changes, the giving/receiving perspective system), the book says so directly and explains why it's hard, rather than glossing over it.

When a simplification is being made (e.g., "the full picture of は vs が will be covered in Stage 3"), the book tells the learner explicitly. Learners respect honesty about scope more than they respect false completeness.

## 4.2 Examples

Examples are **short** — typically one sentence. They illustrate exactly the point being made and no more. They are plausible things a real person might say. They use vocabulary the learner has already encountered where possible.

Every Japanese example has:
1. The Japanese in kana/kanji (no romaji after Chapter 2)
2. A natural English translation (not word-for-word, unless the word-for-word illuminates structure)
3. When the structure is new, a brief structural gloss showing particles and their functions

Example format:
```
> <ruby>私<rt>わたし</rt></ruby>は　<ruby>日本<rt>にほん</rt></ruby><ruby>料理<rt>りょうり</rt></ruby>が　<ruby>好<rt>す</rt></ruby>きです。
> I (topic) Japanese-food (subject) liked-is.
> "I like Japanese food."
```

The structural gloss is used when introducing new grammar and dropped once the pattern is established.

## 4.3 Formatting

- **Chapter structure:** Core explanation → examples → recognition notes → reading passage → vocabulary list
- **Vocabulary lists:** Always include pitch accent notation (⓪, ①, ②, etc.)
- **Verb vocabulary:** Always marked for class (五段, 一段, する, くる)
- **Tables** are used for conjugation paradigms, particle summaries, and vocabulary lists. They should be clean and readable.
- **Readings passages:** Written entirely in Japanese (kana + kanji at the level taught). A translation may follow but is not always necessary — by the later chapters, the learner should be reading without translation support.
- **No bullet points for running explanations.** The book reads as prose. Tables and lists are reserved for reference material (conjugation tables, vocabulary lists, particle summaries).
- **Bold** is used sparingly for key terms on first introduction.
- **Spacing:** Japanese examples use half-width spaces between grammatical phrases for readability at the beginner level. This is a pedagogical aid that mirrors how some Japanese textbooks present text to beginners. It does not represent actual Japanese spacing conventions (Japanese uses no spaces).

## 4.4 Things to Avoid

- Romaji after Chapter 2 (except in Chapter 1's phonology discussion where IPA-like notation is needed)
- Gendered language about the learner
- Assumption that the learner's native language is English (but English is the metalanguage of the book)
- Excessive cultural generalizations ("Japanese people always...")
- Presenting disputed or evolving norms as fixed rules (e.g., ら抜き言葉 is acknowledged as standard in speech)
- Using vocabulary in examples before it has been introduced
- Introducing grammar in reading passages before it has been explained (reading passages are *practice*, not *discovery*)
- Empty filler text or padding — every sentence in the book should teach something or illustrate something

# 6. GENERATION INSTRUCTIONS

## 6.1 Chapter Generation Order

Generate chapters sequentially. Each chapter may reference vocabulary and grammar from all preceding chapters but never from subsequent chapters. Maintain a running vocabulary list and grammar inventory to enforce this constraint.

## 6.2 Chapter Length

Each chapter should be substantial — roughly 2,000-4,000 words of English prose plus Japanese examples. The target is textbook-quality density, not blog-post brevity. A chapter should feel complete: a learner should be able to read one chapter and feel they've learned something real.

Chapters on major grammatical topics (Chapter 8: Verb Classification, Chapter 10: て-Form, Chapter 12: Plain Forms) will be on the longer end. Chapters that are primarily vocabulary and situational (Chapter 18: Food and Shopping) can be shorter.

## 6.3 Vocabulary Management

Maintain a cumulative vocabulary list across chapters. Each chapter's vocabulary section introduces NEW words only. The running total should reach approximately 800 by the end of Chapter 26.

Every vocabulary item must include:
- Japanese (in kana, or kanji + kana if the kanji has been taught or is being introduced)
- Pitch accent number (⓪ = heiban, ① = drop after mora 1, etc.)
- English meaning
- Verb class marker for verbs (五段, 一段, する)

Pitch accent data should be verified against the NHK日本語発音アクセント新辞典 or equivalent reference. When in doubt, mark ⓪ (heiban) and note uncertainty.

## 6.4 Kanji Management

25 kanji are taught explicitly in Chapter 4 (with full radical analysis, both readings, and example compounds). An additional ~75 kanji are introduced through vocabulary items in subsequent chapters.

All vocabulary items that have a standard kanji form should be written in kanji with furigana (ruby annotations) from their first appearance onward. In running text, use HTML ruby tags: `<ruby>学生<rt>がくせい</rt></ruby>`. The first occurrence of each word per section (## heading) uses ruby; subsequent occurrences within the same section use bare kanji. Words that are conventionally written in kana only (e.g., いる, ある, もう, とても, おいしい, particles, demonstratives) remain in kana. For words with okurigana, only wrap the kanji portion: `<ruby>食<rt>た</rt></ruby>べる`. Vocabulary tables retain their existing parenthetical format `学生（がくせい）` since tables are reference material. This approach mirrors how native-reader children's materials use furigana — the learner sees the real kanji form from day one and gradually internalizes it through repeated exposure.

## 6.5 Reading Passages

Most chapters from Chapter 5 onward include a reading passage of 100-350 characters. These passages:
- Use ONLY grammar and vocabulary already taught (or being taught in the current chapter)
- Simulate a real genre (self-introduction, diary entry, email, restaurant review, online profile, directions, etc.)
- Are written entirely in Japanese script (kana + taught kanji)
- May include a translation for early chapters; later chapters should include at most a brief comprehension check, not a full translation
- Should feel natural, not stilted or textbook-artificial

## 6.6 Quality Checks

After generating each chapter, verify:
1. No romaji appears (after Chapters 1-2)
2. No vocabulary is used in examples before its introduction
3. No grammar is used in reading passages before its explanation
4. All Japanese examples are grammatically correct
5. All pitch accent markings are present in vocabulary lists
6. All verbs are marked for class
7. The tone is direct and respectful, not condescending or falsely cheerful
8. Conjugation tables are complete and consistent
9. Reading passages use only previously-taught material

## 6.8 What "Complete" Means

A chapter is complete when:
- All sections listed in the table of contents for that chapter are written
- All grammar points are explained with sufficient depth that a learner encountering them for the first time would understand
- At least 3-5 example sentences are given per new grammar point
- The vocabulary list is present with all required fields
- The reading passage (if specified) is present and uses only taught material
- The chapter could stand alone as a unit of instruction — a learner could read just this chapter and learn something coherent

# 7. SERIES CONTEXT

This is Stage 1 of a 6-stage series:

| Stage | Level | Kanji | Vocabulary | Key Focus |
|-------|-------|-------|------------|-----------|
| 1 | N5 | ~100 | ~800 | Script, basic sentences, verb system, adjectives, foundational social grammar |
| 2 | N4 | ~300 | ~1,500 | Full conjugation paradigm (potential, passive, causative, conditionals), complex sentences, giving/receiving, first keigo exposure |
| 3 | N3 | ~650 | ~3,500 | は/が full treatment, compound particles, sentence-ending modality, aspect system, colloquial grammar recognition, formal written grammar |
| 4 | N2 | ~1,000 | ~6,000 | Classical residues, advanced compound grammar, keigo full system, academic/journalistic style, onomatopoeia |
| 5 | N2→N1 bridge | ~1,500 | ~8,000 | N1 grammar, 四字熟語, proverbs, classical Japanese reading competence, reading stamina, manga/web Japanese |
| 6 | N1 | 2,000+ | 10,000+ | Nuance discrimination, register perception, specialized domains, literary comprehension |

Stage 1 should lay groundwork that later stages build on. Specifically:
- The は/が distinction is introduced honestly but gently, with explicit notice that Stage 3 provides the full treatment
- Keigo is previewed at a recognition level, with explicit notice that Stage 2 provides systematic coverage
- The giving/receiving system is introduced as a perspective system, with notice that て-form extensions come in Stage 2
- Classical grammar is not mentioned at all in Stage 1
- Colloquial contractions are mentioned briefly in Chapter 12 (ている→てる etc.) for awareness, not mastery

# 8. REFERENCE MATERIALS AND INFLUENCES

The design of this textbook draws on (and deliberately corrects weaknesses of):

**Genki (3rd edition):** Good pacing, natural dialogues, clear grammar explanations. Weakness: thin keigo coverage, insufficient pitch accent instruction, relies heavily on classroom pair-work that doesn't translate to self-study. We adopt Genki's general pacing for N5 content but add pronunciation depth and social/pragmatic content.

**Minna no Nihongo:** Comprehensive grammar, excellent drill design, used worldwide. Weakness: explanations are in a separate book, cultural content is thin, all-Japanese approach is great for immersion classrooms but poor for self-study without a teacher. We adopt MnN's grammatical thoroughness but embed explanations in the main text.

**Tobira:** Excellent intermediate bridge, strong cultural content. Weakness: assumes too much from Genki completers, creates an abrupt difficulty jump. Our Stage 2-3 design is partly a response to Tobira's gap. Not directly relevant to Stage 1 but informs our spiral curriculum design.

**Shinkanzen Master (N5-N1):** Best test-prep series, systematic grammar coverage. Weakness: not a teaching textbook — it's a review/drill book. We adopt SKM's grammatical inventory as our "completeness checklist" for each level.

**An Integrated Approach to Intermediate Japanese (IAIJ):** Strong reading-based approach with authentic materials. Weakness: only covers intermediate. Influence on our reading-passage design and our insistence on authentic genres.

**Tae Kim's Guide to Learning Japanese:** Clear, direct, no-nonsense explanations aimed at self-study. Weakness: not a structured course, no controlled vocabulary progression, some idiosyncratic terminology. We adopt Tae Kim's directness of voice and willingness to explain "why" rather than just "what."
