# A Comprehensive Japanese Curriculum: N5 → N1

## Comprehension-Focused, No Handwriting

This curriculum takes a learner from zero to JLPT N1 readiness, structured around **comprehension as the primary channel** — reading and listening — while leaving output (speaking, writing) to emerge naturally or through separate practice. Handwriting is excluded entirely. The design draws on the strengths of Genki, Minna no Nihongo, Tobira, An Integrated Approach to Intermediate Japanese, and Shinkanzen Master, while correcting for their common weaknesses.

---

## Overarching Design Principles

The course is divided into **six stages**, not five, because the N3→N2 and especially N2→N1 transitions are the steepest cliffs in Japanese learning. Each stage specifies **structural goals** (grammar, morphology, phonology), **thematic/situational content** (what the learner can understand texts and speech *about*), and **comprehension competencies** (what the learner can read and listen to, and at what speed and depth). Kanji and vocabulary targets are cumulative.

**Comprehension-first philosophy.** Every structure is taught so the learner can *recognize and parse* it — in text and in speech. The learner is never asked to produce a conjugation from memory, write an essay, or deliver a presentation. However, the grammar is taught with the same rigor and depth as in a four-skills course, because comprehension of Japanese requires understanding *why* a speaker chose one form over another. You cannot understand 食べさせられた without understanding causative-passive morphology; you cannot follow a business meeting without understanding keigo as a system.

**Pronunciation and prosody are comprehension skills.** Pitch accent, mora timing, devoicing, and intonation are taught not for production but because they are *essential for listening comprehension*. A learner who doesn't know that 雨 and 飴 differ only in pitch will mishear both. A learner unaware of devoicing will fail to segment natural speech. These are treated as core skills from Stage 1.

## STAGE 1 — Foundations (≈ N5)

**Duration:** ~150 hours | **Kanji:** ~100 | **Vocabulary:** ~800

### Structural / Grammatical Goals

**Writing systems.** Full recognition of hiragana and katakana. Introduction to kanji not as memorization targets but as a *system*: radicals, semantic components, phonetic components. The learner builds intuition for kanji composition so that encountering a new character is not a blank wall but a puzzle with clues. All 100 kanji are taught with at least one 音読み and one 訓読み reading, and at least two compound words.

**Pronunciation and prosody — first principles.**

- Mora timing: Japanese is mora-timed, not stress-timed. Each kana = one mora. Long vowels (おばあさん), geminate consonants (きって), and the moraic nasal (ん) each occupy one mora. This is drilled through listening discrimination exercises (きて vs きって, おばさん vs おばあさん).
- Pitch accent (高低アクセント): introduced as an awareness system. The learner is not expected to produce correct pitch, but to *hear* it. The four basic Tokyo-dialect noun accent patterns are introduced (頭高、中高、尾高、平板). Minimal pairs are used for training: 箸/橋, 雨/飴, 酒/鮭.
- Devoicing: /i/ and /u/ between voiceless consonants are regularly devoiced (です sounds like [des], not [desu]; 好き sounds like [ski]). This is taught explicitly to prevent the learner from failing to recognize common words in natural speech.
- Basic intonation contours: rising for questions (without か), falling for statements, the confirmation-seeking ね contour.

**Sentence-level basics.**

- X は Y です / じゃありません (topic-comment predication).
- Existence verbs いる (animate) and ある (inanimate), with locative particles に and で.
- Demonstratives: これ/それ/あれ/どれ and この/その/あの/どの — the three-way deictic system (speaker-proximal, addressee-proximal, distal) taught as a spatial and discourse system.

**Verb classification and conjugation.**

- Explicit teaching of the three verb classes: 五段動詞 (consonant-stem / u-verbs: 書く、飲む、話す、待つ etc.), 一段動詞 (vowel-stem / ru-verbs: 食べる、見る、起きる), and irregular verbs (する、来る). The learner is taught to identify verb class from dictionary form, including the tricky cases where a verb *looks* like 一段 but is actually 五段 (e.g., 知る、走る、帰る).
- ます-form: polite non-past affirmative/negative, past affirmative/negative. Taught as the default conversational register.
- て-form: taught as the single most important conjugation in the language. The 五段 sound changes are drilled until automatic (いて、いで、して、って、んで). Applications introduced at this stage:
  - ている: progressive (食べている = is eating) and resultative (知っている = knows; 結婚している = is married). The ambiguity between these two readings is addressed explicitly.
  - てください: polite request.
  - てもいい: permission.
  - てはいけない: prohibition.
- ない-form (plain negative) and its relationship to the ます-stem. The irregular ない-forms (ある→ない) are flagged.
- た-form (plain past), built from て-form knowledge.
- Dictionary form uses: before ことができる (ability), つもり (intention), 前に (before doing).

**Adjectives.**

- い-adjectives: predicate use (高い), prenominal use (高い山), past (高かった), negative (高くない), て-form for chaining (高くて).
- な-adjectives: predicate use (静かだ/静かです), prenominal use (静かな町), past (静かだった), negative (静かじゃない), て-form (静かで).
- The irregular いい/よい (よくない、よかった).

**Particles.**

- は vs が: introduced gently. At this stage, は = topic ("as for X..."), が = subject of existence/adjective sentences and new-information subject. The full picture is explicitly deferred — the learner is told this distinction deepens over time.
- を (direct object), に (direction, time, indirect object, location of existence), で (location of action, means, reason), へ (direction — overlap with に acknowledged), と (with, quotation), も (also), から (from, because), まで (until), の (possession, noun modification), か (question).

**Core grammar patterns.**

- たい (want to — first person), たがる (want to — third person observation).
- ましょう (let's), ませんか (won't you / invitation).
- ほうがいい (had better — with た-form for advice, dictionary form for general suggestion).
- なければならない / なくてはいけない (must).
- でしょう (probably).
- と思う (I think — with plain form).

**Counters.** Taught as a *system*, not piecemeal: つ (general), 人 (people), 本 (long thin objects), 枚 (flat objects), 台 (machines/vehicles), 匹 (small animals), 杯 (cups/glasses), 冊 (books), 階 (floors), 時/分/秒 (time). Sound changes in counter combinations (e.g., 一本 = いっぽん, 三杯 = さんばい) are drilled as listening recognition tasks.

**Time expressions.** 前に (before), 後で (after), 時 (when), ながら (while).

### Thematic / Situational Content

The learner should be able to understand spoken and written texts on these topics:

1. **Self-introduction and personal background.** Family (大家族 vs 核家族). Kinship terms with the うち/そと distinction (母 vs お母さん, 兄 vs お兄さん) — taught as the first instance of a pervasive social-register system.
2. **Daily routine and school/campus life.** Time expressions, sequential actions.
3. **Shopping and prices.** Numbers, counters, いくら, currency.
4. **Food and restaurants.** Ordering language (〜をお願いします, 〜にします), menu reading (katakana-heavy), dietary vocabulary.
5. **Directions and location.** まっすぐ、右に曲がる、〜の隣、map reading.
6. **Describing people, places, and objects.** Adjectives, ている for appearance/state.
7. **Plans and invitations.** ましょう、ませんか、つもり、予定.
8. **Hobbies and interests.** Frequency adverbs (よく、時々、あまり〜ない、全然〜ない).

### Comprehension Competencies

- Understand slow, clearly enunciated speech on everyday topics (textbook audio, beginner podcasts).
- Read and understand simple passages using hiragana, katakana, and ~100 kanji (with furigana support for unknowns).
- Parse basic sentence structure: identify the verb, its tense/polarity, the topic, and major arguments.
- Understand transactional interactions: a shopkeeper giving a price, a waiter taking an order, someone giving directions.

## STAGE 2 — Elementary Completion (≈ N4)

**Duration:** ~150 additional hours | **Kanji:** ~300 cumulative | **Vocabulary:** ~1,500

### Structural / Grammatical Goals

**Verb morphology — the full conjugation paradigm.**

- Potential form: 五段 書く→書ける, 一段 食べる→食べられる, する→できる, 来る→来られる. The learner is made aware of ら抜き言葉 (食べれる) as the dominant spoken reality, so they can recognize both forms.
- Volitional form: 書こう、食べよう. Uses: 〜ようと思う (I'm thinking of doing), 〜ようとする (try to do / be about to do).
- Passive form: 書かれる、食べられる. Three types are distinguished for comprehension:
  - Direct passive (本が読まれている = the book is being read).
  - Indirect / adversative passive (迷惑の受身): 雨に降られた (I was rained on — to my detriment). This distinctively Japanese construction is given special attention.
  - Passive of creation/birth (この小説は夏目漱石によって書かれた).
- Causative form: 書かせる、食べさせる. Meaning range: make someone do / let someone do (disambiguated by context and particles).
- Causative-passive: 書かせられる. The contracted 五段 form (書かされる) is taught alongside the full form as an equally common variant in speech. Meaning: was made to do.
- Imperative (書け) and prohibitive (書くな): taught for recognition — encountered in signs, manga, sports, emergency instructions, and rough speech. Not expected for production.
- **Conditional forms — contrastive presentation:**
  - と: natural/automatic consequence, habitual. Cannot be used for requests/intentions.
  - ば: hypothetical. 五段: 書けば, い-adj: 高ければ, な-adj/noun: 静かであれば. Strong "if" with a sense of sufficient condition.
  - たら: "when/if (completed)." Most versatile, default choice in conversation. Works with requests.
  - なら: "if (what you say is true)." Responds to information just received. The "if" applies to the *premise*, and the main clause can precede the condition chronologically.
  - These four are presented side by side with contrastive examples showing where they overlap and where they cannot substitute for each other.

**Complex sentences.**

- Relative clauses (連体修飾節): Japanese relativizes by gapping — there is no relative pronoun. 本を読んでいる人 = "the person [who is] reading a book." The learner develops the ability to parse left-branching modification, which is the opposite of English structure and a persistent source of comprehension difficulty.
- Noun-modifying clauses more broadly: ニュースを聞いた時 (the time when I heard the news), 友達が作ったケーキ (the cake my friend made).
- Quotation and reported speech: と言う、と思う、と聞いた、と書いてある. Direct vs indirect quotation. The learner becomes comfortable with the pattern [clause]と[verb of saying/thinking].
- **Giving and receiving verbs (授受表現).** This is taught as a *perspective system*, not just three vocabulary items:
  - あげる: I/in-group → other. もらう: I/in-group ← other. くれる: other → I/in-group.
  - て-form extensions: てあげる, てもらう, てくれる — the speaker *always* takes a perspective, and that perspective reveals social relationships and gratitude/obligation.
  - The うち/そと boundary governs the entire system. This is reinforced as a fundamental principle of Japanese social cognition.

**Honorific system — first exposure.** Not for production, but for comprehension. The learner needs this to understand any interaction involving hierarchy.

- 尊敬語 (respectful language, elevating the other): いらっしゃる、おっしゃる、ご覧になる、召し上がる、なさる. The productive pattern お〜になる (お読みになる).
- 謙譲語 (humble language, lowering oneself): 参る、申す、拝見する、いただく. The productive pattern お〜する (お持ちする).
- The learner should be able to follow a conversation in which one party uses keigo and identify who is being elevated or lowered.

**Grammatical patterns.**

- そうだ — two distinct forms: hearsay (plain form + そうだ: 雨が降るそうだ = I hear it will rain) vs appearance (stem + そうだ: 雨が降りそうだ = it looks like it will rain). The learner must distinguish these in context.
- ようだ / みたいだ (seems like, based on evidence), らしい (apparently, based on indirect evidence — also suffix meaning "-like": 男らしい).
- はずだ (should be the case, expectation based on logic).
- ことがある: with past (experience: 日本に行ったことがある) and with present (it sometimes happens).
- ことにする / ことになる: decision (する = I decided; なる = it has been decided — the agentless construction, pervasive in Japanese institutional speech).
- ために (purpose, with volitional verb) vs ように (purpose, with non-volitional verb or potential; so that).
- のに (despite), ても (even if).

**Prosody — deepened.**

- Pitch accent on verbs and adjectives: accented vs unaccented verbs, and how conjugation can shift the accent nucleus.
- Compound word accent rules (basic): the learner begins to notice that compounds often take the accent of the second element.
- Sentence-level prosody: focus intonation (emphasizing new/contrastive information), the prosodic effects of particles like は (creates a phrase boundary) and が (does not, in some environments).

### Thematic / Situational Content

1. **Travel.** Buying train tickets (指定席 vs 自由席, 片道 vs 往復), navigating stations (乗り換え, ホーム, 改札), booking accommodation, travel announcements.
2. **Health and the body.** Understanding a doctor's questions and instructions. Symptom vocabulary: 熱がある、お腹が痛い、咳が出る、めまいがする.
3. **Post office and banking.** Forms, procedures, automated machine instructions.
4. **Experiences and memories.** 〜たことがある, childhood memories, travel stories. Past narration in extended form.
5. **Reasons and explanations.** 〜んです (explanatory の), 〜ので, 〜から and their register differences.
6. **Seasonal events and cultural calendar.** お正月、節分、ひな祭り、お花見、ゴールデンウィーク、お盆、七五三. Cultural literacy as comprehension scaffolding — understanding what people are *talking about* at different times of year.
7. **Part-time jobs and basic workplace interactions.** Instructions, schedules, simple workplace conversations.
8. **Opinions on familiar topics.** 〜と思う、〜んじゃないかと思う、〜かもしれない.

### Comprehension Competencies

- Follow conversations at moderate speed on everyday topics, including conversations involving two distinct register levels.
- Read short authentic texts: simple newspaper headlines, signs, menus, short emails, social media posts (with dictionary support for unknown vocabulary).
- Parse complex sentences involving relative clauses, quotation, and chained て-form clauses.
- Recognize passive, causative, and causative-passive forms in context and determine who did what to whom.
- Follow the giving/receiving perspective in a conversation and understand the social dynamics it implies.

## STAGE 3 — Lower Intermediate (≈ N3)

**Duration:** ~200 hours | **Kanji:** ~650 cumulative | **Vocabulary:** ~3,500

### Structural / Grammatical Goals

**は vs が — full treatment.** This is the stage where the distinction is addressed comprehensively, because the learner is now encountering enough complex text to need it:

- は as topic marker (old/known information, frame-setting).
- は as contrastive marker (対比のは): コーヒーは飲むが、紅茶は飲まない.
- が as neutral-description subject (new information, exhaustive listing): 誰が来ましたか — 田中さんが来ました.
- が as subject within subordinate clauses (relative clauses, 時/前に/後で clauses typically take が, not は).
- が with certain predicates: 好き、嫌い、欲しい、分かる、できる、ある、いる, and perceptual verbs (見える、聞こえる).
- The interaction of は and が with information structure (what the speaker assumes the listener knows vs. what is new).

**Advanced predicate forms.**

- ざるを得ない (have no choice but to; classical ざる = ない).
- 〜かねる (cannot bring oneself to / find it difficult to) and 〜かねない (might well; risk of).
- 〜っぱなし (left in a state of — ドアを開けっぱなし).
- 〜きる / きれる / きれない (completely / able to completely / unable to completely: 食べきれない = can't eat it all).
- 〜がち (tend to, prone to — negative connotation: 忘れがち).
- 〜気味 (slight tendency: 風邪気味 = coming down with a cold).

**Compound particles and conjunctional expressions.** These are the workhorses of intermediate-to-advanced Japanese comprehension. Taught in functional clusters:

- Topic/scope: について (about), に関して (regarding — more formal), をめぐって (concerning — implies controversy).
- Perspective/standpoint: にとって (for, from the standpoint of), に対して (toward, in contrast to).
- Basis: に基づいて (based on), に応じて (in accordance with), に沿って (along, in line with).
- Range/extent: にわたって (spanning, over a range of), にかけて (from X to Y — temporal/spatial range), を通じて / を通して (through, throughout).
- Starting point: をはじめ (starting with, including), を中心に (centered on).
- Context/setting: において (in, at — formal locative), における (attributive form of において).

**Sentence-ending modality — the "explanatory" layer.** These forms signal the speaker's attitude toward the proposition, and misunderstanding them leads to pervasive comprehension errors:

- わけだ (it means that / so that's why — logical conclusion), わけがない (there's no way), わけにはいかない (can't very well do / it's not acceptable to do).
- ものだ: general truth/common sense (子供はよく遊ぶものだ), nostalgia (昔はよく遊んだものだ), mild obligation/expectation.
- ことだ: advice (もっと勉強することだ = you should study more).
- べきだ (ought to, should — stronger than ほうがいい).

**Aspect as a system.** Previously introduced piecemeal, now taught as a coherent aspectual framework:

- ている: progressive, resultative, habitual, experiential.
- ていく / てくる: directional (持っていく = take; 持ってくる = bring) and temporal change (暑くなっていく = it's going to get hotter; 増えてきた = has been increasing up to now).
- てしまう: completion (読んでしまった = finished reading) and regret/unintended consequence (忘れてしまった = ended up forgetting). Casual contractions: ちゃう/じゃう.
- ておく: preparatory action (買っておく = buy in advance). Casual contraction: とく.
- てある: resultative state from intentional action (窓が開けてある = the window has been opened [and is still open, intentionally]).

**Nominalization.** こと vs の in subject and object positions:

- Both work as nominalizers, but の is more colloquial and immediate, こと is more abstract and formal.
- Certain predicates require one or the other: のを見た (saw [someone] doing — direct perception requires の), ことを決めた (decided to — abstract decisions use こと).

**Conjunctions and discourse connectors.** Recognition of text-level cohesion devices:

- Addition: それに、その上、しかも (moreover), おまけに (on top of that).
- Contrast: しかし、ところが (but then, unexpectedly), それにしても (even so), 一方(で) (on the other hand), それに対して.
- Topic shift: ところで (by the way), それはそうと (that aside), さて (now then).
- Summary: 要するに (in short), つまり (that is to say), 結局 (in the end).

**Formal written grammar — first exposure.**

- である style (the assertive copula used in essays, newspaper editorials, academic writing).
- において / における (formal locative — already introduced in compound particles, now practiced in reading).
- ものと考えられる / と言える / と思われる (hedged academic assertions).

**Colloquial grammar — systematic recognition.**

- Contraction patterns the learner must recognize in speech: ている→てる、ておく→とく、てしまう→ちゃう/じゃう、なければ→なきゃ、のだ→んだ、と言う→って.
- Sentence-final particles: よ (assertion/new info), ね (confirmation/agreement-seeking), な (self-directed musing, or masculine softener), ぞ/ぜ (masculine assertion), わ (feminine softening, or emphatic in some dialects), かな (I wonder), っけ (was it? — trying to recall). Gender and generational nuance is addressed honestly: these tendencies are real but not rigid rules.
- Casual question formation: dropping か entirely and relying on rising intonation.

**Aizuchi (相槌) — back-channeling.** Taught explicitly as a comprehension skill. The learner must recognize and interpret:

- うん / ええ / はい (acknowledgment, not necessarily agreement).
- そうですか / そうなんですか (receiving new information).
- なるほど (I see, that makes sense).
- へえ (surprise/interest).
- 本当に？/ マジで？(really?).
- The rhythm and frequency of aizuchi — Japanese conversation expects near-constant back-channeling, and its absence signals disengagement.

### Thematic / Situational Content

1. **Japanese society and education.** 受験 (entrance exams), 塾 (cram schools), 部活動 (club activities), 学歴社会. Understanding conversations and texts about the education system.
2. **Work culture — introduction.** Job hunting basics, workplace hierarchy, 名刺交換, the concept of 報連相 (報告・連絡・相談).
3. **News comprehension.** Transition from NHK Web Easy to simplified real news. Understanding the structure of a Japanese news article (inverted pyramid, formal register).
4. **Technology and daily life.** スマホ依存、SNS、オンラインショッピング — understanding discussions about technology's social impact.
5. **Environment and natural disasters.** 地震、台風、洪水、防災、避難. Japan-specific vocabulary for emergencies. Understanding disaster announcements and safety instructions.
6. **Regional Japan.** 方言 awareness (recognizing that じゃん、やん、〜へん、〜ん are dialectal variants). 関東 vs 関西 cultural differences as a comprehension frame.
7. **Interpersonal relationships.** 上下関係、先輩・後輩、本音と建前. Understanding indirect communication — what is *not* said is often more important than what is.
8. **Summaries and explanations.** Understanding structured paragraphs: opinion pieces, product reviews, how-to articles.

### Comprehension Competencies

- Follow the main points of a news broadcast or podcast on familiar topics at near-natural speed.
- Read and understand the gist of newspaper articles with moderate dictionary support (looking up 5-10 words per article, not 30).
- Parse sentences with multiple layers of subordination (relative clause inside a nominalized clause inside a conditional).
- Recognize formal written register (である) and casual spoken register (contractions, particles) as distinct systems and switch between them.
- Understand extended discourse: follow a 5-minute monologue or a multi-paragraph article and identify the main argument, supporting points, and conclusion.
- Begin reading vertically (縦書き) — encountered in literature, newspapers, and many formal documents. The learner is exposed to vertical text regularly from this stage onward.

## STAGE 4 — Upper Intermediate (≈ N2)

**Duration:** ~250 hours | **Kanji:** ~1,000 cumulative | **Vocabulary:** ~6,000

### Structural / Grammatical Goals

**Classical/literary grammar residues in modern Japanese.** These are not historical curiosities — they appear constantly in written Japanese, proverbs, set phrases, laws, and formal speech:

- 〜ざる: classical negative (食わざる者働くべからず). Recognized in ざるを得ない (already learned) and in written/literary contexts.
- 〜ごとく / 〜ごとき: "like, as if" (literary comparisons).
- 〜べからず: "must not" (seen on signs: 芝生に入るべからず).
- 〜なり: "immediately upon" (見るなり泣き出した = broke into tears the moment she saw it).
- 〜や否や: "the moment that" (ベルが鳴るや否や学生が走り出した).
- 〜ずにはおかない / 〜ないではおかない: "cannot help but" (感動せずにはおかない).

**Advanced compound grammar.** These are taught in functional/semantic clusters for comprehension:

*Concession and counter-expectation:*
- 〜ものの (although — literary: 努力したものの、結果は出なかった).
- 〜ものを (and yet — implies regret/reproach: 言ってくれればよかったものを).
- 〜にもかかわらず (despite, notwithstanding).
- 〜くせに (despite — with negative judgment: 知らないくせに).
- 〜といえども (even though — formal/literary).
- 〜とはいえ (having said that, although).
- 〜たところで (even if one does — implies futility).

*Cause, reason, and consequence:*
- 〜ばかりに (just because — negative consequence: 一言言ったばかりに大変なことになった).
- 〜からには / 〜以上は (now that / since — implies obligation or determination).
- 〜からこそ (precisely because).
- 〜からといって (just because... doesn't mean).
- 〜あまり(に) (to such an extent that — excess leads to consequence).

*Extent and degree:*
- 〜に越したことはない (nothing better than; the more the better).
- 〜ないことには (unless — 試してみないことには分からない).
- 〜ないものでもない (it's not that one can't — double negative hedging).
- 〜っぽい (tendency, -ish: 忘れっぽい = forgetful; 子供っぽい = childish).

*Hypothetical and extreme conditions:*
- 〜ようものなら (if one were to dare — implies drastic consequence).
- 〜ものなら (if only one could — often with できる: できるものならやってみろ).

*Appearance and hearsay — refined:*
- 〜んばかり(に) (as if about to: 泣かんばかりの顔 = a face as if about to cry).
- 〜とばかりに (as if to say).
- 〜かのように (as though).

*Scope limiters:*
- 〜に限って (limited to; when it comes to — often ironic: 急いでいる時に限って電車が遅れる).
- 〜に限らず (not limited to).
- 〜のみならず (not only — formal).
- 〜はおろか (let alone).
- 〜ばかりか / 〜ばかりでなく (not only... but also).

*Other:*
- 〜を余儀なくされる (be forced to — formal: 変更を余儀なくされた).
- 〜を禁じ得ない (cannot suppress — emotion: 涙を禁じ得ない).
- ことにする / ことになる (review and extension — ことになっている = it is the rule/arrangement that).

**Keigo — full system for comprehension.** The learner needs to parse keigo fluently to follow business meetings, formal announcements, customer service interactions, and institutional communication:

- The five-part modern system:
  - 尊敬語 (respectful): いらっしゃる、おっしゃる、ご覧になる、召し上がる、くださる、なさる. Pattern: お/ご〜になる.
  - 謙譲語I (humble, object-oriented — 客体敬語): 伺う、申し上げる、差し上げる、お目にかかる. Pattern: お/ご〜する / お/ご〜いたす.
  - 謙譲語II / 丁重語 (humble, addressee-oriented — maintaining one's own dignity): 参る、申す、いたす、おる. Used regardless of whether there's an "object" of humility.
  - 丁寧語 (polite): です、ます、ございます.
  - 美化語 (beautifying): お水、ご飯、お手洗い.
- Recognition of common keigo errors and hypercorrection: させていただく overuse, 二重敬語 (e.g., お読みになられる), incorrectly using 尊敬語 about oneself. The learner recognizes these as errors, which aids comprehension of *intention* even in imperfect keigo.
- Keigo in business emails: the formulaic openings (お世話になっております) and closings (何卒よろしくお願いいたします), patterns like 〜していただければ幸いです, 〜いただけますでしょうか.

**Written style — academic and journalistic.**

- Academic hedging: 〜と考えられる (it is thought that), 〜と言える (it can be said that), 〜ではないだろうか (might it not be the case that — rhetorical question as gentle assertion), 〜傾向がある (there is a tendency to), 〜とは限らない (it is not necessarily the case that).
- Formal transitions: 第一に…第二に、前述の通り、以上のことから、一方、他方.
- Contrastive は in academic writing: used to set up comparisons across paragraphs.
- Nominalization-heavy prose: understanding sentences where the main content is buried in a nominalized clause before a light verb (検討を行う = 検討する; 影響を与える = 影響する).

**Discourse and text structure.**

- Ellipsis and zero-pronoun reference: Japanese routinely drops subjects, objects, and even verbs when recoverable from context. The learner practices recovering omitted elements in extended text.
- Thematic progression: how は-marked topics chain across sentences and paragraphs to build coherent discourse.
- 起承転結 structure: understanding the traditional Japanese rhetorical pattern (introduction → development → twist/complication → conclusion), which differs from Western thesis-first argumentation.

**Onomatopoeia (オノマトペ) — systematic treatment.** Japanese has an extraordinarily rich system of mimetic words that are pervasive in speech, literature, and even news. They are categorized:

- 擬音語 (phonomimes — sounds): ざあざあ (heavy rain), ガタガタ (rattling), ドキドキ (heartbeat).
- 擬態語 (phenomimes — states, textures, manners): じめじめ (humid/damp), ぴかぴか (sparkling), のろのろ (sluggishly).
- 擬情語 (psychomimes — feelings): わくわく (excited anticipation), いらいら (irritated), うんざり (fed up).

At least 100 are taught, organized by semantic domain (weather, emotions, physical sensations, movement, textures). The hiragana/katakana distinction is addressed: katakana onomatopoeia often feels sharper, more sudden, or more mechanical.

### Thematic / Situational Content

1. **Japanese business culture.** Meetings, ビジネスメール, negotiation styles, 根回し. Understanding what happens in a Japanese workplace and why.
2. **Current affairs and politics.** 国会 (Diet), 選挙、政党、内閣、憲法改正 debate. Enough vocabulary and context to follow NHK political news.
3. **Social issues.** 少子高齢化 (declining birthrate, aging society)、格差社会、ジェンダー平等、外国人労働者、ブラック企業、過労死. Understanding opinion pieces and public debate on these topics.
4. **Media literacy.** Understanding how different newspapers frame the same story (朝日 vs 読売 vs 毎日). Recognizing editorial stance through word choice and grammar.
5. **Literature — first encounters.** Excerpts from modern authors: 夏目漱石、芥川龍之介、太宰治 — simplified where necessary, but moving toward unabridged. Literary vocabulary and style.
6. **Science and technology articles.** Understanding popular science writing (Newton magazine level, 日経サイエンス abstracts).
7. **Legal and bureaucratic Japanese.** Reading contracts, lease agreements (賃貸契約書), government forms (届出), tax notices. The learner won't enjoy this, but they'll encounter it.
8. **Lectures and formal presentations.** Following a university lecture or TED-style talk in Japanese.

### Comprehension Competencies

- Read unsimplified newspaper and magazine articles with only occasional dictionary use.
- Follow keigo-laden business conversations and formal speeches.
- Comprehend university-level lectures on non-specialized topics.
- Read business emails and formal correspondence and understand the pragmatic subtext (what is being requested, refused, or hinted at).
- Listen to and comprehend panel discussions and unscripted interviews at natural speed, including when speakers use casual contractions and overlap.
- Handle vertical text (縦書き) and mixed horizontal/vertical layouts without difficulty.

## STAGE 5 — Pre-Advanced (N2→N1 Bridge)

**Duration:** ~200 hours | **Kanji:** ~1,500 cumulative | **Vocabulary:** ~8,000

This stage exists because the N2→N1 gap is enormous and is where most learners plateau. The focus shifts to **reading stamina** (long texts), **listening at uncontrolled native speed**, **low-frequency but high-impact grammar**, and **cultural-linguistic literacy** (proverbs, four-character compounds, literary allusion).

### Structural / Grammatical Goals

**N1 grammar — systematic coverage.** These forms appear in formal writing, literature, speeches, and news editorials. They are rare in casual conversation but common enough in written Japanese that a learner encounters several per page in a newspaper editorial:

*Emphatic and formal assertion:*
- 〜てやまない (earnestly, from the heart: 成功を願ってやまない = sincerely wish for success).
- 〜に堪えない (unbearable / deeply moved: 感謝に堪えない = deeply grateful).
- 〜極まる / 〜極まりない (extremely: 失礼極まりない = the height of rudeness).
- 〜に足る / 〜に値する (worthy of: 信頼に足る人物 = a person worthy of trust; 注目に値する = noteworthy).
- 〜まじき (unbecoming of — archaic but alive: 教師にあるまじき行為 = conduct unbecoming of a teacher).

*Conditional/circumstantial — formal:*
- 〜ともなると / 〜ともなれば (when it comes to the level of / once you reach the point of).
- 〜とあって (given that — explains why something is the case: 連休とあって道が混んでいる).
- 〜とあれば (if it's a matter of / if it's for the sake of: 君のためとあれば).
- 〜たりとも〜ない (not even: 一瞬たりとも油断できない = can't let one's guard down for even an instant).

*Determination and inevitability:*
- 〜べくして (destined to, bound to: 起こるべくして起こった = happened as it was bound to).
- 〜べくもない (impossible to: 想像するべくもない = beyond imagination).
- 〜をものともせず(に) (undaunted by: 困難をものともせずに前進した).
- 〜をおいて〜ない (no one/nothing other than: 彼をおいて他にいない).
- 〜ならでは (unique to, only possible with: 京都ならではの風景).

*Concession — formal:*
- 〜であれ / 〜であろうと (even if — formal: 誰であれ例外ではない = no one is an exception).
- 〜とて (even — literary: 百万回練習したとて).
- 〜ないまでも (even if not to the extent of: 完璧ではないまでも十分だ = if not perfect, at least sufficient).

**四字熟語 (four-character compounds).** 50-100 essential compounds, taught in thematic clusters:

- Effort/perseverance: 一生懸命、七転八起、臨機応変、試行錯誤.
- Social relations: 以心伝心、一期一会、付和雷同、我田引水.
- Consequence/causality: 自業自得、因果応報、本末転倒.
- Situations: 前代未聞、五里霧中、四面楚歌、危機一髪.
- Character: 品行方正、傍若無人、厚顔無恥.

These are high-frequency in editorials, speeches, and literature. The learner should recognize them instantly.

**Proverbs (ことわざ).** ~30-50 common proverbs, taught not as isolated trivia but as interpretive tools:

- 猿も木から落ちる (even monkeys fall from trees — even experts make mistakes).
- 石の上にも三年 (three years on a stone — persistence pays off).
- 出る杭は打たれる (the nail that sticks out gets hammered down — conformity pressure).
- 井の中の蛙大海を知らず (the frog in the well knows nothing of the great ocean — narrow-mindedness).

Proverbs function as compressed cultural logic. Understanding them aids comprehension of arguments that invoke them.

**Classical Japanese (文語) — reading competence.** Not a course in classical Japanese, but enough to parse the classical forms that leak into modern Japanese constantly:

- The classical copula なり and its modern descendants.
- Basic classical verb classes: 四段 (precursor to modern 五段), 上二段、下二段 and how they collapsed into modern 一段.
- Key classical auxiliary verbs that survive in set phrases: ず (negative: 知らず知らず、やむを得ず), たり (perfective: 堂々たる), べし (obligation/conjecture: 知るべし, するべき), まじ (negative of べし: あるまじき).
- Enough to read the Preamble to the Japanese Constitution, understand proverbs in their full form, and parse literary allusions.

**Extensive onomatopoeia expansion.** An additional 50+ items beyond Stage 4, focusing on less common but expressive forms encountered in literature and natural speech: しんみり (quiet, subdued mood), ほっこり (heartwarming), ぞっとする (shudder), がっくり (dejected), むっとする (offended/stuffy), すんなり (smoothly, without resistance).

### Thematic / Situational Content

1. **Extended reading practice.** Full newspaper editorials (社説), magazine features (文藝春秋 level), long-form journalism. The goal is *stamina*: reading 10+ pages without fatigue.
2. **Academic reading.** Sociology, linguistics, economics abstracts and introductory sections. The learner encounters nominalization-heavy academic prose and develops strategies for unpacking it.
3. **Literary reading.** Unabridged short stories: 太宰治「走れメロス」, 芥川龍之介「蜘蛛の糸」「鼻」, 村上春樹 short fiction. The learner encounters literary style, which differs markedly from the journalistic and academic styles they've been studying.
4. **Unscripted speech.** Podcasts, YouTube videos, panel discussions, variety shows, stand-up comedy (漫才), rakugo. The learner practices comprehension of speech that is fast, overlapping, dialectal, and riddled with contractions and filler.
5. **Documentary and analytical media.** NHKスペシャル, ドキュメンタリー. Understanding rhetorical strategies in long-form documentary narration.
6. **Historical and cultural topics.** 明治維新、大正デモクラシー、戦後日本、高度経済成長、バブル崩壊. Enough historical knowledge to understand cultural references and allusions in contemporary writing.
7. **Debate and argumentation.** Following structured debates: 立論、反駁、結論. Understanding the logical connectors and rhetorical moves of Japanese formal argumentation.

**Manga, light novels, and web Japanese.** These are treated as legitimate registers requiring specific literacy:

- Manga conventions: furigana-heavy text, onomatopoeia as visual elements, speech-bubble conventions (thought vs. speech vs. narration), extreme casual register.
- Light novel style: first-person narration, 〜だった。〜のだ。pacing, internal monologue conventions.
- Web Japanese: abbreviations (w / 草 for laughter, リプ for reply, 乙 for お疲れ, ksk / kwsk for 詳しく), emoji/emoticon conventions, 2ch/5ch/Twitter register.

### Comprehension Competencies

- Read 10+ pages of dense Japanese text (editorial, academic, literary) without major comprehension gaps and without fatigue.
- Follow rapid, unscripted native speech including dialect coloring, generational slang, and overlapping turns.
- Comprehend literary prose with its distinct vocabulary, sentence rhythm, and narrative conventions.
- Recognize and interpret 四字熟語, proverbs, and classical allusions when encountered in modern text.
- Read and understand web content, manga dialogue, and informal digital communication without confusion.

## STAGE 6 — Advanced / N1 Mastery

**Duration:** ~250 hours | **Kanji:** 2,000+ (常用漢字 complete) | **Vocabulary:** 10,000–12,000+

### Structural / Grammatical Goals

**No new grammar is explicitly taught.** Instead, the focus is:

**Nuance discrimination.** The learner systematically compares near-synonymous forms and develops the ability to perceive why a speaker or writer chose one over another:

- について vs に関して vs をめぐって: all mean "regarding," but differ in formality, scope, and connotation (をめぐって implies debate or controversy).
- ものの vs けれども vs にもかかわらず: concession at three different levels of formality and emphasis.
- ようだ vs みたいだ vs らしい vs そうだ: four flavors of "seems like," each encoding a different evidence type.
- ために vs ように: purpose clauses with different volitionality constraints.
- The learner develops a sense for these distinctions that goes beyond rules into intuition.

**Register perception.** The learner can identify and follow rapid register shifts — a politician switching from 国会答弁 style to conversational, a novelist embedding keigo dialogue within casual narration, a news anchor reading formal copy and then ad-libbing in moderate style. The learner perceives these shifts as meaningful social and stylistic signals.

**Error recognition.** The learner can identify common errors made by native speakers (二重敬語, ら抜き言葉 in formal contexts, 的を得る vs 的を射る debates, 全然 + affirmative as a modern shift) and understand the sociolinguistic controversies around them.

**Kanji — deep and wide.**

- Completion of 常用漢字 (2,136).
- Introduction to 表外漢字 commonly encountered in names (e.g., 彦、薗、澤), literature (e.g., 儚い、翳す), and specialized fields.
- 熟字訓 — words where the kanji reading is assigned to the whole word, not individual characters: 大人＝おとな、今日＝きょう、明日＝あした、果物＝くだもの、土産＝みやげ. These are unpredictable and taught as a category.
- 当て字 — kanji used phonetically regardless of meaning: 珈琲（コーヒー）、倶楽部（クラブ）、浪漫（ロマン）.
- Multi-reading awareness: the learner develops strategies for determining which reading of a kanji applies in context (e.g., 生 has dozens of readings; context + compound + genre narrow it down).
- 漢字語構成 (compound-building): systematic vocabulary expansion by studying all major compounds built from high-frequency kanji. Example: 制 → 制度、規制、制限、制御、抑制、体制、制裁、強制、制作、制定. This is the most efficient vocabulary expansion strategy at this level.

**Numbers and mathematical/bureaucratic Japanese.** Addressed systematically if not already solid:

- Large numbers: the 万/億/兆 system (10,000-based grouping, not 1,000-based).
- Fractions (三分の一), percentages (パーセント), decimals.
- 和暦 system (令和、平成、昭和) and instant conversion.
- Phone number and address reading conventions.

### Thematic / Situational Content

1. **Specialized domains.** The learner chooses 1-2 domains for deep reading: law (法律、判例), medicine (medical articles, 診察のやりとり), business (financial reports, IR documents), engineering/IT (technical documentation), academia (journal articles), journalism (investigative long-form). Comprehension vocabulary is built domain-specifically.
2. **Novel-length literary works.** The learner reads complete novels — not excerpts. The goal is sustained literary comprehension: following plot, character development, stylistic shifts, and cultural subtext over 200+ pages.
3. **Humor, irony, sarcasm, and indirectness.** Understanding what is meant when the literal meaning diverges: understatement (ちょっと困りますね meaning "absolutely not"), ironic praise, comedic timing in 漫才, satirical writing.
4. **Sociolinguistic variation.** Gendered language (and its loosening), generational speech differences (若者言葉 vs 年配の言葉), regional variation (major dialect features of 関西弁、東北弁、九州弁 — for recognition, not production), professional jargon, ネット語 evolution.
5. **Japanese rhetoric and argumentation.** How Japanese expository writing differs from Western conventions: 起承転結 vs thesis-first, indirectness as a rhetorical strategy, the role of "reading the air" (空気を読む) in both written and spoken discourse.
6. **Translation and contrastive analysis.** Understanding where Japanese and English diverge structurally (head-final syntax, topic-prominence, zero anaphora, aspect vs. tense emphasis, the perspective-encoding in 授受 and passive) — not for production of translations, but for deepening comprehension by understanding *why* certain structures feel counterintuitive.

### Comprehension Competencies

- Read literary, legal, academic, and journalistic texts with minimal dictionary use (fewer than 2-3 lookups per page).
- Comprehend implicit meaning, cultural allusion, pragmatic nuance, and rhetorical strategy in both written and spoken Japanese.
- Follow any native-speed speech, including rapid colloquial conversation, formal presentations, regional-dialect-colored speech, and media with specialized vocabulary.
- Perceive register shifts, honorific levels, and social positioning in real-time.
- Understand humor, irony, understatement, and indirectness as deployed by native speakers.
- Pass the JLPT N1 comfortably (target: 140+/180).

## Cross-Cutting Design Features (All Stages)

### Kanji Pedagogy

Kanji are never taught in isolation. Each character is introduced with at least two compound words, one 音読み and one 訓読み reading where applicable, and a note on the semantic radical. From Stage 3 onward, kanji are studied in **semantic clusters** (e.g., legal kanji: 法、律、刑、罰、裁、訴; emotional kanji: 怒、悲、恐、驚、憎、悔) in addition to frequency-based ordering. This mirrors how vocabulary is actually organized in the mental lexicon and dramatically improves retention and guessing ability for unknown compounds.

### Listening-Reading Integration

Every chapter at every level includes a listening passage that recycles target grammar and vocabulary but is not a read-aloud of the reading passage. Listening and reading are treated as **parallel but independent skills** that reinforce each other. Listening materials are always slightly *easier* than reading materials at the same stage, because processing speech in real time is inherently more demanding than reading at one's own pace.

### Prosody as a Through-Line

Pitch accent, intonation, and rhythm are not confined to a pronunciation chapter. They are integrated into vocabulary study (every new word has its accent pattern marked), listening comprehension (exercises that hinge on pitch-accent discrimination), and cultural study (prosodic features of keigo, of emotional speech, of regional dialects).

### Extensive Reading and Listening Program

From Stage 2 onward, the curriculum includes an **extensive input track** alongside the intensive instruction. This means:

- Graded readers (Tadoku series, White Rabbit Press) at Stages 2-3.
- NHK Web Easy → NHK News → newspaper articles across Stages 3-5.
- Podcasts, audiobooks, and video at every stage, calibrated to be slightly below the learner's intensive-study level (for fluency building, not struggle).
- Manga and light novels from Stage 4 onward as legitimate extensive reading material.

The extensive track is high-volume, low-pressure. The learner is encouraged to read/listen for flow, not to stop and look up every word.

### Spiral Curriculum

Key structures are revisited at increasing depth across multiple stages:

- は vs が: introduced Stage 1, full treatment Stage 3, nuance refinement Stage 6.
- Passive: forms Stage 2, adversative passive and literary passive Stage 3, passive in academic writing Stage 4.
- Conditionals: four forms Stage 2, nuance discrimination Stage 4, literary conditionals Stage 5.
- Keigo: recognition Stage 2, full system Stage 4, real-world parsing Stage 5-6.
- Aspect (ている etc.): basics Stage 1, system Stage 3, literary/archaic aspect Stage 5.

### Cultural Competence as Comprehension Infrastructure

Culture is not in sidebar boxes. It is woven into grammar (why 授受表現 encodes social perspective), into thematic content (understanding 本音と建前 to parse indirect refusals), and into pragmatic instruction (why a sentence ending in 〜が... with no conclusion is a polite refusal, not an unfinished thought). **Cultural knowledge is treated as a comprehension skill**, because without it, the learner will parse every word correctly and still misunderstand the message.

### Speech Acts for Comprehension

The learner is systematically taught to recognize the Japanese realization of common speech acts, because the form-function mapping differs from English:

- **Refusing:** すみません、ちょっと... (the trailing off *is* the refusal). 考えさせてください (= no). 前向きに検討します (= probably no, in bureaucratic speech).
- **Apologizing:** すみません (mild) → 申し訳ございません (formal) → 謝罪する patterns in public statements. When an apology is actually an expression of gratitude (すみません = thank you for the trouble).
- **Requesting:** the escalating indirectness scale from 〜てくれる？ to 〜ていただければ幸いです, and recognizing where a speaker sits on that scale.
- **Complimenting and deflecting:** understanding that いいえ、まだまだです after a compliment is not false modesty but expected social behavior (and recognizing when it *is* false modesty).

### JLPT Test Strategy (Stages 4-6)

Because the endpoint is N1, the curriculum includes explicit training in JLPT-format comprehension tasks:

- Speed reading for 内容理解 (content comprehension) and 主張理解 (understanding the author's claim).
- Information retrieval (情報検索): scanning ads, schedules, notices for specific data points.
- The listening section's unique format: in some questions, the answer must be chosen *before* the options are read aloud, requiring real-time comprehension and decision-making.
- Time management: the N1 reading section is notoriously long relative to the time allowed.
