# Chapter 17 — Movement, Transport, and Directions

You can describe things, express existence, talk about what you do and when you do it, make requests, grant permission, and narrate sequences of events. What you cannot yet do is get from one place to another — linguistically. This chapter gives you the verbs of movement, the particles that mark paths and means of transport, and the vocabulary for asking and understanding directions. These are among the most practically useful patterns in the language. If you travel to Japan, this chapter will get you from the station to the hotel.

---

## 17.1 Movement Verbs

Japanese has a small set of core movement verbs. You need all of them.

| 辞書形 | 意味 | 動詞の種類 |
|--------|------|-----------|
| いく | to go | 五段 |
| くる | to come | 不規則 |
| かえる | to return (home) | 五段 |
| あるく | to walk | 五段 |
| はしる | to run | 五段 |
| わたる | to cross | 五段 |

You already know くる as one of the two irregular verbs (Chapter 8). Its て-form is きて, its ない-form is こない, its た-form is きた. Review these if they are not automatic.

いく is a regular 五段 verb with one exception: its て-form is いって, not いいて. You learned this in Chapter 10 as the single exception to the く → いて pattern. Make sure you remember it. Every other form of いく is regular: いかない, いきます, いった.

### かえる is 五段

This is worth repeating. かえる ends in える, which makes it look like a 一段 verb. It is not. It is 五段. Chapter 8 listed it explicitly among the deceptive いる/える verbs that are actually 五段. The consequences are real:

| Form | 五段 (correct) | 一段 (wrong) |
|------|---------------|-------------|
| ます-form | かえります | かえます ✗ |
| ない-form | かえらない | かえない ✗ |
| て-form | かえって | かえて ✗ |
| た-form | かえった | かえた ✗ |

Every form is different. If you misclassify かえる, everything you produce is wrong. The same warning applies to はしる — it ends in いる but is 五段: はしります, はしらない, はしって, はしった.

### Basic sentences with movement verbs

> <ruby>学校<rt>がっこう</rt></ruby>に <ruby>行<rt>い</rt></ruby>きます。
> I go to school.

> <ruby>友<rt>とも</rt></ruby>だちが <ruby>来<rt>き</rt></ruby>ます。
> A friend is coming.

> うちに <ruby>帰<rt>かえ</rt></ruby>ります。
> I return home.

> <ruby>公園<rt>こうえん</rt></ruby>を <ruby>歩<rt>ある</rt></ruby>きます。
> I walk through the park.

> <ruby>道<rt>みち</rt></ruby>を <ruby>走<rt>はし</rt></ruby>ります。
> I run along the road.

> <ruby>橋<rt>はし</rt></ruby>を <ruby>渡<rt>わた</rt></ruby>ります。
> I cross the bridge.

Notice that some of these use に and others use を. The difference is the topic of Section 17.4. For now, observe that に marks the destination you are heading toward, while を marks the space you move through.

## 17.2 Transport — で for Means

You already know the particle で as a marker of location where an action takes place (<ruby>図書館<rt>としょかん</rt></ruby>で <ruby>勉強<rt>べんきょう</rt></ruby>します — "I study at the library"). で has a second function: it marks the **means** or **instrument** by which something is done. With movement verbs, で marks the mode of transport.

> <ruby>電車<rt>でんしゃ</rt></ruby>で <ruby>行<rt>い</rt></ruby>きます。
> I go by train.

> バスで 行きます。
> I go by bus.

> <ruby>車<rt>くるま</rt></ruby>で 行きます。
> I go by car.

> <ruby>自転車<rt>じてんしゃ</rt></ruby>で 行きます。
> I go by bicycle.

> タクシーで 行きます。
> I go by taxi.

> <ruby>地下鉄<rt>ちかてつ</rt></ruby>で 行きます。
> I go by subway.

> <ruby>飛行機<rt>ひこうき</rt></ruby>で 行きます。
> I go by airplane.

> <ruby>船<rt>ふね</rt></ruby>で 行きます。
> I go by ship.

The pattern is consistent: **[vehicle]で [movement verb]**. The vehicle noun takes で, and the verb comes at the end.

### The exception: on foot

"On foot" does not use で. You cannot say <ruby>足<rt>あし</rt></ruby>で 行きます to mean "go on foot." Instead, you use the て-form of <ruby>歩<rt>ある</rt></ruby>く:

> <ruby>歩<rt>ある</rt></ruby>いて 行きます。
> I go on foot. (Literally: "walking, I go.")

This is a て-form chain, exactly like the ones you learned in Chapter 11. 歩いて connects to 行きます as a manner of movement. The structure is [て-form of manner verb] + [main movement verb]. You will see this pattern again with <ruby>走<rt>はし</rt></ruby>って 行きます ("go running") and other combinations.

Why not 足で? The particle で marks an instrument or vehicle — something you use. Your feet are not a vehicle in the same grammatical sense as a train or a car. The language treats walking as an action, not a tool. Do not overthink the logic. Just remember: 歩いて 行きます, not 足で 行きます.

### Combining transport with destination

A full sentence includes the destination (に), the transport (で), and the verb:

> <ruby>駅<rt>えき</rt></ruby>に バスで 行きます。
> I go to the station by bus.

> <ruby>空港<rt>くうこう</rt></ruby>に タクシーで 行きます。
> I go to the airport by taxi.

> <ruby>学校<rt>がっこう</rt></ruby>に <ruby>自転車<rt>じてんしゃ</rt></ruby>で 行きます。
> I go to school by bicycle.

> <ruby>東京<rt>とうきょう</rt></ruby>に <ruby>新幹線<rt>しんかんせん</rt></ruby>で 行きます。
> I go to Tokyo by bullet train.

Word order between the destination and the transport is flexible. Both 駅に バスで 行きます and バスで 駅に 行きます are grammatical. The verb must remain at the end.

## 17.3 Asking for and Understanding Directions

### Key vocabulary

Before learning the grammar of directions, you need the words:

| 語 | 意味 |
|-----|------|
| まっすぐ | straight ahead |
| みぎ | right |
| ひだり | left |
| まがる | to turn (五段) |
| しんごう | traffic light |
| こうさてん | intersection |
| かど | corner |
| つぎ | next |
| みち | road, street |
| はし | bridge |

You already know <ruby>右<rt>みぎ</rt></ruby> and <ruby>左<rt>ひだり</rt></ruby> from Chapter 6 (location words). Here they combine with <ruby>曲<rt>ま</rt></ruby>がる to express turning.

### Turning right and left

> <ruby>右<rt>みぎ</rt></ruby>に <ruby>曲<rt>ま</rt></ruby>がります。
> I turn right.

> <ruby>左<rt>ひだり</rt></ruby>に 曲がります。
> I turn left.

The direction takes に because it is the target of the turning movement. 曲がる is 五段: 曲がります, 曲がらない, 曲がって, 曲がった.

### Giving directions with て-form chains

Directions in Japanese are typically given as a sequence of instructions connected by the て-form, ending with ください. You learned てください (polite request) in Chapter 11. Directions are one of its most common real-world applications.

> まっすぐ <ruby>行<rt>い</rt></ruby>ってください。
> Please go straight.

> <ruby>次<rt>つぎ</rt></ruby>の <ruby>信号<rt>しんごう</rt></ruby>を <ruby>右<rt>みぎ</rt></ruby>に <ruby>曲<rt>ま</rt></ruby>がってください。
> Please turn right at the next traffic light.

> ふたつめの <ruby>角<rt>かど</rt></ruby>を <ruby>左<rt>ひだり</rt></ruby>に 曲がってください。
> Please turn left at the second corner.

Longer direction sequences chain multiple て-forms before the final ください:

> まっすぐ 行って、次の 信号を 右に 曲がってください。
> Please go straight and turn right at the next traffic light.

> この <ruby>道<rt>みち</rt></ruby>を まっすぐ 行って、<ruby>交差点<rt>こうさてん</rt></ruby>を <ruby>渡<rt>わた</rt></ruby>って、左に 曲がってください。
> Please go straight along this road, cross the intersection, and turn left.

> <ruby>駅<rt>えき</rt></ruby>を <ruby>出<rt>で</rt></ruby>て、右に 曲がって、<ruby>五<rt>ご</rt></ruby><ruby>分<rt>ふん</rt></ruby>ぐらい <ruby>歩<rt>ある</rt></ruby>いてください。
> Please exit the station, turn right, and walk for about five minutes.

Each instruction is in its て-form, and only the final verb takes ください. This is exactly the same clause-chaining pattern from Chapter 11, applied to a specific context. If you understood て-form chains there, you understand them here.

### Asking for directions

The most basic way to ask for directions:

> すみません、駅は どこですか。
> Excuse me, where is the station?

> すみません、<ruby>郵便局<rt>ゆうびんきょく</rt></ruby>は どう 行きますか。
> Excuse me, how do I get to the post office?

> ここから 駅まで どう 行きますか。
> How do I get from here to the station?

どう means "how" and is an interrogative you have seen in earlier chapters. どう いきますか asks literally "how do I go?" — the natural way to ask for directions to a specific place.

## 17.4 を for Traversal

In Chapter 9 you learned を as the direct object marker: <ruby>本<rt>ほん</rt></ruby>を <ruby>読<rt>よ</rt></ruby>みます ("I read a book"), <ruby>水<rt>みず</rt></ruby>を <ruby>飲<rt>の</rt></ruby>みます ("I drink water"). With movement verbs, を takes on a different function: it marks the **space through which movement occurs**.

> <ruby>道<rt>みち</rt></ruby>を <ruby>歩<rt>ある</rt></ruby>きます。
> I walk along the road.

> <ruby>橋<rt>はし</rt></ruby>を <ruby>渡<rt>わた</rt></ruby>ります。
> I cross the bridge.

> <ruby>公園<rt>こうえん</rt></ruby>を <ruby>走<rt>はし</rt></ruby>ります。
> I run through the park.

> <ruby>交差点<rt>こうさてん</rt></ruby>を 渡ります。
> I cross the intersection.

> この 道を まっすぐ <ruby>行<rt>い</rt></ruby>きます。
> I go straight along this road.

In none of these sentences is the road, bridge, or park a direct object being acted upon. You are not doing something to the road — you are moving through it. The を here marks the path or route of movement.

### How to tell the difference

Context makes it clear. を with a non-movement verb marks a direct object: 本を <ruby>読<rt>よ</rt></ruby>む (read a book). を with a movement verb and a path-like noun marks traversal: 道を <ruby>歩<rt>ある</rt></ruby>く (walk along the road).

There is also a structural clue. Direct-object を typically answers the question なにを ("what?"): なにを 読みますか — 本を 読みます. Traversal を typically answers どこを ("where through?"): どこを 歩きますか — 公園を 歩きます.

### を vs. に with movement verbs

This distinction is important. Compare:

> 公園**に** <ruby>行<rt>い</rt></ruby>きます。
> I go **to** the park. (The park is the destination.)

> 公園**を** <ruby>走<rt>はし</rt></ruby>ります。
> I run **through** the park. (The park is the path.)

に marks where you end up. を marks where you pass through. If you go to the park and stop there, use に. If you run through the park as a route, use を.

Another pair:

> <ruby>橋<rt>はし</rt></ruby>**に** 行きます。
> I go **to** the bridge. (The bridge is the destination.)

> 橋**を** <ruby>渡<rt>わた</rt></ruby>ります。
> I cross the bridge. (The bridge is the path you traverse.)

This is not interchangeable. 橋に 渡ります is ungrammatical. You cross **through** a bridge, not **to** it. Similarly, <ruby>道<rt>みち</rt></ruby>を 行きます means "go along the road," while 道に 行きます would oddly mean "go to the road" as a destination.

### Directions use traversal を

This is why direction instructions use を with <ruby>信号<rt>しんごう</rt></ruby>, <ruby>交差点<rt>こうさてん</rt></ruby>, and <ruby>角<rt>かど</rt></ruby>:

> 信号を <ruby>右<rt>みぎ</rt></ruby>に <ruby>曲<rt>ま</rt></ruby>がってください。
> Turn right at the traffic light.

The 信号 is not the destination. It is the point you pass through as you turn. Hence を, not に.

## 17.5 Distance and Duration

When someone gives you directions, you often want to know how far away the destination is. Japanese expresses distance in terms of time, not meters or kilometers (in casual conversation, at least).

### <ruby>歩<rt>ある</rt></ruby>いて + duration

> <ruby>歩<rt>ある</rt></ruby>いて <ruby>十<rt>じゅっ</rt></ruby><ruby>分<rt>ぷん</rt></ruby>ぐらいです。
> It is about ten minutes on foot.

> 歩いて <ruby>二十<rt>にじゅっ</rt></ruby><ruby>分<rt>ぷん</rt></ruby>ぐらいです。
> It is about twenty minutes on foot.

ぐらい means "about" or "approximately." It follows the number and counter directly. You will hear it constantly in direction-giving contexts because exact durations are rarely stated.

### ここから ～まで + transport + duration

The full pattern for stating distance:

> ここから <ruby>駅<rt>えき</rt></ruby>まで <ruby>歩<rt>ある</rt></ruby>いて <ruby>十五<rt>じゅうご</rt></ruby><ruby>分<rt>ふん</rt></ruby>ぐらいです。
> From here to the station, it is about fifteen minutes on foot.

> 駅から <ruby>空港<rt>くうこう</rt></ruby>まで <ruby>電車<rt>でんしゃ</rt></ruby>で <ruby>三十<rt>さんじゅっ</rt></ruby><ruby>分<rt>ぷん</rt></ruby>ぐらいです。
> From the station to the airport, it is about thirty minutes by train.

> ここから <ruby>病院<rt>びょういん</rt></ruby>まで バスで 十分ぐらいです。
> From here to the hospital, it is about ten minutes by bus.

から means "from" and まで means "to" or "until." Together they mark the start and end points of a journey. The transport (で or あるいて) and the duration follow. です at the end makes it a polite statement.

### ～<ruby>時間<rt>じかん</rt></ruby> for hours

Minutes use ～<ruby>分<rt>ふん</rt></ruby>/～<ruby>分<rt>ぷん</rt></ruby>, which you learned in Chapter 7. For longer durations, you need ～時間 (hours of duration):

| Duration | Japanese |
|----------|---------|
| one hour | いちじかん |
| two hours | にじかん |
| three hours | さんじかん |
| four hours | よじかん |
| five hours | ごじかん |
| six hours | ろくじかん |
| seven hours | ななじかん / しちじかん |
| eight hours | はちじかん |
| nine hours | くじかん |
| ten hours | じゅうじかん |

Note: よじかん (four hours), not よんじかん. This parallels よじ (four o'clock) from the time system.

For hours and minutes combined:

> <ruby>一<rt>いち</rt></ruby><ruby>時間<rt>じかん</rt></ruby> <ruby>二十<rt>にじゅっ</rt></ruby><ruby>分<rt>ぷん</rt></ruby>
> one hour and twenty minutes

> <ruby>二<rt>に</rt></ruby><ruby>時間<rt>じかん</rt></ruby><ruby>半<rt>はん</rt></ruby>
> two and a half hours (<ruby>半<rt>はん</rt></ruby> = half)

> ここから <ruby>大阪<rt>おおさか</rt></ruby>まで <ruby>新幹線<rt>しんかんせん</rt></ruby>で 二時間半ぐらいです。
> From here to Osaka, it is about two and a half hours by bullet train.

## 17.8 Listening Notes

Directions in natural speech come fast. A Japanese speaker giving you directions on the street will typically produce a rapid chain of て-forms with minimal pausing, often while pointing. You will not catch every word. That is normal. Here is how to cope.

**Focus on the key words.** In any set of directions, the critical information is carried by a small number of words: まっすぐ (straight), <ruby>右<rt>みぎ</rt></ruby> (right), <ruby>左<rt>ひだり</rt></ruby> (left), <ruby>信号<rt>しんごう</rt></ruby> (traffic light), <ruby>交差点<rt>こうさてん</rt></ruby> (intersection), and a distance like <ruby>五<rt>ご</rt></ruby><ruby>分<rt>ふん</rt></ruby> (five minutes). If you catch these, you can navigate even if you miss the connecting grammar.

**Watch for pointing.** Japanese speakers will almost always point or gesture while giving directions. Even if you miss the words, follow the hands. A gesture toward the right combined with something-something-曲がって tells you to turn right.

**Te-form chains are your friend.** Each て marks a boundary between one instruction and the next. Listen for the て sound. Every time you hear it, a new instruction begins. まっすぐ 行って — that is one instruction. 右に 曲がって — that is the next. The final instruction ends with ください or a polite verb form.

**Ask for confirmation.** If you are not sure you understood, repeat back what you heard:

> 右に 曲がりますか。
> I turn right?

> 信号の <ruby>次<rt>つぎ</rt></ruby>ですか。
> After the traffic light?

Japanese speakers generally respond well to confirmation questions. They will correct you if you misunderstood or confirm with はい if you got it right.

**Common phrases you will hear:**

> この <ruby>道<rt>みち</rt></ruby>を まっすぐ <ruby>行<rt>い</rt></ruby>って...
> Go straight along this road...

> 次の 信号を...
> At the next traffic light...

> <ruby>右<rt>みぎ</rt></ruby>がわに あります。
> It is on the right side.

> <ruby>左<rt>ひだり</rt></ruby>がわに あります。
> It is on the left side.

がわ means "side." 右がわ = right side, 左がわ = left side. These are not in the formal vocabulary list but appear constantly in direction-giving.

## 17.9 Reading Passage — <ruby>郵便局<rt>ゆうびんきょく</rt></ruby>までの <ruby>道<rt>みち</rt></ruby>

Read the following passage. It is a set of directions from a station to a post office. All grammar and vocabulary come from this chapter and earlier chapters.

---

**<ruby>山田<rt>やまだ</rt></ruby>さんは <ruby>郵便局<rt>ゆうびんきょく</rt></ruby>に <ruby>行<rt>い</rt></ruby>きたいです。<ruby>駅<rt>えき</rt></ruby>の <ruby>前<rt>まえ</rt></ruby>で <ruby>人<rt>ひと</rt></ruby>に <ruby>聞<rt>き</rt></ruby>きました。**

山田：すみません。郵便局は どう 行きますか。

<ruby>人<rt>ひと</rt></ruby>：郵便局ですか。ここから <ruby>歩<rt>ある</rt></ruby>いて <ruby>十<rt>じゅっ</rt></ruby><ruby>分<rt>ぷん</rt></ruby>ぐらいですよ。

山田：どう 行けば いいですか。

人：まず、この <ruby>道<rt>みち</rt></ruby>を まっすぐ 行ってください。<ruby>五<rt>ご</rt></ruby><ruby>分<rt>ふん</rt></ruby>ぐらい <ruby>歩<rt>ある</rt></ruby>きます。<ruby>大<rt>おお</rt></ruby>きい <ruby>交差点<rt>こうさてん</rt></ruby>が あります。その 交差点を <ruby>渡<rt>わた</rt></ruby>って、<ruby>次<rt>つぎ</rt></ruby>の <ruby>角<rt>かど</rt></ruby>を <ruby>右<rt>みぎ</rt></ruby>に <ruby>曲<rt>ま</rt></ruby>がってください。<ruby>少<rt>すこ</rt></ruby>し 歩くと、<ruby>左<rt>ひだり</rt></ruby>がわに 郵便局が あります。<ruby>赤<rt>あか</rt></ruby>い ポストが あるから、すぐ <ruby>分<rt>わ</rt></ruby>かりますよ。

山田：まっすぐ 行って、交差点を 渡って、次の 角を 右ですね。

人：はい、そうです。

山田：ありがとうございます。

### Comprehension check

Answer the following questions about the passage. All answers can be found directly in the text.

1. 山田さんは なにに 行きたいですか。
2. 郵便局まで 歩いて どのぐらいですか。
3. 大きい 交差点で なにを しますか。
4. 交差点の 次の 角で どちらに 曲がりますか。
5. 郵便局は 道の どちらがわに ありますか。

### Notes on the passage

The passage uses several forms from earlier chapters:

- 行きたい (want to go) — たい-form, covered in earlier chapters
- 聞きました (asked) — past polite of <ruby>聞<rt>き</rt></ruby>く (to ask/listen), 五段
- 行けば いいですか (how should I go?) — this conditional form (ば) has not been formally taught yet; treat it as a set phrase for now meaning "what is the best way to go?"
- 歩くと (when you walk) — the と conditional connecting a condition to a result
- 分かります (to understand / to be able to tell) — here meaning "you will recognize it"
- すぐ — immediately, right away
- ポスト — post (mailbox), from English "post"

### Translation

Yesterday, I went from Tokyo Station to Asakusa. I wanted to go to Sensoji temple in Asakusa.

First, I bought a subway ticket inside the station. It was 200 yen. I went down to the Marunouchi Line platform and boarded the train.

I got off the train at Ginza Station. From there, I transferred to the Ginza Line. I rode the Ginza Line to Asakusa Station. It was about thirty minutes in total.

When I came out of Asakusa Station, there was a large avenue. I went straight and turned right at the first corner. After walking a little, I could see a large red gate. It was Kaminarimon. There were many people.

Sensoji is a very old temple. On the road to the inside, there are many shops. They were selling souvenirs and sweets. It was very fun.

### Notes

- 乗り換えました — past of <ruby>乗<rt>の</rt></ruby>り<ruby>換<rt>か</rt></ruby>える (to transfer), a 一段 verb. This is a compound of <ruby>乗<rt>の</rt></ruby>る (to ride) and <ruby>換<rt>か</rt></ruby>える (to change).
- <ruby>見<rt>み</rt></ruby>えました — past of 見える (to be visible). Unlike <ruby>見<rt>み</rt></ruby>る (to look at), 見える means something comes into view on its own.
- <ruby>雷門<rt>かみなりもん</rt></ruby> — Kaminarimon, the famous "Thunder Gate" at the entrance to Sensoji.
- <ruby>売<rt>う</rt></ruby>っていました — past progressive of 売る (to sell). The shops "were selling" things.

## 17.10 Chapter Summary

This chapter covered five things:

**First**, the core movement verbs: いく, くる, かえる, あるく, はしる, わたる. Pay special attention to verb classes — かえる and はしる are 五段 despite looking like 一段.

**Second**, the particle で for means of transport: 電車で 行きます, バスで 行きます. The exception is "on foot," which uses the て-form: 歩いて 行きます.

**Third**, direction vocabulary and the grammar of giving directions, built on て-form chains ending in ください.

**Fourth**, the particle を for traversal — marking the path through which movement occurs, as opposed to を for direct objects. 道を 歩く (walk along the road), 橋を 渡る (cross the bridge).

**Fifth**, expressing distance and duration: ここから 駅まで 歩いて 十分ぐらいです.

These patterns combine freely. A single sentence can include a starting point (から), a destination (に or まで), a mode of transport (で or あるいて), a path (を), and a duration. The word order is flexible as long as the verb comes last. Practice building sentences with multiple elements until the particle assignments feel natural.
