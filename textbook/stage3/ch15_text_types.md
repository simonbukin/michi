# Chapter 15 — Recognizing Text Types and Rhetorical Structure

Every piece of writing has a purpose, and that purpose shapes its language. A newspaper article does not read like a personal blog. An instruction manual does not read like an advertisement. Even before you understand every word, recognizing the text type tells you what to expect — and what kind of reading strategy to use.

In Chapters 13 and 14, you learned the connectors that hold paragraphs together and the formal written style that dominates serious prose. This chapter steps back to look at the bigger picture: how different text types in Japanese are structured, what linguistic features signal each type, and how to adjust your reading approach accordingly.

We examine five text types: newspaper articles, opinion essays, instruction manuals, advertisements, and personal narratives. For each, we identify the structural conventions, the characteristic grammar and vocabulary, and the reading strategies that work best. The chapter closes with a set of short reading passages — one in each style — so you can practice identifying and adapting to different text types.

## 15.1 Newspaper Articles — Inverted Pyramid, Headlines, Attribution

### The inverted pyramid

Japanese newspaper articles follow the same inverted pyramid structure used worldwide: the most important information comes first, and details follow in decreasing order of importance.

- **Paragraph 1 (リード文):** The lead. Answers the core questions: who, what, when, where, why. This single paragraph should give you the essential story.
- **Paragraph 2-3:** Key details and context.
- **Later paragraphs:** Background, quotes, secondary details.

This means that if you can read only the first paragraph of a news article, you already have the main point. This is an important strategy for learners: do not feel obligated to read every paragraph. The first paragraph is the most valuable.

### Headline conventions

Japanese newspaper headlines have their own grammar. They are compressed, often dropping particles and using special abbreviations.

Common features:
- **Particles omitted:** 首相、訪米へ (The Prime Minister, to visit the US) — particles like が and を are frequently dropped.
- **体言止め (noun-ending):** Headlines often end with a noun instead of a verb. This creates a punchy, declarative feel: 新制度、来月開始 (New system, starting next month).
- **へ for future plans:** 政府、対策強化へ (Government, toward strengthening countermeasures) — へ signals direction or intention.
- **か for questions/uncertainty:** 景気回復か (Economic recovery?) — か signals that the outcome is uncertain.
- **Abbreviations:** 米 for アメリカ, 英 for イギリス, 日 for 日本, 首相 for 内閣総理大臣, 政府 for 日本政府.

### Attribution patterns

Newspaper articles attribute information to sources. Common patterns:

> ～によると — "according to"
> 気象庁によると、来週は全国的に気温が下がる見通しだ。
> "According to the Meteorological Agency, the outlook is for temperatures to drop nationwide next week."

> ～ことが分かった — "it was learned that, it became clear that"
> 調査の結果、利用者の半数以上が不満を感じていることが分かった。
> "As a result of the survey, it became clear that more than half of users feel dissatisfied."

> ～と発表した — "announced that"
> 市は新しい条例を導入すると発表した。
> "The city announced that it will introduce a new ordinance."

> ～という — "it is said that, reportedly"
> 被害は数十億円に上るという。
> "The damage reportedly amounts to tens of billions of yen."

> ～とのことだ — "it is reported that" (slightly more formal than という)
> 工事は来年三月に完了するとのことだ。
> "It is reported that construction will be completed in March of next year."

### Reading strategy for news articles

1. Read the headline for the topic and general direction.
2. Read the lead paragraph carefully — this is the core.
3. Scan for attribution markers (によると, と発表した) to identify whose perspective is being presented.
4. Use connector knowledge from Chapter 13 to follow the argument.

## 15.2 Opinion Essays — Thesis, Support, Counter, Conclusion

### Western vs Japanese essay structure

English-language essays typically follow a linear structure: thesis statement → supporting evidence → counterargument → conclusion. Japanese essays often follow a structure called 起承転結 (きしょうてんけつ):

| Stage | Meaning | Function |
|---|---|---|
| 起 (き) | Introduction | Raise the topic |
| 承 (しょう) | Development | Expand and support the topic |
| 転 (てん) | Turn | Introduce a twist, a different angle, or a complication |
| 結 (けつ) | Conclusion | Wrap up and synthesize |

The critical difference is 転 — the "turn." Where a Western essay might counter an objection and then refute it, a Japanese essay often introduces a genuinely different perspective that complicates the thesis. The conclusion then synthesizes rather than simply restating the original position.

In practice, modern Japanese newspaper editorials and JLPT-level opinion essays often blend both structures. But awareness of 起承転結 helps you anticipate the "turn" that often appears in the third paragraph of a Japanese opinion piece.

### Linguistic markers of opinion essays

Opinion essays use a distinctive set of patterns:

**Stating a thesis:**
> ～と考える / ～と思う — "I think that..."
> ～べきである — "one ought to..."
> ～ではないだろうか — "is it not the case that...?" (rhetorical)

**Supporting with reasons:**
> まず / 第一に — "first"
> なぜなら～からである — "because..." (formal reason-giving)
> ～という理由がある — "there is the reason that..."

**Introducing the "turn":**
> しかし / 一方で / たしかに～が — "however" / "on the other hand" / "certainly..., but"
> ～という意見もある — "there is also the opinion that..."

**Concluding:**
> 以上のことから — "from the above"
> したがって / つまり — "therefore" / "in other words"
> ～ことが重要である — "it is important to..."

### The たしかに...しかし pattern

One of the most important patterns to recognize in opinion writing is たしかに...しかし (certainly..., however). This is a concession-pivot structure: the writer acknowledges the opposing view (たしかに) and then pivots to their own position (しかし).

> たしかに、リモートワークには多くの利点がある。しかし、長期的に見ると、コミュニケーション不足が深刻な問題になる可能性がある。
> "Certainly, remote work has many advantages. However, looking at it long-term, insufficient communication could become a serious problem."

When you see たしかに in an essay, immediately look for the しかし or だが or けれども that follows. The writer's real position is after the pivot, not in the concession.

A variation of this pattern uses もちろん (of course) instead of たしかに:

> もちろん、経済成長は重要である。しかし、環境を犠牲にしてまで成長を追い求めるべきではない。
> "Of course, economic growth is important. However, we should not pursue growth to the point of sacrificing the environment."

### なぜなら...からである — Formal reason-giving

In opinion essays, reasons are often introduced with なぜなら and concluded with からである (or からだ in less formal writing). This frames the reason as a formal explanation.

> この政策には反対である。なぜなら、短期的な効果しか期待できないからである。
> "I oppose this policy. The reason is that only short-term effects can be expected."

The なぜなら...からである frame is a strong signal that you are in the "support" section of an opinion essay.

### Reading strategy for opinion essays

1. Identify the thesis — usually in the first paragraph after the topic is introduced.
2. Watch for たしかに...しかし — this pattern concedes a point and then pivots.
3. Look for なぜなら...からである for the writer's reasoning.
4. Look for the 転 — the paragraph that shifts the perspective.
5. Find the conclusion — signaled by 結局, 以上, したがって, or similar.

## 15.3 Instruction Manuals — Imperative Chains, Conditional Sequences

### Structure

Instruction manuals and how-to texts follow a sequential structure: step 1, step 2, step 3. They prioritize clarity and order above all else.

### Linguistic features

**て-form chains for sequential instructions:**
> ボタンを押して、画面が変わるのを待ってください。
> "Press the button and wait for the screen to change."

**Conditional sequences (たら, と, ば):**
> エラーが表示されたら、電源を切ってください。
> "If an error is displayed, please turn off the power."

> このボタンを押すと、設定画面が開きます。
> "When you press this button, the settings screen opens."

**Plain imperative (for concise instructions):**
> 次に、ふたを開ける。中の部品を取り出す。
> "Next, open the lid. Remove the parts inside."

Note: In instruction manuals, the plain form (開ける, 取り出す) is used for concise, impersonal directions. This is not rude — it is simply the register of technical writing.

**Prohibition:**
> 水に濡らさないでください。
> "Please do not get it wet."

> 分解しないこと。
> "Do not disassemble." (こと ending = rule/instruction)

**Noun-ending instructions (こと):**
The pattern ～こと at the end of a sentence functions as a rule or instruction, common in official notices and manuals:

> 使用前に必ず説明書を読むこと。
> "Be sure to read the instructions before use."

### Numbering and sequencing

Many instruction manuals use explicit numbering or sequencing words to make the order clear:

> 1. 電源を入れる。
> 2. 言語を選択する。
> 3. 利用規約に同意する。

When numbering is not used, look for these sequencing words:

| Word | Meaning |
|---|---|
| まず | first |
| 次に | next |
| それから | after that |
| 続いて | following that |
| 最後に | finally |

### Warning and caution language

Instructions often include warnings. Recognize these patterns:

> ご注意ください — "Please be careful"
> ～おそれがあります — "There is a risk of..." (formal warning)
> 必ず～してください — "Be sure to..."
> 絶対に～しないでください — "Absolutely do not..."

> 濡れた手で触ると、感電のおそれがあります。
> "If you touch it with wet hands, there is a risk of electric shock."

### Reading strategy for instructions

1. Follow the sequence — look for まず, 次に, それから, 最後に.
2. Identify conditions (たら, と, ば) — these tell you when to do what.
3. Pay attention to prohibitions (ないでください, ないこと, てはいけない).
4. Read warnings carefully — they use おそれがある, ご注意, and 必ず.

## 15.4 Advertisements — Nominalizations, Incomplete Sentences, Superlatives

### Structure

Advertisements do not argue or explain. They attract, persuade, and create desire. Their structure is loose: a hook, a key benefit, and a call to action.

### Linguistic features

**Incomplete sentences:**
Advertisements frequently use fragments — noun phrases without predicates, or sentences that trail off.

> 新しい自分へ。
> "Toward a new you."

> 家族の笑顔のために。
> "For the smiles of your family."

These fragments are intentional. They create atmosphere and let the reader fill in the meaning.

**Nominalization for impact:**
Instead of full sentences, advertisements use nominalized phrases that function as slogans.

> 美しさを追求すること。それが私たちの使命です。
> "Pursuing beauty. That is our mission."

**Superlatives and strong claims:**
> 最高の品質 — "the finest quality"
> 業界初 — "first in the industry"
> 驚きの価格 — "an astonishing price"
> 圧倒的な人気 — "overwhelming popularity"

**Questions directed at the reader:**
> あなたは本当に満足していますか。
> "Are you truly satisfied?"

> こんな経験はありませんか。
> "Haven't you had this kind of experience?"

**Katakana loan words:**
Advertisements tend to use more katakana loan words than other text types, giving a modern or international feel:
> プレミアム, リニューアル, キャンペーン, ブランド, スタイル

### Reading strategy for advertisements

1. Do not expect logical argument — look for emotional appeal.
2. Identify the product/service and the key benefit being promised.
3. Watch for incomplete sentences and fill in the implied meaning.
4. Recognize that superlatives are marketing, not factual claims.

## 15.5 Personal Narratives and Blogs — Casual Register, Emotional Markers

### Structure

Personal narratives and blog posts follow the writer's thoughts and feelings. Structure varies widely — some are chronological, others are thematic, and many are associative (one thought leading to another).

### Linguistic features

**Casual copula and sentence endings:**
> 昨日、友達と買い物に行ったんだけど、すごくいい店を見つけた。
> "Yesterday I went shopping with a friend, and we found a really great shop."

The んだけど (explanatory + concessive) is characteristic of casual narrative.

**Emotional markers:**
> 本当にびっくりした！ — "I was really surprised!"
> めちゃくちゃおいしかった — "It was incredibly delicious"
> ちょっと悲しかったなあ — "I was a little sad, you know"

Sentence-final particles like なあ (reflection, emotion), よね (seeking agreement), and かな (wondering) appear frequently.

**Contractions:**
> ている → てる: 最近忙しくしてる
> てしまう → ちゃう: 食べちゃった
> なければ → なきゃ: 早く行かなきゃ
> と言う → って: 友達が来るって

These are the colloquial contractions you will study in detail in Chapter 16. In blogs and personal writing, they are the default.

**First-person perspective:**
The writer's feelings and opinions are front and center. Expect frequent use of:
> ～と思った (I thought)
> ～て嬉しかった (I was happy that)
> ～のが楽しかった (doing X was fun)

**Onomatopoeia:**
Personal narratives use onomatopoeia more freely than formal writing:
> わくわくした (excited)
> のんびりした (relaxed, leisurely)
> ぐっすり眠れた (slept soundly)

### Temporal markers in narratives

Personal narratives frequently use time expressions to move the story forward:

> 先日 — "the other day"
> ある日 — "one day"
> その時 — "at that time"
> しばらくして — "after a while"
> 気がつくと — "before I knew it"

These markers orient the reader in the timeline and are the narrative equivalent of the discourse connectors you studied in Chapter 13.

### Mixing registers

Blog writers frequently shift between casual and slightly more formal language. They might use です/ます when addressing the reader directly, then shift to plain form for narrative sections:

> 皆さん、こんにちは。今日は先週の旅行について書きます。
> 実は、先週の金曜日、急に休みが取れたんだよね。それで、前から行きたかった鎌倉に行ってみた。

The first sentence addresses the audience politely. The second shifts into casual narrative mode. This register-mixing is normal and expected in blog writing.

### Reading strategy for personal narratives

1. Identify the writer's emotional state — this is often the real "point" of the text.
2. Do not expect tight logical structure — follow the associative flow.
3. Use casual grammar knowledge to parse contractions.
4. Watch for sentence-final particles that signal the writer's attitude.
5. Note register shifts — the writer may move between polite address and casual narration.

## 15.6 Reading Multiple Short Passages in Different Text Types

Below are four short passages. For each, identify the text type before reading the translation.

---

### Passage A

政府は二十五日、来年度の予算案を閣議決定した。総額は約百十兆円で、過去最大となった。社会保障費の増加が主な要因であるとのことだ。野党は「財政規律が失われている」と批判している。

**Translation:**

The government approved the budget proposal for the next fiscal year at a cabinet meeting on the 25th. The total amount is approximately 110 trillion yen, the largest ever. The main factor is reportedly the increase in social security costs. The opposition party is criticizing this, saying "fiscal discipline has been lost."

**Text type:** Newspaper article. Features: inverted pyramid (key fact first), attribution (とのことだ, と批判している), compressed headline-style opening, objective tone.

---

### Passage B

お肌の悩み、もう終わりにしませんか。天然成分だけで作られた新しいスキンケア。敏感肌の方にも安心。今なら初回限定で三十パーセントオフ。美しい毎日を、あなたに。

**Translation:**

Why not put an end to your skin worries? New skincare made with only natural ingredients. Safe even for sensitive skin. Now, first-time limited offer: 30% off. Beautiful every day, to you.

**Text type:** Advertisement. Features: question directed at reader, incomplete sentences (fragments), superlative claims (天然成分だけ), emotional appeal, call to action (今なら).

---

### Passage C

先日、初めて一人旅をしてみた。行き先は京都。正直、ちょっと不安だったんだけど、行ってみたら全然大丈夫だった。一人だと自分のペースで動けるし、知らない人とも気軽に話せる。お寺を回ったり、おいしいものを食べたり、のんびり過ごした。一人旅、思っていたよりずっと楽しかった。またやりたいなあ。

**Translation:**

The other day, I tried solo traveling for the first time. The destination was Kyoto. Honestly, I was a little anxious, but when I actually went, it was totally fine. When you're alone, you can move at your own pace, and you can casually talk to strangers too. I visited temples, ate delicious food, and spent my time in a relaxed way. Solo travel was way more fun than I expected. I want to do it again.

**Text type:** Personal blog / narrative. Features: casual register (んだけど, 全然, なあ), first-person emotional perspective, onomatopoeia (のんびり), sentence-final particle なあ expressing wistful reflection, informal structure following the flow of thought.

---

### Passage D

ご使用の前に、必ず本書をお読みください。まず、本体の裏にあるカバーを外す。次に、付属の電池を入れる。電池の向きに注意すること。カバーを元に戻し、電源ボタンを三秒間長押しする。画面にロゴが表示されたら、初期設定を開始してください。

**Translation:**

Before use, please be sure to read this manual. First, remove the cover on the back of the unit. Next, insert the included batteries. Pay attention to the orientation of the batteries. Replace the cover and press and hold the power button for three seconds. When the logo appears on the screen, please begin the initial setup.

**Text type:** Instruction manual. Features: sequential structure (まず, 次に), plain imperative for steps (外す, 入れる, 注意すること), conditional (表示されたら), polite framing at start and end (お読みください, 開始してください), prohibition-style instruction (注意すること).

---

### Summary: What to look for

As you read each passage above, you likely noticed how quickly the text type becomes apparent — often within the first sentence. A headline-style opening with attribution signals news. Fragments and emotional appeals signal advertising. Sequential markers and conditionals signal instructions. Casual contractions and sentence-final particles signal personal writing. Training yourself to recognize these signals within the first few seconds of reading a passage will help you adjust your reading strategy immediately, rather than struggling with unexpected language patterns.
