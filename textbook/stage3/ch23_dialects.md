# Chapter 23 — Regional Japan and Dialect Awareness

Until now, everything in this textbook has been in 標準語（ひょうじゅんご）-- standard Japanese, based on the Tokyo dialect. But Japan is a country of extraordinary linguistic diversity. Every region has its own 方言（ほうげん, dialect）, and some are so different from standard Japanese that speakers from opposite ends of the country cannot understand each other without switching to 標準語.

As an N3 learner, you are not expected to speak any dialect. But you are expected to recognize that dialects exist, to not be thrown off when you encounter them in media, and to have a basic familiarity with the most prominent dialect -- 関西弁（かんさいべん）, the dialect of the Osaka-Kyoto-Kobe region. Dialects appear in films, anime, novels, comedy, and everyday conversation. Ignoring them means missing a significant dimension of Japanese language and culture.

This chapter provides recognition-level knowledge. The goal is comprehension, not production.

---

## 23.1 方言 as a Concept

### What Counts as 方言?

The term 方言 in Japanese covers both **vocabulary differences** (different words for the same thing) and **grammatical differences** (different verb endings, different particles, different sentence-final forms). It also includes **pronunciation and intonation** differences, which are often the first thing a listener notices.

> 方言とは、特定の地域で使われる言葉の体系のことを指す。語彙、文法、発音のすべてにおいて標準語と異なる場合がある。

> "Dialect refers to the linguistic system used in a specific region. There are cases where it differs from standard Japanese in all aspects: vocabulary, grammar, and pronunciation."

### 方言 vs 標準語

標準語 (standard Japanese) is sometimes also called 共通語（きょうつうご, common language）. It is the variety taught in schools, used in news broadcasts, and understood nationwide. However, even native speakers of 標準語 have regional coloring in their speech -- slight pronunciation differences, vocabulary preferences, and intonation patterns that mark them as being from a particular area.

> 標準語とは、全国どこでも通じる日本語のことであり、主にテレビのニュースや教科書で使われている。

> "Standard Japanese is the Japanese that is understood everywhere in the country, and it is primarily used in television news and textbooks."

### Why Dialects Matter for Learners

Even if you only speak 標準語, you will encounter dialects in the following situations:

1. **Movies, dramas, and anime** -- Characters from specific regions speak in dialect to signal their background, personality, or social role. The tough character in a crime drama often speaks 関西弁. A gentle grandmother character may speak 東北弁.

2. **Comedy** -- Much of Japanese stand-up comedy (漫才, まんざい) is performed in 関西弁, as Osaka is considered the center of Japanese comedy.

3. **Travel** -- When visiting regions outside Tokyo, you will hear local speech. Knowing that certain unfamiliar words are dialectal rather than unknown standard Japanese prevents confusion.

4. **Workplaces and universities** -- People from different regions carry their dialects with them, even if they modify their speech toward 標準語 in formal situations.

> 方言を知らなくても日本語は使えるが、方言に対する基本的な知識があれば、日本語の理解がより深まるものだ。

> "Even without knowing dialects you can use Japanese, but if you have basic knowledge about dialects, your understanding of Japanese will deepen further -- that's how it works."

The sentence-ending ものだ (Chapter 8) here expresses a general truth.

## 23.2 関西弁 Basics

関西弁 is the dialect family spoken in the Kansai region (関西地方), encompassing Osaka, Kyoto, Kobe, Nara, and surrounding areas. It is by far the most widely recognized dialect in Japan due to the region's cultural influence -- comedy, food culture, historical significance, and a strong sense of regional pride.

Below are the key features of 関西弁 that a learner should recognize. Remember: the goal is recognition, not production.

### Copula: や (= だ)

In standard Japanese, the plain copula is だ. In 関西弁, it is **や**.

| Standard | 関西弁 | Meaning |
|----------|--------|---------|
| そうだ | そうや | "That's right" |
| 本当だ | ほんまや | "It's true" |
| 学生だ | 学生や | "I'm a student" |

The past form そうだった becomes そうやった. The negative そうじゃない becomes そうやない or ちゃう.

> 標準語の「そうだね」は、関西弁では「そうやな」になる。

> "Standard Japanese 'sou da ne' becomes 'sou ya na' in Kansai dialect."

### Negative: ～へん / ～ん (= ～ない)

Where standard Japanese uses ～ない for negation, 関西弁 often uses **～へん** or **～ん**.

| Standard | 関西弁 | Meaning |
|----------|--------|---------|
| 食べない | 食べへん | "don't eat" |
| 分からない | 分からへん | "don't understand" |
| 行かない | 行かへん | "don't go" |
| 知らない | 知らん | "don't know" |
| できない | でけへん / できひん | "can't do" |

The ～ん form (as in 知らん) is actually used across many dialects and even informally in some standard speech.

### いる → おる

Standard いる (to exist, for animate beings) is replaced by **おる** in 関西弁.

| Standard | 関西弁 | Meaning |
|----------|--------|---------|
| いる | おる | "to exist, to be" |
| いない | おらん / おらへん | "is not there" |
| 何している？ | 何しとる？ / 何しとん？ | "What are you doing?" |

> 標準語の「何をしているの？」は、関西弁では「何しとん？」になることがある。

> "Standard 'nani wo shiteiru no?' can become 'nani shiton?' in Kansai dialect."

### ～はる -- Kansai Respectful Form

関西弁 has its own respectful suffix: **～はる**. It sits between plain and formal -- roughly equivalent to using someone's action with gentle respect, without the full formality of 尊敬語.

| Standard (respectful) | 関西弁 | Meaning |
|----------------------|--------|---------|
| 行かれる / いらっしゃる | 行かはる / 行きはる | "goes" (respectful) |
| 食べられる / 召し上がる | 食べはる | "eats" (respectful) |
| している | してはる | "is doing" (respectful) |

> 先生、もう帰りはりましたか。

> "Has the teacher gone home already?" (Kansai respectful)

In standard Japanese, this would be 先生、もうお帰りになりましたか or 先生、もう帰られましたか.

### めっちゃ -- Intensifier

**めっちゃ** means "very, extremely" and is the 関西弁 equivalent of standard すごく or とても. It has spread far beyond Kansai and is now used nationwide by younger speakers, though it remains informal.

> このラーメン、めっちゃうまいわ。

> "This ramen is really delicious." (Kansai style)

Note: うまい is used instead of おいしい in casual speech (across dialects), and the sentence-final わ in 関西弁 is used by all genders (unlike the traditionally feminine わ in standard Japanese).

### なんでやねん -- The Kansai Retort

**なんでやねん** is the quintessential 関西弁 expression. It literally means "Why is that?!" (なんで = why, や = copula, ねん = emphatic ending) and is used as a comedic retort -- the "straight man" (ツッコミ) line in Kansai comedy.

> 漫才では、ボケとツッコミの掛け合いの中で「なんでやねん」が欠かせない。

> "In manzai comedy, 'nandeyanen' is indispensable in the back-and-forth between the funny man and the straight man."

Other common 関西弁 expressions for recognition:

| 関西弁 | Standard equivalent | Meaning |
|--------|-------------------|---------|
| ほんま | 本当 | really, truly |
| あかん | だめ | no good, not allowed |
| おおきに | ありがとう | thank you |
| しんどい | 疲れた / 大変だ | tired, tough |
| ちゃう | 違う | different, wrong |
| ～やんか | ～じゃないか | isn't it? (seeking agreement) |

## 23.3 Other Dialect Features for Awareness

### 東北弁 -- Tohoku Dialects

The dialects of the Tohoku region (northeastern Honshu) are sometimes the most difficult for standard-Japanese speakers to understand, due to significant vowel and consonant changes.

Key features for awareness:

- **Vowel reduction**: Unstressed vowels are often weakened or dropped. Words sound "swallowed."
- **Voicing of か/た/さ lines**: か may sound like が, た may sound like だ.
- **～べ / ～べし** as sentence endings expressing intention or suggestion (from classical Japanese).
- **～だべ** (= だろう): "It's probably..." / "Right?"

> 東北弁の特徴として、母音が弱くなることや、清音が濁音に変わることが挙げられる。

> "As features of Tohoku dialect, the weakening of vowels and the change of voiceless consonants to voiced consonants can be cited."

### 九州弁 -- Kyushu Dialects

Kyushu dialects vary significantly by area (Hakata-ben in Fukuoka, Kagoshima-ben in the south, etc.), but some common features include:

- **～ばい / ～たい** as sentence-final particles (Hakata): confirmatory or assertive
- **～と** as an explanatory ending (= の in standard): 何しとると？ ("What are you doing?")
- **よか** (= いい / 良い): "good"
- **～けん / ～き** (= から): "because"

> 「よかよか」は九州弁で「いいよ、いいよ」という意味である。

> "'Yoka yoka' means 'It's fine, it's fine' in Kyushu dialect."

### 北海道弁 -- Hokkaido Dialect

Hokkaido was settled largely by migrants from various regions in the Meiji era, so its dialect is a blend with some distinctive features:

- **～しょ** (= でしょう): "right?" / "probably"
- **なまら** (= とても / すごく): "very" (similar to めっちゃ)
- **～っしょ** as a casual confirmation tag
- **しばれる** (= とても寒い): "extremely cold"

> 北海道では「とても寒い」ことを「しばれる」と言う。

> "In Hokkaido, being very cold is expressed as 'shibareru.'"

## 23.4 Standard vs Dialect in Media -- Code-Switching

### When Speakers Switch

Japanese speakers constantly **code-switch** (使い分ける, つかいわける) between standard Japanese and their regional dialect. The general pattern:

- **Formal / public situations**: 標準語 (or close to it)
- **Informal / private situations**: 方言 (with people from the same region)
- **Mixed settings**: A blend, with speakers "dialing up" or "dialing down" their dialect features

> 関西出身のビジネスパーソンは、取引先との会議では標準語を使い、同僚との雑談では関西弁に切り替えることが多い。

> "Business people from Kansai often use standard Japanese in meetings with clients and switch to Kansai dialect in casual conversation with colleagues."

### Dialect in Fiction and Media

In novels, manga, and anime, dialect serves as a character marker:

- **関西弁** often signals: energetic, funny, direct, street-smart, or merchant-like
- **東北弁** often signals: rural, honest, hardworking, traditional
- **九州弁** (especially male speech) often signals: tough, masculine, passionate
- **京都弁** (a sub-variety of 関西弁) often signals: refined, indirect, slightly mysterious

These are stereotypes, of course, but recognizing them helps you understand characterization in Japanese media.

> フィクションにおいて方言は、登場人物の出身地だけでなく、性格や社会的立場を示す手段としても機能している。

> "In fiction, dialect functions as a means of indicating not only a character's place of origin but also their personality and social standing."

The compound particle において (Chapter 5) provides the formal locative, and のみならず could replace だけでなく for even higher formality (Chapter 6).

### Dialect Pride and Regional Identity

方言 is a source of pride for many Japanese people. Regional dialect contests, dialect-themed goods, and local mascots using dialect speech are common. At the same time, dialect speakers have historically faced pressure to use 標準語 in education and professional settings, leading to complex feelings about linguistic identity.

> 方言に対する意識は世代によっても異なり、若い世代ほど方言を「かわいい」「おもしろい」と肯定的にとらえる傾向がある。

> "Attitudes toward dialect also differ by generation, and younger generations tend to view dialect positively as 'cute' or 'interesting.'"

## 23.5 Reading Passage -- A Conversation with Dialect

### 大阪の友達

私が大学で初めて出会った友達の一人は、大阪出身の山田さんだった。東京で育った私にとって、山田さんの話し方は新鮮で面白かった。

初めて一緒にご飯を食べに行ったとき、山田さんはメニューを見てこう言った。

「このお好み焼き、めっちゃうまそうやな。食べたことある？」

私が「ないよ」と答えると、山田さんは笑って言った。

「ほんまに？ あかんあかん、お好み焼き食べたことないとか、ありえへんわ。」

注文した後、山田さんが作り方を教えてくれた。

「まず生地を広げて、その上にキャベツをのせるんや。ひっくり返すタイミングが大事やで。焦ったらあかんねん。」

お好み焼きはとてもおいしかった。「めっちゃうまいやろ？」と山田さんが得意そうに言った。確かにそうだった。あの日以来、お好み焼きは私の好物になった。そして、山田さんの関西弁も、すっかり聞き慣れてしまった。

### English Translation

**My Friend from Osaka**

One of the first friends I met at university was Yamada, who was from Osaka. For me, having grown up in Tokyo, Yamada's way of speaking was fresh and interesting.

The first time we went to eat together, Yamada looked at the menu and said:

"This okonomiyaki looks really good, doesn't it? Have you ever had it?"

When I answered "No," Yamada laughed and said:

"Really?! That's no good -- never having eaten okonomiyaki is unthinkable!"

After we ordered, Yamada taught me how to make it.

"First you spread out the batter, and put cabbage on top. The timing of flipping it is important. You can't rush it."

The okonomiyaki was very delicious. "It's really good, right?" Yamada said proudly. It certainly was. Since that day, okonomiyaki became one of my favorite foods. And I had completely gotten used to Yamada's Kansai dialect before I knew it.

### Dialect Notes from the Passage

| Dialect form | Standard equivalent | Meaning |
|-------------|-------------------|---------|
| うまそうやな | おいしそうだね | looks delicious, huh |
| ほんまに | 本当に | really |
| あかん | だめ | no good |
| ありえへん | ありえない | unthinkable |
| ～んや | ～んだ / ～のだ | explanatory ending |
| ～やで | ～だよ | assertive ending |
| あかんねん | だめなんだ | it's no good (emphatic) |
| うまいやろ | おいしいだろう | it's good, right? |
