# Chapter 25 — Kanji: Semantic Clusters and Compound Building

In Stages 1 and 2, kanji were introduced by frequency — the most commonly encountered characters came first, regardless of semantic category. That approach makes sense at the beginning: you need to read 人, 大, 日, 学 immediately because they appear everywhere. But at the N3 level, a frequency-only approach starts to lose efficiency. You now know enough kanji that new characters cluster naturally into semantic fields, and learning them in groups dramatically improves both retention and the ability to predict meanings of unfamiliar compounds.

This chapter introduces three semantic clusters — legal/institutional, emotional, and economic — and teaches you a systematic method for analyzing and predicting compound meanings.

---

## 25.1 From Frequency to Clusters

Frequency-based learning answers the question: "Which kanji will I see most often?" Cluster-based learning answers a different question: "Which kanji belong together conceptually, and how do they combine?"

The advantage of clusters is threefold:

1. **Memory anchoring** — Kanji learned in a semantic group reinforce each other. When you learn 法 (law), 律 (regulation), 規 (rule), and 制 (system) together, each one strengthens your memory of the others.

2. **Compound prediction** — Once you know the individual components, you can predict the meaning of compounds you have never seen before. If 経 means "pass through / manage" and 済 means "settle / save," then 経済 ("manage + settle") points toward "economy" — and it does.

3. **Reading transfer** — Many kanji that share a phonetic component also share an 音読み. Learning to spot these patterns lets you guess the reading of unfamiliar characters with surprising accuracy.

The three clusters in this chapter are chosen because they cover vocabulary domains that appear constantly in N3-level reading: news articles, opinion essays, workplace documents, and social commentary.

## 25.5 漢字語構成 — Predicting Compound Meaning from Components

Most two-character kanji compounds (二字熟語) follow one of several structural patterns. Recognizing these patterns lets you make educated guesses about unfamiliar compounds.

### Pattern 1: Modifier + Modified (修飾関係)

The first character modifies or describes the second.

| Compound | Structure | Meaning |
|----------|-----------|---------|
| 新聞 | new + hear → new things heard | newspaper |
| 高速 | high + speed | high speed |
| 大学 | great + study | university |
| 人権 | person + right | human rights |
| 税金 | tax + money | tax money |

### Pattern 2: Verb + Object (動目関係)

The first character is a verbal action; the second is its object.

| Compound | Structure | Meaning |
|----------|-----------|---------|
| 読書 | read + book | reading (books) |
| 作曲 | make + music | composition |
| 納税 | deliver + tax | tax payment |
| 就職 | take up + position | getting a job |
| 制限 | control + limit | restriction |

### Pattern 3: Parallel / Synonym (並列関係)

Both characters have similar meanings; together they reinforce.

| Compound | Structure | Meaning |
|----------|-----------|---------|
| 道路 | road + road | road, highway |
| 規則 | rule + rule | regulation |
| 権利 | right + benefit | right, entitlement |
| 悲哀 | sad + sad | sorrow |
| 支援 | support + assist | support |

### Pattern 4: Antonym Pair (対立関係)

The two characters have opposite meanings; together they name a spectrum or category.

| Compound | Structure | Meaning |
|----------|-----------|---------|
| 収支 | income + expenditure | income and outgo |
| 売買 | sell + buy | trade, buying and selling |
| 攻防 | attack + defend | offense and defense |
| 愛憎 | love + hate | love and hate |
| 損益 | loss + gain | profit and loss |

### Pattern 5: Subject + Predicate (主述関係)

The first character is the subject; the second describes it.

| Compound | Structure | Meaning |
|----------|-----------|---------|
| 国営 | country + manage | state-operated |
| 自律 | self + regulate | self-discipline |
| 民主 | people + govern | democracy |
| 地震 | ground + shake | earthquake |

### Using the Patterns

When you encounter an unfamiliar compound:

1. Identify the individual meanings of each character.
2. Try each pattern: Is the first modifying the second? Is the first acting on the second? Are they synonyms? Antonyms? Subject-predicate?
3. The pattern that produces a coherent meaning is likely correct.

This does not always work perfectly — some compounds are semantically opaque (当たり前 "obvious" does not decompose neatly) — but it works surprisingly often, especially with 音読み compounds in formal or technical vocabulary.

## 25.6 Common 音読み Patterns — Shared Phonetic Components

Many kanji contain a **phonetic component** (音符) — a part of the character that signals the 音読み. When you learn to spot these, you can often guess the reading of an unfamiliar character.

### The 青 Family — セイ・ショウ

The component 青 (blue, セイ) appears in several kanji that share the reading セイ or ショウ:

| Kanji | Reading | Meaning | Note |
|-------|---------|---------|------|
| 青 | セイ | blue, green | base character |
| 清 | セイ | clean, pure | 氵 + 青 |
| 精 | セイ | spirit, refined | 米 + 青 |
| 晴 | セイ | clear (weather) | 日 + 青 |
| 静 | セイ | quiet | 青 + 争 |
| 請 | セイ・シン | request | 言 + 青 |
| 情 | ジョウ | emotion, feeling | 忄 + 青 (exception: ジョウ, not セイ) |

### The 工 Family — コウ

| Kanji | Reading | Meaning | Note |
|-------|---------|---------|------|
| 工 | コウ | craft, work | base character |
| 功 | コウ | achievement | 工 + 力 |
| 攻 | コウ | attack | 工 + 攵 |
| 紅 | コウ | crimson | 糸 + 工 |
| 空 | クウ | sky, empty | 穴 + 工 (exception: クウ, not コウ) |

### The 反 Family — ハン

| Kanji | Reading | Meaning | Note |
|-------|---------|---------|------|
| 反 | ハン | anti-, reverse | base character |
| 飯 | ハン | rice, meal | 食 + 反 |
| 板 | ハン・バン | board, plank | 木 + 反 |
| 版 | ハン | printing, edition | 片 + 反 |
| 販 | ハン | sell | 貝 + 反 |
| 坂 | ハン | slope | 土 + 反 |

### The 可 Family — カ

| Kanji | Reading | Meaning | Note |
|-------|---------|---------|------|
| 可 | カ | possible | base character |
| 何 | カ | what | 亻 + 可 |
| 河 | カ | river | 氵 + 可 |
| 歌 | カ | song | 可 + 欠 |
| 荷 | カ | luggage, load | 艹 + 何 |

### Using Phonetic Components

The system is not perfectly reliable — exceptions exist, and some phonetic components have been distorted over centuries of character evolution. But as a guessing strategy, it works well enough to be useful:

- When you see an unfamiliar character containing a component whose reading you know, try that reading first.
- If the compound appears in a context where the topic is clear, combining your phonetic guess with context often produces the correct word.
- Over time, you build an intuitive sense of which components are reliable phonetic markers and which are not.
