# Chapter 26 — Vertical Text and Real-World Reading

Until now, every reading passage in this textbook has been presented in horizontal format (横書き), running left to right across the page. This is the standard for language textbooks, websites, and most modern business documents. But a large portion of Japanese text in the real world — novels, newspapers, manga, formal letters, poetry, and many magazines — is written vertically (縦書き).

This chapter teaches the conventions of vertical text, the differences in punctuation and layout, and how mixed-format publications work. It also addresses furigana as a reading tool and provides practice passages in real-world formats.

---

## 26.1 縦書き — Vertical Text Conventions

### Basic Direction

In 縦書き:

- **Characters run from top to bottom** within each column.
- **Columns run from right to left** across the page.
- **Pages turn from right to left** — the binding is on the right side (the opposite of Western books). A Japanese novel opens from what an English reader would consider the "back."

This means reading begins at the top-right corner of the page and proceeds down to the bottom of the first column, then jumps to the top of the next column to the left.

### Where Vertical Text Appears

| Medium | Format | Notes |
|--------|--------|-------|
| 小説（novels） | 縦書き | Almost always vertical |
| 新聞（newspapers） | 縦書き (articles), 横書き (ads, data) | Mixed layout |
| 雑誌（magazines） | Mixed | Varies by genre |
| 漫画（manga） | 縦書き | Speech bubbles read top to bottom |
| 公文書（official documents） | 縦書き or 横書き | Formal letters often vertical |
| 教科書（textbooks） | 国語 is 縦書き; 数学/理科 is 横書き | Subject-dependent |
| ウェブサイト | 横書き | Almost always horizontal |
| メール / チャット | 横書き | Always horizontal |

### Numbers in Vertical Text

- **漢数字** (一、二、三...) are used for most numbers in vertical text.
- Dates: 二〇二六年三月二日 rather than 2026年3月2日.
- Large numbers may use 万 and 億 as normal.
- When 算用数字 (Arabic numerals) must appear in vertical text, two-digit numbers are sometimes written sideways in a single character space (縦中横).

### Alphabetic Characters and Katakana

- **Katakana loanwords** are written vertically without difficulty — each character occupies one space in the column.
- **Alphabetic abbreviations** (DNA, SNS) are typically rotated 90 degrees or written one letter per space vertically. There is no single standard, and publishers vary.
- **Long katakana words** can look unusual in vertical layout, but the reading process is the same.

## 26.2 Punctuation in Vertical Text

### 句読点 (Punctuation Marks)

The characters 。and 、are the same in both formats, but their **position within the character cell** changes:

| Mark | 横書き position | 縦書き position |
|------|----------------|----------------|
| 。(句点) | Bottom-right of cell | Top-right of cell |
| 、(読点) | Bottom-right of cell | Top-right of cell |

In practice, this means the period and comma appear at the upper right corner of their space when text is vertical, rather than the lower right as in horizontal text.

### Quotation Marks

| Type | 横書き | 縦書き | Usage |
|------|--------|--------|-------|
| Primary quotes | 「 」 | ﹁ ﹂ | Direct speech, titles |
| Nested quotes | 『 』 | ﹃ ﹄ | Quotes within quotes, book titles |

The characters are the same but oriented differently. 「 has its opening at the top in horizontal text and at the right in vertical text.

### Other Marks

| Mark | Name | Notes |
|------|------|-------|
| ー (長音) | 長音符 | Written as a vertical line ︱ in 縦書き |
| ～ | 波線 | Also rotated to vertical |
| ・ (中点) | 中点 | Centered in the cell in both formats |
| …… (三点リーダー) | 三点リーダー | Runs vertically down |
| ── (ダッシュ) | ダッシュ | Runs vertically |

### Line Breaks and Prohibited Breaks (禁則処理)

Certain characters must not appear at the start of a line (行頭禁則):

- Closing brackets: 」、）、】
- Small kana: ぁ、ぃ、ぅ、ぇ、ぉ、っ、ゃ、ゅ、ょ
- Punctuation: 。、、

Certain characters must not appear at the end of a line (行末禁則):

- Opening brackets: 「、（、【

These rules apply to both horizontal and vertical text but are particularly visible in vertical typesetting where column width is narrow.

## 26.3 Mixed Horizontal and Vertical Layouts

### Newspaper Layout

A typical Japanese newspaper page combines:

- **Main articles** in 縦書き — the primary news text runs vertically.
- **Headlines** in varying sizes, sometimes horizontal for emphasis.
- **Tables and data** in 横書き — stock prices, weather data, sports scores.
- **Advertisements** in 横書き — especially modern ads with product names in katakana or English.
- **Photo captions** — may be either format depending on placement.

The reader must switch between formats constantly. This feels disorienting at first but becomes natural with practice.

### Magazine Layout

Magazines vary enormously by genre:

- **文芸誌** (literary magazines): primarily 縦書き.
- **ファッション誌**: primarily 横書き with Western-influenced design.
- **週刊誌** (weekly news/gossip): mixed, with vertical text for articles and horizontal for sidebars, captions, and pull quotes.
- **漫画雑誌**: 縦書き for text, with panel reading order right-to-left, top-to-bottom.

### Strategy for Mixed Layouts

When encountering a mixed-format page:

1. Identify the **main text direction** by finding the longest continuous text block.
2. Look for **visual cues** — headers, sidebars, and boxed text often use the opposite format from the main text.
3. For newspapers, start with the **largest headline** and follow the article text below or beside it.
4. Do not try to read everything on a page in order — Japanese page layout assumes the reader scans and selects.

## 26.4 Furigana — Tool, Not Crutch

### What Furigana Is

Furigana (振り仮名) are small kana written alongside kanji to indicate their reading. In vertical text, furigana appear to the right of the kanji. In horizontal text, they appear above.

### When Furigana Appears

| Context | Furigana policy |
|---------|----------------|
| 小学校の教科書 | All kanji have furigana until that kanji's grade level |
| 新聞 | Only for kanji outside the 常用漢字表 (2,136 characters) or unusual readings |
| 小説（一般向け） | Rare; usually only for unusual names or non-standard readings |
| 小説（若者向け/ライトノベル） | More frequent, sometimes on most kanji |
| 漫画 | Typically full furigana (少年向け) or partial (青年向け) |
| 公文書 | Rare; legal terms assumed known |
| ウェブサイト | Varies; some sites offer furigana as an accessibility option |

### Special Uses of Furigana

Furigana are not always just pronunciation guides. Japanese writers sometimes use furigana creatively:

- **義訓** (semantic furigana): The kanji says one thing; the furigana says another. For example, 世界（ほし）— the kanji means "world" but the furigana reads ほし ("star"). This is common in manga, light novels, and song lyrics as a literary device.
- **Name readings**: Japanese personal names and place names frequently have readings that cannot be predicted from the kanji. Furigana are essential here.

### Using Furigana Strategically

At N3 level, the recommended approach is:

1. **Try to read without furigana first.** If you recognize the kanji, you do not need the furigana.
2. **Use furigana to confirm, not to substitute.** If you think you know the reading, glance at the furigana to check.
3. **Note cases where furigana taught you a new reading.** These are the moments when furigana earns its value.
4. **Wean yourself off full-furigana materials gradually.** If you are still relying on furigana for kanji you learned in Stage 1, you are using it as a crutch.

## 26.5 Reading Practice — Newspaper Column

The following passage is written in the style of a newspaper opinion column (コラム). In an actual newspaper, this would appear in 縦書き.

### 本文

都市部では、子どもの遊び場が年々減少している。公園はあるが、ボール遊び禁止や声を出すことへの苦情により、子どもたちが自由に遊べる場所は限られている。一方、地方では広い空間があるにもかかわらず、少子化の影響で遊ぶ子どもの数そのものが減っている。

この問題の背景には、都市部における土地利用の変化がある。かつて空き地や路地裏は子どもたちの遊び場として機能していたが、開発が進み、そうした空間は駐車場やマンションに変わった。加えて、安全に対する意識の高まりが、子どもだけで外で遊ぶことへの不安につながっている。

専門家は、子どもの遊びが身体的・精神的発達に不可欠であると指摘する。遊びを通じて、子どもたちは社会性やコミュニケーション能力を身につける。遊び場の減少は、単なる空間の問題ではなく、次の世代の成長に関わる社会的課題である。

行政と地域住民が協力し、子どもの遊び場を確保するための具体的な取り組みが求められている。

### Translation

In urban areas, play spaces for children are decreasing year by year. Parks exist, but due to bans on ball games and complaints about noise, the places where children can play freely are limited. Meanwhile, in rural areas, despite the availability of wide open spaces, the number of children who play there is itself declining due to the effects of the declining birthrate.

Behind this problem lies a change in land use in urban areas. Empty lots and back alleys once functioned as playgrounds for children, but as development progressed, those spaces turned into parking lots and apartment buildings. In addition, a heightened awareness of safety has led to anxiety about children playing outside unsupervised.

Experts point out that play is essential to children's physical and mental development. Through play, children acquire social skills and communication abilities. The decline in play spaces is not merely a matter of physical space but a societal issue that affects the growth of the next generation.

Cooperative efforts between government and local residents to secure play spaces for children are being called for.

## 26.6 Reading Practice — Magazine Excerpt

The following passage is written in the style of a culture magazine feature article. In print, this might appear in either 縦書き or 横書き depending on the magazine's design.

### 本文

最近、「推し活」という言葉をよく耳にするようになった。「推し」とは、自分が応援しているアイドルやキャラクター、スポーツ選手などのことで、「推し活」はその応援活動全般を指す。グッズを集めたり、ライブに行ったり、同じ推しを持つ仲間と交流したりと、その形はさまざまである。

推し活が注目されている理由の一つは、その経済的影響力である。推しのためなら普段は買わないものにもお金を使うという消費行動が、関連産業を大きく支えている。コンサートやイベントの開催は地域経済にも貢献し、いわゆる「推し経済」は無視できない規模に成長した。

しかし、推し活には注意すべき点もある。過度な出費による経済的負担や、推しに依存しすぎることによる精神的な不安定さが指摘されている。バランスを保ちながら、自分なりの楽しみ方を見つけることが大切だと言えるだろう。

推し活は単なる趣味を超え、現代の日本社会における一つの文化現象となっている。好きなものを応援する喜びは、日常生活に彩りを与え、多くの人にとって生きがいにもなっている。

### Translation

Recently, the term "推し活" (supporting one's favorite) has become something we hear often. "推し" refers to the idol, character, athlete, or other figure that one supports, and "推し活" means the full range of activities involved in that support. Collecting merchandise, attending live events, socializing with others who share the same favorite — the forms are varied.

One reason 推し活 has attracted attention is its economic impact. The consumer behavior of spending money on things one would not normally buy, if it is for one's favorite, significantly supports related industries. The holding of concerts and events also contributes to regional economies, and the so-called "推し economy" has grown to a scale that cannot be ignored.

However, there are points to be cautious about with 推し活. Excessive spending leading to financial burden and psychological instability from becoming too dependent on one's favorite have been noted. It can be said that it is important to find one's own way of enjoying it while maintaining balance.

推し活 has gone beyond being a mere hobby and become a cultural phenomenon in contemporary Japanese society. The joy of supporting what one loves adds color to daily life and has become, for many people, a reason for living.
