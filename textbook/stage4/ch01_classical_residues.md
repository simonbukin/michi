# Chapter 1 — Classical Residues: Why They Matter

Modern Japanese is full of fragments from classical Japanese grammar. These are not curiosities confined to literature classes — they appear on warning signs, in proverbs, in legal documents, in formal speeches, and in everyday set phrases that native speakers use without thinking. If you have ever seen a sign at a park, read a proverb, or encountered formal written Japanese, you have already met classical grammar whether you knew it or not.

This chapter introduces four classical forms that survive actively in modern Japanese: the negative ～ざる, the negative continuative ～ず/～ずに, the prohibitive ～べからず, and the comparative ～ごとく/～ごとき. None of these require you to study classical Japanese as a system. You simply need to recognize what they mean and how they function in the modern contexts where they appear.

If you studied Stage 3 Chapter 12, you already encountered ざるを得ない ("cannot help but"). That expression contains the classical negative ざる, and this chapter will show you the broader pattern it comes from.

---

## 1.1 Why Learn Classical Forms

Classical Japanese (文語, *bungo*) was the standard written language of Japan until the late nineteenth century. When Japan modernized its written language to match spoken Japanese (口語, *kougo*), many classical forms did not disappear entirely. Instead, they became fossilized in specific modern contexts:

- **Proverbs and sayings**: 猿も木から落ちる is modern grammar, but many proverbs preserve older forms.
- **Signs and rules**: Public prohibitions like 芝生に入るべからず use classical grammar to sound authoritative.
- **Legal and bureaucratic language**: Laws still use classical-derived constructions.
- **Literary and formal style**: Writers use classical forms to create elevated tone.
- **Set phrases**: Expressions like 知らず知らず (unknowingly) and やむを得ない (unavoidable) contain classical pieces.

You do not need to conjugate classical verbs productively. What you need is **recognition** — the ability to see a classical form in a modern sentence and understand what it means. That is what this chapter provides.

---

## 1.2 ～ざる — Classical Negative (= ない)

ざる is the classical equivalent of ない in its attributive form (the form that modifies nouns). In modern Japanese, ない directly modifies nouns: 知らない人 ("a person who doesn't know"). In classical Japanese, the same idea was expressed with ざる: 知らざる人.

Today, ざる survives primarily in set expressions and formal or literary writing.

### Formation

| Verb type | Modern negative | Classical ざる form |
|---|---|---|
| 五段 verb (書く) | 書かない | 書かざる |
| 一段 verb (食べる) | 食べない | 食べざる |
| する | しない | せざる |
| 来る | 来ない | 来（こ）ざる |

The formation rule is: take the classical 未然形 (irrealis stem), which for most verbs matches the あ-stem used before ない, and add ざる. For する, the classical irrealis stem is せ, giving せざる.

### Examples

> 働かざる者、食うべからず。
> "Those who do not work shall not eat." (A well-known Japanese rendering of the Biblical proverb from 2 Thessalonians 3:10.)

> 彼は　あらゆる　手段を　用いざるを得なかった。
> "He had no choice but to use every means available."

> 予期せざる　事態が　発生した。
> "An unforeseen situation occurred."

> 名も　知らざる　花が　咲いていた。
> "Flowers whose names were unknown were blooming."

> これは　許されざる　行為だ。
> "This is an unforgivable act."

### Connection to ざるを得ない

The expression ざるを得ない, which you learned in Stage 3 Chapter 12, literally means "cannot obtain the state of not doing" — that is, "cannot help but do." Now you can see that ざる is the classical negative form, and を得ない means "cannot obtain/avoid." The whole expression is a double negative: you cannot not-do it.

> 認めざるを得ない事実だ。
> "It is a fact that cannot help but be acknowledged."

## 1.3 ～ず / ～ずに — Classical Negative Continuative (= ないで)

ず is the classical negative in its continuative (連用形) function. Where modern Japanese uses ないで or なくて to connect a negative clause to what follows, classical Japanese used ず. This form is extremely common in modern Japanese — far more common than ざる — because it appears in everyday speech and writing, not just literary contexts.

ずに is the form used when the meaning is "without doing X" (equivalent to ないで as a manner expression). Plain ず appears in set phrases and in formal writing where it functions like a connective ("did not do X, and...").

### Formation

| Verb type | ず form | ずに form |
|---|---|---|
| 五段 verb (書く) | 書かず | 書かずに |
| 一段 verb (食べる) | 食べず | 食べずに |
| する | せず | せずに |
| 来る | 来（こ）ず | 来（こ）ずに |

Note: The irregular form for する is せず (not しず). This is because the classical irrealis stem of す (classical する) is せ.

### Examples

> 何も　食べずに　学校に　行った。
> "I went to school without eating anything."

> 知らず知らず、涙が　出ていた。
> "Without realizing it, tears were coming out."

> 彼は　一言も　言わず、部屋を　出て行った。
> "Without saying a single word, he left the room."

> 休まず　働き続けた。
> "I continued working without resting."

> 何の　準備も　せずに　試験を　受けた。
> "I took the exam without any preparation."

> 諦めず　最後まで　頑張ろう。
> "Let's not give up and do our best until the end."

### ～ず vs ～ないで — Are they interchangeable?

In most cases where ないで means "without doing," ずに can substitute directly:

> 傘を　持たないで　出かけた。 = 傘を　持たずに　出かけた。
> "I went out without bringing an umbrella."

However, ず/ずに sounds slightly more literary or formal than ないで. In casual spoken Japanese, ないで is far more natural. In writing, ずに is common and unremarkable.

The bare ず (without に) is more restricted. It appears in set phrases (知らず知らず, 絶えず, やむを得ず) and in literary or formal connective use:

> 雨が　降らず、作物が　枯れた。
> "The rain did not fall, and the crops withered."

### Common set phrases with ず

| Phrase | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 知らず知らず | unknowingly, before one realizes |
| 絶えず | constantly, incessantly |
| 少なからず | not a little, considerably |
| やむを得ず | unavoidably, having no choice |
| 限らず | not limited to |

## 1.4 ～べからず — "Must Not" (Signs and Rules)

べからず is the classical prohibitive form, meaning "must not" or "shall not." It combines べし (the classical auxiliary of obligation/propriety, equivalent to modern べきだ) with the classical negative ず in its terminal form. So べからず literally means "should-not."

In modern Japanese, べからず appears almost exclusively on signs, in rules, and in proverbs. It gives a tone of absolute authority — a commandment, not a suggestion.

### Formation

| Verb type | べからず form |
|---|---|
| 五段 verb (入る) | 入るべからず |
| 一段 verb (捨てる) | 捨てるべからず |
| する | するべからず |

べからず attaches to the dictionary form of the verb.

### Examples

> 芝生に　入るべからず。
> "Keep off the grass." (Literally: "One must not enter the lawn.")

> ここに　ゴミを　捨てるべからず。
> "Do not dump garbage here."

> 初心　忘るべからず。
> "One must not forget the beginner's mind." (A famous saying attributed to Zeami, founder of Noh theatre.)

> 働かざる者、食うべからず。
> "Those who do not work shall not eat." (Cf. section 1.2)

> 関係者以外、立ち入るべからず。
> "No entry for unauthorized persons."

### べからず vs modern prohibitions

| Expression | Tone |
|---|---|
| ～てはいけない | Standard prohibition (rules, instructions) |
| ～な (command) | Blunt spoken prohibition |
| ～べからず | Classical/authoritative prohibition (signs, maxims) |
| ～こと | Institutional rule style (校則: 携帯電話を持ち込まないこと) |

You will not use べからず in conversation. But you will see it on signs, in classical quotations, and occasionally in writing that aims for a grand or humorous tone.

### ～べからざる — "that which must not be"

When べからず is used as a modifier (attributive form), it becomes べからざる:

> 許すべからざる　行為だ。
> "It is an act that must not be forgiven."

> 見逃すべからざる　問題がある。
> "There is a problem that must not be overlooked."

This is quite formal and literary, but it appears in journalism and legal contexts.

## 1.5 ～ごとく / ～ごとき — "Like, As If" (Literary Comparison)

ごとく and ごとき are classical forms meaning "like" or "as if," equivalent to modern ～のように (adverbial) and ～のような (adjectival). ごとく is the adverbial form (modifying verbs), while ごとき is the attributive form (modifying nouns).

These forms appear in literary writing, formal speeches, proverbs, and set expressions. They carry a tone of elegance or gravity that ように/ような does not.

### Formation

| Preceding element | ごとく (adverbial) | ごとき (adjectival) |
|---|---|---|
| Noun + の | 嵐のごとく | 嵐のごとき |
| Verb (が) + ごとく | 飛ぶがごとく | 飛ぶがごとき |

The particle の connects nouns to ごとく/ごとき. With verbs, が (or no particle) is used.

### Examples

> 嵐のごとく　彼は　去っていった。
> "He departed like a storm."

> 光陰　矢のごとし。
> "Time flies like an arrow." (A famous proverb. ごとし is the terminal form of ごとく.)

> 彼女は　水を　得た　魚のごとく　生き生きと　働いた。
> "She worked vibrantly, like a fish that has found water."

> 夢のごとき　日々が　過ぎ去った。
> "Dream-like days passed away."

> 弾丸のごとき　速さで　走り抜けた。
> "He ran through at bullet-like speed."

> 飛ぶがごとく　走っていった。
> "He ran as if flying."

### ～ごとく vs ～ように

| Form | Tone | Usage |
|---|---|---|
| ～のように / ～のような | Neutral, everyday | Conversation, general writing |
| ～ごとく / ～ごとき | Literary, elevated | Formal writing, proverbs, rhetoric |

In casual conversation, using ごとく would sound strange or deliberately theatrical. In writing, it adds weight and elegance.

### ごとし — The sentence-final form

The sentence-final (terminal) form is ごとし. It appears mainly in proverbs:

> 光陰　矢の　ごとし。
> "Time is like an arrow."

> 人生は　旅の　ごとし。
> "Life is like a journey."

## 1.6 Reading Passage

次の文章は、ある日本語教師が学生に向けて書いたエッセイの一部です。

---

日本語を学ぶ皆さんへ

日本語を勉強していると、時々不思議な表現に出会うことがあるでしょう。公園に行けば「芝生に入るべからず」という看板があり、新聞を読めば「予期せざる事態」という表現が出てきます。これらは現代の文法では説明できない形ですが、日本人は毎日のように目にしています。

これらは古典文法の名残です。「べからず」は「してはいけない」、「せざる」は「しない」という意味の古い形です。知らず知らずのうちに、皆さんも<ruby>既<rt>すで</rt></ruby>にいくつかの古典的表現を覚えているはずです。「やむを得ず」「絶えず」「少なからず」——これらは<ruby>全<rt>すべ</rt></ruby>て古典の「ず」を含んでいます。

古典文法を体系的に学ぶ必要はありません。しかし、現代に残っている形を知らずに過ごすわけにはいきません。光陰矢のごとし。時間は限られています。効率よく、必要な形だけを確実に覚えましょう。

初心忘るべからず。日本語を学び始めた時の気持ちを忘れずに、一歩一歩進んでいきましょう。

---

**Translation:**

To all of you studying Japanese,

When studying Japanese, you will sometimes encounter mysterious expressions. If you go to a park, there are signs reading "Keep off the grass" (芝生に入るべからず), and if you read a newspaper, expressions like "unforeseen circumstances" (予期せざる事態) appear. These are forms that cannot be explained by modern grammar, but Japanese people see them every day.

These are remnants of classical grammar. べからず is the old form of "must not" (してはいけない), and せざる is the old form of "not doing" (しない). Without realizing it, you have probably already memorized several classical expressions. やむを得ず, 絶えず, 少なからず — all of these contain the classical ず.

There is no need to study classical grammar systematically. However, you cannot get by without knowing the forms that survive in modern Japanese. Time flies like an arrow. Time is limited. Let us efficiently and reliably learn just the forms we need.

Do not forget the beginner's mind. Without forgetting the feeling you had when you first began studying Japanese, let us move forward step by step.
