# Chapter 6 — Hypothetical and Extreme Conditions

Japanese has a rich set of conditional expressions beyond the basic と, ば, たら, and なら covered in earlier stages. The patterns in this chapter deal with extreme or dramatic hypotheticals, worst-case scenarios, and conditions that exclude or include without regard to category. They appear frequently in warnings, advice, formal writing, and any context where the speaker wants to emphasize the severity of a consequence or the breadth of a condition.

These expressions are staples of the N2 grammar inventory. Several of them use もの or 限り as their structural core, so recognizing these anchor words will help you parse unfamiliar sentences. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to express drastic consequences, wishful impossibilities, irreversible actions, necessary conditions, and categorical inclusion.

All patterns in this chapter attach to plain forms unless otherwise noted. Review Stage 1 Chapter 12 and Stage 2 Chapters 1-6 if you need to refresh plain form conjugation.

---

## 6.1 ～ようものなら — "If One Were to Dare"

ようものなら presents a hypothetical action and warns that its consequence would be severe. The speaker is saying: "If someone were so reckless as to do X, then Y (something terrible) would happen." The consequence clause almost always contains a negative or extreme outcome.

This pattern uses the volitional form (よう/おう), but it does not express actual volition. Instead, it frames the action as something one might attempt, with the implication that attempting it would be a serious mistake.

### Formation

| Preceding element | Pattern |
|---|---|
| Verb (volitional form) | 遅刻しよう + ものなら |
| Verb (volitional form) | 言おう + ものなら |

Note: This pattern is used exclusively with verbs. The volitional form of 一段 verbs ends in よう (食べよう), and 五段 verbs end in おう (行こう).

### Examples

> 会議に　遅刻しよう　ものなら、部長に　ひどく　叱られる。
> "If you were to be late to the meeting, you'd be severely scolded by the department head."

> あの先生の　授業で　居眠りしよう　ものなら、教室から　追い出される。
> "If you dare fall asleep in that teacher's class, you'll be thrown out of the classroom."

> 彼女の　前で　元カノの　話を　しよう　ものなら、大変な　ことに　なる。
> "If you bring up your ex-girlfriend in front of her, there will be hell to pay."

> この機密情報を　外部に　漏らそう　ものなら、即座に　解雇される。
> "If you were to leak this confidential information externally, you would be fired immediately."

> 締め切りを　一日でも　過ぎよう　ものなら、契約が　無効に　なる。
> "If you miss the deadline by even a single day, the contract becomes void."

### Key nuance

ようものなら is not a neutral conditional. It always carries a warning tone. You would not use it for positive or neutral outcomes. Compare:

> 遅刻**したら**、電話してね。(Neutral conditional — "If you're late, call me.")

> 遅刻**しようものなら**、クビになるぞ。(Extreme warning — "If you dare be late, you'll be fired.")

## 6.2 ～ものなら — "If Only One Could"

ものなら with the potential form expresses a wish for something the speaker believes is difficult or impossible. The English equivalent is "if only I could" or "if it were possible to." It often appears with やってみろ or やってみたい, expressing either a challenge or a longing.

There are two distinct uses:

1. **Wishful/longing:** With potential form — "if only it were possible"
2. **Challenge/provocation:** With dictionary or potential form + みろ — "go ahead and try, if you can"

### Formation

| Use | Pattern |
|---|---|
| Wishful | Verb (potential form) + ものなら + たい/ほしい |
| Challenge | Verb (dictionary/potential) + ものなら + みろ/みなさい |

### Examples (wishful)

> 戻れる　ものなら、学生時代に　戻りたい。
> "If I could go back, I'd want to return to my student days."

> やり直せる　ものなら、最初から　やり直したい。
> "If I could start over, I'd want to redo everything from scratch."

> 会える　ものなら、もう一度　祖母に　会いたい。
> "If I could see her, I'd want to meet my grandmother one more time."

### Examples (challenge)

> できる　ものなら　やって　みろ。
> "Go ahead and try — if you can."

> 逃げられる　ものなら　逃げて　みろ。
> "Run if you can — I dare you."

> 勝てる　ものなら　勝って　みなさい。
> "Win if you think you can."

### ようものなら vs ものなら

These two patterns share the word もの but function very differently:

| Pattern | Meaning | Tone |
|---|---|---|
| ようものなら | "If one dares to do X" → terrible consequence | Warning |
| ものなら (potential) | "If only one could do X" | Wishing / challenging |

## 6.3 ～たが最後 / ～たら最後 — "Once You Do... It's Over"

たが最後 and たら最後 express that once an action is taken, there is no turning back — an irreversible negative consequence follows. The literal sense is "if you do it, that's the last" — meaning the last chance, the point of no return. Both forms are interchangeable in meaning.

### Formation

| Form | Pattern |
|---|---|
| たが最後 | Verb (た form) + が最後 |
| たら最後 | Verb (たら form) + 最後 |

### Examples

> あのゲームを　始めたが最後、何時間も　やめられなくなる。
> "Once you start that game, you won't be able to stop for hours."

> 彼に　お金を　貸したら最後、二度と　返ってこない。
> "Once you lend him money, it will never come back."

> この犬は　散歩に　出たが最後、帰りたがらない。
> "Once this dog goes out for a walk, it refuses to come home."

> あの店の　ケーキを　食べたが最後、他の店の　ケーキでは　満足できなくなる。
> "Once you eat that shop's cake, you'll never be satisfied with cake from anywhere else."

> 社長が　話し始めたら最後、一時間は　終わらない。
> "Once the president starts talking, it won't end for at least an hour."

### Usage note

Despite the dramatic tone, たが最後 / たら最後 is sometimes used humorously for everyday situations, as in the cake and dog examples above. The core meaning remains: the action triggers something you cannot undo or escape.

## 6.4 ～ない限り(は) — "Unless, As Long As... Not"

ない限り(は) sets a necessary condition by negation. It means "unless X happens" or "as long as X does not happen." The implication is that the situation in the main clause will continue or hold true until the condition changes.

The affirmative form 限り also exists with the meaning "as long as," but ない限り specifically emphasizes a negative condition that must be met for change to occur.

### Formation

| Preceding element | Pattern |
|---|---|
| Verb (ない form) | 努力しない + 限り(は) |
| い-adjective (くない) | 安くない + 限り(は) |
| な-adjective + でない | 必要でない + 限り(は) |
| Noun + でない | 会員でない + 限り(は) |

### Examples

> 自分で　努力しない　限り、上達は　しない。
> "Unless you make the effort yourself, you won't improve."

> 熱が　下がらない　限り、学校には　行かせない。
> "As long as your fever doesn't go down, I won't let you go to school."

> 本人が　謝らない　限り、私は　許さない。
> "Unless he apologizes himself, I will not forgive him."

> 契約書に　サインしない　限り、この取引は　成立しない。
> "Unless you sign the contract, this deal will not go through."

> 特別な　事情が　ない　限り、出席して　ください。
> "Unless there are special circumstances, please attend."

### Affirmative 限り — "as long as"

For reference, the affirmative form means "as long as (the condition holds)":

> 日本に　いる　限り、日本語を　使い続けたい。
> "As long as I am in Japan, I want to keep using Japanese."

> 体が　元気な　限り、働き続ける　つもりだ。
> "As long as I'm healthy, I intend to keep working."

## 6.5 ～を問わず / ～は問わず — "Regardless of"

を問わず means "regardless of" or "irrespective of." It is used to state that something applies across all values of a given category. The verb 問う means "to question" or "to ask about," so を問わず literally means "without questioning X" — that is, X is not a relevant factor.

は問わず is a variation commonly seen in job listings and formal notices, where は marks the topic being set aside as irrelevant.

### Formation

| Pattern | Example |
|---|---|
| Noun + を問わず | 性別を問わず |
| Noun + は問わず | 経験は問わず |
| Paired nouns + を問わず | 昼夜を問わず |

を問わず frequently appears with paired or contrasting nouns: 男女, 昼夜, 国籍, 年齢, 経験の有無, 天候, etc.

### Examples

> この仕事は　年齢・性別を　問わず、誰でも　応募できます。
> "Anyone can apply for this job regardless of age or gender."

> 経験は　問わず。やる気の　ある方を　募集しています。
> "Experience not required. We are looking for motivated people."

> 昼夜を　問わず、緊急時には　ご連絡ください。
> "Please contact us in emergencies regardless of the time of day or night."

> 国籍を　問わず、<ruby>全<rt>すべ</rt></ruby>ての　住民が　対象です。
> "All residents are eligible regardless of nationality."

> 天候を　問わず、イベントは　予定通り　開催します。
> "The event will be held as scheduled regardless of the weather."

## 6.6 ～にかかわらず / ～にかかわりなく — "Regardless of"

にかかわらず and its variant にかかわりなく also mean "regardless of." This pattern was introduced in Stage 3 in the context of にもかかわらず ("despite"). Here, we focus on the form without も, which expresses categorical inclusion rather than concession.

The verb かかわる means "to be related to" or "to be involved with." So にかかわらず literally means "without being related to X" — X does not affect the outcome.

### Formation

| Pattern | Example |
|---|---|
| Noun + にかかわらず | 結果にかかわらず |
| Verb (plain) + か + にかかわらず | 参加するかしないかにかかわらず |
| Paired items + にかかわらず | 賛成・反対にかかわらず |

### Examples

> 成績の　良し悪しに　かかわらず、全員に　修了証を　出します。
> "Certificates of completion will be issued to everyone regardless of grade performance."

> 参加するか　しないかに　かかわらず、返事を　ください。
> "Please reply regardless of whether you will participate or not."

> 天候に　かかわらず、試合は　行われます。
> "The match will take place regardless of the weather."

> 理由の　いかんに　かかわらず、暴力は　許されない。
> "Violence is not permitted regardless of the reason."

> 結果に　かかわりなく、ベストを　尽くすことが　大切だ。
> "What matters is doing your best, regardless of the result."

### Comparison: を問わず vs にかかわらず

Both mean "regardless of," but there are subtle differences in usage:

| Feature | を問わず | にかかわらず |
|---|---|---|
| Typical context | Job listings, official notices, rules | Broader range: formal writing, rules, general statements |
| Common with paired nouns | Yes: 男女を問わず, 昼夜を問わず | Yes: 賛成・反対にかかわらず |
| Used with か questions | Rare | Common: 行くか行かないかにかかわらず |
| Nuance | "We don't ask about X" | "X has no bearing on the matter" |
| Formality | Formal | Formal |

In many cases they are interchangeable:

> 天候**を問わず** / 天候**にかかわらず**、実施します。
> "We will carry it out regardless of the weather." (Both natural)

However, にかかわらず is more flexible because it can follow か-questions and longer phrases, while を問わず tends to follow simple nouns or paired nouns.

## 6.7 Reading Passage

次の文章は、ある会社の社内掲示板に掲載されたお知らせと、それを読んだ社員たちの会話です。

---

【お知らせ】情報セキュリティ研修の実施について

全社員を対象に、情報セキュリティ研修を実施いたします。部署・役職を問わず、全員が受講してください。やむを得ない事情がない限り、欠席は認められません。

日時：六月十五日（金）十四時〜十六時
場所：本社五階　大会議室
対象：正社員・契約社員にかかわらず全従業員

---

佐藤：情報セキュリティ研修のお知らせ、見た？

鈴木：見たよ。部署を問わず全員参加だって。やむを得ない事情がない限り欠席できないらしい。

佐藤：まあ、当然だよね。最近、他の会社で情報漏洩があったじゃない。うちの会社で同じことが起きようものなら、信用を完全に失うよ。

鈴木：確かに。顧客データを漏らそうものなら、会社の存続にかかわるからね。

佐藤：前の会社では、パスワードを付箋に書いて画面に貼っている人がいたんだけど、それを上司に見られたが最後、始末書を書かされていたよ。

鈴木：<ruby>酷<rt>ひど</rt></ruby>いな。でも、セキュリティ意識が低いままでは、どんなシステムを入れたところで意味がない。

佐藤：そうだね。正社員か契約社員かにかかわらず全員が対象だし、会社も本気だということだよ。

鈴木：できるものなら研修をサボりたいけど、欠席したら最後、人事部に目をつけられるだろうな。

佐藤：はは、そうだね。<ruby>大人<rt>おとな</rt></ruby>しく出席しよう。

---

**Translation:**

The following is a notice posted on a company's internal bulletin board, followed by a conversation between employees who read it.

---

[Notice] Implementation of Information Security Training

Information security training will be conducted for all employees. Regardless of department or position, everyone must attend. Unless there are unavoidable circumstances, absences will not be permitted.

Date: Friday, June 15th, 14:00-16:00
Location: Headquarters, 5th floor, large conference room
Eligible: All employees regardless of whether full-time or contract

---

Sato: Did you see the notice about the information security training?

Suzuki: I did. They say everyone has to attend regardless of department. Apparently you can't miss it unless you have unavoidable circumstances.

Sato: Well, of course. Recently there was a data breach at another company, right? If the same thing were to happen at our company, we'd completely lose our credibility.

Suzuki: That's true. If someone were to leak customer data, it would threaten the company's very survival.

Sato: At my previous company, there was someone who wrote their password on a sticky note and stuck it on their screen. Once their boss spotted it, they were made to write an incident report.

Suzuki: That's rough. But if security awareness stays low, no matter what system you put in place, it's meaningless.

Sato: Right. Full-time or contract, everyone is included — the company is serious about this.

Suzuki: If I could, I'd love to skip the training, but once I'm absent, I'd probably be flagged by HR.

Sato: Ha, true. Let's just attend quietly.
