# Chapter 13 — Academic Writing Style

Japanese academic writing operates under a set of conventions that are quite different from both spoken Japanese and journalistic prose. The most striking feature is its systematic avoidance of direct assertion. Where an English academic paper might state "This proves that X," a Japanese paper is far more likely to say "From this, it can be considered that X" or "It may not be an overstatement to say that X." This indirectness is not vagueness — it is a disciplined rhetorical strategy for presenting claims in a way that acknowledges complexity, invites counterargument, and positions the writer as a careful thinker rather than an overconfident one.

This chapter covers the core patterns of academic Japanese: hedging expressions that soften assertions, formal transitions that structure arguments, the contrastive use of は for setting up comparisons, and expressions for distanced or attributed claims. Many of these patterns recombine elements you already know — と考えられる uses the potential passive, にほかならない uses a classical negative — but their function in academic context is specific and must be understood on its own terms.

If you plan to read Japanese research papers, university textbooks, or policy documents, this chapter is essential. Even if you do not, the patterns here appear in editorials, formal reports, and any context where a writer wants to sound careful and authoritative at the same time.

## 13.1 Academic Hedging

Academic hedging refers to expressions that soften the force of a claim. Rather than stating something as absolute fact, the writer presents it as a conclusion that can be drawn, a possibility that merits consideration, or a position that is defensible but open to challenge.

### Formation

| Pattern | Function | English equivalent |
|---|---|---|
| ～と考えられる | "It can be considered that" | potential passive — invites agreement |
| ～と言えよう | "One could say that" | classical volitional — literary hedging |
| ～ではないだろうか | "Is it not the case that?" | rhetorical question as soft assertion |
| ～と言っても過言ではない | "It is no exaggeration to say" | strong claim in hedged form |
| ～可能性がある | "There is a possibility that" | explicit uncertainty |
| ～傾向がある / 見られる | "There is a tendency / it can be observed" | describing patterns without overstating |

### ～と考えられる

This is the most common academic hedging expression. The potential passive of 考える ("to consider") creates a form meaning "it can be considered" — presenting a conclusion as something reasonable people would arrive at, rather than as the writer's personal assertion.

### Examples

> 以上の　結果から、ストレスが　学業成績に　影響を　与えている　**と考えられる**。
> "From the above results, it can be considered that stress is affecting academic performance."

> この　現象は　経済的要因だけでなく、文化的要因も　関係している　**と考えられる**。
> "It can be considered that this phenomenon is related not only to economic factors but also to cultural factors."

### ～と言えよう

言えよう uses the classical volitional form of 言える ("to be able to say"). The ～よう ending (from the classical auxiliary む) gives it a literary, speculative tone: "one might say" or "it could be said."

### Examples

> この点において、日本の　教育制度は　独自の　発展を　遂げてきた　**と言えよう**。
> "In this regard, it could be said that Japan's educational system has achieved unique development."

> 少子化対策が　十分な　効果を　上げていない　ことは　明らかで　**あろう**。
> "It is probably clear that measures against the declining birthrate have not produced sufficient results."

Note: であろう is the literary form of でしょう and frequently appears in academic prose as a milder assertion.

### ～ではないだろうか

This rhetorical question is one of the most characteristic patterns of Japanese academic writing. By phrasing an assertion as a question ("Is it not the case that...?"), the writer presents a strong claim while maintaining an appearance of intellectual modesty.

### Examples

> 従来の　研究には　方法論的な　問題が　ある　**のではないだろうか**。
> "Is it not the case that previous research has methodological problems?"

> このような　結果が　得られたのは、調査方法に　原因が　ある　**のではないか**　と考える。
> "I consider that the reason such results were obtained may lie in the survey methodology."

### ～と言っても過言ではない

This expression asserts something strongly by denying that it would be an exaggeration: "It is no exaggeration to say that..." It is used when the writer wants to make a bold claim but still wants to sound measured.

### Examples

> グローバル化が　現代社会の　あらゆる　側面に　影響を　及ぼしている　**と言っても　過言ではない**。
> "It is no exaggeration to say that globalization is affecting every aspect of modern society."

> この　研究が　当該分野の　基礎を　築いた　**と言っても　過言ではない**だろう。
> "It would not be an exaggeration to say that this research laid the foundation of the field."

## 13.2 Formal Transitions

Academic writing requires clear signposting to guide the reader through an argument. Japanese academic prose uses a specific set of transitional expressions.

### Formation

| Function | Expression | English |
|---|---|---|
| Ordering points | 第一に…第二に…第三に | First... Second... Third... |
| Ordering points | まず…次に…さらに…最後に | First... Next... Furthermore... Finally... |
| Referring back | 前述の通り / 上述のように | As mentioned above |
| Referring forward | 後述する / 下記の通り | As will be discussed below / As shown below |
| Concluding | 以上のことから / 以上を踏まえて | Based on the above |
| Adding | さらに / 加えて / その上 | Furthermore / In addition / Moreover |
| Contrasting | 一方 / 他方 / これに対して | On the other hand / In contrast |
| Conceding | 確かに…しかし | Certainly... however |
| Summarizing | 要するに / つまり / すなわち | In short / That is to say / Namely |

### Examples

> **第一に**、先行研究の　問題点を　整理する。**第二に**、新たな　仮説を　提示する。**第三に**、データ分析の　結果を　示す。
> "First, we organize the problems in previous research. Second, we present a new hypothesis. Third, we show the results of data analysis."

> **前述の通り**、この分野の　研究は　一九九〇年代から　本格化した。
> "As mentioned above, research in this field began in earnest in the 1990s."

> **以上の　ことから**、従来の　モデルには　限界が　あると　結論づけることが　できる。
> "Based on the above, we can conclude that the conventional model has limitations."

> 詳細については　**後述する**が、この点は　本稿の　中心的な　議論に　関わる。
> "The details will be discussed later, but this point is related to the central argument of this paper."

### Paragraph-level structure

In Japanese academic writing, a common paragraph structure is:

1. Topic sentence (主張)
2. Evidence or explanation (根拠)
3. Concession (確かに…)
4. Counter-argument (しかし…)
5. Restatement of claim (したがって…)

This structure, sometimes called 確かに…しかし… construction, is extremely common:

> **確かに**、経済成長は　国民の　生活水準を　向上させてきた。**しかし**、成長の　恩恵が　すべての　層に　均等に　行き渡っているとは　**言い難い**。
> "Certainly, economic growth has raised the standard of living. However, it is hard to say that the benefits of growth have reached all segments equally."

## 13.3 Contrastive は in Academic Writing

The particle は has a contrastive function that becomes especially prominent in academic writing. When は marks a noun that could be marked with が, it often signals that the writer is setting up a comparison — explicitly or implicitly.

### Formation

The pattern often takes the form: "A は X. B は Y." By marking both A and B with は, the writer signals that they are being compared.

### Examples

> 英語**は**　主語が　必須である。日本語**は**　主語を　省略することが　多い。
> "In English, a subject is required. In Japanese, the subject is frequently omitted."

> 量的研究**は**　数値データに　基づく。質的研究**は**　インタビューや　観察に　基づく。
> "Quantitative research is based on numerical data. Qualitative research is based on interviews and observation."

> 先行研究**は**　主に　都市部のデータを　分析しているが、本研究**は**　農村部にも　焦点を　当てる。
> "Previous research primarily analyzes urban data, but this study also focuses on rural areas."

### Implicit contrast

Sometimes only one は appears, but the contrast is implied:

> 経済的要因**は**　多くの　研究で　指摘されている。
> "Economic factors have been pointed out in many studies." (Implied: but other factors have not been sufficiently discussed.)

This implicit contrast is what makes は so powerful in academic prose. A reader familiar with the convention understands that the writer is preparing to introduce a contrasting point.

## 13.4 Distanced Assertion: ～ものと思われる / ～と推測される

Academic writers frequently present their conclusions not as personal opinions but as logical deductions that any reasonable person would make. This is achieved through passive and impersonal constructions.

### Formation

| Pattern | Function | Formality |
|---|---|---|
| ～と思われる | "It seems that, it is thought that" | Standard academic |
| ～ものと思われる | "It is thought to be the case that" | More formal, more distanced |
| ～と推測される | "It can be inferred/speculated that" | Explicitly speculative |
| ～と推察される | "It can be surmised that" | Formal, slightly literary |
| ～と判断される | "It is judged that" | Stronger, near-conclusive |

### ～ものと思われる

The addition of もの ("thing, fact") before と思われる adds a layer of objectivity. It frames the claim as an objective fact that happens to be perceived, rather than a personal impression.

### Examples

> この　変化は　社会構造の　転換に　起因する　**ものと思われる**。
> "This change is thought to originate from the transformation of social structure."

> 調査対象者の　回答には　一定の　偏りが　ある　**ものと推測される**。
> "It can be inferred that there is a certain bias in the respondents' answers."

> 今後も　この　傾向が　続く　**ものと判断される**。
> "It is judged that this trend will continue going forward."

### ～と推測される vs ～と考えられる

Both present conclusions cautiously, but と推測される implies that the conclusion is based on indirect evidence or inference, while と考えられる suggests a more established line of reasoning.

> データの　欠損は　機器の　不具合に　よる　**と推測される**。
> "It is inferred that the data loss was caused by equipment malfunction." (We cannot confirm this directly.)

> データの　欠損が　結果に　影響を　与えた　**と考えられる**。
> "It can be considered that the data loss affected the results." (This is a reasonable conclusion from the evidence.)

## 13.5 にほかならない and と言わざるを得ない

These two expressions represent the stronger end of the academic assertion spectrum. They are used when the writer wants to make a forceful claim — stronger than a hedge, but still cast in a formal frame.

### にほかならない — "is nothing other than"

This expression, covered in Chapter 9, takes on special importance in academic writing as a way of identifying the essential nature of something.

### Formation

Noun / clause + にほかならない

### Examples

> この　問題の　本質は、制度的な　矛盾に　**ほかならない**。
> "The essence of this problem is nothing other than an institutional contradiction."

> 教育の　目的が　単なる　知識の　伝達に　とどまるべきでないことは、言うまでも　ない。教育とは　人間形成に　**ほかならない**のである。
> "It goes without saying that the purpose of education should not stop at the mere transmission of knowledge. Education is nothing other than the formation of human character."

### と言わざるを得ない — "one must say that, one is compelled to say that"

This expression combines the classical negative ざる (= ない) with を得ない ("cannot obtain/achieve"), creating "cannot not say" = "must say." It expresses a reluctant but unavoidable conclusion.

### Formation

Clause + と言わざるを得ない

### Examples

> 現行の　政策には　問題が　ある　**と言わざるを得ない**。
> "One must say that the current policy has problems."

> この　結果は　予想に　反しており、従来の　理論では　説明が　困難で　ある　**と言わざるを得ない**。
> "This result is contrary to expectations, and one must say that it is difficult to explain with conventional theory."

> 十年以上にわたる　取り組みにもかかわらず、目立った　成果が　見られないのは　残念だ　**と言わざるを得ない**。
> "Despite more than ten years of effort, one must reluctantly say that no notable results can be seen."

### The tone of と言わざるを得ない

This expression carries a tone of reluctance — the writer would prefer not to have to say this, but the evidence compels them. It is often used in critical evaluations and policy assessments.

## 13.6 Reading Passage

次の文章は、ある社会学の論文の序論部分です。

---

近年、日本の労働市場における非正規雇用者の割合は増加の一途をたどっている。総務省の調査によると、二〇二三年の時点で非正規雇用者は労働者全体の約三七パーセントを占めており、この数字はこの二十年間でほぼ倍増したことになる。

従来、非正規雇用の問題は主に経済学的な視点から論じられてきた。すなわち、賃金格差や社会保障の不備といった観点からの分析が中心であった。確かに、こうした経済的側面は極めて重要であり、多くの有益な知見が蓄積されてきたと言えよう。しかし、非正規雇用が労働者の社会的アイデンティティや人間関係に与える影響については、十分な研究がなされてきたとは言い難い。

本稿では、非正規雇用者の「社会的孤立」に焦点を当てる。先行研究は主に経済的不安定性に注目してきたが、本研究は非正規雇用がもたらす社会関係の希薄化という側面に着目する。正規雇用者は職場を通じて社会的ネットワークを構築する機会を得やすいのに対し、非正規雇用者はそうした機会が限定されている可能性がある。この点は、非正規雇用問題の本質に関わるものであり、看過すべきではないと考えられる。

第一に、先行研究のレビューを行い、非正規雇用研究における社会学的アプローチの位置づけを明らかにする。第二に、インタビュー調査の方法と対象について述べる。第三に、調査結果を提示し、分析を行う。以上を踏まえて、非正規雇用と社会的孤立の関係について考察を加えたい。

非正規雇用の問題が単なる雇用形態の問題にとどまらず、社会参加や人間の尊厳に関わる問題であることは、言うまでもない。この問題に社会学の立場からアプローチすることの意義は大きいと言わざるを得ない。

---

**Translation:**

The following is the introductory section of a sociology paper.

---

In recent years, the proportion of non-regular workers in Japan's labor market has been on a consistent upward trajectory. According to a Ministry of Internal Affairs survey, as of 2023, non-regular workers accounted for approximately 37 percent of the total workforce, meaning this figure has nearly doubled over the past twenty years.

Traditionally, the problem of non-regular employment has been discussed primarily from an economic perspective. That is, analysis centered on aspects such as wage gaps and deficiencies in social security has been predominant. Certainly, these economic dimensions are extremely important, and it could be said that much useful knowledge has been accumulated. However, it is hard to say that sufficient research has been conducted on the impact of non-regular employment on workers' social identity and interpersonal relationships.

This paper focuses on the "social isolation" of non-regular workers. While previous research has primarily focused on economic instability, this study examines the aspect of weakening social ties brought about by non-regular employment. Regular employees tend to have greater opportunities to build social networks through the workplace, whereas non-regular employees may have limited access to such opportunities. This point is related to the essence of the non-regular employment problem and should not be overlooked.

First, we conduct a review of previous research and clarify the position of sociological approaches in non-regular employment studies. Second, we describe the methods and subjects of the interview survey. Third, we present the survey results and conduct analysis. Based on the above, we wish to add a discussion on the relationship between non-regular employment and social isolation.

It goes without saying that the problem of non-regular employment is not merely a matter of employment type, but a matter concerning social participation and human dignity. One must say that the significance of approaching this problem from a sociological standpoint is substantial.
