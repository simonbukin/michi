# Chapter 16 — The Onomatopoeia System

Japanese onomatopoeia is not what English speakers expect it to be. In English, onomatopoeia is a minor stylistic device — words like "buzz," "splash," and "crunch" that imitate sounds. They are optional. You can always rephrase without them.

In Japanese, onomatopoeia is a core part of the language. It carries meanings that no other word can replace. A doctor will ask a patient to describe their pain using onomatopoeia (ずきずきしますか？ちくちくしますか？). A weather report will use しとしと for light rain and ざあざあ for heavy rain. A novelist will write ぐっすり眠った rather than 深く眠った because ぐっすり conveys something that 深く does not — not just depth of sleep but the particular quality of sound, satisfying unconsciousness. Manga without onomatopoeia would be incomprehensible.

This chapter covers the system — the categories, the script conventions, and the grammatical behavior. The next chapter provides an extensive domain-by-domain reference of over 100 individual onomatopoeia.

## 16.1 Why a Full Chapter

Learners of Japanese often treat onomatopoeia as curiosities to be memorized one at a time. This is a mistake. Japanese onomatopoeia forms a system with predictable patterns, and understanding the system makes individual words easier to learn and use.

Consider the following:

| Onomatopoeia | Meaning |
|---|---|
| さらさら | smooth and dry (flowing hair, sand, stream) |
| ざらざら | rough, coarse (sandpaper, unshaven skin) |

The difference is a single voicing mark (濁点). The voiced version (ざらざら) is heavier, rougher, more intense. This is not a coincidence — it is a systematic pattern throughout Japanese onomatopoeia.

| Voiceless | Voiced | Pattern |
|---|---|---|
| さらさら (smooth) | ざらざら (rough) | Voicing adds roughness/heaviness |
| とんとん (light knocking) | どんどん (heavy pounding) | Voicing adds force |
| ぱたぱた (light flapping) | ばたばた (frantic commotion) | Voicing adds intensity |
| ころころ (light rolling) | ごろごろ (heavy rolling/rumbling) | Voicing adds weight |
| ぴかぴか (sparkling) | びかびか (not standard) | Pattern has limits |

Other systematic features:

- **Reduplication** (ABAB pattern): indicates continuation or repetition (どきどき, きらきら, ぐるぐる)
- **っ (small tsu)**: indicates a sudden, sharp action (ぱっと, ぐっと, はっと)
- **ん**: adds weight or resonance (どんどん, がんがん, ぽんぽん)
- **り ending**: softens the action, makes it gentler or more gradual (ぐったり, のんびり, うっかり)

Understanding these patterns means that when you encounter an unfamiliar onomatopoeia, you can often guess its approximate meaning from its sound structure.

## 16.2 Three Categories

Japanese linguistics traditionally divides onomatopoeia (オノマトペ) into three categories. In practice, the boundaries blur, but the categories are useful for understanding what kind of meaning each word carries.

### 擬音語 (ぎおんご) — Sound-imitating words

These imitate actual sounds in the physical world. They are closest to what English speakers think of as onomatopoeia.

| 擬音語 | Sound represented |
|---|---|
| わんわん | dog barking |
| にゃあにゃあ | cat meowing |
| ざあざあ | heavy rain |
| がたがた | rattling |
| ばたん | door slamming |
| ぴんぽん | doorbell |
| ごくごく | gulping (drinking) |
| ぱちぱち | clapping, crackling |

### 擬態語 (ぎたいご) — State/manner-describing words

These describe states, textures, manners of action, or visual appearances. They do not represent sounds — they represent how something looks, feels, or behaves. This category is uniquely developed in Japanese and has no real parallel in English.

| 擬態語 | State or manner described |
|---|---|
| きらきら | sparkling, glittering |
| つるつる | smooth, slippery |
| ふわふわ | fluffy, soft, floating |
| べたべた | sticky, clingy |
| のろのろ | sluggishly, slowly |
| すらすら | smoothly, fluently |
| ぐるぐる | spinning around and around |
| じめじめ | damp, humid, gloomy |

### 擬情語 (ぎじょうご) — Feeling-describing words

These describe internal emotional or psychological states. Some linguists classify them as a subcategory of 擬態語, but they are worth distinguishing because they describe subjective experience rather than observable states.

| 擬情語 | Feeling described |
|---|---|
| どきどき | heart pounding (excitement, nervousness) |
| わくわく | excited anticipation |
| いらいら | irritation, frustration |
| うんざり | fed up, disgusted |
| ほっと | relieved |
| がっかり | disappointed |
| ぞくぞく | chills (fear or excitement) |
| しみじみ | deeply, with quiet emotion |

### Overlap between categories

Some onomatopoeia span categories depending on context:

> ごろごろ — 擬音語 when describing thunder (the sound of rolling), 擬態語 when describing someone lazing around (the manner of lying about doing nothing)

> ぞくぞく — 擬情語 when describing a thrill of excitement, but also 擬態語 when describing physical chills from cold or fever

## 16.3 Hiragana vs Katakana

Japanese onomatopoeia can be written in either hiragana or katakana, and the choice of script carries meaning.

### General tendencies

| Script | Tendency | Example |
|---|---|---|
| ひらがな | Softer, warmer, more organic, literary | ふわふわ, しとしと, のんびり |
| カタカナ | Sharper, colder, more mechanical, manga-style | ガタガタ, バタン, ドカン |

### When katakana is preferred

- **Loud, sudden, or violent sounds**: ドカン (explosion), ガシャン (crash), バキッ (crack/snap)
- **Mechanical or artificial sounds**: ピピッ (electronic beep), ガチャ (lock clicking), ブーン (motor humming)
- **Manga sound effects**: Nearly all onomatopoeia in manga panels are written in katakana
- **Emphasis or stylistic forcefulness**: A writer might use カチカチ instead of かちかち to emphasize the hardness or tension

### When hiragana is preferred

- **Gentle or quiet states**: しとしと (gentle rain), ほんわか (warm and fuzzy), そよそよ (gentle breeze)
- **Emotional or psychological states**: どきどき, わくわく, いらいら (though katakana is also possible)
- **Literary prose**: Novels and essays tend to prefer hiragana for onomatopoeia to maintain a softer visual texture
- **Everyday casual writing**: Conversational onomatopoeia tends toward hiragana

### The same word in both scripts

> ふわふわした布団で寝た。
> "I slept in a fluffy futon." (Soft, cozy feeling)

> フワフワした食感のケーキだ。
> "It's a cake with a fluffy texture." (Slightly more analytical, food-review style)

The katakana version can feel slightly more detached or descriptive, while the hiragana version feels more experiential. This is a tendency, not a rule — writers make individual choices.

## 16.4 Grammatical Behavior

Onomatopoeia in Japanese are not grammatically uniform. They function in several distinct ways depending on what follows them.

### と — Adverbial use

The most common grammatical pattern for onomatopoeia. The particle と attaches the onomatopoeia to a verb, functioning like an adverb.

> 雨がざあざあと降っている。
> "Rain is falling heavily." (ざあざあと modifies 降る)

> 彼女はにこにこと笑った。
> "She smiled with a beaming face." (にこにこと modifies 笑う)

> 心臓がどきどきと鳴っている。
> "My heart is pounding." (どきどきと modifies 鳴る)

> 子どもたちがきゃっきゃっと遊んでいる。
> "The children are playing with squeals of delight."

Note: と is sometimes omitted in casual speech and writing:

> 雨がざあざあ降っている。

This omission is especially common with very familiar onomatopoeia.

### する — Verbal use

Many onomatopoeia can attach to する to form verbs. This is especially common with 擬態語 and 擬情語.

> どきどきする。
> "I'm nervous / My heart is pounding."

> いらいらする。
> "I'm irritated."

> ふわふわしたパンが好きだ。
> "I like fluffy bread." (ふわふわする → ふわふわした as modifier)

> 頭がずきずきする。
> "My head is throbbing."

When onomatopoeia + する is conjugated (した, している, して), it behaves like any other する-verb.

### な / の — Adjectival use

Some onomatopoeia can modify nouns directly using な (like な-adjectives) or の.

> ふわふわな毛布
> "A fluffy blanket"

> ぴかぴかの靴
> "Shiny shoes"

> べたべたな手
> "Sticky hands"

> きらきらの星
> "Sparkling stars"

The choice between な and の is not always predictable. の tends to be used when the onomatopoeia describes a state the noun is in (ぴかぴかの — in a sparkling state), while な tends to be used when the onomatopoeia describes a quality of the noun. In practice, many onomatopoeia accept both.

### Standalone use

In casual speech, manga, and some literary prose, onomatopoeia can stand alone without any grammatical attachment.

> 「お腹空いた？」「ぺこぺこ。」
> "Are you hungry?" "Starving."

> ドアが開いた。ばたん。
> "The door opened. Slam."

> 外を見ると——しーん。誰もいなかった。
> "I looked outside — dead silence. No one was there."

### Summary table

| Pattern | Function | Example |
|---|---|---|
| ～と + verb | Adverb (modifies action) | にこにこと笑う |
| ～(と) + verb | Adverb (と omitted) | にこにこ笑う |
| ～する | Verb (describes state/action) | どきどきする |
| ～した + noun | Past/stative modifier | ふわふわした布団 |
| ～な + noun | Adjectival modifier | べたべたな手 |
| ～の + noun | Adjectival modifier | ぴかぴかの靴 |
| Standalone | Independent expression | ばたん。しーん。 |

## 16.5 Sound Symbolism Patterns

Beyond voicing, Japanese onomatopoeia contains broader sound-symbolic patterns that help learners predict meaning from form.

### Vowel patterns

| Vowel | Tendency | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| あ (a) | Open, large, expansive | ばあっと (spreading out), ぱあっと (opening wide) |
| い (i) | Small, thin, sharp | ちくちく (pricking), ぴりぴり (stinging) |
| う (u) | Heavy, gloomy, inward | じめじめ (damp), ぐずぐず (sluggish/whiny) |
| お (o) | Large, heavy, resonant | ごろごろ (rumbling), どかどか (stomping in) |

### Consonant patterns

| Consonant | Tendency | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| p- | Light, small, popping | ぱちぱち, ぽんぽん, ぴかぴか |
| b- | Heavy, forceful (voiced p-) | ばたばた, ぼろぼろ, びしょびしょ |
| k- | Hard, dry, crisp | かりかり, こつこつ, かちかち |
| g- | Heavy, rough (voiced k-) | がりがり, ごつごつ, がちがち |
| s- | Smooth, quiet, flowing | さらさら, すらすら, しとしと |
| z- | Rough, buzzing (voiced s-) | ざらざら, ずるずる, じめじめ |
| t- | Light tapping, precise | とんとん, たらたら, ちょこちょこ |
| d- | Heavy impact (voiced t-) | どんどん, だらだら, どかどか |
| n- | Soft, slow, sticky | ぬるぬる, ねばねば, のろのろ |

These are tendencies, not rules. But they are strong enough that native speakers often agree on the "feeling" of a nonsense onomatopoeia based on its sound structure alone.

## 16.6 Reading Passage

次の文章は、日本語学の授業でオノマトペの役割について書かれたエッセイです。

---

日本語を学ぶ外国人の多くは、オノマトペの多さに驚く。英語にも "buzz" や "splash" のような擬音語はあるが、日本語ほど日常生活のあらゆる場面で使われることはない。

日本語のオノマトペが特殊なのは、音の模倣だけでなく、目に見える状態や、心の中の感情までを「音」として表現する点にある。<ruby>例<rt>たと</rt></ruby>えば「きらきら」は実際には音を出さない。光の様子を、あたかも音であるかのように描写しているのだ。「わくわく」も同様で、期待感という目に見えない感情に、音の形を与えている。

こうした表現が可能なのは、日本語の音韻体系に「音象徴」と呼ばれる仕組みがあるからだとされている。濁音は重さや荒さを、半濁音は軽さや小ささを、「っ」は瞬間的な動きを、「ん」は響きや余韻をそれぞれ表す傾向がある。「さらさら」と「ざらざら」の違いを日本語話者に聞けば、誰もが前者は滑らかで、後者はざらついていると答えるだろう。

オノマトペなしの日本語は、色のない絵のようなものだと言えよう。

---

**Translation:**

Many foreigners studying Japanese are surprised by the sheer number of onomatopoeia. English has sound-imitating words like "buzz" and "splash" too, but they are not used in every aspect of daily life the way Japanese onomatopoeia are.

What makes Japanese onomatopoeia special is that they represent not only the imitation of sounds but also visible states and emotions within the heart — all expressed as "sound." For example, "kirakira" does not actually make a noise. It describes the appearance of light as though it were a sound. "Wakuwaku" is similar — it gives a sound-form to the invisible emotion of anticipation.

Such expression is possible, it is said, because the Japanese phonological system has a mechanism called "sound symbolism." Voiced consonants tend to represent heaviness and roughness, semi-voiced consonants lightness and smallness, the small "tsu" represents momentary movement, and "n" represents resonance and lingering. If you ask a Japanese speaker about the difference between "sarasara" and "zarazara," everyone would answer that the former is smooth and the latter is rough.

Japanese without onomatopoeia, one could say, is like a picture without color.
