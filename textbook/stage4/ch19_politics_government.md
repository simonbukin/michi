# Chapter 19 — Politics and Government

Understanding Japanese political news requires a specific vocabulary set that rarely appears in everyday conversation or even in general news articles. This chapter equips you with the terminology needed to read political reporting, follow election coverage, and understand discussions about constitutional issues.

Japan's political system has its own structural vocabulary — terms for the two houses of parliament, the cabinet system, the relationship between ruling and opposition parties, and the legislative process. Much of this vocabulary appears in Chinese-character compounds (漢語) that are transparent in meaning once you know the individual characters. This chapter organizes the vocabulary systematically so that you can build on what you already know.

The reading passage at the end is a political news article covering an election and its aftermath. It integrates vocabulary from all three sections of this chapter.

## 19.1 Government Structure

### The National Diet (国会)

Japan's parliament is called the 国会 (こっかい, National Diet). It consists of two chambers.

| 単語 | 読み | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| 国会 | こっかい | National Diet (parliament) |
| 衆議院 | しゅうぎいん | House of Representatives (lower house) |
| 参議院 | さんぎいん | House of Councillors (upper house) |
| 議員 | ぎいん | member of parliament, legislator |
| 衆議院議員 | しゅうぎいんぎいん | member of the House of Representatives |
| 参議院議員 | さんぎいんぎいん | member of the House of Councillors |
| 議長 | ぎちょう | speaker (of the house), chairperson |
| 国会議事堂 | こっかいぎじどう | National Diet Building |
| 通常国会 | つうじょうこっかい | ordinary session of the Diet |
| 臨時国会 | りんじこっかい | extraordinary session of the Diet |

The 衆議院 is considered the more powerful chamber. It has the power to override decisions of the 参議院 with a two-thirds majority, and the prime minister is typically the leader of the majority party in the 衆議院.

> 衆議院が解散され、総選挙が行われることになった。
> "The House of Representatives was dissolved, and a general election will be held."

## 19.3 Constitutional Vocabulary

Japan's constitution (日本国憲法) was enacted in 1947 and has never been amended, though the debate over constitutional revision (改憲) is one of the central issues in Japanese politics. Understanding the vocabulary around this debate is essential for following political news.

### Core terms

| 単語 | 読み | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| 憲法 | けんぽう | constitution |
| 日本国憲法 | にほんこくけんぽう | Constitution of Japan |
| 改正 | かいせい | amendment, revision |
| 改憲 | かいけん | constitutional revision |
| 護憲 | ごけん | defending the constitution |
| 条文 | じょうぶん | article, clause (of a law) |
| 条 | じょう | article number (第九条 = Article 9) |
| 項 | こう | paragraph/subsection of an article |

| 義務 | ぎむ | duty, obligation |

### Article 9 and the peace clause

Article 9 of the Japanese constitution renounces war and prohibits the maintenance of military forces. The debate over its interpretation and possible revision is a defining issue in Japanese politics.

> 第九条は戦争の放棄と戦力の不保持を定めている。
> "Article 9 stipulates the renunciation of war and the non-maintenance of military forces."

| 単語 | 読み | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| 戦争 | せんそう | war |
| 放棄 | ほうき | renunciation, abandonment |
| 戦力 | せんりょく | military force |
| 自衛隊 | じえいたい | Self-Defense Forces |
| 集団的自衛権 | しゅうだんてきじえいけん | right of collective self-defense |
| 平和主義 | へいわしゅぎ | pacifism |
| 安全保障 | あんぜんほしょう | national security |

> 憲法改正の是非をめぐって国民的な議論が続いている。
> "A national debate continues over the question of whether to revise the constitution."

### Constitutional process

To amend the constitution, a proposal must be approved by a two-thirds majority of both houses of the Diet, followed by a national referendum.

| 単語 | 読み | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| 発議 | はつぎ | proposal, initiative (for amendment) |
| 国民投票 | こくみんとうひょう | national referendum |
| 過半数 | かはんすう | majority |
| 三分の二 | さんぶんのに | two-thirds |
| 承認 | しょうにん | approval, ratification |

> 憲法改正には、両院の三分の二以上の賛成と国民投票での過半数の承認が必要だ。
> "Constitutional amendment requires the approval of two-thirds or more of both houses and a majority in a national referendum."

## 19.4 Reading Passage

次の文章は、衆議院選挙の結果を報じるニュース記事です。

---

衆議院選挙は二十七日に投開票が行われ、与党の民主自由党が単独で過半数の議席を確保し、政権を維持することが確実となった。民主自由党は小選挙区と比例代表を合わせて二百六十議席を獲得し、改選前の二百四十五議席から上積みした。

一方、最大野党の国民進歩党は百二十議席にとどまり、改選前から二十議席減らす結果となった。佐藤党首は「厳しい結果を真摯に受け止めなければならない」と述べ、党の立て直しを図る考えを示した。

投票率は五十三・七パーセントで、前回選挙の五十五・九パーセントをやや下回った。特に二十代の投票率は三十パーセント台にとどまっており、若年層の政治離れが改めて浮き彫りとなった。

選挙の最大の争点は経済政策と安全保障であった。与党は「景気回復の成果を国民に届ける」と訴え、消費税率の据え置きと規制緩和による成長戦略を掲げた。野党は「格差是正と社会保障の充実」を主張し、法人税の引き上げと教育無償化を公約に掲げたが、政権交代への期待は高まらなかった。

田中首相は選挙結果を受けて記者会見を行い、「国民の信任を得た以上、掲げた政策を着実に実行していく」と語った。また、来年の通常国会で憲法改正の発議を目指す考えを改めて表明し、野党との協議に前向きな姿勢を示した。

政治評論家の中には、「与党の勝利は政策への支持というよりも、野党への失望の裏返しだ」との分析もあり、今後の国会運営が注目される。

---

**Translation:**

Voting and ballot counting for the House of Representatives election took place on the twenty-seventh, and it became certain that the ruling Democratic Liberal Party would secure a majority of seats on its own and maintain power. The Democratic Liberal Party won 260 seats combining single-seat constituencies and proportional representation, adding to its pre-election total of 245 seats.

Meanwhile, the largest opposition party, the National Progressive Party, managed only 120 seats, losing 20 seats from its pre-election total. Party leader Sato stated, "We must sincerely accept this harsh result," and indicated his intention to work on rebuilding the party.

Voter turnout was 53.7 percent, slightly below the 55.9 percent of the previous election. In particular, turnout among voters in their twenties remained in the thirty-percent range, once again highlighting the disengagement of young people from politics.

The biggest issues in the election were economic policy and national security. The ruling party appealed to voters with the message of "delivering the fruits of economic recovery to the people" and campaigned on maintaining the consumption tax rate and a growth strategy through deregulation. The opposition advocated "correcting inequality and strengthening social security," pledging corporate tax increases and free education, but expectations for a change of government did not rise.

Prime Minister Tanaka held a press conference in response to the election results and said, "Having received the trust of the people, we will steadily implement the policies we campaigned on." He also reiterated his intention to aim for a constitutional amendment proposal in next year's ordinary Diet session and showed a positive attitude toward consultations with the opposition.

Among political commentators, there are analyses suggesting that "the ruling party's victory is less a sign of support for its policies than the flip side of disappointment with the opposition," and attention is focused on how the Diet will be managed going forward.
