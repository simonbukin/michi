# Chapter 23 — Ellipsis, Zero Pronouns, and Recovering Omitted Elements

Japanese is a language that omits what English insists on making explicit. Subjects are routinely dropped. Objects disappear. Even verbs can be left unspoken when the context makes them obvious. This is not laziness or ambiguity for its own sake. It is a fundamental feature of how Japanese encodes information: what is already known or recoverable from context does not need to be stated.

For learners who come from languages like English, where "he," "she," "it," and "they" fill every sentence, Japanese ellipsis can be disorienting. You read a paragraph and realize no one has been named as the subject for five consecutive sentences. Who is doing what? The answer is almost always recoverable, but you need to know where to look.

This chapter teaches you the principles behind Japanese ellipsis, gives you strategies for tracking referents across sentences and paragraphs, explains the role of は in maintaining topic continuity, and examines cases where ambiguity is not a problem to be solved but a deliberate communicative choice.

---

## 23.1 What Gets Dropped and Why

Japanese drops any element that is recoverable from context. The most commonly omitted elements are:

### Subjects (zero pronouns)

The subject is the most frequently omitted element. In conversation, if both speakers know who is being discussed, the subject simply does not appear.

> A：昨日どこに行った？
> B：映画を見に行った。
> A: "Where did you go yesterday?"
> B: "(I) went to see a movie."

In English, "I" is required. In Japanese, it is not only unnecessary but would sound slightly unnatural if included.

### Objects

Objects are dropped when the referent is obvious.

> A：この本、読んだ？
> B：うん、もう読んだ。<ruby>面白<rt>おもしろ</rt></ruby>かったよ。
> A: "Did you read this book?"
> B: "Yeah, (I) already read (it). (It) was interesting."

### Verbs

Even verbs can be omitted in responses.

> A：コーヒーにする？紅茶にする？
> B：コーヒーに。
> A: "Will you have coffee? Or tea?"
> B: "Coffee." (The verb する is understood.)

### Multiple omissions in sequence

In natural Japanese, several elements may be missing from a single sentence.

> A：明日のパーティー、行く？
> B：行きたいけど、ちょっと。
> A: "Are (you) going to tomorrow's party?"
> B: "(I) want to go, but (it's) a little (difficult)."

B's response omits the subject, the object of difficulty, and the adjective or verb that would complete the thought. Everything is understood.

## 23.2 Identifying Who and What Is Being Talked About

When reading Japanese text, especially longer passages, you need strategies for tracking who the subject is and what is being discussed. Here are the key principles.

### Principle 1: The は-marked topic continues until replaced

Once a topic is established with は, it remains the implicit subject (or topic of discussion) until a new は-marked element appears.

> 田中さんは大学で日本文学を教えている。毎朝六時に起きて、通勤電車の中で本を読む。帰宅後は論文の執筆に取りかかることが多い。
> "Tanaka teaches Japanese literature at a university. (He) wakes up at six every morning and reads books on the commuter train. After returning home, (he) often sets to work on writing papers."

Every sentence after the first has 田中さん as its implicit subject. No pronoun is needed.

### Principle 2: が marks a new or contrasted subject

When が appears, it typically introduces a new subject or highlights who specifically performed an action.

> 田中さんは部屋で本を読んでいた。そこに、山田さんが入ってきた。
> "Tanaka was reading a book in the room. Then Yamada came in."

The が on 山田さん signals a new actor on the scene. After this point, you need to track which person each subsequent sentence refers to.

### Principle 3: Honorific and humble forms mark the referent

In passages using keigo, the form of the verb tells you who the subject is.

> 部長がお見えになった。プロジェクトについてご説明申し上げた。
> "The department head arrived (honorific = he came). (I) explained about the project to (him) (humble = I explained)."

The honorific お見えになる marks the department head as subject. The humble 申し上げる marks the speaker as subject.

### Principle 4: Perspective and emotional language indicate the viewpoint character

In narrative fiction, words expressing inner experience (思う, 感じる, 気がする, as well as expressions like だろう, かもしれない used without attribution) tell you whose perspective the passage is in.

> 彼女は窓の外を見ていた。何を考えているのだろう。
> "(She) was looking out the window. What could (she) be thinking?"

The second sentence has no explicit subject, but だろう signals someone's internal wondering. Since the passage is about a character observing her, the implicit subject of だろう is the observer, not her.

## 23.3 Tracking Referents Across Paragraphs

Longer texts require you to track referents over many sentences. The role of は, the introduction of new subjects with が, and paragraph boundaries all provide clues.

### Practice Passage 1

Read the following passage and identify who is the subject of each underlined verb.

---

鈴木さんは朝から忙しかった。①会議の資料を準備し、②メールを何通も送った。昼休みになっても③食堂に行く時間がなかった。

午後になると、上司の高橋部長が④声をかけてきた。「鈴木くん、⑤今日のプレゼンの件だが」と⑥切り出した。⑦緊張が走った。

「⑧よくまとめてあるね。⑨感心したよ」

⑩ほっとした。⑪何日もかけて準備した甲斐があったと⑫思った。

---

**Referent tracking:**

| Number | Verb | Subject | How you know |
|--------|------|---------|-------------|
| ① | 準備し | 鈴木 | は-topic continues |
| ② | 送った | 鈴木 | は-topic continues |
| ③ | 行く時間がなかった | 鈴木 | は-topic continues |
| ④ | 声をかけてきた | 高橋部長 | が marks new subject |
| ⑤ | (utterance) | 高橋部長 | direct speech follows が-subject |
| ⑥ | 切り出した | 高橋部長 | continues from ④ |
| ⑦ | 緊張が走った | 鈴木 | emotional reaction = viewpoint character |
| ⑧ | まとめてある | 鈴木 (as agent) | 高橋 is praising 鈴木's work |
| ⑨ | 感心した | 高橋部長 | direct speech by 高橋 |
| ⑩ | ほっとした | 鈴木 | emotional reaction = viewpoint character |
| ⑪ | 準備した | 鈴木 | agent of the preparation |
| ⑫ | 思った | 鈴木 | viewpoint character's thought |

### Practice Passage 2

---

美咲は図書館で勉強していた。隣の席に男の子が座った。教科書を広げたが、すぐにスマホを取り出した。動画を見ているらしい。時々小さく笑っている。集中できない。迷惑だと思ったが、何も言えなかった。

しばらくすると、静かになった。見ると、眠っていた。

---

**Referent tracking:**

The passage opens with 美咲 as the は-topic. A boy is introduced with が. From 教科書を広げた through 笑っている, the boy is the subject (actions following his が-introduction). At 集中できない, the subject shifts back to 美咲: this is her inability to concentrate, her viewpoint. 迷惑だと思った is also 美咲 (internal thought). 何も言えなかった is 美咲 (she couldn't say anything).

In the second paragraph, 静かになった has no explicit subject (the environment became quiet). 見ると has 美咲 as subject (she looked). 眠っていた has the boy as subject (he was sleeping).

## 23.4 When Ambiguity Is Intentional

Not all ellipsis is meant to be resolved. Japanese speakers and writers sometimes leave referents deliberately vague, and understanding this is itself a reading skill.

### Conversational vagueness

> ちょっと、あれなんだけど。
> "It's, well, you know, that thing."

This is not a failure of communication. Both speakers understand what あれ refers to, or the speaker is deliberately avoiding naming something embarrassing or sensitive.

### Literary ambiguity

In fiction, deliberate ambiguity about who is speaking or thinking creates a particular aesthetic effect.

> 二人は黙って歩いていた。早く帰りたかった。でも、まだ言わなければならないことがあった。
> "The two of them walked in silence. (Someone) wanted to go home soon. But there was still something that had to be said."

Which of the two wanted to go home? The text does not tell us. This ambiguity is the point: the reader experiences the scene without knowing whose inner world is being revealed. Both characters may feel the same thing. The vagueness is a literary choice.

### Cultural context

Japanese communication often values 察する (reading the air, inferring meaning) over explicit statement. This extends to written language. When a text leaves something unstated, sometimes the appropriate response is not to ask "who?" but to accept that the text is presenting a shared or general feeling.

> 別れは突然だった。涙は出なかった。ただ、胸の奥が重くなった。
> "The parting was sudden. No tears came. Only, deep in the chest, a heaviness settled."

The passage does not say whose tears, whose chest. It could be either person, or both, or the reader. The omission draws the reader into the emotional state rather than assigning it to a specific character.

## 23.5 Practice Exercises

### Exercise 1: Identify all omitted subjects

Read the following passage and write in who the subject of each sentence is.

---

中村さんは新しい職場に慣れてきた。最初は知らない人ばかりで不安だったが、少しずつ同僚と話すようになった。特に隣の席の佐藤さんとは気が合った。昼休みに一緒に食堂に行くことが多くなった。趣味が同じだと分かってからは、休日にも会うようになった。

ある日、佐藤さんが深刻な顔で話しかけてきた。「実は来月で辞めることになった」と言った。驚いた。何と言っていいか分からなかった。

「急に決まったんだ。本当は言いたくなかったけど」

黙ってうなずいた。寂しくなるな、と思った。

---

| Sentence | Omitted subject |

| 少しずつ同僚と話すようになった | 中村 |
| 特に隣の席の佐藤さんとは気が合った | 中村 |
| 昼休みに一緒に食堂に行くことが多くなった | 中村（と佐藤） |
| 趣味が同じだと分かってからは… | 中村（と佐藤） |
| 「実は来月で辞めることになった」と言った | 佐藤 |

| 「急に決まったんだ…」 | 佐藤 |
| 黙ってうなずいた | 中村 |
| 寂しくなるな、と思った | 中村 |

### Exercise 2: Resolve or accept ambiguity

Read the following and decide: can you identify the subject, or is the ambiguity intentional?

> 電話が鳴った。出なかった。また鳴った。やはり出なかった。三回目が鳴り終わると、静かになった。それでよかったのだと、自分に言い聞かせた。

Who didn't answer the phone? The passage does not name the person. But 自分に言い聞かせた (told oneself) confirms there is a single viewpoint character. The omission of their name creates an intimate first-person-like effect without using a first-person pronoun.
