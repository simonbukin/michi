# Chapter 24 — Rhetorical Structure: 起承転結 and Beyond

When you read a Japanese essay, editorial, or opinion piece, you may notice that the main point does not always appear in the first paragraph. Instead, the writer often introduces a topic, develops it with examples, introduces an unexpected angle or complication, and only then states a conclusion. This is not disorganized writing. It follows a rhetorical tradition called 起承転結, a four-part structure borrowed from classical Chinese poetry and deeply embedded in Japanese discourse.

Understanding rhetorical structure makes you a faster and more confident reader. If you know where to expect the main argument, you can skim intelligently. If you know that a 転 (turn) is coming, you will not be surprised when the essay shifts direction. And if you can recognize signposting language, you can follow the logical flow of even dense, unfamiliar texts.

This chapter introduces the 起承転結 pattern, compares it with Western rhetorical conventions, explains why structure awareness improves reading speed, and reviews the signposting language that Japanese writers use to guide their readers.

---

## 24.1 起承転結 — The Four-Part Pattern

起承転結 (きしょうてんけつ) is a four-part structure that organizes many types of Japanese written discourse: essays, editorials, speeches, and even some business presentations.

| Part | Name | Function |
|------|------|----------|
| 起 | き | Introduction: present the topic, set the scene |
| 承 | しょう | Development: elaborate, provide examples, give background |
| 転 | てん | Turn: introduce a new angle, complication, or counterpoint |
| 結 | けつ | Conclusion: state the main point, wrap up |

### How each part works

**起 (Introduction):** The writer opens with a general observation, a question, a personal anecdote, or a description of a situation. The purpose is to draw the reader in and establish what the text will be about.

> 最近、電車の中でスマートフォンを見ている人が増えたように感じる。
> "Recently, I feel that the number of people looking at smartphones on the train has increased."

**承 (Development):** The writer expands on the introduction with data, examples, or further description. This section builds the foundation for the argument.

> 総務省の調査によると、スマートフォンの利用率は全世代で八割を超えている。特に若い世代では、一日の平均利用時間が四時間を超えるという報告もある。
> "According to a survey by the Ministry of Internal Affairs, smartphone usage rates exceed eighty percent across all generations. There are also reports that the average daily usage time exceeds four hours, particularly among younger generations."

**転 (Turn):** The writer introduces a surprising element, a counterargument, or a shift in perspective. This is the most distinctive part of the 起承転結 structure and the one that most surprises readers trained in Western rhetoric.

> しかし、スマートフォンの利用が増えた一方で、人々の読書量が減ったかというと、必ずしもそうとは限らない。電子書籍の普及により、スマートフォンで読書をする人も少なくないのである。
> "However, while smartphone usage has increased, whether people's reading volume has decreased is not necessarily the case. With the spread of e-books, there are not a few people who read on their smartphones."

**結 (Conclusion):** The writer states the main point or draws a conclusion from the preceding discussion. In 起承転結, the conclusion often incorporates the unexpected element from the 転 section.

> 重要なのは、スマートフォンをどう使うかということであろう。道具は使い方次第で、害にも益にもなる。私たちに求められているのは、情報との付き合い方を自覚的に選ぶ姿勢ではないだろうか。
> "What is important is how we use our smartphones. A tool can become either harmful or beneficial depending on how it is used. What is required of us is perhaps an attitude of consciously choosing how we engage with information."

## 24.2 How It Differs from Western Argumentation

English-language academic and journalistic writing typically follows a thesis-first structure:

| Western pattern | Function |
|----------------|----------|
| Thesis statement | State the main argument up front |
| Supporting evidence | Provide reasons and data |
| Counterargument | Address objections |
| Conclusion | Restate and summarize |

In this structure, the reader knows the writer's position from the very first paragraph. Everything that follows supports or defends that position.

The 起承転結 pattern differs in a crucial way: the main point may not appear until the final section. The reader is expected to follow the journey of thought — from observation, through elaboration, through a surprising turn, to the conclusion.

### Practical implications for reading

If you approach a Japanese editorial expecting the thesis in the first paragraph, you may feel lost. The opening may seem vague or tangential. The development may seem to wander. The turn may seem like a contradiction. But if you recognize the 起承転結 pattern, you understand that the writer is building toward a conclusion and that the 転 is not a contradiction but a deepening of the argument.

### Not all Japanese writing follows 起承転結

It is important to note that modern Japanese academic writing, business reports, and scientific papers often follow Western thesis-first conventions. The 起承転結 pattern is most common in:

- Newspaper editorials and opinion columns
- Essays (随筆)
- Speeches and presentations
- School compositions (作文)

When reading, ask yourself: is this text organized thesis-first, or is it building toward a conclusion? The signposting language in Section 24.4 will help you determine the structure quickly.

## 24.3 Why Understanding Structure Aids Reading Speed

Reading speed in a second language is not just about knowing vocabulary and grammar. It is also about knowing where to look for the information you need. Structural awareness provides a map of the text.

### For thesis-first texts

If you recognize that a text follows a thesis-first pattern, you can:

- Read the first paragraph carefully for the main argument
- Skim the middle sections for supporting evidence
- Read the conclusion to confirm your understanding

### For 起承転結 texts

If you recognize the 起承転結 pattern, you can:

- Note the topic in the 起 section (but do not expect the argument yet)
- Skim the 承 section for background information
- Pay close attention to the 転 section, where the text's unique contribution often lies
- Read the 結 section carefully for the main point

### Signposting as navigation

Japanese writers use signposting language (discussed in 24.4) to mark transitions between sections. Recognizing these markers allows you to identify the structure quickly, even when the text is long or complex.

> When you see しかし, ところが, or 一方 in the middle of an essay, you are likely entering the 転 section.
> When you see つまり, 以上のことから, or したがって, you are likely entering the 結 section.

## 24.4 Signposting Language Review

Japanese writers use a rich set of transitional expressions to signal the structure of their argument. Here is a comprehensive review, organized by function.

### Opening and framing

| Expression | Function |
|-----------|----------|
| はじめに | "First of all" — marks the beginning |
| まず | "First" — introduces the first point |
| ～について考えてみたい | "I would like to consider..." — frames the topic |
| ～が問題になっている | "...has become a problem" — identifies an issue |

### Adding and sequencing

| Expression | Function |
|-----------|----------|
| 次に | "Next" |
| さらに | "Furthermore, moreover" |
| また | "Also, in addition" |
| そのうえ | "On top of that" |
| 加えて | "In addition" |
| 第一に…第二に…第三に | "First... second... third" |

### Contrast and turn

| Expression | Function |
|-----------|----------|
| しかし | "However" |
| ところが | "However" (unexpected) |
| 一方 | "On the other hand" |
| 一方で | "On the other hand" (slightly softer) |
| それに対して | "In contrast to that" |
| にもかかわらず | "Despite that" |
| とはいえ | "Having said that" |
| だが | "But" (written) |
| ただし | "However, provided that" |

### Cause and reason

| Expression | Function |
|-----------|----------|
| なぜなら | "Because" (introduces reason) |
| というのは | "The reason is" |
| そのため | "For that reason" |
| したがって | "Therefore" (formal) |
| その結果 | "As a result" |

### Conclusion and summary

| Expression | Function |
|-----------|----------|
| つまり | "In other words, in short" |
| 要するに | "In summary" |
| 以上のことから | "From the above" |
| 結論として | "In conclusion" |
| このように | "In this way" |
| 以上 | "That is all" (signals the end) |

### Examples

> **まず**、少子化の現状について確認しておきたい。**次に**、その原因を分析する。**さらに**、諸外国の事例と比較した上で、**最後に**、日本が取るべき対策について提言する。
> "First, I would like to confirm the current state of the declining birthrate. Next, I will analyze its causes. Furthermore, after comparing with examples from other countries, finally, I will make recommendations about the measures Japan should take."

## 24.5 Reading Passage — Analyzing Rhetorical Structure

次の文章は、ある新聞の社説から構成を学ぶために書かれた練習用の文章である。読みながら、起承転結の構造を意識してほしい。

---

【起】

読書離れが進んでいると言われて久しい。文化庁の調査では、一ヶ月に一冊も本を読まないと答えた人が全体の半数近くに上った。特に若い世代の読書量の減少が顕著であり、「活字離れ」は深刻な問題として繰り返し取り上げられている。

【承】

確かに、紙の書籍の売上は年々減少している。書店の数も最盛期の半分以下にまで落ち込んだ。街から本屋が消えていく光景は、読書文化の衰退を象徴しているかのようである。学校の図書館でも、貸出冊数が減少傾向にあるという報告がある。こうしたデータを見れば、読書離れは疑いようのない事実のように思える。

【転】

しかし、ここで立ち止まって考えてみたい。「読書」とは何か。紙の本を読むことだけが読書なのだろうか。

近年、電子書籍の市場は急速に拡大している。スマートフォンやタブレットで小説を読む人、ウェブ上の記事を毎日大量に読む人、オーディオブックで通勤時間に読書をする人。こうした新しい形の「読書」を含めると、人々が文字に触れる時間はむしろ増えているという見方もある。

さらに、SNSの普及により、一般の人々が文章を書く機会も格段に増えた。文章を書くということは、文章を読むということでもある。つまり、「活字離れ」という表現そのものが、現実を正確に反映していない可能性があるのだ。

【結】

問題の本質は、読書の量ではなく、質にあるのではないだろうか。短い情報を次々と消費するだけでは、深い思考力は養われない。一冊の本をじっくり読み、著者の考えと向き合う経験こそが、読書の本当の価値であろう。

私たちに必要なのは、「本を読め」という単純なメッセージではなく、「どう読むか」を問い直すことではないか。読書の形が変わっても、読むという行為の意味は変わらない。むしろ、情報が<ruby>溢<rt>あふ</rt></ruby>れる時代だからこそ、深く読む力がこれまで以上に求められているのである。

---

**Translation:**

[Introduction]

It has long been said that people are reading less. In a survey by the Agency for Cultural Affairs, nearly half of all respondents said they do not read even one book per month. The decline in reading volume among younger generations is particularly notable, and "moving away from the printed word" is repeatedly raised as a serious problem.

[Development]

Indeed, sales of paper books have been declining year after year. The number of bookstores has fallen to less than half of their peak. The sight of bookshops disappearing from towns seems to symbolize the decline of reading culture. There are also reports that the number of books checked out at school libraries is on a downward trend. Looking at such data, the decline in reading appears to be an undeniable fact.

[Turn]

However, let us pause and think. What is "reading"? Is reading only about reading paper books?

In recent years, the e-book market has been expanding rapidly. People who read novels on smartphones and tablets, people who read vast amounts of online articles every day, people who read through audiobooks during their commute. If we include these new forms of "reading," there is a view that the time people spend engaging with text has actually increased.

Furthermore, with the spread of social media, opportunities for ordinary people to write have also increased dramatically. Writing text also means reading text. In other words, the expression "moving away from the printed word" itself may not accurately reflect reality.

[Conclusion]

Is not the essence of the problem in the quality of reading, rather than the quantity? Merely consuming short pieces of information one after another does not cultivate deep thinking ability. It is the experience of carefully reading a single book and engaging with the author's ideas that is the true value of reading.

What we need is not the simple message of "read books" but rather a re-examination of "how to read." Even if the form of reading changes, the meaning of the act of reading does not change. Rather, precisely because we live in an age overflowing with information, the ability to read deeply is demanded more than ever before.
