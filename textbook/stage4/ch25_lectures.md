# Chapter 25 — Lectures and Extended Spoken Discourse

University lectures, conference presentations, and public talks occupy a unique register in Japanese. They are spoken, yet formal. They address an audience directly, yet maintain intellectual distance. A lecturer may shift between the academic authority of である and the polite accessibility of です/ます within a single paragraph, and listeners are expected to follow without difficulty. For learners of Japanese, lectures present a distinctive challenge: the language is neither the casual speech of daily conversation nor the dense written prose of academic papers, but something in between.

This chapter examines the register of Japanese lectures, the discourse markers that structure extended spoken arguments, the hedging strategies that speakers use to soften claims, and the techniques that lecturers employ to signal the organization of their talks. The chapter concludes with a full lecture transcript on the topic of Japanese urbanization, accompanied by note-taking strategies for sustained listening comprehension.

---

## 25.1 Lecture Register: Formal but Spoken

The most striking feature of Japanese lecture language is its mixed style. In written academic prose, the である style dominates. In polite conversation, です/ます is the norm. In lectures, the two coexist.

### Why speakers mix styles

A lecturer who used only である throughout a sixty-minute talk would sound stiff and detached, as though reading aloud from a paper. A lecturer who used only です/ます might sound too conversational, lacking the intellectual weight that an academic setting demands. The solution is to alternate: である for assertions of fact, analytical claims, and quotations from scholarship; です/ます for direct address, transitions, and moments of engagement with the audience.

### Formation: Common patterns of style mixing

| Context | Style used | Example |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| Stating a factual claim | である | これは明治期に始まった現象である。 |
| Explaining to the audience | です/ます | この点について、少し詳しく見ていきましょう。 |
| Quoting data or scholarship | である | 田中（2019）によれば、人口の六割が都市部に集中しているのである。 |
| Transitioning between topics | です/ます | では、次のテーマに移りたいと思います。 |
| Drawing a conclusion | である / です | 以上のことから、この政策は失敗であったと言わざるを得ない。 |
| Asking a rhetorical question | です/ます | これは本当に正しいのでしょうか。 |

### Examples

> これは戦後の日本社会に特有の現象である。なぜそう言えるのか、少し考えてみましょう。
> "This is a phenomenon unique to postwar Japanese society. Let us consider briefly why we can say this."

> データを見ると、一九七〇年代以降、地方の人口は減少し続けている。これは都市化の進行を示すものです。
> "Looking at the data, the population in rural areas has continued to decline since the 1970s. This indicates the progress of urbanization."

### A note on のである / のです

The explanatory の construction bridges the two styles. のである adds emphasis and explanatory force in the である register, while のです (or its more casual variant んです) does the same in the です/ます register. In lectures, both appear frequently.

> この時期に大きな変化が起きたのである。
> "It was during this period that a major change occurred." (である style, explanatory)

> つまり、従来の説明では不十分なんです。
> "In other words, the conventional explanation is insufficient." (です/ます style, explanatory)

## 25.2 Discourse Markers in Lectures

Extended spoken discourse requires signposting. Without it, listeners lose track of where the argument is heading. Japanese lecturers rely on a set of discourse markers to open topics, transition between points, introduce digressions, and return to the main thread.

### Opening and transitioning markers

| Marker | Function | Typical usage |
|--------|----------|---------------|
| さて | "Well then" — signals a shift to a new topic or the start of the main content | さて、本題に入りましょう。 |
| では | "Well then, so" — signals a transition or next step | では、具体的な事例を見ていきます。 |
| ところで | "By the way" — introduces a related digression or side topic | ところで、この点に関して面白いデータがあります。 |
| まず | "First" — introduces the first point in a sequence | まず、背景を確認しておきたいと思います。 |
| 次に | "Next" — moves to the second or subsequent point | 次に、この現象の原因を分析します。 |
| 最後に | "Finally" — introduces the last point | 最後に、今後の課題について触れておきます。 |
| それでは | "Well then" — slightly more formal than では | それでは、始めたいと思います。 |

### Returning and refocusing markers

| Marker | Function | Typical usage |
|--------|----------|---------------|
| 話を戻しますと | "Returning to the topic" | 話を戻しますと、先ほどのデータが示すように… |
| いずれにしても | "In any case" — wraps up a digression | いずれにしても、この点は重要です。 |
| ここで重要なのは | "What is important here is" | ここで重要なのは、時代背景を考慮することです。 |

### Examples

> さて、今日は日本の都市化について話をしたいと思います。まず、歴史的な背景を見てみましょう。
> "Well then, today I would like to talk about urbanization in Japan. First, let us look at the historical background."

> ところで、先ほど「過疎化」という言葉を使いましたが、この概念自体が一九六〇年代に作られたものです。では、話を戻しましょう。
> "By the way, I used the word 'depopulation' a moment ago, but this concept itself was coined in the 1960s. Now, let us return to the topic."

## 25.3 Hedging in Speech

Academic speakers rarely state claims as absolute truths. They hedge: they soften assertions, acknowledge uncertainty, and leave room for alternative interpretations. Japanese has a rich inventory of hedging strategies, and lectures are where these strategies appear most densely.

### Common hedging expressions

| Expression | Meaning | Degree of hedging |
|-----------|---------|-------------------|
| ～と思うんですけれども | "I think that..., but" | Moderate: presents a personal view tentatively |
| ～ではないかと（思います） | "I wonder if it might be..." | Strong: suggests without asserting |
| まあ | "Well..." | Light: softens what follows |
| 一応 | "For now, tentatively" | Moderate: marks something as provisional |
| ～と言えるかもしれません | "It might be possible to say that..." | Strong: double hedging |
| 必ずしも～とは限らない | "It is not necessarily the case that..." | Moderate: partial negation |
| ～という側面もある | "There is also the aspect that..." | Light: adds a perspective without denying others |

### Formation: ～と思うんですけれども

This is one of the most characteristic lecture hedges. It combines the explanatory んです with the tentative と思う and the trailing けれども, which leaves the sentence feeling open-ended rather than conclusive.

| Component | Function |
|-----------|----------|
| [claim] | The speaker's observation or opinion |
| と思う | "I think" — marks it as personal rather than objective |
| んです | Explanatory tone — frames the statement as offering an explanation |
| けれども | "But, however" — leaves the door open for other views |

### Examples

> この現象は、経済成長だけでは説明できないのではないかと思うんですけれども。
> "I think this phenomenon cannot be explained by economic growth alone, but [what do you think]."

> まあ、一応この時期を「転換期」と呼んでおきますが、もちろん議論の余地はあります。
> "Well, for now I will call this period a 'turning point,' but of course there is room for debate."

> 必ずしも都市化が悪いとは限らないという側面もあるわけです。
> "There is also the aspect that urbanization is not necessarily a bad thing."

### Comparative note: Hedging in English vs. Japanese lectures

English-language lectures also use hedges ("I would argue," "it seems that," "perhaps"), but Japanese hedging tends to be more layered. A single sentence may stack multiple hedging devices. Consider the following:

> これはある種の文化的な要因が関係しているのではないかと思うんですけれども、まあ、この点については一応そういうふうに考えておきたいと思います。
> "I think that some kind of cultural factor may be involved, but, well, for now I would like to tentatively think of it that way."

This sentence contains ではないか, と思う, んです, けれども, まあ, and 一応 — six hedging devices in a single utterance. In English, this degree of hedging might sound evasive. In a Japanese lecture, it sounds appropriately careful and intellectually modest.

### Why hedging matters for listeners

Learners often miss hedges and treat hedged statements as definitive claims. If a lecturer says ～ではないかと思うんですけれども, the speaker is offering a tentative hypothesis, not a firm conclusion. Recognizing hedges helps you accurately gauge how strongly the speaker holds a position.

## 25.4 How Speakers Signal Structure

Beyond individual discourse markers, effective lecturers use structural signaling techniques: they number their points, preview what is coming, summarize what has been said, and explicitly mark the boundaries between sections.

### Numbering points

Lecturers often announce the number of points they will make, then label each one as they proceed.

> 今日は三つのことについて話します。第一に、歴史的な経緯。第二に、現在の状況。そして第三に、今後の展望です。
> "Today I will talk about three things. First, the historical background. Second, the current situation. And third, the future outlook."

### Previewing

Previewing tells the listener what to expect. It reduces cognitive load by providing a framework before the details arrive.

> この後、具体的なデータをお見せしますが、その前に、なぜこの問題が重要なのかを説明しておきたいと思います。
> "I will show you specific data shortly, but before that, I would like to explain why this problem is important."

### Summarizing

Summaries consolidate what has been said and prepare the listener for the next section.

> ここまでの話をまとめますと、明治以降、日本の人口は急速に都市に集中してきたということになります。
> "To summarize what I have said so far, since the Meiji era, Japan's population has rapidly concentrated in cities."

### Marking transitions explicitly

Lecturers often use meta-commentary to signal shifts.

> 以上が前半の内容です。後半では、この問題に対する政策的な対応を見ていきたいと思います。
> "That concludes the first half. In the second half, I would like to examine policy responses to this problem."

### Formation: Common structural phrases

| Function | Japanese expression |
|----------|-------------------|
| Numbering | 第一に、第二に、第三に |
| Listing | 一つ目は…、二つ目は…、三つ目は… |
| Previewing | この後～について話します |
| Summarizing | ここまでの話をまとめますと |
| Transitioning | 以上で～を終わりまして、次に… |
| Concluding | 最後にまとめとして |
| Inviting questions | 何かご質問はありますでしょうか |

### Examples of combined signaling

Skilled lecturers often combine numbering, previewing, and summarizing in rapid succession. Notice how the following passage uses multiple techniques within a few sentences:

> 以上が一つ目のポイントでした。二つ目に移る前に、ここまでの内容を簡単に振り返っておきましょう。先ほど申し上げたのは、都市化の第一段階は工業化と結びついていたということです。では、二つ目のポイントに入ります。
> "That was the first point. Before moving to the second, let us briefly review what we have covered so far. What I said earlier was that the first stage of urbanization was tied to industrialization. Now, let us move to the second point."

Here the speaker marks the end of a section (以上が), previews a brief review (振り返っておきましょう), delivers the summary (先ほど申し上げたのは), and then signals the transition forward (では、二つ目のポイントに入ります). This kind of layered signaling is characteristic of well-organized academic speech.

## 25.5 Listening Notes — Following a Lecture with Note-Taking Strategies

Listening to a ten-minute lecture in Japanese requires different skills from reading the same content on paper. You cannot re-read a sentence. You cannot pause to look up a word. The information arrives at the speaker's pace, not yours. Effective note-taking is the bridge between hearing and understanding.

### Strategy 1: Listen for structural signals first

Before trying to capture every detail, listen for the markers from Section 25.2. When you hear まず, write a "1." When you hear 次に, write a "2." When you hear 最後に, write a "3." This skeletal structure gives you a framework to fill in.

### Strategy 2: Capture keywords, not full sentences

You do not need to transcribe. Write the key noun or verb from each point. If the lecturer says 明治以降、地方から都市への人口移動が加速した, your note might simply be: 明治〜 人口移動↑.

### Strategy 3: Mark hedged vs. asserted claims

Use a simple notation. For strong claims, write the point plainly. For hedged claims, add a question mark or write "maybe." This distinction matters when you review your notes later.

### Strategy 4: Note discourse markers as navigation aids

If you hear ところで, draw a small arrow to the side — the speaker is digressing. If you hear 話を戻しますと, the digression is over. These markers tell you where you are in the lecture's architecture.

### Strategy 5: Leave space for the summary

Lecturers almost always summarize at the end. Leave the bottom of your page blank and fill it in when you hear ここまでの話をまとめますと or 以上をまとめますと.

## 25.6 Lecture Transcript — 日本の都市化：その歴史と課題

The following is a transcript of a lecture on Japanese urbanization. Read it once for overall comprehension, then re-read it with attention to the discourse features discussed in this chapter: style mixing, discourse markers, hedging, and structural signaling.

---

　皆さん、こんにちは。では、始めたいと思います。今日のテーマは「日本の都市化」です。都市化という現象を歴史的に振り返りながら、その光と影について考えてみたいと思います。

　今日は大きく分けて三つのことをお話しします。まず、都市化の歴史的な流れ。次に、都市化がもたらした社会的な変化。そして最後に、現代の都市が抱える課題について触れたいと思います。

　さて、まず歴史的な背景から始めましょう。日本の都市化は、明治維新とともに始まったと言われています。一八六八年の時点で、日本の人口の大部分は農村部に住んでいた。都市人口の割合は、おそらく二割程度であったと推定されています。

　しかし、明治政府が進めた近代化政策、とりわけ殖産興業の政策により、工場が都市部に建設され、労働力として多くの人々が農村から都市に移動しました。これがいわゆる「離村向都」と呼ばれる現象であります。

　ところで、ここで一つ注意しておきたいのですが、「都市化」と言っても、その中身は時代によって大きく異なります。明治期の都市化は工業化と結びついていましたが、戦後の都市化はサービス産業の成長と密接に関係している。この違いは重要ですので、覚えておいていただきたいと思います。

　話を戻しますと、戦後、特に一九五〇年代から七〇年代にかけて、日本は急速な高度経済成長を経験します。この時期に、地方から三大都市圏、つまり東京、大阪、名古屋への人口移動が大規模に起こりました。集団就職という言葉を聞いたことがあるでしょうか。中学や高校を卒業した若者が、列車に乗って都市の工場や商店に就職していった。これは日本の社会構造を根本から変えた現象であると言えるのではないかと思うんですけれども。

　次に、都市化がもたらした社会的な変化について見ていきましょう。まあ、これは非常に多岐にわたるテーマですので、今日は二つの点に絞ってお話しします。

　一つ目は、家族構造の変化です。農村では、三世代が同居する大家族が一般的でありました。しかし、都市に移住した若者たちは核家族を形成します。夫婦と子どもだけの世帯が急増し、それに伴って、地域のつながりや世代間の交流が薄れていった。一応、これを「家族の近代化」と呼ぶ研究者もいますが、果たしてそれを「近代化」と呼ぶべきかどうかは議論の余地があるところです。

　二つ目は、地方の衰退です。都市に人口が集中する裏側で、農村部では過疎化が深刻な問題になっていきます。若い世代が出ていき、残されたのは高齢者ばかりという集落が増えていく。いわゆる「限界集落」という概念が広まったのは二〇〇〇年代のことですが、問題自体はもっと前から存在していたのであります。

　最後に、現代の都市が抱える課題について触れておきたいと思います。日本の都市化率は現在、約九割に達しています。しかし、都市化が進んだからといって、すべてがうまくいっているわけではありません。

　まず、住宅問題があります。東京の住宅価格は一般的な勤労者の年収の十倍を超えることも珍しくない。通勤時間は平均で片道一時間近くに及びます。次に、孤立の問題です。都市では隣に誰が住んでいるかも知らないという状況が珍しくなくなっています。地域社会のつながりが希薄になり、孤独死という痛ましい現象も報告されるようになりました。

　ここまでの話をまとめますと、日本の都市化は明治以降、工業化とともに始まり、戦後の高度成長期に加速した。その結果、家族構造の変化と地方の過疎化という二つの大きな社会変動を引き起こした。そして現在、都市は住宅問題や社会的孤立といった新たな課題に直面しているということになります。

　都市化は不可逆的な流れであると思われがちですが、近年では「地方創生」や「Uターン・Iターン」といった動きも見られます。必ずしも一方通行ではないという側面もあるのではないかと思うんですけれども、この点については、また別の機会にお話ししたいと思います。

　以上で今日の講義を終わります。何かご質問はありますでしょうか。

---

**Translation:**

Good afternoon, everyone. Well then, I would like to begin. Today's theme is "urbanization in Japan." I would like to look back at the phenomenon of urbanization historically and consider both its bright and dark sides.

Today I will broadly discuss three things. First, the historical flow of urbanization. Next, the social changes that urbanization brought about. And finally, I would like to touch on the challenges that modern cities face.

Well then, let us start with the historical background. It is said that urbanization in Japan began with the Meiji Restoration. At the time of 1868, the majority of Japan's population lived in rural areas. The proportion of urban population is estimated to have been around twenty percent.

However, due to the modernization policies promoted by the Meiji government, particularly the policy of promoting industry, factories were built in urban areas, and many people moved from rural areas to cities as labor. This is the phenomenon known as "leaving the village for the city."

By the way, I would like to note one thing here: even though we say "urbanization," its substance differs greatly depending on the era. Urbanization in the Meiji period was tied to industrialization, but postwar urbanization is closely related to the growth of the service industry. This distinction is important, so I would like you to keep it in mind.

Returning to the topic, in the postwar period, particularly from the 1950s to the 1970s, Japan experienced rapid high economic growth. During this period, large-scale population movement occurred from rural areas to the three major metropolitan areas, that is, Tokyo, Osaka, and Nagoya. Have you heard the term "group employment"? Young people who had graduated from middle school or high school would ride trains to take jobs at urban factories and shops. I think this can be said to be a phenomenon that fundamentally transformed Japan's social structure.

Next, let us look at the social changes brought about by urbanization. Well, this is a topic that covers an extremely wide range, so today I will narrow it down to two points.

The first is the change in family structure. In rural areas, large families with three generations living together were common. However, young people who migrated to cities formed nuclear families. Households consisting of only a married couple and children increased rapidly, and along with this, community ties and intergenerational exchange gradually weakened. Some researchers tentatively call this the "modernization of the family," but whether it should really be called "modernization" is a matter of debate.

The second is the decline of rural areas. Behind the concentration of population in cities, depopulation became a serious problem in rural areas. Young generations left, and the number of settlements left with only elderly residents grew. The concept of "marginal settlements" became widespread in the 2000s, but the problem itself had existed much earlier.

Finally, I would like to touch on the challenges that modern cities face. Japan's urbanization rate has currently reached approximately ninety percent. However, just because urbanization has progressed does not mean that everything is going well.

First, there is the housing problem. It is not uncommon for housing prices in Tokyo to exceed ten times the annual income of an ordinary worker. Commuting time averages nearly one hour each way. Next, there is the problem of isolation. In cities, it has become common not to know who lives next door. Community ties have become thin, and the tragic phenomenon of solitary death has also come to be reported.

To summarize what I have discussed so far, urbanization in Japan began along with industrialization after the Meiji era and accelerated during the postwar high-growth period. As a result, it triggered two major social transformations: changes in family structure and rural depopulation. And currently, cities are facing new challenges such as housing problems and social isolation.

Urbanization tends to be seen as an irreversible trend, but in recent years movements such as "regional revitalization" and "U-turn and I-turn migration" can also be observed. I think there is also the aspect that it is not necessarily a one-way street, but I would like to discuss this point on another occasion.

This concludes today's lecture. Are there any questions?
