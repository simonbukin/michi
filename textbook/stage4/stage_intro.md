# Stage 4: Advanced Proficiency (N2)

## What This Stage Covers

Stage 4 is where you stop studying Japanese and start using it. The grammar, vocabulary, and reading strategies from Stages 1-3 gave you the ability to handle adapted and lightly simplified texts. Stage 4 removes the scaffolding entirely: you will read unsimplified authentic texts, produce keigo fluently, process natural-speed spoken Japanese, and internalize the dense compound grammar that characterizes real written Japanese at the professional and academic level.

## Why Classical Grammar Comes First

The opening two chapters of this stage — `ch01_classical_residues` and `ch02_classical_temporal` — cover grammar inherited from classical Japanese: ざる, ず, べからず, ごとく, なり, や否や, つつ, and similar forms. This is unusual. Most textbooks treat classical grammar as an advanced afterthought or skip it entirely. Stage 4 puts it first, and the reasoning matters.

Modern formal Japanese is saturated with classical residues. A newspaper editorial uses ざるを得ない and 〜つつある in its first paragraph. A government notice opens with 〜にあたり and closes with 〜なければならない. A literary novel narrates with ごとく and 〜たり〜たり. A legal document is built on べからず and 〜の限り. If you postpone classical grammar until "after" the modern N2 inventory, you cannot read any of these texts when you encounter them in the journalistic, academic, and literary chapters later in this stage — and those chapters are where the bulk of your reading practice happens.

Treating these forms as classical does not mean treating them as archaic. They are productive features of modern register-shifted Japanese. A learner who knows that ざる is the classical negative, that べき is its hortative cousin, and that 〜つつ is a continuative converb has unlocked a whole layer of formal writing that opaque pattern-matching cannot.

The same principle applies in reverse: a learner who only knows the modern colloquial system will misparse formal Japanese systematically, treating these forms as exotic rather than as the standard register variants they are. Stage 4 starts with classical grammar to prevent that misparsing for the rest of the stage.

This stage covers:

- **Classical grammar residues in modern Japanese** — ざる, ず, べからず, ごとく, なり, や否や, and other forms inherited from classical Japanese that appear routinely in formal modern writing
- **Advanced compound grammar** — The full N2 inventory: concession (ものの, とはいえ, にもかかわらず), cause/reason (ばこそ, あまり, ことから), extent/degree/limit (に至るまで, を限りに, をもって), hypothetical extremes (たところで, ようものなら, ないまでも), appearance/manner (かのように, とばかりに, んばかりに), scope limiters (に限って, をおいて, ならでは), and formal emotions (てならない, を禁じ得ない, 極まりない)
- **Keigo productive command** — Full mastery of the 5-part system (尊敬語, 謙譲語I, 謙譲語II/丁重語, 丁寧語, 美化語), business communication patterns, and common errors and hypercorrection
- **Academic and journalistic written style** — The grammar and rhetoric of newspaper editorials, academic papers, formal reports, and legal/bureaucratic documents
- **Onomatopoeia as a system** — 100+ items organized by phonological patterns, not as isolated vocabulary but as a productive system with internal logic (濁音 = heaviness/negativity, 促音 = sharpness/suddenness, etc.)
- **Thematic expansion** — Business and economics, politics, social issues, literature, and legal/bureaucratic language
- **Discourse and text-level comprehension** — Ellipsis recovery, rhetorical structure analysis, lecture comprehension, and tracking arguments across multi-paragraph texts
- **Kanji expansion** — ~350 new kanji to reach ~1,000 cumulative, with focus on compound reading prediction and semantic networks

## What Changes from Stage 3

- **Reading passages are unsimplified authentic texts** — Newspaper editorials, business emails, literary excerpts, lease contracts, and academic abstracts, presented without adaptation
- **Grammar shifts from "here is a pattern" to "here is a cluster of related patterns — understand the system"** — Instead of learning individual grammar points in isolation, you will study families of expressions organized by function, understanding how they differ in formality, nuance, and distribution
- **Listening targets natural-speed lectures and unscripted interviews** — No more artificially slowed or carefully enunciated audio; you will practice with material produced for native speakers
- **Keigo moves from recognition to productive command** — In Stage 3, you learned to understand keigo when you encountered it; in Stage 4, you learn to produce it correctly and fluently in business and formal contexts
- **Onomatopoeia taught as a systematic category, not just individual words** — Rather than memorizing one onomatopoeia at a time, you will learn the phonological rules that let you predict meaning from sound shape
- **No furigana for kanji within the expected ~650** — Furigana appears only for kanji beyond the Stage 3 cumulative count

## Prerequisites Checklist

Before beginning Stage 4, you should be able to:

- [ ] Explain the full は/が distinction and apply it in context
- [ ] Use compound particles fluently: について, に対して, にとって, において, に基づいて, をはじめ, にわたって, etc.
- [ ] Understand sentence-ending modality: わけ, もの, こと, べき as discourse markers encoding speaker stance
- [ ] Read and write in formal written style using である and related forms
- [ ] Recognize colloquial contractions, dropped particles, fillers, and basic Kansai dialect
- [ ] Perform speech acts appropriately: refusing, apologizing, requesting, and complimenting using indirect strategies
- [ ] Parse advanced predicate endings: ざるを得ない, かねる, っぱなし, きる, がち, 気味, 得る
- [ ] Navigate discourse connectors and use them as comprehension anchors in longer texts
- [ ] Recognize approximately 650 kanji in context
- [ ] Know approximately 3,500 vocabulary items

If any of these feel shaky, review the relevant Stage 3 chapters before proceeding.

## The N2 Leap

At N3, you could read adapted texts and parse most sentence structures with effort. At N2, something qualitatively different happens: you stop being a "Japanese student" and start being a person who reads and understands Japanese. Unsimplified texts — newspaper articles, novels, contracts, academic papers — become accessible not as decoding exercises but as things you actually read for their content.

The challenge at this level is not any individual grammar pattern. Most N2 grammar points, taken one at a time, are straightforward. The challenge is density. Authentic Japanese texts routinely combine multiple N2 patterns in a single sentence: a classical grammar residue embedded inside a compound grammar expression, wrapped in keigo, with an onomatopoeia modifying the verb. Each piece is manageable alone; the difficulty is processing them simultaneously at reading speed.

This is why Stage 4 emphasizes systems over individual points. You cannot look up every pattern mid-sentence — there are too many, and they interact. You need internalized frameworks: the keigo system, the onomatopoeia system, the concession/cause/degree grammar families. When you understand these as systems, a new pattern within the system is immediately parseable. When you have only memorized individual items, each new combination is a puzzle.

The path through this stage is the same as it was at N3, but more so: volume. Read extensively, listen extensively, and use this textbook as the map that tells you what you are encountering. The grammar explanations here give you the categories; the authentic input gives you the fluency.
