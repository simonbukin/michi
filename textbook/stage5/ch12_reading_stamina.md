# Chapter 12 — Building Reading Stamina

Reading Japanese at the advanced level is not only a question of grammar and vocabulary. It is a question of endurance. The N1 reading section presents multiple long texts under strict time pressure. Real-world reading — novels, academic papers, business reports — demands sustained attention over thousands of characters. If you can parse every sentence but lose focus after two paragraphs, your effective reading ability is far below your grammatical knowledge.

This chapter addresses the practical side of advanced reading: how to build the stamina to read long texts without fatigue defeating comprehension, how to manage the decision of whether to look up an unknown word or press on, and how to use extensive reading to build fluency that intensive study alone cannot provide. The chapter concludes with two timed practice texts — a newspaper editorial and a magazine feature — that simulate the kind of sustained reading the N1 exam and real life demand.

---

## 12.1 Why Stamina Matters

### The N1 reading section

The JLPT N1 reading section (読解) includes approximately 3,500 to 4,000 characters of text distributed across multiple passages. Test-takers have roughly 70 minutes for the entire reading section. This means you must read, comprehend, and answer questions about dense material at a pace of roughly 50-60 characters per minute — while also thinking about the questions.

Many test-takers who understand every grammar point and know every vocabulary word still run out of time. The bottleneck is not knowledge but processing speed and sustained concentration.

### Real-world reading demands

Beyond the exam, advanced Japanese reading involves:

- **Newspapers:** A front-page article in 朝日新聞 or 日経新聞 typically runs 1,500-2,500 characters. Reading the morning paper means processing 10,000-20,000 characters.
- **Novels:** A typical novel page contains approximately 600-800 characters. A 300-page novel requires sustained engagement with roughly 200,000 characters.
- **Academic papers:** Journal articles in Japanese run 8,000-15,000 characters and demand precise comprehension.
- **Business reports:** Internal documents, proposals, and analyses require careful reading with attention to detail and nuance.

In all of these contexts, the ability to maintain focus and reading speed over long stretches separates functional readers from those who technically understand Japanese but cannot use it for sustained tasks.

## 12.2 Strategies: Reading Speed, When to Look Up vs Skip, Managing Fatigue

### Reading speed

There is no shortcut to reading speed. It comes from volume — the more you read, the faster you read. However, several habits can accelerate the process:

**1. Suppress subvocalization for known material.**

If you are mentally pronouncing every character, your reading speed is limited to your speaking speed. For passages where the grammar and vocabulary are familiar, practice letting your eyes move ahead of your inner voice. You do not need to pronounce 東京都 syllable by syllable — you can recognize it as a single unit.

**2. Read in chunks, not word by word.**

Train yourself to take in phrases and clauses as units:

> 政府は / 新たな経済対策を / 来月中に / 発表する方針だ。

Four chunks, not fourteen words. Each chunk is a semantic unit: subject, object, time frame, predicate.

**3. Use the paragraph topic sentence.**

Japanese expository writing typically states the topic or main claim in the first sentence of each paragraph. Read this sentence carefully. If you understand the paragraph's direction, the supporting sentences become easier to process quickly.

**4. Do not re-read unless necessary.**

The habit of going back to re-read a sentence you already understood wastes time and breaks flow. Re-read only when comprehension has genuinely failed.

### When to look up vs skip

This is the single most important strategic decision in extended reading. The rule:

> **Look up a word only if it is essential to understanding the main point of the passage.**

Apply this test:

| Situation | Action |
|-----------|--------|
| Unknown word is the subject or main verb of a key sentence | Look up |
| Unknown word is a modifier in a supporting detail | Skip |
| Unknown word appears multiple times | Look up (it is clearly important) |
| Unknown word is in a quotation or example | Skip unless the example is the main point |
| You can infer the meaning from context | Do not look up — trust your inference |

In timed reading (exams or practice), the cost of looking up a word is not just the time spent looking — it is the break in concentration and flow. Every lookup resets your mental engagement with the text.

### Managing fatigue

Reading fatigue is real. Your brain consumes more energy processing a second language than a first. Strategies:

**1. Take structured breaks.**

When reading for extended periods, use a pattern: 25 minutes of focused reading, then a 5-minute break. This is more productive than reading until exhaustion.

**2. Alternate difficulty levels.**

If you have been reading a dense academic text, switch to something lighter — a blog post, a manga, a simple news article. This maintains reading volume without burning out.

**3. Read at a consistent time.**

Your brain performs differently at different times of day. Find the time when you read Japanese most effectively and protect it.

**4. Accept imperfect understanding.**

Perfectionism is the enemy of stamina. If you understand 85% of a passage, that is enough to keep moving. The remaining 15% will often clarify itself as you read further, or it will turn out to be nonessential detail.

## 12.3 Extensive Reading Principle — High Volume, Low Pressure

Extensive reading (多読, たどく) is the practice of reading large amounts of material at or slightly below your current level, without stopping to look up every unknown word. It is the single most effective method for building reading speed, expanding passive vocabulary, and developing intuitive grammar.

### The principles

| Principle | Explanation |
|-----------|-------------|
| Read easy material | If you are looking up more than 2-3 words per page, the text is too hard for extensive reading |
| Read a lot | Volume matters more than difficulty. 100 pages of easy material builds more fluency than 10 pages of hard material |
| Read for pleasure | Choose material you genuinely want to read. Forced reading builds resentment, not fluency |
| Do not use a dictionary | Or use one sparingly — only when curiosity is overwhelming |
| Stop if it is not interesting | Abandoning a boring book is not failure; it is good reading strategy |
| Read faster than feels comfortable | Push yourself to read slightly faster than your natural pace. Comprehension may dip slightly, but speed will improve |

### What extensive reading builds

- **Automatic recognition:** Words you have seen 20 times in context do not require conscious processing. They are simply known.
- **Collocational knowledge:** You learn which words appear together — not from a textbook list but from encountering them repeatedly in natural text.
- **Structural prediction:** After enough exposure, you begin to anticipate how sentences will end. This prediction speeds comprehension dramatically.
- **Stamina:** Reading for an hour without fatigue is a trained skill. Extensive reading trains it.

### What extensive reading does not build

Extensive reading is not a substitute for intensive study. It does not teach you grammar rules you have never encountered. It does not correct persistent misunderstandings. It works best as a complement to the structured study you are already doing — the grammar and vocabulary chapters in this textbook, for instance.

## 12.4 Graded Reader Recommendations and Transition to Ungraded

### Graded readers

At the N1 level, most graded readers will feel too easy. That is acceptable for extensive reading purposes — remember, the goal is volume and speed, not challenge. Recommended series:

| Series | Publisher | Level range | Notes |
|--------|-----------|-------------|-------|
| にほんご多読ブックス | 大修館書店 | Level 0-4 | Short, varied topics; good for warming up |
| Japanese Graded Readers | ASK Publishing | Level 0-4 | Literary adaptations and original stories |
| 読む読む文庫 | くろしお出版 | Intermediate-Advanced | Longer texts; closer to native material |

For N1-level learners, Level 3 and Level 4 graded readers can be read quickly and in volume. Use them as warm-up material or for days when you want to read without strain.

### Transitioning to ungraded material

The gap between graded readers and native material is real but crossable. The key is choosing native material strategically:

| Material type | Difficulty | Good for |
|---------------|-----------|----------|
| NHK News Web Easy | Low-intermediate | Not challenging enough for N1, but useful for speed drills |
| NHK News Web (regular) | Upper-intermediate | Current events; manageable sentence length |
| Yahoo!ニュース | Intermediate-advanced | Varied topics; reader comments add colloquial exposure |
| 新書 (しんしょ) | Advanced | Short non-fiction books on focused topics; excellent bridge to serious reading |
| エッセイ集 | Advanced | Personal essays; often lighter vocabulary than fiction |
| 短編小説集 | Advanced | Short story collections; manageable length with literary depth |
| 新聞社説 | Advanced | Editorials; dense but short (800-1,200 characters) |

### The 新書 bridge

新書 (pocket-sized non-fiction books published by major houses like 岩波, 中公, 講談社) deserve special mention. They are typically 200-250 pages, focused on a single topic, written for a general educated audience, and published on every conceivable subject. A 新書 on a topic you already know something about — your field of work, a hobby, a historical period you have studied — is an ideal first native book. Your background knowledge compensates for vocabulary gaps.

## 12.5 Practice Text 1 — Newspaper Editorial (~1,000 Characters)

次の文章は、新聞社説の形式で書かれた論説文である。タイマーをセットして読むことを推奨する。目標時間：八分。

---

少子化対策は、もはや先送りの許されない課題である。政府は過去二十年にわたり、さまざまな施策を打ち出してきた。育児休業制度の拡充、保育所の増設、児童手当の引き上げ。だが、出生率の低下傾向に歯止めはかかっていない。二〇二四年の合計特殊出生率は過去最低を更新する見通しであり、このまま推移すれば、日本の人口は今世紀末に現在の半分以下に減少するという推計もある。

問題の根は深い。若者の経済的不安定さ、長時間労働の常態化、住宅費の高騰、そして「子どもを持つことは贅沢だ」という社会的雰囲気。これらは個別の政策では解決しがたい構造的問題である。育児休業を制度として整えても、実際に取得できる職場環境がなければ意味がない。保育所を増やしても、保育士の待遇が改善されなければ人材は集まらない。

必要なのは、個別施策の積み上げではなく、社会全体の価値観の転換である。子どもを育てる人々が不利益を被らない社会。労働時間の短縮が生産性の向上と両立する経済。男女ともに家事・育児を担うことが当然とされる文化。こうした変革は、一つの省庁や一つの法律では実現できない。

政府には、省庁横断的な取り組みと、長期的な視点に立った政策立案を求めたい。そして私たち一人ひとりも、この問題を「誰かの問題」ではなく「自分たちの問題」として考える姿勢が必要であろう。少子化は、社会の持続可能性そのものに関わる問題なのだから。

---

**Translation:**

Countermeasures against the declining birthrate are a challenge that can no longer be postponed. Over the past twenty years, the government has put forward various policies. Expansion of childcare leave systems, construction of more nurseries, increases in child allowances. Yet the downward trend in the birth rate has not been halted. The total fertility rate for 2024 is expected to set a new record low, and there are projections that if current trends continue, Japan's population will decline to less than half its current level by the end of this century.

The roots of the problem run deep. The economic instability of young people, the normalization of long working hours, soaring housing costs, and the social atmosphere that says "having children is a luxury." These are structural problems that individual policies can hardly solve. Even if childcare leave is established as a system, it is meaningless without workplace environments where people can actually take it. Even if nurseries are increased, personnel will not gather if the working conditions of childcare workers are not improved.

What is needed is not an accumulation of individual measures but a transformation of society's values as a whole. A society in which people raising children do not suffer disadvantages. An economy in which shorter working hours are compatible with increased productivity. A culture in which it is taken for granted that both men and women share housework and childcare. Such transformation cannot be achieved by a single ministry or a single law.

We would like the government to pursue cross-ministerial initiatives and policy planning grounded in a long-term perspective. And each one of us, too, needs the attitude of thinking about this problem not as "someone else's problem" but as "our own problem." For the declining birthrate is a problem that concerns the very sustainability of society.

## 12.6 Practice Text 2 — Magazine Feature Excerpt (~1,200 Characters)

次の文章は、雑誌の特集記事の形式で書かれたものである。目標時間：十分。

---

「リモートワーク」という言葉が日常語になって数年が経つ。だが、この働き方が社会に与えた影響は、まだ十分に理解されていないのかもしれない。

表面的には、リモートワークは「通勤がなくなる」ということに尽きるように見える。往復二時間の通勤が消え、その時間を自由に使えるようになる。満員電車のストレスから解放される。確かに、これだけでも大きな変化だ。しかし、より本質的な変化は別のところにある。

リモートワークは、「働く場所」と「暮らす場所」を切り離した。従来、日本のサラリーマンは会社の近くに住むか、長い通勤に耐えるか、どちらかを選ぶしかなかった。住む場所は、事実上、勤務先によって決められていた。だが、出社の必要がなくなれば、この制約は消える。

実際、コロナ禍以降、東京都心から郊外や地方への移住が増加している。不動産データによれば、千葉県、埼玉県、茨城県南部の住宅需要は顕著に伸びた。さらに、長野県や静岡県といった、従来は「退職後の移住先」と見なされていた地域に、三十代、四十代の現役世代が移り住む例も珍しくなくなった。

こうした動きは、単なる住所の変更にとどまらない。地方の人口減少に歯止めをかける可能性がある。地元経済に新たな消費をもたらす。学校の児童数が増え、存続の危機にあった地域コミュニティが活性化する例も報告されている。

一方で、課題も少なくない。リモートワーカーと地元住民の間に生じる文化的な摩擦。地方のインフラ整備の遅れ。都市部の企業が「やはり出社を」と方針を転換するリスク。そして何より、リモートワークが可能な職種は限られているという現実がある。

それでもなお、この変化は不可逆的であるように思われる。一度「場所に縛られない働き方」を経験した人々が、再び全員を満員電車に戻すことに合意するとは考えにくい。問われているのは、リモートワークを「する」か「しない」かではなく、この新しい現実にどう適応していくかだろう。

---

**Translation:**

Several years have passed since the term "remote work" became an everyday word. But the impact this way of working has had on society may not yet be fully understood.

On the surface, remote work appears to amount to nothing more than "the commute disappears." A two-hour round-trip commute vanishes, and that time becomes available for free use. One is liberated from the stress of packed trains. Certainly, even this alone is a major change. However, the more fundamental change lies elsewhere.

Remote work severed the connection between "where one works" and "where one lives." Previously, Japanese company employees had no choice but to either live near the office or endure a long commute. Where one lived was, in effect, determined by one's employer. But if the need to come to the office disappears, this constraint vanishes.

In fact, since the pandemic, migration from central Tokyo to suburbs and rural areas has increased. According to real estate data, housing demand in Chiba Prefecture, Saitama Prefecture, and southern Ibaraki Prefecture has grown markedly. Furthermore, it has become not uncommon for people in their thirties and forties — the active working generation — to relocate to areas like Nagano Prefecture and Shizuoka Prefecture, regions that were previously regarded as "places to move after retirement."

These movements do not stop at mere changes of address. They have the potential to halt population decline in rural areas. They bring new consumption to local economies. There are reported cases where the number of schoolchildren has increased and regional communities that were on the verge of extinction have been revitalized.

On the other hand, challenges are not few. Cultural friction arising between remote workers and local residents. Delays in rural infrastructure development. The risk that urban companies will reverse course and say "come back to the office after all." And above all, there is the reality that the types of jobs that allow remote work are limited.

Even so, this change appears to be irreversible. It is hard to imagine that people who have once experienced "a way of working not bound by location" would all agree to return to packed trains. What is being asked is not whether to "do" or "not do" remote work, but how to adapt to this new reality.
