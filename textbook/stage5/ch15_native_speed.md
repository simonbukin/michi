# Chapter 15 — Unscripted Native-Speed Speech

Everything you have studied so far — textbook dialogues, graded listening exercises, even the dramas and news broadcasts of earlier chapters — has been, to some degree, prepared. Actors rehearse their lines. News anchors read from scripts. Even "natural" textbook audio is recorded at a deliberate pace with clean enunciation. Native-speed unscripted speech is a different animal entirely. Words collapse into each other. Sounds vanish. Speakers start sentences, abandon them, restart from a different angle, pepper their speech with fillers, and talk over each other. This chapter prepares you for the gap between scripted and unscripted Japanese, equipping you with the tools to decode what real speakers actually say.

---

## 15.1 What Changes at Native Speed

When Japanese speakers talk at their natural pace — roughly 300 to 400 morae per minute in casual conversation — several phonological and structural phenomena emerge that textbooks rarely address.

### Elision

Sounds drop out. Vowels between voiceless consonants weaken or disappear entirely, a phenomenon you may have encountered in earlier stages but which intensifies at speed.

| Standard form | What you hear | Notes |
|--------------|---------------|-------|
| ～ています | ～てます | い drops |
| ～てしまう | ～ちゃう / ～ちまう | て + しまう contracts |
| ～ておく | ～とく | ておく → とく |
| ～なければ | ～なきゃ / ～なけりゃ | Conditional contracts |
| ～ている | ～てる | い drops |
| ～てしまった | ～ちゃった / ～ちまった | Past tense contraction |
| ～のだ | ～んだ | の → ん |

> 昨日やっとくって言ったのに、全然やってないじゃん。
> "You said you'd do it yesterday, but you haven't done it at all."

> あ、もう食べちゃった？早くない？
> "Oh, you already ate it? Isn't that fast?"

### Contraction stacking

At native speed, speakers do not limit themselves to a single contraction per phrase. Multiple contractions stack on top of each other, sometimes compressing three or four morphemes into a single blur.

| Full form | Contracted | Stacked contraction |
|-----------|-----------|-------------------|
| ～なくてはいけない | ～なくちゃいけない | ～なきゃ |
| ～と言っていた | ～って言ってた | ～っつってた |
| ～てしまっている | ～ちゃってる | — |
| ～ではないか | ～じゃないか | ～じゃんか / ～じゃん |
| ～なければならない | ～なきゃならない | ～なきゃ |

> もう行かなきゃ。遅刻しちゃう。
> "I gotta go already. I'm gonna be late."

> なんかさ、あいつっつってたけど、ほんとかな。
> "Like, that guy was saying that, but I wonder if it's true."

### False starts and self-correction

Speakers frequently begin a sentence, realize they want to say something differently, and restart. In written Japanese, this is invisible because writers edit. In speech, these false starts are constant.

> あの映画さ、昨日——いや、おととい見たんだけど、すごくよかった。
> "That movie, yesterday — no, the day before yesterday I saw it, and it was really good."

> それって、なんていうか、ちょっと……うーん、微妙じゃない？
> "That's, how should I say, a little... hmm, isn't it kind of iffy?"

### Speed and the listener

The challenge is not merely that sounds are missing. It is that your brain must reconstruct the missing sounds while simultaneously processing the meaning of the sentence and anticipating what comes next. This is a skill that develops only through exposure to unscripted speech.

## 15.2 Filler Inventory Expanded

Fillers are not empty sounds. They carry pragmatic meaning — they signal that the speaker is thinking, hedging, shifting topics, or softening a statement. At the N1 level, you should recognize fillers instantly and understand what each one communicates.

### Core fillers and their functions

| Filler | Reading | Function | Register |
|--------|---------|----------|----------|
| えーっと | えーっと | Searching for a word or idea; buys thinking time | Neutral to casual |
| あのー | あのー | Hesitation; softer than えーっと; often precedes a request or sensitive topic | Neutral |
| まあ | まあ | Softening; downplaying; "well, you know" | Neutral to casual |
| なんか | なんか | Vague filler; "like"; also hedges or softens | Casual |
| ほら | ほら | Drawing attention; "see?" or "you know, that thing" | Casual |
| やっぱり / やっぱ | やっぱり / やっぱ | "As expected" / "after all" / speaker confirming own expectation | Neutral / Casual |
| 一応 | いちおう | "Tentatively" / "just in case" / "for now" | Neutral |
| 別に | べつに | "Not particularly"; often used to deflect or downplay | Casual |

### えーっと and あのー

These are the two most basic fillers. えーっと signals active searching — the speaker is trying to retrieve a word, a name, a number. あのー is softer and often precedes something the speaker finds awkward to say.

> えーっと、あれなんだっけ、駅の近くのカフェ。名前忘れちゃった。
> "Um, what was it, that cafe near the station. I forgot the name."

> あのー、ちょっとお聞きしたいんですけど……
> "Um, I'd like to ask you something..."

### なんか

This filler is extremely common in casual speech, sometimes appearing multiple times in a single sentence. It functions as a hedge ("sort of"), a softener ("like"), or simply a pause filler.

> なんか、最近なんか忙しくて、なんか全然寝れてないんだよね。
> "Like, recently I've been, like, busy, and like, I can't sleep at all."

The triple なんか in that sentence would sound excessive in written Japanese, but in casual speech it is unremarkable. Learners should not try to assign precise meaning to every instance — sometimes なんか is simply the speaker's verbal equivalent of taking a breath.

### ほら

ほら functions as an attention-getter. It says: "You know what I mean" or "Look, think about it." It often accompanies a reference to shared knowledge.

> ほら、前に言ってたやつ。あれ、どうなった？
> "You know, that thing you mentioned before. What happened with that?"

### やっぱり and やっぱ

やっぱり (or its casual form やっぱ) expresses confirmation of an expectation. "As I thought." In filler usage, it often means the speaker is returning to an original instinct after considering alternatives.

> やっぱさ、あそこのラーメン最高だよね。
> "Yeah, as I thought, the ramen there is the best."

> うーん、やっぱりやめとく。
> "Hmm, actually, I'll pass after all."

### 一応 and 別に

一応 marks something as provisional or minimal. "I did it, but only the bare minimum." 別に deflects or downplays. "It's not that I care" or "Not especially."

> 一応聞いてみたけど、まだ返事来てない。
> "I asked just in case, but I haven't gotten a reply yet."

> 「怒ってる？」「別に。」
> "'Are you angry?' 'Not really.'"

## 15.3 Self-Correction Patterns

Speakers correct themselves constantly. Japanese has specific grammatical patterns for mid-sentence correction, and recognizing these patterns prevents you from confusing the abandoned fragment with the intended meaning.

### ～じゃなくて

The most direct correction. The speaker negates what they just said and replaces it.

| Structure | Meaning |
|-----------|---------|
| A じゃなくて B | "Not A, but B" |
| A じゃなくて、えーっと、B | "Not A — um — B" |

> 来週じゃなくて、再来週だった。ごめん。
> "Not next week — it was the week after next. Sorry."

### ～というか / ～っていうか

These signal a reformulation rather than a flat correction. The speaker is not saying they were wrong — they are searching for a better way to express the same idea.

> 難しいっていうか、めんどくさいっていうか。
> "It's not so much 'difficult' as... 'annoying,' I guess."

> あの人、優しいというか、お人好しというか。
> "That person is, how to put it, kind — or maybe naive."

### ～じゃなくて vs. ～っていうか: when to use which

じゃなくて corrects factual errors: wrong dates, wrong names, wrong numbers. っていうか refines impressions, feelings, and descriptions. If you hear じゃなくて, the speaker made a mistake. If you hear っていうか, the speaker is searching for the right nuance.

> 三時じゃなくて、四時だった。
> "It was four o'clock, not three." (Factual correction)

> 疲れたっていうか、もう限界って感じ。
> "It's not so much 'tired' as — I feel like I'm at my limit." (Nuance refinement)

### Other correction markers

| Pattern | Function |
|---------|----------|
| ～じゃない、～だ | Flat negation and replacement (casual) |
| ～ていうかさ | Reformulation with casual emphasis |
| いや、～ | "No, (what I mean is)..." — quick self-correction |
| 違う違う | "No no" — correcting oneself or someone else |

> いや、そういう意味じゃなくて。もっとこう、なんていうか……
> "No, that's not what I mean. It's more like, how should I say..."

## 15.4 Overlapping Speech and How Japanese Manages It

In English, interrupting someone mid-sentence is often perceived as rude. In Japanese conversation, listeners routinely produce overlapping speech — but the function is different from interruption.

### Aizuchi: the expectation of listener response

Japanese conversation expects active listener participation. Silence from the listener is not neutral — it is uncomfortable. The listener is expected to produce regular backchannels (相槌) such as うん, ええ, そうそう, はいはい, なるほど, and へえ. These overlap with the speaker's utterance and are not interruptions — they are signals that the listener is engaged.

| Backchannel | Function |
|-------------|----------|
| うん / ええ | "I'm listening" |
| そうそう | "Yes, exactly" — agreement |
| はいはい | "Yes, yes" — acknowledgment (can sound dismissive if overdone) |
| なるほど | "I see" — understanding |
| へえ | "Really?" — mild surprise or interest |
| うそ / まじで | "No way" / "Seriously?" — strong surprise |
| そうなんだ | "Is that so" — registering new information |

### When overlapping is genuine interruption

True interruptions do occur, especially in heated discussions or group conversations. The interrupter often begins with あ、ちょっと待って ("Ah, wait a moment") or あ、でもさ ("Ah, but you know"). In casual settings, speakers may simply start talking louder until the other person yields the floor.

> A: だからさ、あの時——
> B: あ、ちょっと待って。それって、あの件と関係ある？
> A: "So, at that time—"
> B: "Oh, wait. Is that related to that matter?"

### Turn-taking signals

Japanese speakers signal that they are about to finish their turn with falling intonation, sentence-final particles (ね, よ, さ), or trailing off with けど or が. Recognizing these signals helps you anticipate when a new speaker will begin.

> この前のやつ、結構よかったんだけど——
> "That thing from before was pretty good, but—" (trailing off, inviting response)

## 15.5 Listening Strategies for Unscripted Media

Podcasts, YouTube videos, variety shows, and live-streamed conversations are the richest source of unscripted Japanese. But they are also the most challenging. The following strategies will help you build comprehension systematically.

### Strategy 1: Start with podcasts that have transcripts

Several Japanese podcasts provide written transcripts. Begin with these. Listen once without the transcript, then read the transcript, then listen again. The gap between what you heard and what was actually said reveals your specific weak points.

### Strategy 2: Use speed controls without shame

YouTube and most podcast apps allow 0.75x playback speed. There is no dishonor in slowing speech down while you are training your ear. Over weeks, gradually return to 1.0x, then try 1.25x as a stretch exercise.

### Strategy 3: Shadow short segments

Choose a 15-to-30-second clip and attempt to repeat exactly what the speaker says, matching their speed and intonation. This forces your brain to process the contracted forms, fillers, and rhythm that characterize natural speech. You do not need to understand every word while shadowing — the goal is acoustic pattern matching.

### Strategy 4: Focus on one speaker in multi-person content

Variety shows and group podcasts are overwhelming because multiple speakers talk at once, joke, and react simultaneously. Choose one speaker and track only their contributions. Ignore the rest. On a second listen, switch to a different speaker.

### Strategy 5: Build a contraction dictionary

As you encounter contractions you do not recognize, write them down with their full forms. Over time, you will build a personalized reference that reflects the specific speakers and genres you listen to most.

## 15.6 Listening Passage — A Casual Conversation About Weekend Plans

The following passage simulates unscripted casual speech between two friends, A and B. It includes fillers, contractions, self-corrections, and overlapping backchannels. Read it once for comprehension, then re-read with attention to the features discussed in this chapter.

---

A: ねえねえ、今週の土曜ってさ、なんか予定ある？

B: えーっと、土曜？土曜はー……あ、一応午前中は歯医者入ってんだけど、午後はたぶん暇。

A: あ、そうなんだ。じゃあさ、前から言ってた——ほら、あの駅前にできた——

B: うんうん。

A: なんだっけ、イタリアンじゃなくて、フレンチ？いや、やっぱイタリアンだ。あそこ行かない？

B: あー、あそこね。なんか予約いるっぽくない？

A: あ、そうかも。一応ネットで見てみるわ。まあ、だめだったらさ、別にあそこじゃなくてもいいんだけど。

B: うん、まあね。なんかさ、最近外食全然してなくて、もうなんでもいいっていうか、どこでもいいっていうか。

A: わかるわかる。じゃあ、とりあえず調べとくね。

B: うん、おねがい。あ、でもさ、夜はちょっと——えーっと、なんだっけ、あ、そうだ、友達と飲み会入ってんだった。だから、ランチがいいかな。

A: 了解。ランチにしよう。じゃあまた連絡する。

B: はーい。

---

**Translation:**

A: Hey hey, this Saturday, do you have any plans?

B: Um, Saturday? Saturday... oh, I tentatively have a dentist appointment in the morning, but I'm probably free in the afternoon.

A: Oh, really. Then, that place we've been talking about — you know, the one that opened in front of the station —

B: Yeah yeah.

A: What was it, not Italian, French? No, it was Italian after all. Wanna go there?

B: Ah, that place. Don't you think you probably need a reservation?

A: Oh, maybe. I'll check online just in case. Well, if that doesn't work out, it doesn't have to be there or anything.

B: Yeah, true. Like, recently I haven't eaten out at all, and at this point, anything is fine — or anywhere is fine, really.

A: I get it, I get it. Well, I'll look into it for now.

B: Yeah, please. Oh, but, the evening is a bit — um, what was it, oh right, I have a drinking thing with friends that night. So, lunch would be better I think.

A: Got it. Let's do lunch. I'll message you again.

B: Okaaay.
