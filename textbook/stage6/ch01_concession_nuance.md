# Chapter 1 — Near-Synonym Grammar: Concession and Contrast

You have now encountered every major concessive and contrastive expression in Japanese. You know ものの, けれども, にもかかわらず, とはいえ, ながらも, のに, ても, たところで, 一方で, 反面, and more. Individually, you understand what each one means. The question that remains is the harder one: when two or more of them could fit, which do you choose, and why?

This chapter introduces no new grammar. Instead, it places the concessive and contrastive patterns you have already learned side by side and examines the differences that matter. These differences fall into four categories: register (how formal), emotional weight (how much the speaker feels about the gap between expectation and reality), clause relationship (does the speaker accept, resist, or dismiss the contradiction?), and information structure (is the concession the main point, or a stepping stone?).

Mastering these distinctions is what separates a learner who knows the grammar from a speaker who uses it naturally.

---

## 1.1 ものの vs けれども vs にもかかわらず vs とはいえ vs ながらも

These five expressions all translate as "although" or "despite," but they are not interchangeable. The following table summarizes the key differences.

### Comparison Table

| Expression | Register | Emotional weight | Typical use |
|---|---|---|---|
| けれども / けど | Casual to neutral | Low — simple contrast | Everyday conversation and writing |
| ものの | Neutral to literary | Mild disappointment or being at a loss | Written narrative, essays |
| ながらも | Neutral to formal | Acknowledges contradiction with a note of resilience or modesty | Set phrases, formal speech, essays |
| とはいえ | Semi-formal | Qualification — "that said" | Editorials, semi-formal speech |
| にもかかわらず | Formal, written | Objective emphasis on the gap | News, academic, official documents |

### When each is preferred

**けれども** is the default. When in doubt, けれども (or its casual forms けど, けれど, けども) works in almost any situation. It makes no particular claim about the speaker's attitude.

> 日本語は　難しいけれども、面白い。
> "Japanese is difficult, but it is interesting."

**ものの** is chosen when the speaker has done or acknowledged something, but the expected payoff has not come. There is often a sense of "I did my part, but..."

> 準備は　したものの、本番では　うまくいかなかった。
> "Although I prepared, it did not go well in the actual performance."

**ながらも** highlights a contradiction between two qualities of the same subject, often with admiration or resilience. It frequently appears in set phrases (残念ながら, 微力ながら).

> 小さいながらも、しっかりした　会社だ。
> "Although small, it is a solid company."

**とはいえ** functions as a discourse pivot. The speaker first concedes a point, then steers toward a qualification. It often begins a new sentence or clause that redirects the argument.

> 景気は　回復している　とはいえ、まだ　油断は　できない。
> "Although the economy is recovering, we still cannot let our guard down."

**にもかかわらず** is reserved for formal contexts where the speaker wants to emphasize that result B happened contrary to strong expectation A. It is objective and carries no personal regret — just the factual force of the contradiction.

> 警告が　出された　にもかかわらず、多くの　住民が　避難しなかった。
> "Despite warnings being issued, many residents did not evacuate."

### Substitution test

Consider the sentence: "Although she is young, she has remarkable insight."

| Version | Effect |
|---|---|
| 若いけれども、すばらしい洞察力がある。 | Neutral statement of fact. |
| 若いものの、すばらしい洞察力がある。 | Slightly literary; mild surprise that youth did not prevent insight. |
| 若いながらも、すばらしい洞察力がある。 | Admiration — "young as she is, she still..." |
| 若いとはいえ、すばらしい洞察力がある。 | Concedes youth, then pivots to praise. Slightly argumentative. |
| 若いにもかかわらず、すばらしい洞察力がある。 | Formal, objective emphasis on the contradiction. |

All five are grammatically correct, but the impression they leave on the listener is different in each case.

## 1.2 のに vs ても vs たところで

These three all express situations where an expected outcome does not materialize, but they differ in the speaker's emotional stance and the reality status of the condition.

### Comparison Table

| Expression | Condition status | Speaker's emotion | Core nuance |
|---|---|---|---|
| のに | Real (already happened) | Frustration, complaint, reproach | "It happened, and I'm unhappy about the result" |
| ても | Hypothetical or real | Neutral acceptance | "Even if / even though — either way, the result holds" |
| たところで | Hypothetical (usually) | Dismissal, futility | "Even if you did it, it would be pointless" |

### のに — Frustration with reality

のに is inherently emotional. It tells the listener that the speaker expected something different and is disappointed or annoyed.

> せっかく　作ったのに、誰も　食べなかった。
> "I went to the trouble of making it, and nobody ate it."

> 約束したのに、来なかった。
> "He promised, and yet he didn't come."

The key signal is that the condition in the first clause is real and accomplished. The speaker invested effort or trust, and the outcome betrayed that investment.

### ても — Neutral conditional concession

ても is the most versatile. It can refer to real or hypothetical situations and carries no inherent emotional charge. The speaker simply states that the result does not change regardless of the condition.

> 薬を　飲んでも、治らないかもしれない。
> "Even if I take medicine, I might not recover."

> 何度　読んでも、この本は　面白い。
> "No matter how many times I read it, this book is interesting."

### たところで — Futility and dismissal

たところで conveys that the action would be pointless. The speaker has already decided the effort is wasted.

> 今から　走った　ところで、電車には　間に合わない。
> "Even if I run now, I won't catch the train."

> 彼に　聞いた　ところで、答えは　分からないだろう。
> "Even if I ask him, he probably won't know the answer."

### Diagnostic: Can you swap them?

Consider: "Even if I apologize, she won't forgive me."

| Version | Nuance |
|---|---|
| 謝っても、許してくれないだろう。 | Neutral conjecture — "even if I do, probably not." |
| 謝ったところで、許してくれないだろう。 | Dismissive — "apologizing would be pointless." |
| 謝ったのに、許してくれなかった。 | Past tense, frustration — "I DID apologize, and she STILL didn't forgive me." |

Notice that のに requires the event to have already happened. You cannot say ×謝るのに許してくれないだろう for a hypothetical future.

## 1.3 一方(で) vs 反面 vs 他方 vs それに対して

These expressions all introduce contrast between two sides, but they differ in what kind of contrast they express and how they connect to the discourse.

### Comparison Table

| Expression | Register | Contrast type | Structure |
|---|---|---|---|
| 一方(で) | Neutral to formal | Parallel contrast or simultaneous trend | Same subject or different subjects |
| 反面 | Neutral to formal | Two-sided nature of the same thing | Same subject (positive and negative sides) |
| 他方 | Formal, written | Balanced counterpoint | Different subjects or perspectives |
| それに対して | Neutral to formal | Direct opposition or comparison | Different subjects, often different sentences |

### 一方(で) — "On the one hand" / "Meanwhile"

一方で presents two things happening in parallel or two contrasting aspects. It is the most general of the four.

> 都市部では　人口が　増えている　一方で、地方では　減少が　続いている。
> "While the population is increasing in urban areas, the decline continues in rural areas."

> 彼女は　優しい　一方で、厳しい　ところも　ある。
> "While she is kind, she also has a strict side."

### 反面 — "On the flip side"

反面 emphasizes that the same subject has two contrasting faces. It is particularly natural when discussing the advantages and disadvantages of a single thing.

> この仕事は　やりがいがある　反面、ストレスも　大きい。
> "This job is rewarding, but on the flip side, the stress is also great."

> 便利な　反面、セキュリティの　リスクが　ある。
> "It is convenient, but on the flip side, there are security risks."

### 他方 — "On the other hand" (formal)

他方 is the formal, written counterpart to 一方で. It often appears in academic or analytical writing when presenting balanced arguments.

> 賛成派は　経済効果を　強調している。他方、反対派は　環境への　影響を　懸念している。
> "Proponents emphasize economic benefits. On the other hand, opponents are concerned about environmental impact."

### それに対して — "In contrast to that"

それに対して marks a direct comparison or opposition, typically between different subjects. It often starts a new sentence, pointing back to the previous one.

> 日本では　電車が　時間通りに　来る。それに対して、この国では　遅れるのが　当たり前だ。
> "In Japan, trains come on time. In contrast, in this country, delays are normal."

> 兄は　社交的だ。それに対して、弟は　内向的である。
> "The older brother is sociable. In contrast, the younger brother is introverted."

### Choosing the right one

| Situation | Best choice |
|---|---|
| Same subject, two sides (pros and cons) | 反面 |
| Two parallel trends or facts | 一方(で) |
| Formal balanced argument with two perspectives | 他方 |
| Direct comparison between two different things | それに対して |

## 1.4 Practice Exercises

### Exercise A — Choose the best expression

For each blank, select the most natural expression from the options given.

**1.** 彼は　日本に　10年　住んでいる（　　）、日本語が　あまり　上手ではない。

a) にもかかわらず　　b) とはいえ　　c) ながらも

**2.** 努力した（　　）、結果が　出なくて　悔しい。

a) ところで　　b) のに　　c) ても

**3.** 今から　勉強した（　　）、明日の　試験には　間に合わないよ。

a) のに　　b) ても　　c) ところで

**4.** このアプリは　無料で　使える。（　　）、有料版は　さらに　機能が　豊富だ。

a) 反面　　b) それに対して　　c) 一方で

**5.** スマートフォンは　便利な（　　）、依存しすぎると　健康に　悪い。

a) 一方で　　b) 反面　　c) それに対して

**6.** 小さい（　　）、よく　まとまった　論文だ。

a) ものの　　b) ながらも　　c) にもかかわらず

### Exercise B — Error correction

Each sentence contains an unnatural expression choice. Identify it and explain why a different expression would be better.

**1.** せっかく　プレゼントを　買ったところで、彼女は　喜ばなかった。

**2.** この薬は　効果がある反面、副作用もある。他方、あの薬は高い反面、副作用が少ない。

**3.** 残念にもかかわらず、ご希望に　沿えません。

### Exercise C — Authentic text analysis

Read the following passage and identify each concessive or contrastive expression. For each one, explain why the author chose that particular form over alternatives.

> 日本の教育制度は世界的に高く評価されている。とはいえ、課題がないわけではない。学力テストの成績が高い一方で、創造性や自主性の育成は十分とは言えない。詰め込み教育は知識を効率的に伝達できる反面、考える力を養う機会を奪いかねない。こうした問題が指摘されているにもかかわらず、改革の歩みは遅い。

## 1.5 Answer Key

### Exercise A

1. **a) にもかかわらず** — 10 years of residence is a strong condition; the formal, emphatic concession fits the objective tone. ながらも would emphasize a quality of the person rather than a factual contradiction; とはいえ would pivot to a qualification rather than state the contradiction directly.

2. **b) のに** — The speaker is expressing frustration about a real past event. ところで would imply futility about a hypothetical; ても would be emotionally neutral.

3. **c) ところで** — The speaker dismisses studying now as pointless. のに requires a real past event; ても would be neutral rather than dismissive.

4. **c) 一方で** — This presents two parallel facts (free version exists; paid version has more features). 反面 would suggest two sides of the same thing; それに対して would imply direct opposition.

5. **b) 反面** — Two sides of the same subject (smartphones). 一方で is possible but 反面 is more precise for pros and cons. それに対して requires different subjects.

6. **b) ながらも** — Expresses admiration for quality despite small size ("small though it is"). ものの would imply disappointment; にもかかわらず is too formal for this evaluative context.

### Exercise B

1. ところで implies futility about a hypothetical, but this sentence describes a real past event with emotional frustration. Change to **のに**: せっかくプレゼントを買ったのに、彼女は喜ばなかった。

2. 反面 is for two sides of the same subject. Using it twice in a comparative structure about two different medicines is unnatural. The second sentence should use **それに対して** or **一方**: それに対して、あの薬は高いものの、副作用が少ない。

3. 残念 is an emotional evaluation, not a factual condition. にもかかわらず requires a factual clause. The natural set phrase is **残念ながら**: 残念ながら、ご希望に沿えません。
