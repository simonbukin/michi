# Chapter 5 — Register Choice as Meaning

In all the previous chapters of Stage 6, we have been comparing near-synonymous grammar forms and asking why a native speaker chooses one over another. In every case, part of the answer has been register — the level of formality, the social context, the relationship between speaker and listener. This chapter makes register itself the subject.

Register is not decoration. It is not a layer of politeness applied on top of meaning. In Japanese, register is meaning. The choice between saying something casually and saying it formally changes what you communicate, not just how polite you sound. It reveals your relationship to the listener, your emotional distance from the topic, your institutional role, and your social positioning.

This chapter examines how the same idea transforms as it moves through five register levels, how speakers mix registers for strategic effect, and how violations of expected register function as powerful communicative signals.

---

## 5.1 The Same Idea at Five Register Levels

Japanese register is not a binary switch between polite and casual. It operates on a spectrum with at least five distinguishable levels, each with its own grammar, vocabulary, and sentence-ending patterns.

### The Five Levels

| Level | Name | Typical Context | Sentence Endings | Vocabulary Layer |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Casual (くだけた) | Friends, family, internal monologue | Plain forms, contracted forms, sentence-final particles | 和語 (native Japanese), slang |
| 2 | Polite (丁寧) | Standard polite conversation, customer service | です/ます | Mixed 和語 and 漢語 |
| 3 | Formal (改まった) | Business writing, formal speeches, news | である, noun-heavy, complex compound sentences | 漢語 dominant |
| 4 | Academic (学術的) | Research papers, legal documents, policy | である, passive constructions, nominalization | Technical 漢語, specialized terms |
| 5 | Literary (文語的) | Literature, poetry, traditional arts, proverbs | Classical endings (なり, たり, べし), archaic vocabulary | Classical Japanese, literary 漢語 |

### Demonstration 1: "This problem is difficult to solve"

**Level 1 — Casual:**

> この問題、解決すんのマジむずい。
> "This problem — solving it is seriously hard."

Features: Topic marker は dropped; する contracted to すん; マジ (slang intensifier); むずい (colloquial shortening of 難しい); no copula.

**Level 2 — Polite:**

> この問題は解決するのが難しいです。
> "This problem is difficult to solve."

Features: Standard です ending; clear topic-comment structure; neutral vocabulary.

**Level 3 — Formal:**

> この問題の解決は容易ではない。
> "The resolution of this problem is not easy."

Features: Nominalization (解決 as a noun rather than a verb); 容易 (formal vocabulary for "easy"); ではない (plain negative, appropriate in formal written style).

**Level 4 — Academic:**

> 当該問題の解決は極めて困難であると言わざるを得ない。
> "One must say that the resolution of the problem in question is extremely difficult."

Features: 当該 (formal demonstrative "the said, the relevant"); 極めて (formal intensifier); 困難 (formal for "difficult"); である (academic copula); 言わざるを得ない (double negative construction meaning "cannot help but say").

**Level 5 — Literary:**

> この難題、解くこと叶わず。
> "This difficult problem — to solve it is not possible."

Features: Classical negative 叶わず (from 叶う "to be possible" + classical negative ず); 難題 (literary compound for "difficult problem"); elliptical style.

### Demonstration 2: "I think we should reconsider"

**Level 1 — Casual:**

> もう一回考え直した方がよくない？
> "Wouldn't it be better to think it over again?"

Features: Question ending seeking agreement; よくない？ (casual negative question for suggestion); もう一回 (casual "one more time").

**Level 2 — Polite:**

> 再検討した方がいいと思います。
> "I think it would be good to reconsider."

Features: と思います (polite opinion marker); 再検討 (slightly formal verb but acceptable in polite speech).

**Level 3 — Formal:**

> 再検討の必要があるものと考える。
> "I consider that there is a need for reconsideration."

Features: Nominalized structure (再検討の必要); ものと考える (formal hedged assertion); no です/ます.

**Level 4 — Academic:**

> 本件については、再検討を要するものと思料する。
> "Regarding this matter, it is deliberated that reconsideration is required."

Features: 本件 (formal "this matter"); 思料する (highly formal "to deliberate/consider" — bureaucratic language); を要する (formal "to require").

**Level 5 — Literary:**

> 改めて慮るべし。
> "One should reconsider."

Features: Classical auxiliary べし (expressing obligation/advisability); 慮る (おもんぱかる — literary verb "to deliberate, to ponder").

### Demonstration 3: "The number of tourists is increasing"

**Level 1 — Casual:**

> 観光客めっちゃ増えてるよね。
> "Tourists are super increasing, right?"

Features: めっちゃ (casual intensifier); ている contracted to てる; よね (seeking agreement); subject marker が dropped.

**Level 2 — Polite:**

> 観光客の数が増えています。
> "The number of tourists is increasing."

Features: Standard ています; explicit 数 ("number"); neutral and informative.

**Level 3 — Formal:**

> 観光客数は増加傾向にある。
> "The number of tourists is on an increasing trend."

Features: 観光客数 (compound noun without の); 増加傾向 (formal compound "increasing trend"); にある (formal existential); no です/ます.

**Level 4 — Academic:**

> 訪日外国人観光客数は前年比15.2%増の3,188万人に達し、過去最高を更新したことが確認される。
> "It is confirmed that the number of inbound foreign tourists reached 31.88 million, a 15.2% increase over the previous year, setting a new record."

Features: 訪日外国人 (formal compound "inbound foreigners"); 前年比 (year-on-year comparison); 確認される (passive, impersonal); precise statistics.

**Level 5 — Literary:**

> 異国の客、年ごとに数を増し、今や空前の賑わいを見す。
> "Foreign guests, increasing in number year by year, now display unprecedented prosperity."

Features: 異国 (literary "foreign country"); 年ごとに (literary phrasing); 空前 (literary "unprecedented"); 賑わい (somewhat archaic "bustling prosperity").

### Demonstration 4: "I don't understand"

| Level | Expression | Context |
|---|---|---|
| Casual | 分かんない。/ 分かんないんだけど。 | Close friends, family |
| Polite | 分かりません。 | Standard polite speech |
| Formal | 理解しかねます。 | Business, formal refusal |
| Academic | 当該論旨は不明瞭であると言わざるを得ない。 | Academic critique |
| Literary | 解し難し。 | Classical, archaic |

Each of these expresses the same basic meaning, but they position the speaker entirely differently. "分かんない" is intimate and unguarded. "理解しかねます" is distanced and institutional — it says "I represent an organization that cannot understand." "解し難し" is the voice of centuries.

## 5.2 How Register Choice Communicates Social Positioning, Emotional Distance, and Institutional Context

### Social Positioning

Register choice tells the listener where the speaker places themselves in the social hierarchy relative to the listener, the topic, and the institution they represent.

**Example: A doctor speaking to a patient**

> まあ、大丈夫ですよ。ちょっと薬を出しますから、一週間飲んでみてください。
> "Well, you'll be fine. I'll prescribe some medicine, so try taking it for a week."

This is Level 2 — polite but warm. The doctor uses です/ます but also uses まあ and ちょっと, which are casual softeners. This positions the doctor as an authority figure who is also approachable and reassuring.

Now compare:

> 検査の結果、軽度の炎症が認められます。抗炎症薬を一週間分処方いたしますので、用法に従って服用してください。
> "As a result of the examination, mild inflammation has been observed. I will prescribe one week's worth of anti-inflammatory medication, so please take it according to the instructions."

This is Level 3 moving toward Level 4 — formal medical register. The same doctor, delivering a more serious diagnosis, shifts upward. The formality communicates: "I am speaking to you now as a medical professional, and I want you to take this seriously."

### Emotional Distance

Higher register creates emotional distance. Lower register creates intimacy. Speakers exploit this to control how close or distant they appear.

**Example: Expressing sympathy**

> それは大変だったね。
> "That must have been tough." (Casual — intimate, warm, personal)

> それは大変でしたね。
> "That must have been difficult." (Polite — sympathetic but maintains social distance)

> ご心痛のほど、お察しいたします。
> "I understand the extent of your distress." (Formal — institutional sympathy, emotionally distant)

The same sympathy is expressed at three levels. The casual version is something a friend says. The polite version is what a colleague says. The formal version is what appears in an official condolence letter. The higher the register, the more the sympathy is performed as a social obligation rather than felt as a personal emotion.

### Institutional Context

Certain registers signal that the speaker is representing an institution rather than speaking as an individual.

**Example: A station announcement**

> 次は渋谷、渋谷です。お出口は右側です。
> "Next is Shibuya, Shibuya. The exit is on the right side."

This is institutional Level 2-3. The use of お出口 (honorific prefix), the repetition of the station name, and the impersonal phrasing all signal "I am the voice of the train company, not an individual person."

Compare an informal version that would never be used:

> ~~次、渋谷だよ。右から降りてね。~~
> "Next is Shibuya. Get off on the right, okay?"

This sounds like a friend on the train giving directions. The absence of institutional markers makes it inappropriate for a public announcement.

### Register and Power

The ability to control register is itself a form of social power. People who can shift fluidly between registers — speaking casually with friends, formally with clients, and academically in writing — are perceived as educated and socially competent.

Conversely, being unable to produce the appropriate register creates social friction:

- Using casual register with a superior: perceived as rude or immature
- Using overly formal register with close friends: perceived as cold or distant
- Using academic register in casual conversation: perceived as pretentious
- Using casual register in a business email: perceived as unprofessional

## 5.3 Register Mixing — When and Why Speakers Shift Register Mid-Sentence

Register mixing is one of the most revealing features of natural Japanese. Speakers do not maintain a single register throughout a conversation — they shift, sometimes within a single sentence. These shifts are always meaningful.

### Pattern 1: Formal Frame with Casual Core

A speaker maintains a formal outer structure but drops to casual register for the emotional core of their statement.

> 正直に申しますと、もう限界なんです。
> "To be honest, I've reached my limit."

正直に申しますと is formal (申す is humble speech). But もう限界なんです uses the casual-feeling なんです (explanatory の + です). The shift signals: "I am maintaining politeness, but I am letting you see my real feelings."

This pattern is extremely common in Japanese workplace communication. The formal frame shows respect; the casual core shows sincerity.

### Pattern 2: Casual Speech with Sudden Formal Insert

A speaker is talking casually and suddenly uses a formal expression, creating emphasis or humor.

> いやー、昨日のテスト、まじで壊滅状態だったわ。
> "Man, yesterday's test — I was seriously in a state of total destruction."

壊滅状態 (かいめつじょうたい — "state of total destruction") is formal/military vocabulary, used here in a casual sentence for dramatic, humorous effect. The register clash between まじで (very casual) and 壊滅状態 (very formal) creates comedy.

This is a common pattern in Japanese humor — using absurdly formal or literary language in casual contexts.

### Pattern 3: Gradual Downshift in Extended Conversation

Two colleagues who are not yet close begin a conversation formally and gradually shift to more casual register as comfort increases.

> A: 田中さん、この件についてお聞きしたいことがあるのですが。
> "Tanaka-san, there is something I'd like to ask you about this matter."

> B: ああ、いいですよ。何でしょうか。
> "Oh, sure. What is it?"

> A: 報告書の締め切りなんですけど、来週まで延ばしてもらえませんか。
> "About the report deadline — could you extend it until next week?"

> B: うーん、ちょっと難しいかもしれないけど、一日くらいなら。
> "Hmm, that might be a bit difficult, but if it's about one day..."

> A: ありがとう。助かります。
> "Thanks. That helps."

Notice the trajectory: お聞きしたいことがあるのですが → なんですけど → ありがとう. Speaker A starts at Level 3, moves to Level 2, and ends at the border of Level 1-2. Speaker B's けど (dropping ですが) signals permission for A to relax as well.

### Pattern 4: Strategic Upshift for Authority

A manager speaks casually with their team but shifts upward when making an official decision.

> まあ、みんなの意見は分かった。いろいろ考えてくれてありがとう。ただ、本件については、予定通り進めることとする。
> "Well, I understand everyone's opinions. Thanks for thinking about this. However, regarding this matter, it is decided that we proceed as planned."

The final sentence — 本件については、予定通り進めることとする — is Level 3-4, with ことにする elevated to the more authoritative ことと する. The manager uses the formal register to signal: "This is no longer a discussion. This is a decision."

### Pattern 5: Register Drop for Emotional Breakthrough

A speaker who has been maintaining formal register drops to casual in a moment of genuine emotion.

> 長い間、ご支援をいただきまして、誠にありがとうございました。皆様のお力添えなくしては、ここまで来ることはできませんでした。…本当に、ありがとう。
> "For a long time, I have received your support, and I am truly grateful. Without everyone's assistance, I could not have come this far. ...Really, thank you."

The final ありがとう (plain form, no ございました) breaks from the formal speech. This register drop signals authentic emotion — the speaker has stepped out of their institutional role and is speaking as a person.

This technique is used in farewell speeches, retirement addresses, and other emotionally significant moments. The register break is what makes the audience feel the sincerity.

### Pattern 6: Written Register Mixing in Literature

Novelists deliberately mix registers to create character voice. Consider this internal monologue from a character who is an office worker:

> 課長のあの言い方はないだろう。プロジェクトの進捗状況に鑑み、再考の余地があるものと思われます、だと？要するに、やり直せってことだろ。ふざけんな。
> "The section chief's way of saying it was wrong. 'In light of the project's progress, it is thought that there is room for reconsideration' — is that it? Basically, he means redo it. Give me a break."

The passage shifts from casual complaint (ないだろう) to quoted formal business language (に鑑み, ものと思われます) back to rough casual (やり直せ, ふざけんな). The register mixing reveals the character's bitter interpretation of corporate euphemism.

## 5.4 Recognizing Register Violations — What Unexpected Register Signals

When a speaker uses a register that does not match the expected context, it always means something. Register violations are never random — they are communicative acts.

### Violation 1: Unexpectedly Formal — Creating Distance

**Situation:** Two friends who normally speak casually. One suddenly becomes formal.

> A: 明日、映画見に行かない？
> "Want to go see a movie tomorrow?"

> B: 申し訳ございませんが、明日は予定がございまして。
> "I am terribly sorry, but I have plans tomorrow."

B's response is absurdly formal for a friendship context. This signals one of several things:
- **Anger:** B is upset with A and using formality to create distance. ("We are not close enough for you to ask me that.")
- **Sarcasm:** B is being humorous, playing at formality to deflect.
- **Genuine discomfort:** B really does not want to go and is hiding behind formality.

The listener must read the context to determine which interpretation is correct. But the register violation itself is unmistakable — something is off.

### Violation 2: Unexpectedly Casual — Asserting Intimacy or Dominance

**Situation:** A new employee speaks casually to a senior colleague.

> 先輩、これ、もう終わったよ。次何やればいい？
> "Senpai, I finished this already. What should I do next?"

In a traditional Japanese workplace, this casual register from a junior to a senior would be a violation. It could signal:
- **Regional norms:** Some industries and regions are more casual.
- **Cluelessness:** The new employee does not know the norms.
- **Intentional assertion:** The employee is signaling that they do not accept the hierarchical relationship.

### Violation 3: Formal Language in Intimate Settings — Emotional Withdrawal

**Situation:** A married couple. One partner suddenly becomes formal.

> A: 今日の晩ご飯、何がいい？
> "What do you want for dinner tonight?"

> B: 何でも構いません。
> "Anything is fine." (Polite/formal)

Between spouses who normally speak casually, 構いません (polite negative) instead of 構わない (casual) or いいよ (casual) signals emotional withdrawal. B is upset, disengaged, or creating distance. The formality is a wall.

Japanese speakers are acutely sensitive to this kind of register shift. A partner who suddenly becomes polite is communicating anger, hurt, or disconnection without saying a word about it directly.

### Violation 4: Casual Language in Formal Settings — Deliberate Defiance or Calculated Approachability

**Situation:** A politician at a press conference.

> 記者: 今回の不祥事について、どのようにお考えですか。
> "What are your thoughts on this scandal?"

> 政治家: いやー、もうほんとにね、申し訳ないなと思ってます。
> "Yeaaah, I really, truly, you know, feel sorry about it."

The politician uses casual hedging (いやー, ほんとにね) in a formal press conference. This can signal:
- **Calculated approachability:** "I'm being real with you. I'm not hiding behind formal language."
- **Evasion:** The casual register makes the apology feel less weighty and less committed.
- **Incompetence:** The politician cannot produce formal register under pressure.

Experienced listeners will distinguish between these interpretations based on tone, body language, and political context.

### Violation 5: Academic Register in Everyday Life — Intellectualizing or Humor

**Situation:** A friend describing their weekend.

> 土日は基本的に自宅で過ごした。体力の回復を最優先事項と位置づけ、睡眠時間の確保に努めた。
> "The weekend was fundamentally spent at home. I positioned physical recovery as the highest priority item and endeavored to secure sleep time."

This is absurdly academic for describing a lazy weekend. The speaker is being humorous — using Level 4 language for a Level 1 activity. This kind of register play is a staple of Japanese internet humor and among certain friend groups.

### Violation 6: Literary Register in Modern Context — Gravitas or Parody

**Situation:** A social media post about a minor inconvenience.

> コンビニにて最後のプリンを我が手にせんとせし刹那、隣の者にかっさらわれたり。無念なり。
> "At the convenience store, at the very moment I was about to take the last pudding into my hand, it was snatched away by the person next to me. How regrettable."

Classical grammar (せんとせし, られたり, なり) applied to a trivial modern situation. This creates comedy through the absurd mismatch between literary gravitas and pudding.

### Summary: What Register Violations Communicate

| Violation Type | Possible Signals |
|---|---|
| Unexpectedly high register | Distance, anger, sarcasm, institutional authority, discomfort |
| Unexpectedly low register | Intimacy, dominance, sincerity, disrespect, humor |
| Fluctuating register | Nervousness, uncertainty, emotional instability, strategic manipulation |
| Academic in casual context | Humor, intellectualizing, social signaling |
| Casual in formal context | Approachability, evasion, defiance, incompetence |
| Literary in modern context | Humor, gravitas, parody, cultural performance |

## 5.5 Practice: Identifying Register and Analyzing What It Communicates

### Exercise 1: Identify the Register Level

For each sentence, identify the register level (1-5) and explain what features mark it.

**1.** 本報告書は、令和七年度における環境政策の実施状況を取りまとめたものである。

Register: **Level 4 (Academic/Bureaucratic)**

Features: 本報告書 ("this report" — institutional demonstrative); 令和七年度における ("in fiscal year Reiwa 7" — formal time expression with における); 取りまとめたものである (nominalized with もの + である copula); no personal subjects.

**2.** えー、ちょっと待って。それ、まじで言ってんの？

Register: **Level 1 (Casual)**

Features: えー (filler); ちょっと待って (plain imperative); まじで (slang "seriously"); 言ってんの (contracted ている → てん + の question particle).

**3.** 皆様のご理解とご協力を賜りますよう、お願い申し上げます。

Register: **Level 3-4 (Formal/Institutional)**

Features: 皆様 (honorific "everyone"); 賜る (たまわる — very humble "to receive"); ますよう (formal volitional); お願い申し上げます (highest-level request formula).

**4.** 秋深き隣は何をする人ぞ。

Register: **Level 5 (Literary/Classical)**

Features: Classical adjective form 深き (modern: 深い); ぞ (classical emphatic particle); overall haiku-like compression. This is a famous haiku by Matsuo Basho.

**5.** 最近ちょっと忙しくて、なかなか連絡できなくてすみません。

Register: **Level 2 (Polite)**

Features: すみません (polite apology); but also ちょっと and なかなか (conversational softeners); a blend of polite ending with casual phrasing.

### Exercise 2: Analyze Register Shifts in a Conversation

Read the following conversation between a department head (部長) and a subordinate (部下) and identify every register shift. Explain what each shift communicates.

> 部下: 部長、少々お時間をいただけますでしょうか。
> 部長: うん、いいよ。どうした？
> 部下: 実は、来月の人事異動の件なのですが…
> 部長: ああ、あの件ね。
> 部下: はい。正直なところ、今のチームを離れるのは…その…
> 部長: 嫌なんだろ？
> 部下: …はい。
> 部長: まあ、気持ちは分かるよ。俺だって同じ立場なら嫌だ。ただな、これは会社としての決定だから。
> 部下: 承知しております。ただ、もし可能であれば、時期を少し…
> 部長: そこは難しいな。上からの話だし。でもな、お前のことは俺がちゃんと見てるから。新しい部署でも、何かあったら言え。
> 部下: ありがとうございます。
> 部長: よし。じゃ、頑張れよ。

### Analysis

**部下's opening:** Level 3-4 (少々お時間をいただけますでしょうか — very polite, formal request). This is the expected register for approaching a superior with a request.

**部長's response:** Level 1 (うん、いいよ。どうした？). The department head immediately drops to casual. This signals: "You can relax. I'm approachable."

**部下's explanation:** Level 2-3 (実は…の件なのですが). Still polite but slightly relaxed from the opening, influenced by the boss's casual tone.

**部長's interjection:** Level 1 (ああ、あの件ね). Casual recognition. The boss is tracking the conversation without formality.

**部下's hesitation:** Level 2 dropping toward 1 (正直なところ…その…). The subordinate begins to express genuine feeling. The hesitation (その…) shows emotional difficulty breaking through formal composure.

**部長's direct question:** Level 1 (嫌なんだろ？ — "You don't want to, right?"). The boss cuts through the subordinate's politeness barrier with a blunt, casual question. This is an act of intimacy — "I can see what you're feeling, so let me say it for you."

**部下's response:** Minimal (はい). Simple, honest. The subordinate accepts the boss's reading of their feelings.

**部長's empathy then authority:** Level 1 → Level 2-3 (まあ、気持ちは分かるよ → これは会社としての決定だから). The boss begins with casual empathy (分かるよ) then shifts upward with 会社としての決定 ("the company's decision"). The upshift signals: "I personally sympathize, but I am now speaking as a representative of the organization."

**部下's formal acceptance:** Level 3-4 (承知しております). The subordinate returns to formal register to acknowledge the institutional decision. This signals: "I understand my role."

**部長's promise:** Level 1 (お前のことは俺がちゃんと見てるから。何かあったら言え). Very casual, even rough (お前, 俺, 言え — plain imperative). This is the language of personal loyalty and mentorship. By dropping to the lowest register, the boss is saying: "Forget the company talk. I'm telling you as a person that I have your back."

**部下's gratitude:** Level 2 (ありがとうございます). Polite but warm. The subordinate does not match the boss's casual register (that would be presumptuous) but the ございます carries genuine feeling.

**部長's closing:** Level 1 (よし。じゃ、頑張れよ). Casual encouragement. The meeting ends on a personal, warm note.

### Exercise 3: Rewrite at Different Register Levels

Take the following Level 2 sentence and rewrite it at all five levels. Then explain what each version communicates differently.

**Base sentence (Level 2):** この問題について、もっと議論する必要があると思います。
"I think we need to discuss this problem more."

**Level 1:**
> この問題、もっと話し合わないとまずくない？
> "This problem — don't we need to talk more about it or it's gonna be bad?"

Communicates: Urgency, personal investment, peer-level relationship. The speaker is emotionally engaged and speaking to equals.

**Level 2:** (Given above.)

Communicates: Standard professional opinion. Appropriate for meetings, emails, and polite conversation with colleagues.

**Level 3:**
> この問題については、さらなる議論が必要である。
> "Regarding this problem, further discussion is necessary."

Communicates: The speaker is presenting an objective assessment, not a personal opinion. The removal of と思います signals institutional or analytical authority rather than individual belief.

**Level 4:**
> 当該問題に関しては、より包括的かつ多角的な議論を要するものと思料される。
> "Regarding the matter in question, it is deliberated that more comprehensive and multifaceted discussion is required."

Communicates: Bureaucratic authority. The passive construction and specialized vocabulary signal that this is an institutional pronouncement, not an individual opinion. The speaker represents a committee, a ministry, or a research body.

**Level 5:**
> この件、なお議すべし。
> "This matter — further deliberation is warranted."

Communicates: Classical authority and gravitas. This might appear in a historical drama, a formal proclamation, or a literary essay. It has an air of finality and wisdom.

> 佐藤さんから打ち合わせの依頼が来ました。
> 例の提案の件で、いくつか質問があるみたいです。
> 来週のどこかで時間もらえますか？
> 自分も同席した方がいいですかね。
>
> 山田

### Analysis

**Email 1** is Level 3-4 business Japanese. Every sentence uses keigo or formal structures:
- いつもお世話になっております (set phrase, business opening)
- ご提案いただいた (humble receipt of the client's proposal)
- 検討いたしました (humble past of 検討する)
- 恐れ入りますが (formal apology for the imposition)
- いただけますと幸いです (very indirect request)
- 何卒よろしくお願い申し上げます (highest-level closing)

This register communicates: institutional respect, professional distance, the client is valued and treated with deference.

**Email 2** is Level 1-2. The same information is repackaged for an internal audience:
- 打ち合わせの依頼が来ました (plain reporting, no keigo)
- みたいです (casual evidential — Email 1 used ございます)
- 時間もらえますか？ (direct request without keigo softening)
- いいですかね (casual-leaning question with ね)

This register communicates: the internal relationship is relaxed, the boss is approachable, efficiency matters more than formality. The dramatic register drop between the two emails is completely normal in Japanese business culture — the same person shifts from Level 4 externally to Level 2 internally within minutes.

### Exercise 5: Register Violation Detection

For each exchange, identify the register violation and explain what it might communicate.

**1.**
> 上司: 明日の会議の資料、準備できた？
> 部下: 当該資料につきましては、本日中に完成の見込みでございます。
> 上司: …大丈夫か？

The subordinate responds to a casual question with Level 4 bureaucratic language. The boss's confused response (大丈夫か？ — "Are you okay?") shows the violation is noticed. Possible interpretations: the subordinate is being sarcastically formal because they are annoyed about the last-minute request; the subordinate is nervous and over-correcting; or the subordinate is making a joke.

**2.**
> 取引先: 納品日程について確認させていただきたいのですが。
> 営業担当: ああ、それね。大丈夫、ちゃんとやってるから。

The sales representative responds to a formal client inquiry with Level 1 casual language. This is a serious violation in business context. It could signal: incompetence (the representative does not know how to speak to clients), excessive familiarity (the representative assumes a closer relationship than exists), or deliberate casualness (some industries tolerate this).

**3.**
> 妻: ねえ、今週末どこか行かない？
> 夫: 検討いたします。

A husband responds to his wife's casual invitation with Level 3-4 business language. This is clearly not genuine formality — it is either humor (playing at being a corporate robot) or distance (signaling that he does not want to engage warmly). The wife would immediately recognize this as non-standard and interpret it accordingly.

## Reading Passage: 敬語と現代社会

> 敬語は日本語の根幹をなす体系であり、社会的関係を言語的に表現する手段として、千年以上にわたって発達してきた。しかし、現代社会において、敬語のあり方は大きく変容しつつある。
>
> かつては、年齢や社会的地位に基づく上下関係が明確であり、それに応じた敬語の使い分けも比較的単純であった。目上の人には尊敬語を、目下の人には普通体を使えばよかった。
>
> ところが、現代の職場では、年下の上司や年上の部下が珍しくなくなった。フラットな組織構造を志向する企業も増えている。「敬語を使うべき相手」と「使わなくてもいい相手」の境界が曖昧になりつつあるのだ。
>
> さらに、SNSやメッセージアプリの普及により、文字によるコミュニケーションが爆発的に増加した。メールでは丁寧に書く人も、LINEでは「了解」の一言で済ませることが多い。同じ相手に対して、メールとLINEで全く異なるレジスターを使い分けるという現象は、従来の敬語研究では想定されていなかった事態である。
>
> 若い世代の間では、「丁寧語は使うが、尊敬語と謙譲語の区別がつかない」という人が増えているとされている。「ご覧になりますか」と言うべき場面で「見られますか」と言う、あるいは「拝見します」と言うべきところで「見させていただきます」と言う——こうした「マニュアル敬語」や「させていただく敬語」の蔓延は、敬語体系の単純化の兆候なのかもしれない。
>
> 言語学者の中には、これを「敬語の崩壊」として嘆く者もいれば、「敬語の進化」として前向きに捉える者もいる。確かなのは、敬語——すなわちレジスターの選択——が、今なお日本語話者のアイデンティティと社会的立場を映し出す鏡であるということだ。言葉遣いは変わる。しかし、言葉遣いが意味を持つという事実は変わらない。

### Translation

> Keigo is a system that forms the foundation of the Japanese language, and as a means of linguistically expressing social relationships, it has developed over more than a thousand years. However, in modern society, the nature of keigo is undergoing significant transformation.
>
> In the past, hierarchical relationships based on age and social status were clear, and the corresponding use of keigo was relatively simple. One needed only to use honorific language with superiors and plain language with subordinates.
>
> However, in today's workplace, younger bosses and older subordinates are no longer uncommon. Companies aiming for flat organizational structures are also increasing. The boundary between "people you should use keigo with" and "people you don't need to use keigo with" is becoming increasingly ambiguous.
>
> Furthermore, due to the spread of social media and messaging apps, text-based communication has increased explosively. Even people who write politely in emails often settle for a single word like "了解" (roger) on LINE. The phenomenon of using completely different registers with the same person via email versus LINE was a situation not anticipated by traditional keigo research.
>
> Among younger generations, the number of people who "use polite language but cannot distinguish between honorific and humble speech" is said to be increasing. Saying "見られますか" where one should say "ご覧になりますか," or saying "見させていただきます" where one should say "拝見します" — the spread of such "manual keigo" and "させていただく keigo" may be a sign of simplification of the keigo system.
>
> Among linguists, there are those who lament this as "the collapse of keigo" and those who view it positively as "the evolution of keigo." What is certain is that keigo — that is, the choice of register — remains a mirror that reflects the identity and social position of Japanese speakers. Word choice changes. But the fact that word choice carries meaning does not change.
