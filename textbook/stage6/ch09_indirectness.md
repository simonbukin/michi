# Chapter 9 — Indirectness as Communication

In most language textbooks, you learn what words mean. In this chapter, you learn what silence means, what half-finished sentences mean, and what polite non-answers mean. Japanese communication relies heavily on indirectness — not as evasion, but as a sophisticated system where what is left unsaid carries as much weight as what is spoken. A speaker who says everything explicitly can sound aggressive, childish, or foreign. A speaker who says just enough and trusts the listener to complete the thought sounds mature, considerate, and socially fluent.

This is not a cultural curiosity. It is a core language skill. If you cannot interpret an unfinished sentence, a deflected question, or a bureaucratic non-answer, you will misread conversations constantly — even when you understand every word.

The goal of this chapter is to make you fluent in what Japanese does not say.

## 9.1 ちょっと... as Universal Softener and Refusal

### The Power of the Trailing Sentence

The single most important indirect speech act in Japanese is the unfinished sentence. And the single most important word in that unfinished sentence is ちょっと.

On its surface, ちょっと means "a little" or "a bit." But in practice, a trailing ちょっと... (with the sentence left incomplete) is a complete communication act. It means: "I cannot / do not want to do what you are asking, and I am giving you the opportunity to understand that without forcing me to say no explicitly."

> A: 今度の土曜日、飲みに行かない？
> A: "Want to go drinking this Saturday?"

> B: 土曜日は　ちょっと...
> B: "Saturday is a little..." (= I can't / I don't want to.)

Speaker B has not said no. Speaker B has not given a reason. Speaker B has merely indicated that Saturday presents some kind of difficulty — and trusts Speaker A to interpret this as a polite refusal without pressing further.

### Why This Works

The mechanism depends on a shared cultural contract: the listener is expected to read the implication and not force the speaker to make the refusal explicit. Pressing for details ("What's wrong with Saturday? Why not?") violates this contract and puts the speaker in an uncomfortable position. A socially skilled listener responds with something that acknowledges the refusal gracefully.

> A: 今度の土曜日、飲みに行かない？
> A: "Want to go drinking this Saturday?"

> B: 土曜日は　ちょっと...
> B: "Saturday is a little..."

> A: そっか。じゃあ、また今度ね。
> A: "I see. Well, next time then."

This exchange is complete. Both parties understand that the invitation was declined. No reason was given or requested. No one lost face.

### Variations on the Trailing Sentence

ちょっと is the most common filler, but many other words and phrases serve the same trailing-sentence function.

### Trailing Expressions Table

| Expression | Literal meaning | Pragmatic meaning when trailing |
|---|---|---|
| ちょっと... | "A little..." | Soft refusal or expression of difficulty |
| それは　ちょっと... | "That is a little..." | Slightly stronger refusal — the proposal itself is problematic |
| う〜ん、ちょっと... | "Hmm, a little..." | Hesitation leading to refusal |
| 難しいかなと... | "I wonder if it's difficult..." | Refusal framed as objective assessment |
| どうかなと思って... | "I was thinking whether..." | Non-committal hedge, often negative |
| ちょっと考えさせて... | "Let me think a little..." | Delay that often signals eventual refusal |
| あまり... | "Not really..." | Soft negative without completing the thought |
| ちょっとそれは... | "That, a little..." | The proposal is being singled out as problematic |

### What They Said vs. What They Meant

| What was said | What was meant | Social function |
|---|---|---|
| 土曜日はちょっと... | I can't go on Saturday. | Refuses without giving a reason |
| その映画はちょっと... | I don't want to see that movie. | Rejects the specific proposal |
| う〜ん、どうかな... | Probably not. | Expresses doubt that functions as refusal |
| 行きたいのは行きたいんだけど... | I want to go but I can't. | Validates the invitation while declining |
| ちょっと最近忙しくて... | I'm too busy (real or as excuse). | Provides a face-saving reason |
| また今度... | Let's not do this now (and maybe never). | Postponement that may be permanent |

### Dialogue: Declining an Invitation

> 佐藤：来週の金曜日、部署の歓迎会があるんですけど、田中さんも来ませんか。
> Sato: "There's a welcome party for the department next Friday — won't you come too, Tanaka-san?"

> 田中：金曜日ですか... ちょっとその日は予定が入っていて...
> Tanaka: "Friday... I have plans that day, a little..."

> 佐藤：あ、そうですか。残念ですね。
> Sato: "Oh, is that so? That's a shame."

> 田中：すみません。また誘ってください。
> Tanaka: "I'm sorry. Please invite me again."

> 佐藤：もちろん。また今度ぜひ。
> Sato: "Of course. Definitely next time."

**Analysis:** Tanaka never says "I can't go" or "No." She says she has plans (予定が入っていて) and trails off. The て-form left hanging is the grammatical mechanism — it implies a continuation ("...so I can't come") that is never spoken. Sato immediately understands and does not press. Tanaka's すみません and また誘ってください are social formulas that maintain the relationship.

### The て-Form Trail-Off

The most grammatically significant pattern in Japanese indirectness is leaving a sentence at the て-form. The て-form inherently signals continuation — it says "and then..." or "so..." — but when the speaker stops there, the listener must supply the conclusion.

> ちょっと今、手が離せなくて...
> "I can't take my hands off what I'm doing right now, so..." (= I can't help you now.)

> 実は明日、出張が入っていまして...
> "Actually, a business trip came up for tomorrow, so..." (= I can't attend.)

> 申し訳ないんですが、予算の関係で...
> "I'm sorry, but due to budget constraints..." (= We can't do what you're asking.)

In each case, the speaker has given enough information for the listener to construct the refusal independently. The speaker never has to utter the negative conclusion.

### Register and the Trailing Sentence

The trailing sentence works across all registers, but its form changes.

### Register Variations Table

| Register | Example | Context |
|---|---|---|
| Casual | 土曜はちょっと... | Friends, same-age colleagues |
| Polite | 土曜日はちょっと難しいかもしれません... | Acquaintances, colleagues |
| Formal | 土曜日は少々都合が悪く... | Business, senior colleagues |
| Humble/business | 土曜日はあいにく別件がございまして... | Client-facing, formal business |

Note how the vocabulary escalates (ちょっと → 少々, 都合が悪い → 別件がございまして) but the underlying strategy — trailing off before the explicit refusal — remains identical.

| Word | Reading | Pitch | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| 歓迎会 | かんげいかい | ⓪ | welcome party |
| 予定 | よてい | ⓪ | plans, schedule |
| 手が離せない | てがはなせない | — | cannot step away (from what one is doing) |
| 出張 | しゅっちょう | ⓪ | business trip |
| 予算 | よさん | ① | budget |
| 別件 | べっけん | ⓪ | another matter |

## 9.2 Negative Questions as Suggestions

### The Indirectness Spectrum of Proposals

When you want someone to do something, Japanese offers a spectrum of directness. The most indirect forms use negative questions — asking whether the listener would not do something, rather than telling them to do it. This section maps that spectrum and explains when each level is appropriate.

### Directness Spectrum Table

| Form | Example | Directness | Typical context |
|---|---|---|---|
| Imperative | 行け。 | Very direct | Military, anger, male rough speech |
| ～なさい | 行きなさい。 | Direct, authoritative | Parent to child, teacher to student |
| ～てください | 行ってください。 | Standard polite request | General polite speech |
| ～たらどうですか | 行ったらどうですか。 | Suggestion (mild) | Advice-giving, somewhat detached |
| ～ませんか | 行きませんか。 | Invitation | Social invitations |
| ～たらいかがですか | 行ったらいかがですか。 | Suggestion (formal) | Business, formal advice |
| ～ないんですか | 行かないんですか。 | Surprise / implicit suggestion | When expected action hasn't happened |
| ～てみては | 行ってみては... | Tentative suggestion | Gentle advice, trailing off |

### ～たらどうですか — Suggestion with Distance

This form suggests an action while maintaining emotional distance. The speaker is not invested in whether the listener follows the advice — they are merely pointing out an option.

> 疲れているなら、少し休んだらどうですか。
> "If you're tired, how about resting a bit?"

> そんなに嫌なら、辞めたらどうですか。
> "If you hate it that much, how about quitting?"

Note the second example. Depending on tone, ～たらどうですか can sound cold or even confrontational. It can imply "Why haven't you done this obvious thing already?" This is a critical pragmatic distinction: the form is indirect grammatically but can be quite pointed in effect.

### What They Said vs. What They Meant

| What was said | Tone | What was meant |
|---|---|---|
| 病院に行ったらどうですか。 | Concerned | You should see a doctor. I'm worried. |
| 病院に行ったらどうですか。 | Cold/flat | Why haven't you gone to the doctor already? |
| 自分で調べたらどうですか。 | Annoyed | Stop asking me. Look it up yourself. |
| 早めに出たらどうですか。 | Neutral advice | You should leave early (to avoid traffic, etc.). |

### ～ませんか — The Social Invitation

～ませんか is the standard form for invitations and joint proposals. Unlike ～たらどうですか, the speaker is included in or invested in the proposed action.

> 一緒に昼ごはんを食べませんか。
> "Won't you eat lunch together (with me)?"

> 今度、展覧会を見に行きませんか。
> "Won't you go see the exhibition sometime?"

The negative question form (won't you...?) is softer than the affirmative (will you...? = 食べますか). By framing the question in the negative, the speaker makes it easier for the listener to decline — the no is already built into the question's grammar.

### ～ないんですか — Surprise Masking a Suggestion

This form expresses surprise that something has not happened, and through that surprise, implies that it should happen. It is more assertive than ～ませんか because it carries an assumption that the action was expected.

> まだ申し込んでないんですか。
> "You haven't applied yet?" (= You should have applied. Why haven't you?)

> あの本、読んでないんですか。
> "You haven't read that book?" (= I expected you to have read it. You should.)

> 彼女に言ってないんですか。
> "You haven't told her?" (= You definitely should have told her by now.)

### Dialogue: Navigating Suggestion Forms

> 山本：最近、目が疲れやすくてさ。
> Yamamoto: "Lately my eyes get tired easily."

> 石川：ブルーライトカットの眼鏡、使ってみたら？
> Ishikawa: "How about trying blue-light-blocking glasses?" (casual ～たら)

> 山本：うーん、効果あるのかな。
> Yamamoto: "Hmm, I wonder if they're effective."

> 石川：え、まだ試してないの？結構変わるよ。
> Ishikawa: "What, you haven't tried them yet? They make quite a difference." (～てないの = casual ないんですか)

> 山本：そうなんだ。どこで買った？
> Yamamoto: "Really? Where did you buy them?"

> 石川：駅前の眼鏡屋。一緒に見に行かない？
> Ishikawa: "The glasses shop in front of the station. Want to go look together?" (～ない = casual ませんか)

**Analysis:** Ishikawa uses three different suggestion forms in a natural progression. First, ～たら (casual suggestion). Then, ～てないの (surprise that Yamamoto hasn't tried). Finally, ～ない (invitation to go together). Each form has a different pragmatic weight, and Ishikawa escalates naturally from suggestion to surprise to invitation.

### The Formal Equivalents

In business and formal contexts, the same functions use elevated language.

### Formal Suggestion Forms Table

| Casual | Formal | Function |
|---|---|---|
| ～たらどう？ | ～たらいかがでしょうか。 | Suggestion |
| ～ない？ | ～ませんか。/ ～ませんでしょうか。 | Invitation |
| ～てないの？ | ～ていらっしゃらないんですか。 | Surprise / implicit suggestion |
| ～てみたら？ | ～てみてはいかがでしょうか。 | Tentative suggestion |
| ～した方がいいよ | ～された方がよろしいかと... | Strong advice (trailing off) |

### Dialogue: Business Context Suggestions

> 課長：来月のプレゼン、資料はもう作成されましたか。
> Section Chief: "Have you already prepared the materials for next month's presentation?"

> 部下：いえ、まだ取りかかっていないんですが...
> Subordinate: "No, I haven't started yet..."

> 課長：そうですか。できれば今週中に着手されてはいかがでしょうか。来週は他の案件も入ってきますので。
> Section Chief: "I see. If possible, how about starting on it within this week? Other projects will come in next week."

> 部下：はい、おっしゃる通りです。早速取りかかります。
> Subordinate: "Yes, you're absolutely right. I'll get started right away."

**Analysis:** The section chief's ～されてはいかがでしょうか is formally a suggestion, but pragmatically it is an instruction. The subordinate correctly reads it as such and responds with compliance, not with "I'll think about it." Recognizing when a formal suggestion is actually an order is a key N1-level pragmatic skill.

|---|---|---|---|
| 展覧会 | てんらんかい | ③ | exhibition |
| 申し込む | もうしこむ | ④ | to apply |
| ブルーライト | ぶるーらいと | ④ | blue light |
| 眼鏡 | めがね | ① | glasses |
| 取りかかる | とりかかる | ④ | to begin, to start on |
| 着手する | ちゃくしゅする | ① | to commence (formal) |
| 案件 | あんけん | ⓪ | matter, project, case |
| 早速 | さっそく | ⓪ | right away, immediately |
| 効果 | こうか | ① | effect, effectiveness |
| おっしゃる通り | おっしゃるとおり | — | exactly as you say (humble agreement) |

## 9.3 前向きに検討します and Other Bureaucratic Non-Answers

### The Art of Saying Nothing Affirmatively

Japanese business and bureaucratic language has developed an entire vocabulary of phrases that sound positive but commit to nothing. These phrases allow the speaker to avoid direct refusal while also avoiding a promise. For a non-native speaker, they are among the most dangerous traps in professional Japanese — because they sound like agreement.

### The Core Phrase: 前向きに検討します

> 前向きに検討します。
> "We will consider it positively." (= We will probably not do it.)

This is perhaps the most famous Japanese bureaucratic non-answer. 前向き means "forward-looking" or "positive." 検討します means "we will consider/examine." Together, they sound like enthusiastic agreement. In practice, they almost always mean the proposal will be shelved.

The phrase works because it satisfies two requirements simultaneously: it gives the proposer something positive to hear, and it commits the speaker to nothing beyond "considering."

### The Bureaucratic Non-Answer Lexicon

### Non-Answer Phrases Table

| Japanese | Literal translation | Actual meaning | Commitment level |
|---|---|---|---|
| 前向きに検討します | "We'll consider it positively" | We'll probably let this die quietly | Very low |
| 善処します | "We'll deal with it well" | We acknowledge the issue but promise nothing | Very low |
| 持ち帰って検討します | "We'll take it back and consider" | We need to discuss internally (and may never respond) | Low |
| 検討させていただきます | "We'll humbly consider it" | We'll think about it (no timeline, no promise) | Low |
| 難しいかもしれません | "It may be difficult" | It's almost certainly not going to happen | Low (disguised refusal) |
| 関係部署と調整します | "We'll coordinate with relevant departments" | This will enter bureaucratic limbo | Low to medium |
| できる限り対応します | "We'll respond to the extent possible" | We'll do what's convenient for us | Medium |
| 方向で進めたいと思います | "We'd like to proceed in that direction" | We're somewhat positive but not committed | Medium |
| 確認して折り返します | "We'll check and get back to you" | Genuine — someone will actually follow up | Medium to high |
| ぜひお願いしたいと思います | "We'd very much like to ask you" | Genuine positive — close to agreement | High |

### What They Said vs. What They Meant: Meeting Scenarios

| What the client/boss said | What they meant | What you should do |
|---|---|---|
| 前向きに検討させていただきます。 | We're not going to do this. | Don't count on it. Follow up in 2 weeks to confirm. |
| 善処いたします。 | We'll try, but don't expect much. | Lower your expectations significantly. |
| 社内で検討して改めてご連絡します。 | We need consensus. Could go either way. | Wait, but send a gentle follow-up after a week. |
| 難しいところもあるかと思いますが... | There are serious obstacles. | Prepare a compromise or alternative proposal. |
| 趣旨は理解いたしました。 | I understand what you want. (But I didn't agree.) | Note that understanding ≠ agreement. |
| おっしゃることはごもっともです。 | Your point is valid. (But we still won't do it.) | Being right doesn't mean you'll get what you want. |

### The 検討 Spectrum

The word 検討する (to examine, consider) appears in many of these phrases. Its meaning shifts depending on the modifiers around it.

### 検討 Modifier Table

| Expression | Nuance |
|---|---|
| 検討します | Neutral — could go either way |
| 前向きに検討します | Sounds positive, usually negative |
| 慎重に検討します | "We'll consider carefully" — slow-walking, often leads to no |
| 早急に検討します | "We'll consider urgently" — slightly more likely to result in action |
| 真剣に検討します | "We'll consider seriously" — relatively genuine |
| 検討の余地があります | "There is room for consideration" — genuinely open |
| 検討に値します | "It's worth considering" — positive assessment |

### Dialogue: The Business Meeting

> 山田部長：本日は新しい業務提携の件について、ご提案をいただきましてありがとうございます。
> Director Yamada: "Thank you for your proposal today regarding the new business partnership."

> 鈴木（取引先）：こちらこそ、お時間をいただきまして恐縮です。ご検討いただけますでしょうか。
> Suzuki (client): "Thank you for your time. Would you be able to consider it?"

> 山田部長：はい、大変興味深いお話でした。社内に持ち帰りまして、関係部署と調整の上、改めてご連絡させていただきます。
> Director Yamada: "Yes, it was a very interesting proposal. We'll take it back to the company, coordinate with relevant departments, and contact you again."

> 鈴木：ありがとうございます。いつ頃ご回答いただけますでしょうか。
> Suzuki: "Thank you. When might we receive a response?"

> 山田部長：そうですね... できるだけ早くと思っておりますが、少々お時間をいただくかもしれません。
> Director Yamada: "Let me see... We're thinking as soon as possible, but it may take a little time."

> 鈴木：承知いたしました。お忙しいところ恐れ入りますが、よろしくお願いいたします。
> Suzuki: "Understood. I apologize for the trouble during your busy time, but we look forward to hearing from you."

**Analysis:** Director Yamada has said nothing negative. Every sentence sounds positive or at least neutral. But notice what he has not said: he has not expressed personal enthusiasm, he has not given a timeline, and he has not committed to any specific action beyond "coordinating" and "contacting." An experienced Japanese businessperson would read this as a 50/50 at best. The real signal will come in the follow-up — or the lack thereof.

### 善処します — The Politician's Favorite

善処します deserves special attention because it is the phrase most associated with political non-answers in Japan. It literally means "I will handle it well" (善 = good, 処 = deal with), but in political and bureaucratic contexts, it has become so emptied of meaning that it is practically a joke.

> 記者：大臣、この問題についてどのように対応されるのでしょうか。
> Reporter: "Minister, how will you respond to this issue?"

> 大臣：善処してまいりたいと思います。
> Minister: "I would like to deal with it well going forward."

This answer provides zero information. It does not acknowledge a specific problem, does not outline a plan, does not give a timeline, and does not accept responsibility. It merely states an intention to do something good — which no one could disagree with but no one can hold the speaker to.

### How to Detect a Non-Answer

When you hear a Japanese response in a business or official context, ask yourself these diagnostic questions:

1. **Did they commit to a specific action?** ("We will send the revised proposal by Friday" = real commitment. "We'll consider it" = no commitment.)
2. **Did they give a timeline?** (No timeline usually means no urgency and possibly no intention.)
3. **Did they use 検討 with a positive modifier?** (前向きに検討 is almost always a soft no.)
4. **Did they reference other people or departments?** (Deferring to others often means no individual is taking responsibility.)
5. **Did they mirror your enthusiasm?** (If you're excited and they respond with measured language, the gap is informative.)

## 9.4 The Spectrum from Direct to Indirect

### How Directness Maps to Social Context

Japanese is not an inherently indirect language. Friends tease each other bluntly. Parents scold children directly. Coaches shout instructions. What Japanese has is a wider spectrum of directness than many languages, and more finely tuned social rules about which point on the spectrum is appropriate for which situation.

This section maps the full spectrum and shows how directness level correlates with social variables.

### The Five Levels of Directness

### Directness Level Table

| Level | Label | Characteristics | Example (refusing food) |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Direct / Blunt | Bare statement, no softening | いらない。 ("I don't want it.") |
| 2 | Softened direct | States refusal but with courtesy markers | すみません、結構です。 ("Sorry, I'm fine.") |
| 3 | Indirect with reason | Gives a reason, often trails off | お腹いっぱいで... ("My stomach is full, so...") |
| 4 | Highly indirect | Hints without stating refusal | ちょっと最近、食事制限していて... ("Lately I've been on a diet...") |
| 5 | Non-verbal / contextual | Silence, hesitation, topic change | 〔pause〕 あ、そういえば明日の会議って... ("Oh, speaking of which, tomorrow's meeting...") |

### Social Variables That Determine Directness

### Social Variable Table

| Variable | More direct when... | More indirect when... |
|---|---|---|
| Relative status | Speaking to subordinates or equals | Speaking to superiors or clients |
| Familiarity | Close friends, family | Acquaintances, strangers |
| Topic sensitivity | Low stakes (lunch choice) | High stakes (rejection, criticism, bad news) |
| Public vs. private | Private conversation | Public or group setting |
| Gender expectations | (traditionally) Male speakers | (traditionally) Female speakers |
| Regional variation | Kansai, rural areas (more direct) | Tokyo, formal institutions (more indirect) |

### Case Study: Delivering Bad News at Five Levels

Situation: You need to tell a colleague that their report has errors.

**Level 1 — Direct:**
> この報告書、間違いだらけだよ。
> "This report is full of mistakes."

**Level 2 — Softened direct:**
> この報告書、いくつか修正が必要な箇所がありますね。
> "This report has several places that need correction."

**Level 3 — Indirect with reason:**
> この報告書なんですが、データの部分がちょっと気になったんですけど...
> "About this report — the data section concerned me a little..."

**Level 4 — Highly indirect:**
> 報告書、拝見しました。もう一度確認された方がいいかもしれませんね。
> "I've read the report. It might be good to double-check it."

**Level 5 — Maximum indirectness:**
> 報告書、ありがとうございました。ちなみに、元データの出典って確認されましたか？
> "Thank you for the report. By the way, did you check the source of the original data?"

At Level 5, the speaker never mentions errors at all. Instead, they ask a pointed question that, if the colleague is perceptive, will lead them to discover the errors on their own. This is 察する (reading the air) in action — the speaker trusts the listener to figure it out.

### When Directness Is Appropriate

It is a common misconception that Japanese always favors indirectness. In fact, directness is appropriate and even preferred in several contexts.

### Contexts Favoring Directness Table

| Context | Why directness works | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Emergency / safety | No time for indirection | 逃げろ！ ("Run!") |
| Close friendship | Directness signals intimacy | 無理すんなよ。 ("Don't push yourself.") |
| Parent to child | Authority relationship | 早く寝なさい。 ("Go to bed now.") |
| Teacher to student | Educational clarity | ここは間違っています。 ("This is wrong.") |
| Sports coaching | Immediate instruction | もっと腰を落として！ ("Lower your hips more!") |
| Kansai casual speech | Regional communication style | あかんて、それ。 ("That's no good.") |

### Dialogue: The Same Request at Different Directness Levels

Context: Asking a colleague to cover your shift.

**Casual / Direct (close colleague, same age):**
> 悪い、来週の水曜、シフト代わってくれない？
> "Sorry, can you swap shifts with me next Wednesday?"

**Polite / Somewhat indirect (colleague you know but aren't close with):**
> すみません、来週の水曜日のシフトのことなんですが、もし都合がよければ代わっていただけないかと思いまして...
> "Excuse me, about the shift next Wednesday — I was thinking, if it's convenient, whether you might be able to swap..."

**Formal / Very indirect (asking a senior colleague):**
> お忙しいところ大変恐縮なのですが、来週水曜日の件で少しご相談させていただきたいことがございまして... もしご都合がつくようでしたら、シフトの調整をお願いできればと思うのですが...
> "I'm terribly sorry to trouble you when you're busy, but there's something I'd like to consult you about regarding next Wednesday... If your schedule permits, I was wondering if I could ask you to adjust the shift..."

**Analysis:** In the formal version, the speaker:
- Apologizes before making the request (恐縮)
- Uses ご相談 (consultation) instead of お願い (request) to frame it as collaborative
- Uses double conditionals (もし...ようでしたら)
- Ends with と思うのですが, which is itself a trailing clause — they never actually complete the request sentence

### Reading Between Levels

A critical skill is recognizing what level of directness someone is using and what that level choice tells you about the situation.

If someone who is normally direct with you suddenly becomes indirect, something has changed. They may be upset, the topic may be sensitive, or a third party may be listening. Conversely, if someone who is normally indirect becomes direct, the matter is urgent or they have reached their limit.

> いつもはっきり言う上司が「ちょっと考えてみてくれないかな」と言った場合、よほど慎重に扱うべき案件だと理解した方がいい。
> "If a boss who normally speaks bluntly says 'could you think about this a bit for me?', you should understand this is a matter that requires extremely careful handling."

### Exercise 1: What Did They Really Mean?

Read each exchange and choose the best interpretation of what Speaker B actually means.

**1-1.**
> A：今度の日曜日、引っ越しの手伝いをお願いできないかな。
> A: "Could I ask you to help with my move this Sunday?"

> B：日曜日ね... ちょっとその日は用事があって...
> B: "Sunday... I have something to do that day..."

What does B mean?
- (a) B has plans and will come after finishing them.
- (b) B is declining the request.
- (c) B wants more information before deciding.

**Answer:** (b). The trailing て-form combined with ちょっと and the vague 用事 (errand/commitment) is a standard soft refusal. B almost certainly does not intend to help. The "plans" may or may not be real — their function is to provide a face-saving reason.

**1-2.**
> A：この企画書、いかがでしょうか。

> B: "An interesting perspective. Changing the direction a bit might be one option."

What does B mean?
- (a) The proposal is excellent as-is.
- (b) The proposal needs significant revision.
- (c) B is suggesting a minor tweak.

**Answer:** (b). 面白い視点ですね (interesting perspective) is acknowledgment without approval. 方向性を変えてみる (change the direction) is a euphemism for major revision. かもしれませんね softens what is actually a firm suggestion. B is telling A to substantially rework the proposal.

**1-3.**
> A（取引先）：納期を一週間早めていただくことは可能でしょうか。

What does B mean?
- (a) Absolutely, we'll move the deadline.

**Answer:** (b). B has acknowledged the request (理解いたしました) but has not agreed to it. The phrase 社内で調整の上 (after internal coordination) defers the decision. ### Exercise 3: Dialogue Interpretation

Read the full dialogue and answer the questions below.

> 中村課長：木村君、例の企画の件なんだけど。
> Section Chief Nakamura: "Kimura, about that project we discussed."

> 木村：はい、進めております。
> Kimura: "Yes, I'm working on it."

> 中村課長：うん。それでね、上からちょっと話があって。
> Nakamura: "Yeah. So, there was a little talk from above."

> 木村：何かございましたか。
> Kimura: "Did something come up?"

> 中村課長：いや、大した話じゃないんだけど... まあ、方向性についてね。少し見直した方がいいんじゃないかっていう声もあってさ。
> Nakamura: "No, it's nothing major, but... well, about the direction. There are voices saying it might be good to reconsider a bit."

> 木村：見直し、ですか。具体的にはどのあたりでしょうか。
> Kimura: "Reconsider? Specifically, what areas?"

> 中村課長：まあ、全体的にっていうかな。予算のこともあるし、タイミングもあるし。一度、白紙に戻して考え直すっていうのもありかなと。
> Nakamura: "Well, overall, I'd say. There's the budget, and the timing. Starting over from scratch might be one option."

> 木村：...承知しました。
> Kimura: "...Understood."

**Questions:**

**Q1:** What is Nakamura actually telling Kimura?

**A1:** The project is being cancelled or fundamentally restructured. Despite the casual tone and the softening language (ちょっと話, 大した話じゃない, 見直し, ありかなと), the phrase 白紙に戻す (return to a blank slate) is the key signal. Combined with references to budget and timing, this means the project in its current form is dead. Nakamura is delivering this news as gently as possible because he knows Kimura has invested effort.

**Q2:** Why does Nakamura say 上からちょっと話があって?

**A2:** By attributing the decision to 上 (above = senior management), Nakamura distances himself from the decision. He is framing himself as a messenger rather than the decision-maker, which protects both his relationship with Kimura and his own position. The ちょっと minimizes the gravity of what is actually a major decision.

**Q3:** Why does Nakamura say 大した話じゃない when it clearly is?

**A3:** This is a classic Japanese minimization strategy. By calling it "nothing major," Nakamura tries to reduce the emotional impact of the news. It also gives Kimura a chance to absorb the information without feeling that it is a crisis, even though it effectively is one.

**Q4:** What does Kimura's pause before 承知しました tell us?

**A4:** The pause (marked by ...) indicates that Kimura has read between the lines and understands the real message. He is absorbing the disappointment. His choice of 承知しました (formal acknowledgment) rather than a question or protest shows that he accepts the decision — and that he understood the full weight of what was not said.

### Exercise 5: Identify the Non-Answer

In each response, identify whether the speaker has actually committed to anything.

**5-1.**
> 前向きに検討させていただきたいと思います。

**Analysis:** No commitment. 前向きに sounds positive but 検討 (consider) and したいと思います (would like to) add two layers of non-commitment. This is a polite way to end a discussion without agreeing to anything.

**5-2.**
> 来週の月曜日までにお返事いたします。

**Analysis:** Real commitment. A specific day (来週の月曜日) and a specific action (お返事いたします = will reply) constitute a genuine promise. This person intends to respond by Monday.

**5-3.**
> ご趣旨は十分に理解いたしました。関係各所と連携して参りたいと存じます。

**Analysis:** No commitment. 理解いたしました is acknowledgment, not agreement. 関係各所と連携 (coordinate with relevant parties) is a deferral. して参りたいと存じます (I think I would like to proceed) is maximally hedged. Nothing specific has been promised.

**5-4.**
> できる限り、ご期待に沿えるよう努力いたします。

**Analysis:** Weak commitment at best. できる限り (to the extent possible) and 努力いたします (will make efforts) both contain escape clauses. The speaker is saying they will try, not that they will succeed. This may be genuine effort or it may be a face-saving formula.

### Reading Passage: The Unspoken Conversation

> 大学時代の友人から急に連絡が来た。十年ぶりだった。「久しぶり！元気？ ちょっと相談したいことがあるんだけど、今度会えない？」。相談、と聞いて少し身構えた。十年ぶりの連絡で「相談」というのは、大抵お金の話か、何かのセールスか、宗教の勧誘だ。「会えることは会えるけど、来週はちょっと忙しくて...」と返した。本当は来週は暇だった。でも、まず相手の出方を見たかった。相手は「そっか、じゃあ再来週は？ 別に急がないから」と返してきた。急がない、と言っているのに連絡してきたということは、やはり何かある。「再来週の土曜なら。で、相談って何？」と聞いてみた。「会ってから話すよ」。この返事で確信した。メールで言えない内容だということは、対面でないと頼みにくいことだ。つまり、断りにくい状況を作りたいのだろう。結局、会うことにした。予想通り、ネットワークビジネスの勧誘だった。

> I got a sudden message from a college friend. It had been ten years. "Long time no see! How are you? There's something I'd like to consult you about — can we meet up sometime?" Hearing "consult," I stiffened a little. When someone contacts you after ten years to "consult," it's usually about money, some kind of sales pitch, or a religious recruitment. I replied, "I could meet, but next week I'm a little busy..." The truth was, next week was free. But I wanted to see their hand first. They responded, "I see, how about the week after? It's not urgent." The fact that they contacted me while saying it's "not urgent" meant there was definitely something going on. "If it's the Saturday of the week after next. So, what's the consultation about?" I asked. "I'll tell you when we meet." That response confirmed it. If the content can't be said by email, it's something hard to ask for unless face-to-face. In other words, they probably want to create a situation where it's hard to refuse. In the end, I agreed to meet. As predicted, it was a network marketing recruitment.

**Comprehension questions:**

1. Why does the narrator say 来週はちょっと忙しくて when they are actually free?
   - To buy time and observe the friend's response before committing.

2. What does 会ってから話すよ reveal about the friend's intention?
   - The friend knows the request is something the narrator would refuse if told in advance. Face-to-face meetings create social pressure that makes refusal harder.

3. Identify three instances of indirectness in this passage and explain each.
   - (i) ちょっと相談したいこと — "consult" is used to disguise what is actually a recruitment pitch.
   - (ii) 来週はちょっと忙しくて — a false reason given to delay and observe.
   - (iii) 会ってから話すよ — withholding information to control the social situation.

For each exchange, write what Speaker B would most likely say in the indicated register. Then explain why that response works.

> A：今度の日曜日、バーベキューするんですけど、よかったら来ませんか。

**6-3. Formal business — Declining a client's proposal to change the project timeline:**

### Exercise 7: Cross-Cultural Comparison

Consider the following situation and compare how it might be handled in a direct-communication culture versus in Japanese.

**Situation:** A customer has been waiting 30 minutes for food at a restaurant. They want to complain.

**Direct-communication approach:**
> "Excuse me, we've been waiting for 30 minutes. Where is our food? This is unacceptable."

**Japanese indirect approach (polite customer):**
> すみません、注文してからだいぶ経つんですが... まだお時間かかりそうですか。
> "Excuse me, it's been quite a while since we ordered... Will it take more time?"

**Japanese indirect approach (more assertive):**
> あの、先に注文した料理がまだ来ていないんですけれども...
> "Um, the dishes we ordered earlier haven't arrived yet..."

**Analysis:** Even in complaint situations, Japanese speakers tend to state facts (it's been a while, the food hasn't come) rather than make judgments (this is unacceptable). The trailing けれども or が leaves space for the staff to respond and remedy the situation without being accused. This is not weakness — it is a different theory of how complaints work. The Japanese approach assumes the staff will infer the customer's dissatisfaction from the stated facts and act accordingly.

### Chapter Summary

This chapter has covered the following core pragmatic skills:

1. **The trailing sentence** — understanding that an unfinished sentence (especially with ちょっと or て-form) is a complete communication act, usually a refusal.

2. **Negative question forms** — recognizing the pragmatic differences between ～たらどうですか (suggestion), ～ませんか (invitation), and ～ないんですか (surprise/implicit suggestion), and their formal equivalents.

3. **Bureaucratic non-answers** — identifying phrases like 前向きに検討します and 善処します as non-commitments, and knowing how to detect whether a business response contains genuine agreement.

4. **The directness spectrum** — understanding that Japanese has five or more levels of directness, that the appropriate level depends on social variables, and that shifts in someone's usual directness level are themselves informative.

5. **Interpreting the unsaid** — the practical skill of reading between the lines in dialogues, identifying the gap between surface meaning and intended meaning.

The underlying principle across all of these is that Japanese communication is a collaborative act. The speaker provides part of the message. The listener constructs the rest. When both parties do their part, the result is communication that is more considerate, more nuanced, and often more efficient than fully explicit speech — at the cost of occasional misunderstanding when the shared assumptions break down.
