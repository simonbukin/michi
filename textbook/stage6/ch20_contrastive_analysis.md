# Chapter 20 — Japanese vs English: Structural Divergences

This chapter is unlike the others in the series. It introduces no new vocabulary, no new grammar, and no new kanji. Instead, it makes explicit something that has been shaping your comprehension silently from the beginning: the structural differences between Japanese and English that cause persistent misunderstanding, slow processing, and the nagging feeling that you are "translating in your head" rather than understanding directly.

If you are reading this at the N1 level, you have already internalized most of these differences to some degree. You can parse head-final sentences, handle topic-drop, and follow passages with zero pronouns. But there is a gap between unconscious competence and conscious understanding. Making these structural divergences explicit gives you a diagnostic tool: when comprehension breaks down with difficult material, you can identify which structural difference is causing the interference and address it directly.

This chapter draws on linguistic typology — the study of how the world's languages differ structurally — but it is not a linguistics course. Every point is connected to a concrete comprehension problem and a specific strategy for resolving it.

## 20.1 Head-Final vs Head-Initial

### The core difference

English is a head-initial language. The most important element of a phrase comes first, and modifiers follow.

> The book **that I bought yesterday at the bookstore near the station**

Japanese is a head-final language. The most important element comes last, and everything that modifies it precedes it.

> 駅の近くの本屋で昨日買った**本**

In the English version, you know immediately that the topic is "the book." In the Japanese version, you do not encounter 本 until the very end. Everything before it is a single, nested modifier.

### Why this matters for comprehension

The head-final structure means that in Japanese, you must hold an entire modifying chain in working memory without knowing what it modifies until the final noun appears. For English speakers, this creates a processing burden that persists even at advanced levels.

Consider this sentence from a newspaper editorial:

> 政府が長年にわたって十分な対策を講じてこなかったことが明らかになった問題について、野党は厳しく追及する構えだ。

An English speaker's instinct is to try to parse this left-to-right, assigning meaning as words arrive. But in this sentence, the grammatical skeleton is:

> 問題について、野党は追及する構えだ。
> "Regarding the problem, the opposition is poised to press hard."

Everything before 問題 is a single relative clause modifying it:

> 政府が長年にわたって十分な対策を講じてこなかったことが明らかになった **問題**
> "The **problem** [that it became clear [that the government had not taken sufficient measures over many years]]"

### The double-embedding problem

English allows relative clauses too, but they follow the noun. Japanese stacks them before the noun. When there are two or more layers:

> 先月発表された報告書が指摘した課題を踏まえて策定された新しい計画
> "The new plan [that was formulated [based on the issues [that the report [that was published last month] pointed out]]]"

In English, the nested version is nearly unreadable. In Japanese, it flows naturally because the reader processes modifiers incrementally and assigns them to the noun that eventually appears.

### Strategy: stop looking for the subject first

English readers instinctively look for a subject at the start of a sentence. In Japanese, the first element is often a modifier, a topic, or a subordinate clause. Train yourself to:

1. Accept that you will not know the main predicate until the end of the sentence.
2. Process each element as it arrives without trying to predict the overall structure.
3. When you reach the main noun or verb, let the preceding elements snap into place retroactively.

### The verb-final corollary

Just as nouns come after their modifiers, the main verb comes at the end of the clause. This means:

> 田中さんが　昨日　会議で　その提案に　反対した。
> "Tanaka opposed that proposal at the meeting yesterday."

In English, you know the action ("opposed") by the second word. In Japanese, you do not learn the action until the final word. The practical consequence: in fast speech and dense text, the verb — the single most important word — arrives last. If your attention drifts before the end, you miss the point entirely.

> 彼は　前の会社では　マーケティング部で　主に　アジア市場向けの　新製品の　企画を　**担当していた**。
> "At his previous company, in the marketing department, he was **in charge of** planning new products mainly for the Asian market."

The verb phrase 担当していた carries the entire meaning of his role. Every other element is context.

### Reading passage: Head-final structure in the wild

Read the following passage from a fictional editorial. Pay attention to how information accumulates before each head noun and main verb.

> 少子高齢化が急速に進む中で、これまで社会の基盤として機能してきた年金制度が維持できなくなるのではないかという懸念が、専門家の間だけでなく一般市民の間にも広がりつつある。政府は制度改革の必要性を認めながらも、国民の負担増に対する強い反発を恐れて、抜本的な見直しには踏み切れずにいる。一方、現行制度のまま放置すれば、将来世代が背負う負担はさらに膨らむことが各種試算で示されており、問題の先送りはもはや許されないという声が日増しに高まっている。

**Translation:**

> Amid rapidly advancing population decline and aging, concerns that the pension system — which has functioned as a foundation of society until now — may become unsustainable are spreading not only among experts but also among ordinary citizens. While the government acknowledges the need for systemic reform, it fears the strong backlash against increased burden on the public and has been unable to undertake fundamental revision. Meanwhile, various projections show that if the current system is left as-is, the burden borne by future generations will swell further, and calls that postponing the problem is no longer permissible are growing day by day.

Notice:

- 少子高齢化が急速に進む中で — a temporal/circumstantial clause preceding the main clause
- これまで社会の基盤として機能してきた年金制度 — a relative clause modifying 年金制度
- 年金制度が維持できなくなるのではないかという懸念 — a nominalized clause modifying 懸念
- 専門家の間だけでなく一般市民の間にも — parallel scope modification
- The main verb 広がりつつある does not appear until the end of the first sentence

## 20.2 Topic-Prominence vs Subject-Prominence

### What "topic-prominent" means

English is a subject-prominent language. Every sentence must have a grammatical subject, and the subject typically corresponds to the agent of the action.

> **It** is raining. (English requires "it" even though nothing is doing the raining.)

Japanese is a topic-prominent language. Sentences are organized around a topic — what the sentence is about — not around a grammatical subject. The topic need not be the agent, and it need not even appear in the predicate's argument structure.

> この店は　魚が　おいしい。
> "This store, the fish is delicious." (lit.)

In English, "this store" cannot be the subject of "is delicious." In Japanese, it is the topic — the frame within which the comment "the fish is delicious" applies. This is one of the most fundamental structural divergences between the two languages.

### は as frame-setter, not subject marker

The persistent misconception that は marks the subject causes problems even at advanced levels. Consider:

> 象は　鼻が　長い。
> "As for elephants, the nose is long." / "Elephants have long noses."

Here, 象 is the topic (frame), 鼻 is the grammatical subject (marked by が), and 長い is the predicate. English must restructure this entirely because it has no topic-marking mechanism.

More complex examples:

> 来週は　月曜日が　祝日だ。
> "As for next week, Monday is a holiday."

> あの事故は　運転手が　酔っていたことが　原因だ。
> "As for that accident, [the fact that the driver was drunk] was the cause."

In the last example, the topic (あの事故) is not the subject, not the object, and not an argument of any verb in the sentence. It is purely a frame that tells the listener: "I am going to say something about that accident."

### Double-subject construction

Japanese allows what is sometimes called a "double-subject" construction, which has no English parallel:

> 田中さんは　お父さんが　医者だ。
> "As for Tanaka, his father is a doctor."

> 日本は　面積が　小さい。
> "As for Japan, the area is small."

> この大学は　図書館が　立派だ。
> "As for this university, the library is impressive."

In each case, the topic sets a frame, and the が-marked subject operates within that frame. English must express these with possessive constructions ("Tanaka's father"), relative clauses, or completely restructured sentences.

### Subject-drop as a consequence

Because Japanese organizes sentences around topics rather than subjects, the subject is only mentioned when it adds new information. Once established, it disappears:

> 田中さんは　大学を　卒業して、すぐ　商社に　入った。三年間は　大阪で　勤務していたが、去年　東京に　転勤に　なった。今は　新しいプロジェクトを　任されていて、毎日　忙しそうだ。

In English, every sentence would require "he" or "Tanaka": "Tanaka graduated..., he joined..., he worked..., he was transferred..., he has been entrusted..., he seems busy." In Japanese, 田中さんは establishes the topic once, and every subsequent predicate is understood to be about him.

### When the topic shifts

Topic shift is signaled by introducing a new は-marked element:

> 田中さんは　真面目な　人だ。一方、山田さんは　自由奔放な　タイプだ。
> "Tanaka is a serious person. On the other hand, Yamada is the free-spirited type."

The appearance of 山田さんは signals that the frame has changed. Everything after it is about 山田, not 田中.

### Contrastive は

When は is used on an element that would normally take を, に, or で, it signals contrast:

> コーヒーは　飲むが、紅茶は　飲まない。
> "Coffee, I drink, but tea, I don't."

> 東京には　行ったが、大阪には　行かなかった。
> "I went to Tokyo, but I didn't go to Osaka."

English can produce similar constructions with heavy stress ("*Coffee* I'll drink, but *tea* I won't"), but they sound marked and emphatic. In Japanese, contrastive は is ordinary and frequent.

### Reading passage: Topic-prominence in natural text

> 日本語の敬語は、外国人学習者にとって最大の壁の一つとされている。尊敬語と謙譲語の使い分けは確かに複雑だが、根本的な考え方はそれほど難しくない。相手の行為を高め、自分の行為を低める——この原則さえ理解すれば、個々の表現は徐々に身につく。問題は、どの場面でどの程度の敬語を使うかという判断だ。これは文法知識だけでは解決できない。長年の経験と、場の空気を読む力が必要になる。

**Translation:**

> Japanese honorific language is considered one of the biggest barriers for foreign learners. The distinction between respectful language and humble language is indeed complex, but the fundamental concept is not that difficult. Elevate the other person's actions, lower your own — if you understand this principle, individual expressions gradually become familiar. The problem is the judgment of which level of honorifics to use in which situation. This cannot be resolved with grammatical knowledge alone. Years of experience and the ability to read the atmosphere of a situation become necessary.

Topic tracking: 日本語の敬語は (first sentence) sets the frame. 使い分けは shifts to a subtopic. 根本的な考え方は shifts again. 問題は pivots to the core issue. これは refers back. Each は signals: "I am now going to talk about THIS."

## 20.3 Zero Anaphora

### What is zero anaphora?

Anaphora is the linguistic term for referring back to something already mentioned. English uses pronouns: "Tanaka went to the store. **He** bought milk." Japanese uses zero — the referent is simply omitted:

> 田中さんは　店に　行った。∅　牛乳を　買った。
> "Tanaka went to the store. [He] bought milk."

This is zero anaphora: the slot where a pronoun would go in English is filled with nothing. The listener infers the referent from context.

### How extensive is it?

Zero anaphora in Japanese goes far beyond what English speakers expect. Not just subjects, but objects, indirect objects, and even locations can be dropped:

> A: その本、もう読んだ？
> B: うん、∅ ∅ 昨日　∅ 読んだ。

Fully explicit version: うん、**私は** **その本を** 昨日 **家で** 読んだ。
"Yeah, I read it yesterday."

B drops the subject (私), the object (その本), and does not mention a location. All are recoverable from context.

### When Japanese uses pronouns

Japanese does have pronouns (彼, 彼女, 私, あなた, etc.), but they are used far less frequently than English pronouns and in different situations:

1. **Introducing new referents or disambiguating:**

> 田中と山田が来た。彼は（=田中）先に帰った。
> "Tanaka and Yamada came. He (= Tanaka) left first."

Here, 彼 is needed because two potential referents exist.

2. **Emotional or emphatic contexts:**

> あなたが悪いんでしょう？
> "YOU are the one at fault, aren't you?"

Using あなた directly can sound accusatory. In neutral contexts, the subject would simply be dropped.

3. **Written/formal reference:**

> 同氏は記者会見で次のように述べた。
> "Said person stated the following at the press conference."

同氏 is a formal written pronoun, not a conversational one.

### The comprehension challenge

For English speakers, zero anaphora creates a specific comprehension problem: in complex passages with multiple characters, you must track who is doing what without explicit pronoun signals. The cues you rely on instead are:

**1. Predicate type:** Verbs of giving and receiving (あげる, もらう, くれる) inherently specify the direction of the action and thus who is acting.

> ∅ ∅ 教えてくれた。
> "[Someone] taught [me]." (くれる tells you the action was directed toward the speaker.)

**2. Honorific level:** If a verb is in 尊敬語, the unstated subject is someone of higher status.

> ∅ もうお帰りになりました。
> "[The boss / the customer / etc.] has already left." (尊敬語 signals the subject is socially elevated.)

**3. Emotional predicates:** Words like 嬉しい, 悲しい, 寂しい, 欲しい describe the speaker's internal state by default. If someone else's feelings are described, markers like ～がる, ～そうだ, or ～ようだ are required.

> ∅ 嬉しかった。
> "[I] was happy." (Internal state = speaker by default.)

> ∅ 嬉しそうだった。
> "[He/she] seemed happy." (～そうだ signals external observation.)

**4. Discourse continuity:** The most recently established topic continues until explicitly changed.

### Extended example: tracking referents

Read the following passage and track who is performing each action:

> 佐藤さんは朝早く家を出た。駅に着くと、同僚の中村さんがすでにホームで待っていた。「おはよう」と声をかけると、「もう少しで電車が来るよ」と教えてくれた。電車に乗ると、窓際の席に座って、鞄から書類を取り出した。隣に座った中村さんは、スマホを見ながら何か笑っていた。

**Analysis:**

| Clause | Unstated subject | Cue |
|---|---|---|
| 朝早く家を出た | 佐藤 | Topic established by は |
| 駅に着くと | 佐藤 | Continuity |
| すでにホームで待っていた | 中村 | Explicitly named |
| 声をかけると | 佐藤 | Continuity from 佐藤's actions |
| 教えてくれた | 中村 | くれる = action directed to the narrator/佐藤 |
| 電車に乗ると | 佐藤 | Continuity |
| 窓際の席に座って | 佐藤 | Continuity |
| 鞄から書類を取り出した | 佐藤 | Continuity |
| 何か笑っていた | 中村 | Explicitly reintroduced with は |

Notice that 佐藤's actions form an unbroken chain with no pronouns. 中村 is reintroduced each time with a name or is identifiable by くれる.

## 20.4 Aspect vs Tense

### The English bias toward tense

English speakers think about verbs primarily in terms of tense: past, present, future. English has an elaborate tense system with multiple subdivisions (simple past, past progressive, past perfect, past perfect progressive, etc.).

Japanese has only two tense forms: non-past (する) and past (した). There is no dedicated future tense — non-past covers both present and future. There is no past perfect, no future perfect, no "will have been doing."

### Where Japanese excels: aspect

What Japanese encodes with great precision is aspect — how the action unfolds over time, rather than when it happened. The ている form is the primary aspectual marker, but its meaning varies by verb type:

### ている with different verb types

| Verb type | ている meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Continuous action verb | Action in progress | 食べている = "is eating" |
| Instantaneous change verb | Resultant state | 結婚している = "is married" (not "is marrying") |
| Movement verb | Either in progress or resultant state | 行っている = "has gone (and is there)" |
| Habitual | Habitual action | 毎日走っている = "runs every day" |
| Experiential | Experience state | 三回読んでいる = "has read it three times" |

The crucial point: 結婚している does not mean "is in the process of marrying." It means "is in the state that results from having married." This distinction does not exist in the English progressive ("-ing").

### ていた — past aspect

ていた is not the "past progressive" of English. It is the past version of whichever aspectual meaning ている carries:

> 窓が開いていた。
> "The window was open." (Resultant state in the past, not "was being opened.")

> 彼は毎日走っていた。
> "He used to run every day." (Past habitual.)

> 会議はもう始まっていた。
> "The meeting had already started." (Resultant state: the starting had occurred, and the state persisted.)

### てある — intentional resultant state

てある indicates that someone has deliberately put something into a state:

> ドアが開けてある。
> "The door has been opened (by someone, intentionally, and remains in that state)."

Compare:
> ドアが開いている。 = "The door is open." (State, no implication of who or why.)
> ドアが開けてある。 = "The door has been left open (on purpose)."

### ておく — preparation aspect

ておく marks an action done in advance, in preparation:

> 予約しておいた。
> "I made a reservation (in advance, so it's ready)."

> 読んでおいてください。
> "Please read it (beforehand, in preparation)."

English has no dedicated form for this. "I made a reservation" does not inherently communicate advance preparation.

### てしまう — completion/regret aspect

てしまう marks either completion or regret, depending on context:

> 全部食べてしまった。
> "I ate it all up." (Completion — thorough.)

> 財布を忘れてしまった。
> "I forgot my wallet." (Regret — unfortunately.)

In casual speech, てしまう contracts to ちゃう/じゃう:

> 食べちゃった。 = 食べてしまった。
> 忘れちゃった。 = 忘れてしまった。

### てくる / ていく — directional aspect

These encode movement through time, not just space:

> 寒くなってきた。
> "It has gotten cold (change approaching the present moment)."

> これからもっと暑くなっていく。
> "It will get hotter and hotter (change moving away into the future)."

> ずっとやってきた。
> "I have been doing it all along (up to now)."

> これからも続けていく。
> "I will continue doing it (from now onward)."

English can express these ideas, but requires different constructions each time. Japanese uses a single, consistent pair: てくる (toward speaker/present) and ていく (away from speaker/into future).

### The comprehension error

The most common L1 transfer error is reading ている as English "-ing." This leads to misinterpretation of:

> 知っている → "is knowing" ✗ → "knows" ✓
> 持っている → "is holding" (sometimes) → "has/owns" (often) ✓
> 住んでいる → "is living" (sort of) → "lives/resides" ✓
> 死んでいる → "is dying" ✗ → "is dead" ✓

死んでいる means "is in the state resulting from having died" — that is, "is dead." This is perhaps the most dramatic example of how aspectual thinking differs from tense thinking.

### Reading passage: Aspect in narrative

> 彼女が駅に着いたとき、電車はもう出てしまっていた。次の電車まで三十分あったので、駅前のカフェに入ることにした。窓際の席に座ると、外では雨が降り始めていた。朝は晴れていたのに、いつの間にか空が暗くなってきていた。傘を持ってこなかったことを後悔しながら、コーヒーを注文した。温かいカップを手で包んでいると、携帯が鳴った。

**Aspect analysis:**

| Form | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 着いた | Simple past — arrived |
| 出てしまっていた | Completion (しまう) + resultant state (ている) + past (た): the train had already departed and was gone |
| あった | Simple past — there were (30 minutes) |
| 降り始めていた | Inception (始める) + resultant state (ている) + past: it had begun raining (and continued) |
| 晴れていた | Resultant state + past: the weather had been clear |
| 暗くなってきていた | Change (なる) + approach-to-present (てくる) + resultant state (ている) + past: the sky had been gradually darkening |
| 持ってこなかった | Not-brought (along to here) |
| 包んでいると | Progressive action: while wrapping (hands around cup) |
| 鳴った | Simple past — rang |

## 20.5 Perspective Encoding

### What "perspective" means in Japanese grammar

English treats many actions as objective events. Japanese treats them as events seen from a particular perspective. Three grammatical systems are devoted to encoding perspective: 授受 (giving and receiving), the passive, and the causative. None of these have true equivalents in English.

### 授受 (giving and receiving) as perspective

The three-way system of あげる/もらう/くれる is not just about who gives what to whom. It is about whose perspective the speaker adopts:

> 田中さんが私に本をくれた。
> "Tanaka gave me a book." (Perspective: I am the recipient; I feel gratitude.)

> 私が田中さんに本をもらった。
> "I received a book from Tanaka." (Perspective: I am the recipient; I actively frame the receiving.)

> 私が田中さんに本をあげた。
> "I gave Tanaka a book." (Perspective: I am the giver; the action flows away from me.)

In English, "gave" and "received" are simply different verbs describing the same event from different angles. In Japanese, the choice between くれる and もらう is a perspective decision: くれる foregrounds the giver's action as a favor; もらう foregrounds the receiver's active stance.

### The ～てくれる / ～てもらう extension

When attached to other verbs, these become perspective markers on any action:

> 友達が手伝ってくれた。
> "My friend helped me." (I see the friend's action as a favor to me.)

> 友達に手伝ってもらった。
> "I had my friend help me." / "I got my friend to help me." (I frame myself as the one who benefited from arranging the help.)

> 先生が推薦状を書いてくださった。
> "The professor wrote me a recommendation letter." (Honorific version of くれる — I view the professor's action as a gracious favor.)

English has no systematic way to encode this. "My friend helped me" is neutral. Whether you feel gratitude, whether you see it as a favor, whether you arranged it — none of this is grammatically marked in English. In Japanese, the choice of auxiliary verb makes these distinctions explicit.

### Failure to use the perspective system

At the N1 level, the issue is not whether you know these forms but whether you notice when a speaker deliberately does NOT use them:

> 友達が手伝った。 (No ～てくれる)

This is grammatically correct but pragmatically cold. The speaker is not acknowledging the friend's action as a favor. In context, this might signal:

- The speaker is narrating objectively (news, report)
- The speaker does not feel particularly grateful
- The speaker is being deliberately distant

### The passive as perspective marker

The Japanese passive (受身) is not always equivalent to the English passive. Japanese has three types:

**1. Direct passive (same as English):**

> 泥棒に財布を盗まれた。
> "My wallet was stolen by a thief."

**2. Indirect passive (no English equivalent):**

> 雨に降られた。
> "I was rained on." (lit.)

Rain does not act on anyone in English grammar. In Japanese, the indirect passive frames the speaker as adversely affected by the rain. This is sometimes called the "adversity passive" or 迷惑の受身.

> 隣の人にたばこを吸われた。
> "The person next to me smoked." (lit. "I was smoked at by the person next to me.")

The actual event is: the person next to me smoked. The passive reframes it from the speaker's perspective: I was negatively affected by their smoking.

> 子供に泣かれて、仕事ができなかった。
> "The child cried (and I was affected), so I couldn't work."

**3. Possessor passive:**

> 足を踏まれた。
> "My foot was stepped on." (lit. "I was stepped-on with respect to my foot.")

English says "my foot was stepped on," making the foot the subject. Japanese says "I was stepped on regarding my foot," making the person the subject. This keeps the experiencer — the person who feels the event — as the grammatical center.

### The causative as perspective marker

The causative (使役) in Japanese does not simply mean "make/let someone do something." It frames the causer's relationship to the action:

> 母が子供を遊ばせている。
> "The mother is letting the child play." / "The mother is making the child play."

Whether this is "let" or "make" depends on context, not grammar. The causative marks that the mother is the one who enables/controls the child's playing. The perspective is the mother's.

### Causative-passive: double perspective shift

> 野菜を食べさせられた。
> "I was made to eat vegetables."

This combines the causative (someone caused the eating) and the passive (I was the affected party). The perspective is the sufferer's: I did not want to eat vegetables, someone made me, and I am telling you about it from my perspective as the unwilling participant.

### Reading passage: Perspective systems in action

> 小学校の頃、毎日母に野菜を食べさせられた。嫌で嫌でたまらなかったが、今思えば、あのとき無理にでも食べさせてくれたおかげで、好き嫌いがなくなった。大人になってから、母に「あの頃はごめんね」と言われたことがある。でも、感謝しているのはこちらの方だ。「ありがとう」と言ったら、母は少し照れたように笑ってくれた。

**Perspective analysis:**

| Expression | Perspective |
|---|---|
| 食べさせられた | Causative-passive: I (the child) was made to eat; I was the unwilling participant |
| 食べさせてくれた | Causative + くれる: She made me eat, and I now view this as a favor done for my benefit |
| 言われたことがある | Passive: I was told (by mother); the experience happened to me |
| 感謝しているのはこちらの方だ | Active: I am the one who is grateful |
| 笑ってくれた | ～てくれる: She smiled, and I perceive it as something precious she did for me |

Notice the shift from 食べさせられた (resentment) to 食べさせてくれた (gratitude). The same event — the mother making the child eat vegetables — is reframed through a different perspective marker as the narrator's attitude changes.

## 20.6 Why This Matters: L1 Transfer Errors and How Awareness Resolves Them

### What is L1 transfer?

L1 (first language) transfer occurs when you unconsciously apply the structures and assumptions of your native language to the language you are learning. At beginner and intermediate levels, this produces obvious errors (wrong word order, missing particles). At advanced levels, it produces subtle comprehension failures that are harder to diagnose because you do not realize the problem is structural rather than lexical.

### The five persistent errors

Based on the structural divergences discussed in this chapter, here are five comprehension errors that persist even at the N1 level, along with strategies for resolving them.

### Error 1: Parsing failure with long pre-nominal modifiers

**Symptom:** You reach the end of a long noun phrase and cannot remember what the modifiers were about. You re-read the sentence multiple times.

**Cause:** Head-initial expectation. You are trying to identify the main noun first, but it appears last.

**Strategy:** Read the modifier chain as a sequence of events/descriptions. When the head noun appears, it will tell you what that sequence was describing. Trust the process — do not scan ahead for the noun.

> 昨年の夏に友人と二人でヨーロッパを三週間かけて回ったときに撮った**写真**
> "**Photos** taken when I traveled around Europe over three weeks with a friend last summer"

Read it as: "Last summer — with a friend — two people — Europe — three weeks — traveled around — when — took — [WHAT?] — photos." The noun "photos" retroactively makes the chain coherent.

### Error 2: Looking for "he/she" when subjects drop

**Symptom:** In dialogue or narrative with multiple characters, you lose track of who is doing what because no pronouns appear.

**Cause:** English requires explicit subjects. Your processing system expects them.

**Strategy:** Track the topic (は-marked element) and use the cues listed in 20.3 (predicate type, honorific level, emotional predicates, discourse continuity). Build a mental model of who the "default subject" is at each point.

### Error 3: Interpreting ている as "-ing"

**Symptom:** You read 死んでいる as "is dying," 知っている as "is knowing," or 結婚している as "is getting married."

**Cause:** English progressive aspect = "-ing." Japanese ている encodes resultant state, habitual, and progressive. They overlap only partially.

**Strategy:** When you encounter ている, first ask: what type of verb is this? If it is an instantaneous change verb (死ぬ, 結婚する, 届く, 始まる, 閉まる), ている almost always means resultant state, not progressive.

### Error 4: Missing the perspective in ～てくれる / ～てもらう

**Symptom:** You understand the action described but miss the speaker's emotional framing. The passage feels flat, as though the speaker is merely narrating events.

**Cause:** English does not grammatically mark gratitude or perspective on actions. You translate ～てくれた as "did [something]" and lose the perspective.

**Strategy:** When you see ～てくれる or ～てくださる, mentally add "and I appreciate it" or "as a favor to me." When you see ～てもらう, mentally add "I arranged/benefited from." These are not optional politeness — they are core meaning.

### Error 5: Failing to hear the indirect passive as adversity

**Symptom:** You parse 雨に降られた correctly ("I was rained on") but do not feel the adversity. You might even think it is a translation error.

**Cause:** English does not use the passive to express that you were negatively affected by an event. "It rained" is a weather report. 雨に降られた is a complaint.

**Strategy:** When you encounter a passive where the agent is not human, or where the action does not logically take a direct object, read it as: "X happened, and I was adversely affected." This is the indirect passive (迷惑の受身), and the adversity is the point.

### Diagnostic reading exercise

Read the following passage. For each numbered point, identify the structural feature (head-final, topic-prominence, zero anaphora, aspect, or perspective) and explain how L1 transfer might cause misunderstanding.

> ①先月末に本社から突然送られてきた長文のメールに、全員が驚いた。②内容は、来年度の組織再編についてだった。③部長は黙って最後まで読んでいた。④読み終わると、ため息をついた。⑤「こういうことは、もっと早く知らせてほしかった」と、誰かがぽつりと言った。⑥隣に座っていた後輩にそう言われて、部長は何も答えられなかった。

**Analysis:**

1. ①: Head-final — 先月末に本社から突然送られてきた長文のメール is a long pre-nominal modifier. The head noun メール comes last. L1 transfer: trying to find the subject at the start of the sentence.

2. ②: Topic-prominence — 内容は sets a new topic. There is no explicit subject for the predicate だった. The topic carries the sentence.

3. ③: Aspect — 読んでいた is progressive aspect (reading in progress), not resultant state, because 読む is a continuous action verb.

4. ④: Zero anaphora — No subject stated. Continuity from ③: the 部長 is still the actor.

5. ⑤: Perspective — 知らせてほしかった uses ～てほしい (wanting someone to do something for you). The speaker frames the company's failure as something that affected them personally.

6. ⑥: Perspective — 言われて is the passive. 部長は is affected by the statement. The passive frames the 部長 as the one who received the impact of those words. Also zero anaphora: the subject of 答えられなかった is 部長 (continuity from 部長は).

## 20.7 Practice Exercises

### Exercise A — Identify the structural divergence

For each sentence, identify which structural divergence (head-final, topic-prominence, zero anaphora, aspect, perspective) is most likely to cause comprehension difficulty for an English speaker, and explain why.

**1.** 昨日の会議で部長が提案した新しいプロジェクトの進め方について、まだ結論が出ていない。

**2.** この問題は、原因が複雑で、解決が難しい。

**3.** ∅ 朝起きて、∅ 顔を洗って、∅ 朝ごはんを食べて、∅ 会社に行った。

**4.** もう届いている。

**5.** わざわざ駅まで迎えに来てくれた。

**6.** 隣の席の人にずっとペンをカチカチされて、集中できなかった。

### Exercise A — Answer Key

**1.** Head-final. The entire phrase 昨日の会議で部長が提案した新しいプロジェクトの進め方 is a single pre-nominal modifier for について. An English speaker may struggle to hold the modifier chain in memory before reaching the modified element.

**2.** Topic-prominence. この問題は sets the topic, but 原因が and 解決が are both subjects within the comment. The double-layer structure (topic → subject → predicate) has no English parallel. An English speaker would expect "This problem's cause is complex and its solution is difficult."

**3.** Zero anaphora. Four consecutive predicates with no stated subject. The same person (established by context or a preceding sentence) performs all actions. English requires "I" or "he/she" before each verb.

**4.** Aspect. 届いている is resultant state, not progressive. It means "it has arrived (and is here now)," not "it is arriving." An English speaker reading ている as "-ing" would misunderstand.

**5.** Perspective. てくれた frames the action as a favor to the speaker. English "came to meet me at the station" is factual; Japanese adds the perspective that the speaker is grateful. わざわざ further emphasizes the effort, which has no single English equivalent.

**6.** Perspective (indirect passive). ペンをカチカチされて is the indirect passive — the speaker was adversely affected by someone clicking a pen. English "the person next to me kept clicking a pen" describes the event objectively; the Japanese frames the speaker as the victim of the clicking.

### Exercise B — Restructure for English understanding

For each Japanese sentence, provide (a) a literal English translation that preserves the Japanese structure as much as possible, and (b) a natural English translation. The gap between (a) and (b) reveals the structural divergence.

**1.** 象は鼻が長い。

(a) "As for elephants, the nose is long."
(b) "Elephants have long noses."

Gap: English has no topic marker. The topic-comment structure must be restructured as a possessive construction.

**2.** 会議がもう始まっていた。

(a) "The meeting was already in the state resulting from having started."
(b) "The meeting had already started."

Gap: English uses the past perfect ("had started") to convey what Japanese expresses with ている + past (resultant state in the past).

**3.** 先生に褒められて嬉しかった。

(a) "Having been praised by the teacher, I was happy."
(b) "I was happy that the teacher praised me."

Gap: The passive (褒められて) in Japanese puts the speaker in the experiencer role. English restructures this with an active clause.

**4.** 両親が空港まで見送りに来てくれた。

(a) "My parents, as a favor to me, came to the airport to see me off."
(b) "My parents came to see me off at the airport."

Gap: てくれた adds perspective (gratitude) that English cannot encode grammatically.

**5.** 三年前にこの町に引っ越してきた人が最近増えている。

(a) "[People who three years ago to this town moved-here] recently are-increasing."
(b) "There has been a recent increase in people who moved to this town three years ago."

Gap: Head-final structure — the long relative clause 三年前にこの町に引っ越してきた precedes its head noun 人.

### Exercise C — Reading comprehension with structural awareness

Read the following passage. After reading, answer the questions below. Focus not just on content comprehension but on identifying how the structural features discussed in this chapter operate in the text.

> 母が入院したと聞いて、すぐに実家に帰った。病室に入ると、思ったより元気そうだった。「わざわざ来なくてもよかったのに」と言われたが、来てよかったと思った。父は毎日病院に通っているらしい。仕事の後、夜遅くまで付き添っているそうだ。母はそのことを申し訳なく思っているようだが、父は「当然だ」と言っているという。二人の姿を見ていたら、言葉にならない気持ちになった。

**Questions:**

1. Who is the unstated subject of すぐに実家に帰った? What cue tells you?
2. What does 思ったより元気そうだった mean, and which structural feature (aspect, perspective, or evidentiality) does そうだ encode here?
3. In 言われた, why is the passive used? What perspective does it encode?
4. Track the zero anaphora: who is the subject of each predicate in the passage?
5. What is the aspectual meaning of 通っている and 付き添っている — progressive or habitual?
6. What structural feature makes 言葉にならない気持ちになった difficult to translate precisely?

### Exercise C — Answer Key

1. The narrator (the speaker / first person). Cue: the passage begins with 母が入院したと聞いて, establishing a first-person perspective. The narrator heard the news and went home. Zero anaphora: the subject is inferable from context.

2. "She seemed more energetic than I had expected." そうだ here is the appearance-based evidential (～そうだ from the stem), meaning "looks like, seems." It encodes that the narrator is making a judgment based on visual observation, not direct knowledge of the mother's internal state.

3. The passive 言われた frames the narrator as the recipient of the mother's words. It is not adversative here — it simply encodes that the words were directed at the speaker. The perspective is the narrator's: "those words were said to me."

4. Subject tracking:
   - 聞いて → narrator
   - 帰った → narrator
   - 入ると → narrator
   - 元気そうだった → mother (observed by narrator)
   - 言われた → mother said it, narrator received it (passive)
   - 来てよかったと思った → narrator
   - 通っている → father
   - 付き添っている → father
   - 思っている → mother
   - 言っている → father
   - 見ていたら → narrator
   - なった → narrator

5. Both are habitual. 通っている = "goes (regularly, every day)." 付き添っている = "stays with her (as a regular practice)." The らしい and そうだ markers indicate this is reported information, not the narrator's direct observation.

6. 言葉にならない気持ちになった is hard to translate because: (a) 気持ちになった uses the zero-anaphora subject (narrator); (b) 言葉にならない is a relative clause modifying 気持ち (head-final); (c) the emotion itself — seeing elderly parents caring for each other — is culturally resonant in a way that English "I was moved" does not fully capture.

> ①定年退職を迎えた父が、最近、料理を始めた。②母に何十年も作ってもらっていたから、包丁の使い方すら知らなかった。③最初は目玉焼きも焦がしていたが、半年経った今では、かなりの腕前になっている。④先日、父の作った肉じゃがを食べさせてもらったが、母の味にはかなわないものの、十分においしかった。⑤「お父さん、上手になったね」と言ったら、照れたように笑って、「まだまだだ」と小さく言ってくれた。⑥料理をしている父の後ろ姿を見ていたら、何だか切ない気持ちになった。⑦母が元気なうちに、こうして二人で台所に立てることが、どれほど幸せなことか——そう思うと、胸が熱くなった。

| Sentence | Divergences |
|---|---|
| ① | Head-final: 定年退職を迎えた父 (relative clause before noun). Zero anaphora: no "recently" subject needed. |
| ② | Perspective: 作ってもらっていた (receiving the benefit of mother's cooking). Aspect: もらっていた (habitual past — received for many years). |
| ③ | Aspect: 焦がしていた (habitual past — used to burn things). 腕前になっている (resultant state — has reached a skill level and remains there). |

| ⑥ | Head-final: 料理をしている父の後ろ姿 (long modifier before 後ろ姿). Aspect: 見ていたら (progressive — while watching). |
| ⑦ | Topic-prominence: implicit topic shift to a reflection. Zero anaphora: subject of 思うと and 熱くなった is narrator. |

## 20.8 Summary: The Five Divergences at a Glance

### Quick reference table

| Divergence | Japanese | English | Comprehension risk |
|---|---|---|---|
| Head-final | Modifiers precede the head noun/verb | Modifiers follow the head | Losing track of long pre-nominal chains |
| Topic-prominence | は marks the frame (topic); が marks the subject | No topic marker; subject is mandatory | Treating は as subject marker; missing topic shifts |
| Zero anaphora | Subjects, objects, and locations are routinely omitted | Pronouns are required | Losing track of who does what in multi-character passages |
| Aspect over tense | ている encodes progressive, resultant state, habitual | -ing encodes progressive only | Reading 死んでいる as "is dying" instead of "is dead" |
| Perspective encoding | 授受, passive, causative mark speaker's viewpoint | No grammatical perspective markers | Missing gratitude, adversity, and viewpoint in narration |

### The meta-strategy

When you struggle with a difficult passage, ask yourself:

1. Am I confused because the sentence is head-final and I am looking for the main element too early?
2. Am I confused because a topic was established and I lost track of it?
3. Am I confused because subjects are being dropped and I do not know who is acting?
4. Am I confused because I am reading an aspectual form as a tense?
5. Am I confused because a perspective marker (てくれる, passive, causative) is doing work that I am not noticing?

If you can identify which divergence is causing the confusion, you can resolve it. The awareness itself is the tool.
