# Chapter 22 — Completing 常用漢字

This chapter represents the culmination of the kanji strand that has run through all six stages of this textbook. You have been learning kanji since Stage 1, building from the most frequent and visually simple characters toward increasingly specialized and abstract ones. By now you know approximately 1,600 of the 2,136 常用漢字. This chapter covers the remaining ~500, organized not by stroke count or frequency but by semantic domain — because at this level, the most efficient way to learn kanji is in clusters of related meaning.

These final 500 kanji are the ones that appear in legal documents, medical reports, government proclamations, specialized academic writing, and literary texts. You will not encounter most of them in daily conversation, but you will encounter all of them in the reading material that an N1-level learner is expected to handle: newspaper editorials, novels, official documents, and professional texts.

After completing this chapter, you will have covered all 2,136 常用漢字. Section 22.3 then introduces 表外漢字 — kanji outside the official list that appear frequently in names, literature, and specialized fields.

---

## 22.1 The Final Push: Strategy and Approach

### Why the last 500 are hardest

The first few hundred kanji you learned were high-frequency characters that appear in virtually every Japanese text: 人, 日, 大, 学, 生, 出, 行, 食, etc. The kanji you are about to learn are low-frequency characters that may appear only in specific domains. You might see 璽 (imperial seal) only in reference to the Emperor's official stamp. You might see 膵 (pancreas) only in a medical context.

This means two things:

1. **You will encounter these kanji less often in natural reading.** Unlike 人 or 日, which reinforce themselves through sheer frequency, these kanji require deliberate study and periodic review.

2. **Their meanings are more specialized and their readings more predictable.** The kanji at this level tend to have one dominant reading and one core meaning. 璽 is always じ and always means "imperial seal." 膵 is always すい and always means "pancreas." This predictability is an advantage.

### The cluster approach

Rather than learning these kanji in a random order, this chapter organizes them into semantic clusters — groups of kanji that share a domain. Learning 腎, 膵, 脾, 腫, 瘍 together as a "medical/body" cluster is far more efficient than encountering them in isolation, because:

- The shared context helps you remember each character's meaning.
- You often encounter these kanji in the same texts, so learning them together prepares you for real reading.
- Many form two-kanji compounds with each other (腫瘍 = tumor, 腎臓 = kidney).

## 22.3 表外漢字 Awareness

### What are 表外漢字?

表外漢字 (ひょうがいかんじ) are kanji that fall outside the 常用漢字 list. They are not officially required for general literacy, but they appear frequently in three domains: personal and place names, literature, and specialized professional writing.

At the N1 level, you do not need to memorize these kanji, but you need to be aware that they exist and have strategies for handling them when they appear.

### Names (人名・地名)

Japanese names use many kanji outside the 常用漢字 list. The 人名用漢字 (じんめいようかんじ) list adds approximately 860 kanji that are permitted for use in personal names. Some of the most common:

|------|------|-----------------|

| 澤 | さわ | Old form of 沢: 澤田, 金澤 |
| 薗 | その | Place/family names: 御薗 |
| 斎 | さい | 斎藤 (さいとう) |
| 齋 | さい | Old form of 斎: 齋藤 |
| 邊 | へん / べ | Old form of 辺: 渡邊 (わたなべ) |
| 廣 | こう / ひろ | Old form of 広: 廣瀬 |
| 櫻 | おう | Old form of 桜: 櫻井 |

| 漢字 | 読み | 意味 | Literary context |
|------|------|------|-----------------|
| 儚い | はかない | ephemeral, fleeting | 儚い命 (fleeting life) |

## 22.7 Consolidation: How to Study the Final 500

### Recommended approach

The kanji in this chapter will not all stick on the first reading. That is expected. These are low-frequency characters that require deliberate reinforcement. Here are strategies for retaining them:

**1. Domain reading.** Read texts in the domains where these kanji appear. If you study the medical cluster, read a health article in Japanese. If you study the government cluster, read a newspaper article about politics. Real-world encounters reinforce memory far more effectively than flashcard drilling.

**2. Compound-first learning.** Do not try to learn 膵 in isolation. Learn 膵臓 (pancreas) as a unit. The compound gives the kanji context, and context is what makes memory stick.

**3. Radical recognition.** Practice identifying the radical of each new kanji and using it to determine the semantic domain. This builds a transferable skill: even for kanji you have never seen, the radical gives you a starting point.

**4. Write the difficult ones.** For the most visually complex kanji (鬱, 璽, 膵, 鷹, etc.), writing them by hand once or twice is remarkably effective. The motor memory reinforces the visual recognition.

**5. Accept gradual mastery.** You will not memorize all ~500 kanji in one sitting. Aim for recognition, not production. At the N1 level, you need to be able to read these kanji in context — not write them from memory. Focus on recognition and let production develop naturally over time.
