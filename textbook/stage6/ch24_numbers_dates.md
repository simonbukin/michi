# Chapter 24 — Numbers, Dates, and Bureaucratic Formats

Numbers are one of the last areas where even advanced learners hesitate. You can read a newspaper editorial, follow a lecture on economics, and parse a legal contract — but when a news anchor says 三千四百五十二億七千八百万円, you freeze. When a government form asks for your birthday in 和暦, you have to stop and calculate. When someone reads a phone number aloud, you miss a digit because you were still processing the last one.

This chapter addresses that gap. Japanese uses a base-ten-thousand grouping system (万, 億, 兆) rather than the base-one-thousand system used in English (thousand, million, billion). This is not a grammatical issue — it is a mental architecture issue. Your brain needs to build a new set of landmarks for large numbers. This chapter provides the practice to make that happen.

Beyond the number system itself, Japanese official life relies on the 和暦 (Japanese era calendar), specific address and phone number conventions, and document formats that you will encounter on every form, certificate, and official letter. Fluency with these formats is a practical necessity, not a textbook exercise.

---

## 24.1 Large Numbers: The 万/億/兆 System

### The core difference

English groups digits in threes:
- 1,000 = one thousand
- 1,000,000 = one million
- 1,000,000,000 = one billion
- 1,000,000,000,000 = one trillion

Japanese groups digits in fours:
- 一万 (10,000) = 1,0000
- 一億 (100,000,000) = 1,0000,0000
- 一兆 (1,000,000,000,000) = 1,0000,0000,0000

The key insight: Japanese does not have single words for "thousand," "million," or "billion" as unit markers in the same way. Instead, the units are:

| Japanese | Value | Western equivalent |
|---|---|---|
| 一 | 1 | one |
| 十 | 10 | ten |
| 百 | 100 | one hundred |
| 千 | 1,000 | one thousand |
| 一万 | 10,000 | ten thousand |
| 十万 | 100,000 | one hundred thousand |
| 百万 | 1,000,000 | one million |
| 千万 | 10,000,000 | ten million |
| 一億 | 100,000,000 | one hundred million |
| 十億 | 1,000,000,000 | one billion |
| 百億 | 10,000,000,000 | ten billion |
| 千億 | 100,000,000,000 | one hundred billion |
| 一兆 | 1,000,000,000,000 | one trillion |

### Conversion strategy

When you see a large number in English and need to express it in Japanese, mentally regroup the digits in fours from the right:

**Example:** 456,789,012,345

Step 1: Regroup in fours from the right: 4567 | 8901 | 2345

Step 2: Label each group: 4567億 | 8901万 | 2345

Step 3: Read each group: 四千五百六十七億 八千九百一万 二千三百四十五

**Going the other direction** — when you hear a Japanese number and need to convert to Western notation:

> 二兆三千四百五十億六千七百万

Step 1: Parse by unit markers: 2兆 3450億 6700万

Step 2: Write each as a full number:
- 2兆 = 2,000,000,000,000
- 3450億 = 345,000,000,000
- 6700万 = 67,000,000

Step 3: Add them: 2,345,067,000,000

When numbers appear in news broadcasts or formal speech, they are read using the on'yomi system with specific sound changes:

| Number | Reading | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 三百 | さんびゃく | Not さんひゃく |

| 三千 | さんぜん | Not さんせん |
| 八千 | はっせん | Not はちせん |
| 一兆 | いっちょう | Geminate |
| 八兆 | はっちょう | Geminate |
| 十兆 | じゅっちょう | Geminate |

### Numbers in financial contexts

Financial news uses 円 (yen) and frequently abbreviates large figures:

> 今年度の国家予算は約百十四兆円に達した。
> "This fiscal year's national budget has reached approximately 114 trillion yen."

> トヨタの売上高は前年比八パーセント増の三十七兆円だった。
> "Toyota's revenue was 37 trillion yen, an eight-percent increase year-on-year."

> この事業に二千五百億円が投じられた。
> "250 billion yen was invested in this project."

Note: Written financial documents often use Arabic numerals (114兆円, 37兆円) while broadcasts read them in full Japanese.

### 約 (やく) and おおよそ for approximation

> 約三億人が影響を受けた。
> "Approximately 300 million people were affected."

> 被害額はおおよそ五千億円と見られている。
> "The damage is estimated at roughly 500 billion yen."

### Numbers beyond 兆

For completeness, though rarely encountered:

| Unit | Value | Characters |
|---|---|---|
| 京 (けい) | 10^16 | Used in supercomputer naming (京コンピュータ) |
| 垓 (がい) | 10^20 | Essentially never used |
| 𥝱 (じょ) | 10^24 | Archaic |
| 穣 (じょう) | 10^28 | Archaic |
| 無量大数 (むりょうたいすう) | 10^68 | "Immeasurably large number" — known as trivia |

## 24.2 和暦: The Japanese Era Calendar

### The system

Japan officially uses two calendar systems simultaneously: the Western calendar (西暦, せいれき) and the Japanese era calendar (和暦, われき). Government documents, official forms, banking paperwork, and many traditional contexts use 和暦.

Each era (元号, げんごう) begins when a new Emperor ascends the throne. The first year of each era is called 元年 (がんねん), not 一年.

### Era table

| Era name | Kanji | Reading | Start date | End date |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 明治 | 明治 | めいじ | 1868年1月25日 | 1912年7月30日 |
| 大正 | 大正 | たいしょう | 1912年7月30日 | 1926年12月25日 |
| 昭和 | 昭和 | しょうわ | 1926年12月25日 | 1989年1月7日 |
| 平成 | 平成 | へいせい | 1989年1月8日 | 2019年4月30日 |
| 令和 | 令和 | れいわ | 2019年5月1日 | present |

### Conversion formulas

These formulas let you convert between 和暦 and 西暦. For each era, add the base number to the 和暦 year:

| Era | Formula (和暦 → 西暦) | Formula (西暦 → 和暦) |
|---|---|---|
| 令和 | 令和 n年 = n + 2018 | 西暦 - 2018 = 令和 n年 |
| 平成 | 平成 n年 = n + 1988 | 西暦 - 1988 = 平成 n年 |
| 昭和 | 昭和 n年 = n + 1925 | 西暦 - 1925 = 昭和 n年 |
| 大正 | 大正 n年 = n + 1911 | 西暦 - 1911 = 大正 n年 |
| 明治 | 明治 n年 = n + 1867 | 西暦 - 1867 = 明治 n年 |

### How dates are written on forms

Official documents typically use one of these formats:

**Format 1 — Full era name:**
> 令和八年三月二日

**Format 2 — Circle-the-era format (on printed forms):**
> 明治 ・ 大正 ・ 昭和 ・ 平成 ・ ⑥令和　　８年　３月　２日

The applicant circles the correct era and fills in the numbers. This format appears on nearly every government form.

**Format 3 — Abbreviated:**
> R8.3.2　（令和8年3月2日）
> H31.4.1　（平成31年4月1日）
> S45.11.23（昭和45年11月23日）

The single-letter abbreviations are: M (明治), T (大正), S (昭和), H (平成), R (令和).

| 11月 | じゅういちがつ |

Days of the month have irregular readings for 1-10, 14, 20, and 24:

### Historical context in conversation

Japanese people commonly reference eras as cultural shorthand:

> 昭和の考え方だね。
> "That's a very Showa way of thinking." (= old-fashioned, pre-bubble mentality)

> 平成生まれにはわからないよ。
> "Someone born in Heisei wouldn't understand." (= generational gap)

> 令和の時代にそんなことをまだ言っているの？
> "You're still saying that sort of thing in the Reiwa era?" (= out of date)

## 24.3 Fractions, Percentages, Decimals, and Ratios

### Fractions (分数, ぶんすう)

Japanese fractions are read bottom-up (denominator first):

> Structure: [denominator]分の[numerator]

| Fraction | Japanese | Reading |
|---|---|---|
| 1/2 | 二分の一 | にぶんのいち |
| 1/3 | 三分の一 | さんぶんのいち |
| 2/3 | 三分の二 | さんぶんのに |
| 1/4 | 四分の一 | よんぶんのいち |
| 3/4 | 四分の三 | よんぶんのさん |
| 1/5 | 五分の一 | ごぶんのいち |
| 7/8 | 八分の七 | はちぶんのなな |
| 1/10 | 十分の一 | じゅうぶんのいち |
| 1/100 | 百分の一 | ひゃくぶんのいち |

Note: 半分 (はんぶん) is the everyday word for "half," used more often than 二分の一 in casual speech.

> 世界人口の約三分の一がインターネットにアクセスできない。
> "Approximately one-third of the world's population cannot access the internet."

> この国の面積は日本の四分の一にも満たない。
> "This country's area does not even reach one-quarter of Japan's."

### Percentages (パーセント / 割)

Two systems coexist:

**Western-style:** パーセント (often abbreviated % in writing)

> 失業率は二点三パーセントに低下した。
> "The unemployment rate fell to 2.3 percent."

> 消費税は現在十パーセントです。
> "The consumption tax is currently ten percent."

**Traditional Japanese system:** 割 (わり) / 分 (ぶ) / 厘 (りん)

| Unit | Value |
|---|---|
| 一割 | 10% |
| 一分 | 1% |
| 一厘 | 0.1% |

This system survives primarily in:
- Baseball batting averages (三割二分五厘 = .325)
- Sales discounts (三割引き = 30% off)
- Traditional business contexts

> 今日のセールは全品二割引きです。
> "Everything is 20% off in today's sale."

> 彼の打率は三割一分八厘だ。
> "His batting average is .318."

### Decimals (小数, しょうすう)

The decimal point is read as 点 (てん):

| Number | Japanese | Reading |
|---|---|---|
| 0.1 | 零点一 | れいてんいち |
| 3.14 | 三点一四 | さんてんいちよん |
| 0.05 | 零点零五 | れいてんれいご |
| 12.7 | 十二点七 | じゅうにてんなな |
| 99.99 | 九十九点九九 | きゅうじゅうくてんきゅうきゅう |

Note: After the decimal point, each digit is read individually (一四, not 十四).

> 円周率は約三点一四一五九です。
> "Pi is approximately 3.14159."

> 今朝の気温は零点五度でした。
> "This morning's temperature was 0.5 degrees."

### Ratios (比, ひ)

Ratios use 対 (たい) in speech and : (colon) in writing:

| Ratio | Japanese | Reading |
|---|---|---|
| 3:2 | 三対二 | さんたいに |
| 1:1 | 一対一 | いちたいいち / いったいいち |
| 5:3 | 五対三 | ごたいさん |
| 100:1 | 百対一 | ひゃくたいいち |

> 賛成と反対の比は七対三だった。
> "The ratio of for and against was 7 to 3."

> このカクテルはジンとトニックを一対三の割合で混ぜる。
> "This cocktail mixes gin and tonic in a 1:3 ratio."

### Multiples and -fold (倍, ばい)

| Expression | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 二倍 | double, twice |
| 三倍 | triple, three times |
| 十倍 | ten times |
| 百倍 | a hundred times |
| 何倍 | how many times |

> コロナ禍で宅配便の取扱量は約一点五倍に増加した。
> "During the COVID pandemic, parcel delivery volume increased by approximately 1.5 times."

> この望遠鏡は肉眼の百倍の倍率を持つ。
> "This telescope has a magnification of 100 times that of the naked eye."

## 24.4 Phone Numbers, Addresses, and Postal Codes

### Phone numbers

Japanese phone numbers are read digit by digit, with の separating groups:

**Landline format:** (area code) - (local exchange) - (subscriber)

> 03-1234-5678
> ゼロさんの　いちにさんよんの　ごろくななはち

**Mobile format:** 090 / 080 / 070 - XXXX - XXXX

> 090-8765-4321
> ゼロきゅうゼロの　はちななろくごの　よんさんにいち

Reading conventions:
- 0 is read as ゼロ (not れい) in phone numbers
- 4 is read as よん (not し, to avoid association with 死)
- 7 is read as なな (not しち, for clarity)
- 9 is read as きゅう (not く)
- The separator is read as の

**Toll-free numbers** (フリーダイヤル):

> 0120-XXX-XXX
> ゼロいちにゼロの...

**Emergency numbers:**

| Number | Service | Reading |
|---|---|---|
| 110 | 警察 (police) | ひゃくとおばん |
| 119 | 消防・救急 (fire/ambulance) | ひゃくじゅうきゅうばん |

Note: 110 is read as ひゃくとおばん (as a set phrase, not digit by digit).

### Addresses (住所, じゅうしょ)

Japanese addresses are read from largest unit to smallest — the opposite of Western convention:

> Structure: 都道府県 → 市区町村 → 町名 → 丁目 → 番地 → 号 → building name → room number

**Example address:**

> 〒100-0001
> 東京都千代田区千代田一丁目一番一号

Read as:
> ゆうびんばんごう　ひゃくのゼロゼロゼロいち
> とうきょうと　ちよだく　ちよだ　いっちょうめ　いちばん　いちごう

### Address vocabulary

| Term | Reading | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| 都 | と | metropolis (Tokyo only) |
| 道 | どう | circuit (Hokkaido only) |
| 府 | ふ | urban prefecture (Osaka, Kyoto only) |
| 県 | けん | prefecture (43 of them) |
| 市 | し | city |
| 区 | く | ward |
| 町 | まち / ちょう | town |
| 村 | むら / そん | village |
| 丁目 | ちょうめ | city block number |
| 番地 | ばんち | lot number |
| 号 | ごう | building/unit number |

### Postal codes (郵便番号, ゆうびんばんごう)

Japanese postal codes are seven digits in the format XXX-XXXX, preceded by the 〒 symbol:

> 〒163-8001
> Read: いちろくさんの　はちゼロゼロいち

The first three digits identify the region and delivery office. The last four identify the specific area.

### How addresses appear on forms

Official forms typically break the address into fields:

```
郵便番号 [___]-[____]
都道府県 [________________]
市区町村 [________________]
町名番地 [________________]
建物名・部屋番号 [________________]
```

### Reading an envelope

Japanese vertical-format envelopes are addressed as follows:
- Right side: recipient's postal code (in the red-bordered boxes at top)
- Center: recipient's address (written vertically, right to left)
- Left of center: recipient's name with 様 (さま)
- Back: sender's address and name (smaller, on the left flap side)

> 〒150-0001
> 東京都渋谷区神宮前三丁目二十五番十八号
> 山田太郎　様
